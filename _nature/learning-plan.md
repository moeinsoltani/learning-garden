# Trees & Birds of Canada — Learning Plan

A friendly, **picture-first** field course for learning to recognise the common
trees and birds you'll actually meet in Canada — in a city park, a backyard, a
cottage lake, or a walk in the woods. You don't need any science background. By
the end you'll be able to look at a tree and say "that's a spruce, not a pine,"
look at a bird and say "that's a chickadee, not a nuthatch," and understand a
little of the natural year — who arrives in spring, who stays for winter, and
why.

This track is different from the others on the site: it is built around **real
photographs** (every one hotlinked from Wikimedia Commons with its source
credited), because you can't learn to identify a living thing from words alone.
Read each lesson beside the pictures, then go outside and match what you see.

## How this track works

Like the Canada and Leadership tracks, this is a **no-terminal** course. Each
lesson has the same shape:

- A **"Words to know"** box — plain-English definitions and pronunciations for
  any tricky term (bird and tree names get technical fast).
- **Concept** — the mental model and the pictures.
- **Going Deeper** — the details that make identification *click*.
- **Lab — Look & Identify** — a photo-and-scenario exercise: you're shown or
  told about something and asked to identify it or explain it to a friend, then
  you reveal a model answer.
- **Further Reading**, then **Checkpoint** questions and one **Homework** task,
  each with a hidden model answer.

You do not need to live near wilderness. Most of these species turn up in
ordinary cities and suburbs — the point is to make your everyday walk richer.

## Phases & lessons

### Phase 1 — Learning to Look (4 lessons)
The habit of noticing, and a repeatable method for identifying anything.
1. **Why watch trees and birds?** — what's out there, and how to use this course
2. **How to identify a tree** — needles vs leaves, bark, shape, the 30-second method
3. **How to identify a bird** — size & shape, colour, beak, behaviour, and song
4. **Tools, apps & field manners** — binoculars, Merlin & iNaturalist, a notebook, and ethics

### Phase 2 — Trees of Canada (5 lessons)
The trees you'll see most, grouped so the differences are obvious.
5. **Conifers: the evergreens** — spruce, pine, fir, cedar, hemlock, Douglas-fir
6. **The maples** — sugar, red, and silver; the leaf on the flag; syrup
7. **Broadleaf trees** — oaks, birches, aspen/poplar, and beech
8. **Iconic & provincial trees** — the national maple and the provincial emblems
9. **The forests of Canada** — boreal, Great Lakes–St. Lawrence, Acadian, Carolinian, coastal rainforest

### Phase 3 — Birds of Canada (5 lessons)
The birds you'll meet, grouped by where you meet them.
10. **Backyard & city birds** — chickadee, blue jay, robin, cardinal, sparrow, nuthatch
11. **Water & shore birds** — Canada goose, common loon, mallard, great blue heron
12. **Raptors: birds of prey** — bald eagle, red-tailed hawk, snowy owl, osprey
13. **Forest birds** — downy & pileated woodpeckers, ruffed grouse, Canada jay
14. **Iconic Canadian birds** — the loon, the Canada jay, the snowy owl, and the goose

### Phase 4 — Seasons, Habitats & Field Skills (2 lessons)
Putting it together across the year.
15. **Birds through the seasons** — migration, winter stayers, and feeding birds
16. **A walk in the woods (capstone)** — read a real scene, plus a final quiz

## A note on the photographs

Every image is a real Wikimedia Commons photograph, embedded at ~960 px with a
caption crediting the source. Photos show a *typical* individual — real trees
and birds vary with season, age, sex, region, and light, so treat each picture
as "the idea of the species," not a fingerprint. That variation is exactly why
Lesson 02 and Lesson 03 teach a *method*, not a memory game.
