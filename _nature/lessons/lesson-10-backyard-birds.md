# Lesson 10: Backyard & City Birds

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **feeder** — a container of seed/suet hung out to attract birds; the easiest way to watch them.
> - **cap** — a patch of colour on the top of a bird's head (the chickadee's black cap).
> - **bib / throat patch** — a patch of colour on the throat/chest.
> - **crest** — a peak of raised feathers on the head (the cardinal and blue jay have one).
> - **suet** (SOO-it) — hard animal fat put out for birds; loved by woodpeckers and nuthatches.
> - **nuthatch** (NUT-hatch) — a small bird famous for climbing *down* tree trunks head-first.
> - **resident** — a bird that stays all year (vs. a migrant that leaves for winter).

## Concept

The fastest way into birding is a **feeder or a city park**, because the same
handful of bold, tame birds show up again and again — perfect for practising the
size → shape → behaviour method. Meet the six you'll see most in eastern and
central Canada. (In the West you'll swap in a few cousins, but the skills carry
over.)

![A black-capped chickadee: tiny, round, black cap and bib, white cheeks, grey back](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Poecile-atricapilla-001.jpg/960px-Poecile-atricapilla-001.jpg)

***Black-capped chickadee** — start here. Tiny and round, **black cap and black
bib, white cheeks**, grey back. Fearless, acrobatic, and vocal (*chicka-dee-dee-dee*).
Canada's friendliest bird and a year-round resident. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Poecile-atricapilla-001.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### The backyard six

![A blue jay: large, bright blue with a crest, black necklace, white face](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Blue_jay_in_PP_%2830960%29.jpg/960px-Blue_jay_in_PP_%2830960%29.jpg)

***Blue jay** — big, brash, and unmistakable: **bright blue** with a crest, white
face, and a black "necklace." Loud (*jay! jay!*), bold, and a member of the clever
crow family. A resident that also loves acorns. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Blue_jay_in_PP_%2830960%29.jpg).*

![An American robin: grey-brown back, brick-red breast, yellow bill](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/American_robin_%2871307%29.jpg/960px-American_robin_%2871307%29.jpg)

***American robin** — the "medium" reference bird: grey-brown back, **brick-red
breast**, yellow bill. Famous for hopping across lawns, cocking its head, and
yanking up worms. The classic first-sign-of-spring bird (though some stay all
winter). Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:American_robin_%2871307%29.jpg).*

![A male northern cardinal: brilliant red, black face mask, orange bill, crest](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Male_northern_cardinal_in_Central_Park_%2852612%29.jpg/960px-Male_northern_cardinal_in_Central_Park_%2852612%29.jpg)

***Northern cardinal** — the **brilliant red** male with a pointed crest, black
face mask, and thick orange seed-cracking bill. The female is warm tan with red
touches. A resident that has spread north into Canada; loud, clear *cheer-cheer-cheer*
whistles. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Male_northern_cardinal_in_Central_Park_%2852612%29.jpg).*

![A white-throated sparrow: brown streaked, white throat, yellow spot by the eye](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Zonotrichia_albicollis_CT1.jpg/960px-Zonotrichia_albicollis_CT1.jpg)

***White-throated sparrow** — the sparrow worth knowing first: brown and streaky
but with a crisp **white throat**, striped crown, and a tiny **yellow spot before
the eye**. Its whistled song is often heard as **"Oh-sweet-Canada-Canada-Canada."**
Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Zonotrichia_albicollis_CT1.jpg).*

![A white-breasted nuthatch clinging head-down on a trunk](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/White-breasted_nuthatch_%2826471%29.jpg/960px-White-breasted_nuthatch_%2826471%29.jpg)

***White-breasted nuthatch** — small, blue-grey above, white face and underside,
black cap. The **only common bird that climbs *down* a trunk head-first** — that
behaviour alone identifies it. Nasal *yank-yank* call. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:White-breasted_nuthatch_%2826471%29.jpg).*

### Chickadee vs. nuthatch — the classic beginner mix-up

Both are small, grey, and black-capped at a feeder. Separate them instantly by
**behaviour and face**:

| | Chickadee | Nuthatch |
|---|---|---|
| **Face** | Black cap **and black bib**, white cheeks | Black cap, **clean white face, no bib** |
| **Shape** | Round, fluffy, tiny bill | Flatter, **long pointed bill**, "no neck" |
| **Behaviour** | Flits, hangs upside-down | **Walks head-first *down* the trunk** |
| **Voice** | *chicka-dee-dee-dee* | Nasal *yank-yank* |

### Male vs. female: don't be fooled

The cardinal is the headline case: **males are red, females tan**. Same species,
very different look — a live reminder of Lesson 03's rule that **colour confirms;
shape and behaviour decide.** The crest, mask, and heavy bill say "cardinal"
regardless of colour.

{: .note }
> **Start your list with these six**
> Chickadee (black cap + bib), blue jay (blue + crest + necklace), robin (red
> breast, lawn-hopper), cardinal (red + crest + mask, or tan female), white-throated
> sparrow (white throat + "Oh-sweet-Canada"), nuthatch (head-first down the trunk).
> Learn these and most feeder visitors are already familiar.

## Lab — Look & Identify

**Exercise 1 — chickadee or nuthatch?** A small grey bird with a black cap is
working a tree trunk. It's walking **head-first down** the trunk, has a **clean
white face with no black bib**, and a long pointed bill. Which is it, and which
single clue is decisive?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
It's a <strong>white-breasted nuthatch</strong>. The decisive clue is the
<strong>behaviour — climbing *down* the trunk head-first</strong> (no other common
backyard bird does this); the clean white face with no bib and the long bill
confirm it. A chickadee would have a black bib, a stubby bill, and would flit and
hang rather than walk down.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the red-bird trap.** At a feeder you see a warm tan bird with a
pointed crest, a black patch around a thick orange bill — but it isn't red. Your
friend says "not a cardinal, it's not red." Correct them.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "It <em>is</em> a cardinal — a <strong>female</strong>. Only the male is
brilliant red; the female is tan with red highlights. The giveaways aren't the
colour but the <em>shape and marks</em>: the pointed crest, the black around the
face, and the heavy orange seed-cracking bill. Colour confirms; shape decides."
Full marks for using structure over colour, exactly as Lesson 03 taught.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — song scenario.** On a spring walk you hear a clear, wavering
whistle that a birder friend hums back as "Oh-sweet-Canada-Canada." You never see
the bird. What was it, and what does this show about birding?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
It's a <strong>white-throated sparrow</strong>, whose song is famously heard as
"Oh-sweet-Canada-Canada-Canada." It shows that <strong>sound alone can identify a
bird</strong> you never see — many species are detected by ear far more often than
by eye, which is why learning a handful of signature songs (or using an app's
Sound ID) hugely expands what you can find.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Give the single best field mark for the black-capped chickadee, the blue
jay, and the American robin.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Chickadee</strong> — tiny and round with a <strong>black cap and black
bib over white cheeks</strong>. <strong>Blue jay</strong> — large, <strong>bright
blue with a crest</strong> and black necklace. <strong>Robin</strong> — grey-brown
back with a <strong>brick-red breast</strong>, hopping on lawns.
</details>

**Q2.** How do you tell a chickadee from a nuthatch at a feeder, using one
behaviour and one facial mark?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Behaviour:</strong> the nuthatch walks <em>head-first down</em> the trunk;
the chickadee flits and hangs upside-down but doesn't descend trunks head-first.
<strong>Face:</strong> the chickadee has a black <em>bib</em> below a black cap;
the nuthatch has a <em>clean white face with no bib</em> (and a longer, pointed
bill).
</details>

**Q3.** Why can the northern cardinal fool a beginner, and what should you look at
instead of colour?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because only the <strong>male is bright red</strong>; the <strong>female is tan</strong>,
so a beginner keyed on "red = cardinal" misses her. Look instead at
<strong>shape and marks</strong> — the pointed crest, the black face mask, and the
thick orange seed-cracking bill — which identify a cardinal regardless of colour.
</details>

## Homework

Watch a feeder, park, or yard for ten minutes (or set up a simple feeder). Record
every bird you see using the method, and try to name at least **two** of the six
from this lesson. For any bird you can't name, write its size, shape, one colour
mark, and one behaviour so you could look it up later.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong answer logs birds by structure and behaviour, e.g.: "Tiny round bird,
black cap + bib, hung upside-down → <strong>black-capped chickadee</strong>.
Medium bird, red breast, hopping on the grass → <strong>American robin</strong>.
Unknown: sparrow-sized, streaky brown, white throat, whistled a tune — note it and
check later (likely a <strong>white-throated sparrow</strong>)." Full marks for
confidently naming two of the six and describing the rest well enough to identify
afterward.
</details>
