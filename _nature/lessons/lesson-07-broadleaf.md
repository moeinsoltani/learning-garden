# Lesson 07: Broadleaf Trees

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **broadleaf** — a tree with wide flat leaves (as opposed to needles); most are deciduous.
> - **oak** — a broadleaf tree with lobed leaves and **acorns** for seeds.
> - **acorn** — the nut of an oak, sitting in a little cup.
> - **birch** — a slender tree with papery, often white, peeling bark.
> - **aspen / poplar** — fast-growing trees with leaves that flutter in the slightest breeze.
> - **beech** — a tree with smooth grey bark and toothed oval leaves; seeds are "beechnuts."
> - **catkin** (KAT-kin) — a dangling, caterpillar-shaped cluster of tiny flowers (birch, aspen, oak).
> - **deciduous** (deh-SIH-joo-us) — dropping all leaves each autumn.

## Concept

Beyond the maples, four broadleaf trees show up constantly across Canada — the
**oaks**, **birches**, **aspens/poplars**, and **beeches**. Each has one
signature you can spot from across a field:

- **Oak** → **lobed** leaves and **acorns**. Strong, heavy, long-lived.
- **Birch** → **white papery bark** that peels in horizontal strips.
- **Aspen** → leaves that **tremble** in the faintest breeze, on smooth
  greenish-white bark.
- **Beech** → **smooth pale-grey bark** (like an elephant's leg) and toothed oval
  leaves that hang on, tan and dry, into winter.

![A large red oak with a broad, rounded crown](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Quercus_rubra_%40_Tortworth_Court.jpg/960px-Quercus_rubra_%40_Tortworth_Court.jpg)

***Red oak** — a big, strong shade tree with **pointed, bristle-tipped lobes** on
its leaves and rounded acorns in shallow caps. "Red oak group" leaves have
*pointed* lobes; "white oak group" leaves have *rounded* lobes — a useful split.
Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Quercus_rubra_%40_Tortworth_Court.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### Oaks: lobes + acorns, in two camps

All oaks make **acorns** and mostly have **lobed** leaves, but they fall into two
groups worth knowing:

- **Red oak group** — leaf lobes end in **points/bristles**; acorns take ~2 years
  to ripen and are bitter. (Red oak, black oak, pin oak.)
- **White oak group** — leaf lobes are **rounded**; acorns ripen in one year and
  are sweeter. (Bur oak, white oak, swamp white oak.)

![A huge, spreading open-grown bur oak](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Big_Tree_with_spring_picnic.jpg/960px-Big_Tree_with_spring_picnic.jpg)

***Bur oak** — a white-oak-group giant of the prairies and river valleys, with
rounded leaf lobes, thick fire-resistant bark, and big fringed-cap acorns. It's
the oak that ventures farthest into the dry Canadian Prairies. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Big_Tree_with_spring_picnic.jpg).*

### Birches: read the bark

![Slender white paper birches with bright peeling bark](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/White_birch_at_Acadia_National_Park,_ME_IMG_2177.JPG?width=960)

***Paper birch** (white birch) — chalk-**white bark** that peels in papery
horizontal strips, with dark horizontal dashes (lenticels). The classic canoe
bark of the northern forest and the provincial tree of Saskatchewan. Small
toothed oval leaves; dangling catkins. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:White_birch_at_Acadia_National_Park,_ME_IMG_2177.JPG).*

Not every birch is white (yellow birch has shiny bronze, curling bark), but the
peeling-in-strips habit is the family giveaway.

### Aspens & poplars: the trembling leaf

![A grove of trembling aspens in golden autumn colour](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/2013-10-06_15_04_21_Aspens_during_autumn_along_the_Changing_Canyon_Nature_Trail_in_Lamoille_Canyon%2C_Nevada.jpg/960px-2013-10-06_15_04_21_Aspens_during_autumn_along_the_Changing_Canyon_Nature_Trail_in_Lamoille_Canyon%2C_Nevada.jpg)

***Trembling aspen** — Canada's most widespread tree. Its round leaves sit on
**flattened stalks**, so they flutter ("tremble") in the lightest wind; bark is
smooth greenish-white. Aspens grow in **clones** — a whole grove can be one
organism sharing a root system, turning gold together in fall. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:2013-10-06_15_04_21_Aspens_during_autumn_along_the_Changing_Canyon_Nature_Trail_in_Lamoille_Canyon%2C_Nevada.jpg).*

### Beech: the elephant's leg

![An American beech with smooth grey bark and low branches](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Beech_with_Branches.jpg/960px-Beech_with_Branches.jpg)

***American beech** — unmistakable **smooth, pale-grey bark** (the "elephant's
leg" tree, sadly a favourite for carved initials). Toothed oval leaves turn
coppery and often **cling through winter**; seeds are triangular beechnuts prized
by wildlife. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Beech_with_Branches.jpg).*

{: .note }
> **One field mark each**
> **Oak** = lobes + acorns. **Birch** = white bark peeling in strips.
> **Aspen** = leaves trembling on flat stalks, smooth pale bark. **Beech** =
> smooth grey "elephant" bark + tan leaves clinging in winter. Any one of these
> is usually enough to call the tree.

## Lab — Look & Identify

**Exercise 1 — one mark each.** Match the field mark to the tree: (a) chalk-white
bark peeling in horizontal papery strips; (b) round leaves fluttering on flat
stalks; (c) lobed leaves with pointed bristle tips + acorns; (d) smooth pale-grey
bark with dead tan leaves clinging in January.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) → <strong>paper birch</strong>; (b) → <strong>trembling aspen</strong>;
(c) → <strong>oak</strong> (red-oak group, given the pointed bristle-tipped
lobes); (d) → <strong>American beech</strong>. Each of these is a "single-mark"
tree — one glance at the right feature names it.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the two oak camps.** You find two oaks: one has **pointed,
bristle-tipped** leaf lobes; the other has **rounded** lobes and grows on the dry
Prairies with fringed-cap acorns. Which group is each, and name the likely
Prairie species.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Pointed/bristle-tipped lobes → <strong>red oak group</strong>. Rounded lobes,
Prairie habitat, fringed acorn caps → <strong>white oak group</strong>,
specifically <strong>bur oak</strong> (the oak that ranges farthest onto the dry
Canadian Prairies, helped by thick fire-resistant bark). Lobe shape (pointed vs
rounded) is the fast group split for oaks.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — scenario.** Walking a boreal trail in October, a friend points at a
whole hillside of trees all turning gold at once and asks why they're perfectly
synchronised. Explain in 2–3 sentences.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "Those are <strong>trembling aspens</strong>, and a grove like that is
often a single <em>clone</em> — many trunks sharing one root system and the same
genes. Because they're effectively one organism, they change colour together, so
the whole hillside turns gold at the same moment. (Some aspen clones are among the
largest and oldest living things on Earth.)" Full marks for the clone idea —
identical genetics + shared roots → synchronised autumn.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Give the single most reliable field mark for each: oak, birch, aspen,
beech.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Oak</strong> — lobed leaves + <strong>acorns</strong>. <strong>Birch</strong>
— <strong>white bark peeling in papery horizontal strips</strong>.
<strong>Aspen</strong> — leaves that <strong>tremble on flattened stalks</strong>,
smooth greenish-white bark. <strong>Beech</strong> — <strong>smooth pale-grey
bark</strong> plus tan dead leaves that cling into winter.
</details>

**Q2.** How do you split the oaks into their two groups, and what differs besides
leaf shape?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Red-oak group</strong> has <strong>pointed, bristle-tipped</strong> leaf
lobes; <strong>white-oak group</strong> has <strong>rounded</strong> lobes.
Besides leaf shape: red-group acorns take about two years to ripen and are
bitter, while white-group acorns ripen in one year and are sweeter. Bur oak and
white oak are white-group; red, black, and pin oak are red-group.
</details>

**Q3.** Why can a whole aspen grove turn colour at once?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because a trembling-aspen grove is frequently a single <strong>clone</strong> —
many trunks sprouting from one shared root system with identical genes — so they
behave as one organism and change colour in unison. This clonal habit also makes
aspen among the largest and oldest living organisms known.
</details>
