# Lesson 06: The Maples

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **maple** — a broadleaf tree of the genus *Acer*; the leaf on Canada's flag.
> - **lobe** — a pointed or rounded section of a leaf; maple leaves have several lobes meeting at the middle.
> - **opposite leaves** — leaves that grow in matching pairs across the twig (maples do this; most trees don't).
> - **samara** (SAM-ar-ah) — the winged seed of a maple; the "helicopter" or "key" that spins as it falls.
> - **sap** — the watery fluid inside a tree; sugar maple sap is boiled down into maple syrup.
> - **sugar bush** — a stand of sugar maples tapped for syrup.
> - **serrated / toothed** — with a saw-like edge; silver maple leaves are deeply cut and toothed.

## Concept

If you learn one broadleaf tree in Canada, make it the **maple** — it's on the
flag, it makes the syrup, and it paints the country red and orange every
October. All maples share two easy tells:

1. **Opposite leaves** — the leaves and twigs grow in matching **pairs**, not
   staggered. (Most trees are "alternate"; maples are the common exception.)
2. **Lobed, hand-shaped leaves** with a few pointed lobes, plus winged
   "helicopter" seeds (**samaras**) that spin down in pairs.

![The flag of Canada, centred on a stylised red maple leaf](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Flag_of_Canada_%28Pantone%29.svg/960px-Flag_of_Canada_%28Pantone%29.svg.png)

*The flag's stylised leaf (11 points, designed for clarity) is inspired by the
maples — the sugar maple above all. Adopted in 1965, it made the maple leaf
Canada's defining symbol. Image:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Flag_of_Canada_%28Pantone%29.svg).*

The three maples you'll meet most are the **sugar**, **red**, and **silver**
maple. Tell them apart by the *notches between the lobes* and the *leaf underside*.

![A large sugar maple in green summer foliage](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Acer_saccharum_1-jgreenlee_%285098070608%29.jpg/960px-Acer_saccharum_1-jgreenlee_%285098070608%29.jpg)

***Sugar maple** — the syrup tree and the fall-colour champion (fiery
orange-to-red). Leaves have **five lobes** with smooth-sided, **U-shaped** notches
between them and only a few large teeth. The hardest, most valued maple wood, and
the emblem behind the flag. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Acer_saccharum_1-jgreenlee_%285098070608%29.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### Telling the three maples apart

| Maple | Leaf notches | Underside | Fall colour | Vibe |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sugar** | Smooth, **U-shaped** gaps; few teeth | Pale green | Brilliant **orange-red** | The syrup & flag tree; firm, refined leaf |
| **Red** | Shallower; **toothed** edges; often 3 main lobes | Whitish | Early, deep **red** | Red twigs, buds, and leafstalks year-round |
| **Silver** | **Deep**, narrow, **V-shaped** cuts; very toothed | **Silvery-white** | Pale yellow | Ragged, lacy leaf; fast-growing, brittle |

![A red maple with reddish foliage](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Acer_rubrum_15-p.bot-a.rubrum-114.jpg/960px-Acer_rubrum_15-p.bot-a.rubrum-114.jpg)

***Red maple** — named for red *everywhere*: red buds in late winter, red
flowers in early spring, red leafstalks, and the first tree to blaze red in
autumn. Its leaf notches are shallower and more toothed than the sugar maple's.
The most widespread tree in eastern North America. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Acer_rubrum_15-p.bot-a.rubrum-114.jpg).*

![A silver maple with deeply cut, lacy leaves](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Silber-Ahorn_%28Acer_saccharinum%29.jpg/960px-Silber-Ahorn_%28Acer_saccharinum%29.jpg)

***Silver maple** — leaves cut **deeply** into narrow, toothed lobes with a
**silvery-white underside** that flashes when the wind lifts them. A fast-growing
riverside tree, often planted in cities, but brittle and prone to dropping limbs.
Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Silber-Ahorn_%28Acer_saccharinum%29.jpg).*

### The flag and the leaf

Canada's flag (adopted **1965**) centres on a single red maple leaf — a stylised
**11-point** design, not a copy of any one species, chosen so it stays crisp on a
windblown flag. The maple had already been a Canadian emblem for over a century
(on coins, badges, and the coat of arms). No single "maple leaf" species is
*the* national tree by law, but the **sugar maple** is the one most associated
with the symbol, the syrup, and the autumn blaze.

### Maple syrup: why the sugar maple

In late winter, when days thaw and nights still freeze, pressure changes push
**sap** up the sugar maple's trunk. Tap the tree, collect the slightly sweet sap,
and boil off the water — about **40 litres of sap make 1 litre of syrup**. Quebec
produces the large majority of the world's maple syrup. Red and silver maples can
be tapped too, but the sugar maple's sap is sweetest, which is why the "sugar
bush" is a sugar-maple stand.

{: .note }
> **The one-glance maple test**
> Pairs of leaves (opposite) + a hand-shaped lobed leaf + spinning "helicopter"
> seeds = a **maple**. Then check the notches: smooth **U** = sugar; shallow &
> toothed with red parts = red; deep narrow **V** with silver underside = silver.

## Lab — Look & Identify

**Exercise 1 — notch reading.** Three maple leaves: (a) five lobes with smooth,
rounded U-shaped gaps and few teeth; (b) leaf cut very deeply into narrow toothed
lobes, silvery-white underneath; (c) shallow toothed lobes on a twig with red
buds and red leafstalks. Name each maple.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) smooth U-shaped notches, few teeth → <strong>sugar maple</strong>; (b) deep
narrow V-cuts, silvery underside → <strong>silver maple</strong>; (c) shallow
toothed lobes with red buds/stalks → <strong>red maple</strong>. The notch depth
and the leaf underside are the fastest tells among the three.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — scenario (syrup).** A newcomer asks: "Why is maple syrup a *Canada*
thing, and why the sugar maple specifically?" Answer in 3–4 sentences.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "Maple syrup needs a very specific climate — freezing nights and thawing
days in late winter — that pushes sweet sap up the tree, and eastern Canada
(especially Quebec) has exactly that, plus vast forests of sugar maples. The
<em>sugar</em> maple is the tree of choice because its sap is the sweetest, so it
takes less boiling — though it still takes about 40 litres of sap to make 1 litre
of syrup. Indigenous peoples were making maple sugar long before Europeans
arrived, and Quebec now makes most of the world's supply." Full marks for linking
climate + sugar-maple sweetness + the huge boil-down ratio.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — the flag question.** Someone says "the flag's leaf is a sugar maple
leaf." Is that exactly right? Explain in 2 sentences.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "Not exactly — the flag's leaf (adopted 1965) is a <em>stylised</em>
11-point design meant to look clear on a flag, not a botanically accurate copy of
any single species. It's inspired by Canada's maples in general, with the sugar
maple the species most associated with the symbol." Full marks for the nuance:
it's an emblem, not a leaf specimen.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What two features do *all* maples share that let you call "maple" before
you look at the notches?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
(1) <strong>Opposite leaves</strong> — they grow in matching pairs across the
twig (an uncommon arrangement). (2) <strong>Lobed, hand-shaped leaves</strong>
plus paired winged <strong>samaras</strong> ("helicopter"/"key" seeds) that spin
as they fall.
</details>

**Q2.** How do you distinguish sugar, red, and silver maple by their leaves?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Sugar:</strong> smooth <strong>U-shaped</strong> notches between five
lobes, few teeth, pale-green underside. <strong>Red:</strong> shallower,
<strong>toothed</strong> lobes, plus red buds/flowers/leafstalks. <strong>Silver:</strong>
<strong>deep narrow V-shaped</strong> cuts, very toothed, with a
<strong>silvery-white underside</strong>.
</details>

**Q3.** Roughly how much sap makes one litre of maple syrup, and why is the sugar
maple preferred for it?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
About <strong>40 litres of sap → 1 litre of syrup</strong> (boiling off the
water). The sugar maple is preferred because its sap has the highest sugar
content, so it takes the least boiling; hence a tapped sugar-maple stand is
called a "sugar bush." Quebec produces most of the world's maple syrup.
</details>

## Homework

Find a maple (a park, a street tree, a yard). Confirm it's a maple using the
*opposite leaves* + *lobed leaf* + *helicopter seeds* test, then decide which of
the three species it is from the leaf notches and underside. Note anything that
made you unsure (city maples include the introduced **Norway maple**, whose sap is
milky when you snap a leafstalk — a handy tie-breaker).

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong answer confirms "maple" first (opposite paired leaves, lobed hand-shaped
blade, winged seeds), then reads the notches, e.g.: "Smooth U-shaped notches, few
teeth, pale underside → <strong>sugar maple</strong>." If the leaf looked like a
sugar maple but the tree oozed <em>milky</em> sap from a snapped leafstalk and the
leaf was very broad, it's the introduced <strong>Norway maple</strong>, common in
cities — a good example of why one extra check (the milky sap) resolves a
look-alike. Full marks for confirming the genus before naming the species.
</details>
