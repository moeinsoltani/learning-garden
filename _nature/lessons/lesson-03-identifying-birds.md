# Lesson 03: How to Identify a Bird

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **field mark** — a single visible feature used to identify a bird (eye-ring, wing bar, black cap).
> - **plumage** (PLOO-mij) — a bird's feathers, taken together; their colours and pattern.
> - **bill** — a bird's beak. Its shape tells you what the bird eats.
> - **GISS** or **jizz** (rhymes with "fizz") — a bird's overall "feel": size, shape, and the way it moves. Birders identify a lot by jizz before they see any detail.
> - **raptor** (RAP-tor) — a bird of prey (hawk, eagle, owl) with a hooked bill and talons.
> - **passerine** (PASS-er-ine) — a "perching bird" / songbird; most small birds you see.
> - **song vs. call** — a **song** is the longer musical phrase (often to attract a mate); a **call** is a short note (alarm, contact).
> - **eye-ring** — a ring of colour around the eye; **wing bar** — a stripe across the folded wing. Both are classic field marks.

## Concept

Birds don't hold still like trees, so you learn to read them **fast and in a
fixed order** — the same order every experienced birder uses:

1. **Size & shape** — sparrow-small? robin-medium? crow-big? Chunky or slim?
   Long tail, long legs, long bill?
2. **Colour & pattern** — main body colour, plus any obvious marks (a black cap,
   a red chest, a white wing bar, an eye-ring).
3. **Behaviour** — what is it *doing*? Creeping down a trunk head-first?
   Hovering? Bobbing its tail? Hammering wood?
4. **Habitat** — where are you? Pond, forest, feeder, open sky?
5. **Sound** — its song or call, often the surest clue of all.

Beginners reach for **colour** first; experts reach for **size, shape, and
behaviour** first (together called **GISS** or **jizz**). Train yourself toward
the expert habit — colour lies (light, sex, and season change it), but a
bird's *shape and way of moving* are steady.

![A black-capped chickadee — a tiny round grey bird with a black cap and bib and white cheeks — perched on a twig](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Poecile-atricapilla-001.jpg/960px-Poecile-atricapilla-001.jpg)

*Read this bird with the method: **size** — tiny (smaller than a sparrow);
**shape** — round, big-headed, short-billed; **pattern** — black cap and black
bib, white cheeks, grey back; **behaviour** — acrobatic, tame, often hanging
upside-down. That combination is a **black-capped chickadee** and nothing else.
Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Poecile-atricapilla-001.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### Size: measure against birds you know

You can't guess centimetres in the field, so measure against three reference
birds almost everyone knows:

| Reference | Length | Use it for |
|---|---|---|
| **House sparrow** | ~16 cm | "sparrow-sized" small birds (chickadee, finch, warbler) |
| **American robin** | ~25 cm | "robin-sized" medium birds (jay, cardinal, blackbird) |
| **American crow** | ~45 cm | "crow-sized" large birds (hawk, duck, gull) |

"Smaller than a sparrow, round, big-headed" already points at chickadee, kinglet,
or nuthatch — before you've noted a single colour.

### Shape and bill: what a bird *is* and *eats*

- **Bill shape = diet.** Short cone (seed-cracker: sparrow, finch, cardinal);
  thin pointed (insect-picker: warbler, wren); chisel (wood-driller: woodpecker);
  hooked (meat-tearer: hawk, owl); long dagger (fish-spearer: heron); flat
  (dabbler: duck).
- **Body shape.** Plump vs. slim; short-tailed vs. long-tailed; long-legged
  wader vs. short-legged perncher.

### Behaviour: the giveaway most beginners ignore

Behaviour often names a bird faster than colour:

- **Creeping *down* a trunk head-first** → nuthatch (nothing else does this).
- **Creeping *up* a trunk, stiff tail braced** → woodpecker or creeper.
- **Bobbing/pumping its tail** on the ground → phoebe, wagtail, waterthrush.
- **Hovering** over a field → kestrel or rough-legged hawk.
- **Hammering wood** → woodpecker.
- **Walking on water's edge, then a slow deliberate stab** → heron.

### Sound: learn the top ten calls

Many birds are heard far more than seen. You don't need perfect pitch — just a
handful of "signature" sounds: the chickadee's own name (*chicka-dee-dee-dee*),
the blue jay's harsh *jay! jay!*, the robin's cheerful *cheerily-cheer-up*, the
white-throated sparrow's whistled *Oh-sweet-Canada-Canada*, the loon's wail. A
free app (Lesson 04) can name a song from your phone's microphone — a
game-changer for beginners.

{: .note }
> **Colour lies; shape and behaviour don't**
> The same species can look bright or dull depending on **sex** (male cardinals
> are red, females tan), **age**, **season** (many birds are drab in winter),
> and **light**. That's why pros lead with size, shape, and behaviour. Use
> colour to *confirm*, not to decide.

### Don't over-reach at first

"A small songbird," "a duck," "a hawk of some kind" are all fine first calls.
Group first (Is it a raptor? a duck? a songbird?), then narrow. Precision comes
with repetition, exactly as with trees.

**Exercise 1 — run the order.** For the chickadee photo above, list what you'd
note at each of the first three steps (size & shape, colour & pattern,
behaviour), in order.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(1) <strong>Size & shape:</strong> very small — smaller than a sparrow — round,

**Exercise 2 — bill = diet.** You're told about four birds by bill only: (a) a
short thick cone-shaped bill, (b) a long dagger-like bill, (c) a strong hooked
bill, (d) a chisel-like straight bill. Match each to a diet and a likely bird
group: seeds, fish, meat, wood-drilling.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) cone bill → <strong>seeds</strong> → sparrow/finch/cardinal; (b) dagger bill
→ <strong>fish</strong> → heron; (c) hooked bill → <strong>meat</strong> →
raptor (hawk/eagle/owl); (d) chisel bill → <strong>wood-drilling for insects</strong>
→ woodpecker. The shape of the tool tells you the job — a rule that holds across
the whole bird world.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — scenario.** A friend proudly says "I saw a bright red bird, so it
must be a cardinal." Gently explain, in 2–3 sentences, why colour alone can
mislead and what else they should have checked.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "It might well be a cardinal — but red alone isn't proof. Only the
<em>male</em> cardinal is bright red; females are tan, and a few other red-ish
birds exist (house finch, scarlet tanager, even a robin in odd light). The tells
that would clinch it are <em>shape and marks</em>: a cardinal has a pointed crest,
a thick orange seed-bill, and a black mask around the face. Check size, shape,
and behaviour, then let colour confirm." Full marks for the principle — colour
confirms, shape and behaviour decide.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** In what order do experienced birders read a bird, and which clue do
beginners over-rely on?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Order: <strong>size & shape → colour & pattern → behaviour → habitat → sound</strong>.
Beginners over-rely on <strong>colour</strong>, which changes with sex, age,
season, and light; shape, behaviour, and sound are far more reliable and should
come first.
</details>

**Q2.** Name the three reference birds used to judge size, and give an example of
a bird each one helps you gauge.

<strong>House sparrow</strong> (~16 cm) for small birds like a chickadee or
finch; <strong>American robin</strong> (~25 cm) for medium birds like a jay or
cardinal; <strong>American crow</strong> (~45 cm) for large birds like a hawk,

**Q3.** Give two behaviours that identify a bird almost on their own, and say

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>

hammering/drilling wood → <strong>woodpecker</strong>; hovering over a field →
<strong>kestrel</strong> (or rough-legged hawk); slow deliberate stab at the
