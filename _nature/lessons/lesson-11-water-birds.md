# Lesson 11: Water & Shore Birds

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **waterfowl** (WAH-ter-fowl) — swimming birds: ducks, geese, and swans.
> - **dabbling duck** — a duck that tips forward ("bottoms up") to feed at the surface, like a mallard.
> - **diving bird** — one that dives fully underwater to feed, like a loon.
> - **wader** — a long-legged bird that stands in shallow water to hunt, like a heron.
> - **drake** — a male duck; the **hen** is the female.
> - **loon** — a heavy diving bird of northern lakes with a haunting call; on the Canadian $1 coin ("loonie").
> - **plumage** (PLOO-mij) — feather colours; ducks show strong male/female differences.
> - **V-formation** — the flying line geese form to save energy.

## Concept

Any pond, lake, river, or shoreline adds a whole new set of birds — and they're
**easy to watch** because they're out in the open on the water. Four cover most
of what a beginner meets in Canada: the **Canada goose**, the **mallard**, the
**common loon**, and the **great blue heron**. Sort them first by *what they do on
the water*: geese graze and swim, dabbling ducks tip forward, loons dive right
under, and herons stand and stab.

![A Canada goose: black head and neck, white chin-strap, brown body](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Canada_goose.jpg/960px-Canada_goose.jpg)

***Canada goose** — the national icon you already know: **black head and neck with
a white "chin-strap,"** brown body, honking loudly in **V-formation** overhead.
Grazes on grass (parks, fields), swims, and is fiercely protective of its young.
Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Canada_goose.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### The four, by behaviour

![A pair of mallards: the green-headed drake and the mottled brown hen](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Anas_platyrhynchos_male_female_quadrat.jpg/960px-Anas_platyrhynchos_male_female_quadrat.jpg)

***Mallard** — the default duck. The **drake** has a glossy **green head**, yellow
bill, and white neck-ring; the **hen** is mottled brown (both show a blue wing
patch). A **dabbling** duck: it tips "bottoms-up" to feed at the surface rather
than diving. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Anas_platyrhynchos_male_female_quadrat.jpg).*

![A common loon: black head, checkerboard back, red eye, dagger bill](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Gavia_immer_-Minocqua%2C_Wisconsin%2C_USA_-swimming-8.jpg/960px-Gavia_immer_-Minocqua%2C_Wisconsin%2C_USA_-swimming-8.jpg)

***Common loon** — the sound of the northern wilderness. Breeding adults have a
glossy **black head, red eye, a dagger bill, and a checkerboard back**. It sits
**low** in the water and **dives** for fish, staying under a long time. Its wailing,
yodelling call is unforgettable — and it's on the **$1 "loonie" coin**. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Gavia_immer_-Minocqua%2C_Wisconsin%2C_USA_-swimming-8.jpg).*

![A great blue heron standing tall in a river, long neck and dagger bill](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/GreatBlueHeronInARiver.jpg/960px-GreatBlueHeronInARiver.jpg)

***Great blue heron** — the tall, statue-still **wader**: long legs, long S-curved
neck, blue-grey body, and a heavy **dagger bill** for spearing fish and frogs. It
stalks the shallows slowly, then strikes. In flight it folds its neck into an "S"
and trails its legs. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:GreatBlueHeronInARiver.jpg).*

### Goose vs. duck vs. loon vs. heron — the quick sort

| Bird | Sits on water? | Feeds by… | Best mark |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Canada goose** | Yes, rides high | Grazing grass, dabbling | Black neck + **white chin-strap** |
| **Mallard** | Yes | **Dabbling** (tips forward) | Drake's **green head**; hen mottled brown |
| **Common loon** | Yes, rides **low** | **Diving** underwater | **Checkerboard back**, dagger bill, wail |
| **Great blue heron** | No — **stands** in shallows | Stalking & **stabbing** | Tall, long-necked, long-legged, dagger bill |

### Why the loon rides low and the goose rides high

Loons have **dense, heavy bones** (unusual for birds) and legs set far back —
adaptations for diving deep after fish. That's why a loon floats low, like a
half-sunk boat, and can barely walk on land; it needs a long "runway" of water to
take off. A goose, built to graze and fly long distances, rides buoyantly high.
Body plan explains behaviour — the Lesson 03 principle again.

### The Canada goose comeback

The Canada goose was once scarce; conservation and adaptable habits (it loves
mowed parks and golf courses) made it a huge success — sometimes *too* successful,
as city flocks and their droppings show. It's a rare good-news wildlife story with
a messy sequel.

{: .note }
> **Behaviour names the water bird**
> Grazing/honking in a V → **goose**. Tipping bottoms-up → **dabbling duck
> (mallard)**. Riding low and vanishing underwater → **loon**. Standing frozen in
> the shallows, then stabbing → **heron**. What it's *doing* is the fastest clue.

## Lab — Look & Identify

**Exercise 1 — behaviour sort.** Match the action to the bird: (a) tips forward,
tail up, to feed at the surface; (b) stands motionless in shallow water then
spears a fish; (c) rides low and disappears underwater for 30 seconds; (d) grazes
on park grass and honks overhead in a V.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) tips bottoms-up → <strong>mallard</strong> (a dabbling duck); (b) stands and
spears → <strong>great blue heron</strong>; (c) rides low and dives →
<strong>common loon</strong>; (d) grazes and honks in a V → <strong>Canada
goose</strong>. Behaviour alone separates all four before you check a single
colour.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the two mallards.** You see a duck with a glossy green head and
yellow bill beside a mottled brown duck, and your friend thinks they're two
different species. Explain in 2 sentences.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "They're one species — mallards — just male and female. The green-headed
one is the <strong>drake</strong> (male) and the mottled brown one is the
<strong>hen</strong> (female); both share a blue wing patch." Full marks for
recognising strong male/female plumage differences within a single species, a
theme that runs through ducks.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — scenario.** At a cottage a friend asks "why does the loon sit so
low in the water and the goose so high?" Answer in 2–3 sentences using the idea
that body plan explains behaviour.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "The loon is built to <em>dive</em> — it has unusually dense, heavy bones
and legs set far back for underwater pursuit of fish, so it floats low, like a
half-submerged boat, and needs a long watery runway to take off. The goose is
built to <em>graze and fly</em>, so it rides buoyantly high and walks easily on
land. Their shapes are tuned to different jobs, and the way they sit on the water
gives it away." Full marks for connecting the dense-bones/leg-placement adaptation
to the low float and diving lifestyle.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Give the single best field mark for the Canada goose, the mallard drake,
and the common loon.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Canada goose</strong> — black head and neck with a <strong>white
chin-strap</strong>. <strong>Mallard drake</strong> — glossy <strong>green head</strong>
with a yellow bill and white neck-ring. <strong>Common loon</strong> — a
<strong>black-and-white checkerboard back</strong> (plus a dagger bill, red eye,
and its wailing call), riding low in the water.
</details>

**Q2.** How do you tell a diving bird like a loon from a dabbling duck like a
mallard by behaviour alone?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A <strong>dabbling duck (mallard)</strong> feeds at the surface by tipping forward
"bottoms-up," staying on top of the water; a <strong>diving bird (loon)</strong>
disappears completely underwater to chase fish and sits low on the surface between
dives. Surface-tipping vs. full submersion is the tell.
</details>

**Q3.** Why does a loon float low and struggle on land, while a goose rides high
and walks easily?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The loon is adapted for <strong>diving</strong>: it has dense, heavy bones and
legs set far back on its body for underwater propulsion, which makes it ride low
and move awkwardly on land (it needs a long water runway to take off). The goose
is adapted for <strong>grazing and flying</strong>, so it's buoyant and walks
comfortably. Body plan explains the difference.
</details>

## Homework

Visit any water — a pond, lake, river, or even a big puddle-park — and watch for
ten minutes. Identify at least **one** of the four birds by behaviour first, then
confirm with a field mark. Note what each bird was *doing*, since behaviour is
your strongest tool here.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong answer leads with behaviour, e.g.: "A duck kept tipping forward,
bottoms-up, at the surface — a <strong>dabbling duck</strong>; the green head and
yellow bill confirmed a <strong>mallard drake</strong>." Or: "A tall grey bird
stood frozen in the shallows, then stabbed down — a <strong>great blue heron</strong>."
Full marks for using behaviour (dabble / dive / graze / stalk-and-stab) to reach
the ID and then confirming with one visible mark.
</details>
