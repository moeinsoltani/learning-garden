# Lesson 16: A Walk in the Woods (Capstone)

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **capstone** — a final project that pulls together everything you've learned.
> - **field notes** — quick written observations made while outdoors.
> - **habitat cue** — a clue from the *place* (water, deep forest, open field) that narrows what you'll see.
> - **GISS / jizz** — a living thing's overall "feel" (size, shape, movement) — your fastest ID tool.
> - **checklist** — a running list of species observed on an outing (as in the eBird app).

## Concept

This is where it all comes together. There are no new species — instead, you'll
**read a whole scene** the way a naturalist does: letting the *habitat* set your
expectations, then running the tree method (Lesson 02) and the bird method
(Lesson 03) on what you actually find. Then a **cumulative final quiz** covers the
whole course. Treat this lesson as your graduation walk.

![A boreal forest of spruce around a still lake — a scene to read](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Jack_London_Lake_by_bartosh.jpg/960px-Jack_London_Lake_by_bartosh.jpg)

*Reading a scene starts with **habitat**: dark pointed conifers + a lake tells you
"boreal," which tells you *what to expect* — spruce and fir, a loon on the water, a
Canada jay in the trees, a woodpecker drumming. Habitat narrows the field before
you identify a single thing. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Jack_London_Lake_by_bartosh.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### The naturalist's reading order

1. **Habitat first.** Where am I — deep forest, forest edge, water, open field,
   city park? Each predicts a *cast of characters* (Lessons 09, 11, 15). Water →
   expect geese, ducks, loon, heron. Deep boreal → spruce/fir, Canada jay,
   woodpeckers, grouse.
2. **Trees next** (they hold still). Run the 30-second method: silhouette →
   needle/leaf → leaf shape → bark. Name the forest type from the mix.
3. **Birds by GISS.** Size vs. a reference bird → shape → behaviour → colour →
   sound. Lead with what it *is* and what it's *doing*.
4. **Season overlays it all** (Lesson 15). The same woods in June (singing
   migrants everywhere) and January (a few hardy residents) are different scenes.
5. **Write it down.** A few field notes — date, place, species, one detail — turn
   a nice walk into lasting knowledge, and a checklist app (eBird/iNaturalist)
   makes it a record.

### A worked example

*You're at a cottage lake in early October.* Reading the scene:

- **Habitat:** mixed forest + lake → expect water birds *and* forest birds; fall
  colour season.
- **Trees:** a big tree with **five-lobed leaves, smooth U-notches, blazing
  orange-red** → **sugar maple** (Lesson 06), so this is Great Lakes–St. Lawrence
  mixed forest. Nearby, **white bark peeling in strips** → **paper birch**.
- **On the water:** a bird riding **low**, then **diving** and surfacing with a
  fish, checkerboard back → **common loon** (Lesson 11). A **V of honking geese**
  passes overhead → autumn migration (Lesson 15).
- **In the trees:** a tiny bird with a **black cap and bib** hangs upside-down →
  **black-capped chickadee** (Lesson 10, a resident that will stay all winter).
- **Notes:** "Oct 5, Haliburton lake: sugar maple peak colour, 1 loon fishing,
  geese migrating south, chickadees active." You just read an entire scene.

![A sugar maple in fall colour, a classic autumn scene-reader's clue](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Acer_saccharum_1-jgreenlee_%285098070608%29.jpg/960px-Acer_saccharum_1-jgreenlee_%285098070608%29.jpg)

*One tree can date and place a scene: a blazing **sugar maple** says "autumn,
Great Lakes–St. Lawrence mixed forest." Reading trees, birds, and season together
is the whole skill of this course. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Acer_saccharum_1-jgreenlee_%285098070608%29.jpg).*

> **The whole course in one line**
> **Let the habitat set expectations; read the still things (trees) by the
> 30-second method and the moving things (birds) by size-shape-behaviour; let the
> season tell you who's present; write it down.**

## Lab — Look & Identify (Capstone Scene)

**Exercise 1 — read this scene.** *It's a bright day in late May, at a marshy pond
ringed by trees with wide flat lobed leaves in opposite pairs and helicopter
seeds. A tall grey bird stands frozen in the shallows, then stabs the water. A
small grey bird with a black cap and bib flits in the branches, calling its own
name. Overhead, a large bird hovers, then plunges feet-first into the pond and
rises with a fish, white belly flashing.* Identify the trees and all three birds,
and name the season's evidence.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Trees:</strong> opposite paired leaves + lobed blades + helicopter seeds →
<strong>maples</strong>. <strong>Bird 1</strong> (tall, grey, stands then stabs in
shallows) → <strong>great blue heron</strong>. <strong>Bird 2</strong> (tiny, grey,
black cap and bib, says <em>chicka-dee-dee</em>) → <strong>black-capped chickadee</strong>.
<strong>Bird 3</strong> (hovers, plunges feet-first for a fish, white below) →
<strong>osprey</strong>. <strong>Season:</strong> late May with active, calling
birds and a fishing osprey (a migrant that returns to breed) = late spring/early
summer, breeding season. Full marks for habitat-led reading: a pond predicts
heron and osprey; the maples and chickadee fill in the rest.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — your own walk.** Go outside (anywhere with a few trees and birds)
and read the scene in order: habitat, then two trees, then two birds, then the
season's evidence. Write 5–8 lines of field notes as if for your notebook.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
There's no single right scene — full marks means you followed the order and used
real field marks, e.g.: "City park, late June. Trees: sugar maple (opposite lobed
leaves, U-notches) and a white pine (needles in fives). Birds: an American robin
hopping and yanking worms on the lawn; a blue jay screaming in an oak. Season:
everything green and birds singing/feeding → summer breeding.' The grade is in the
*method*: habitat first, trees by silhouette/needle-or-leaf, birds by
size-shape-behaviour, season last — and enough detail that someone else could
picture and verify your scene.
</details>

## Checkpoint — Cumulative Final Quiz

Twenty questions across the whole course. Write your answers, then reveal each.

**Q1.** What single question splits the entire tree world in two, and into which
two groups?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
"<strong>Needle/scale or flat leaf?</strong>" — splitting trees into
<strong>conifers</strong> and <strong>broadleaf</strong> trees.
</details>

**Q2.** Give the three-word rhyme separating spruce, fir, and pine.

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
<strong>Spruce = Square & Sharp; Fir = Flat & Friendly; Pine = Packages</strong>
(needles bundled — count them).
</details>

**Q3.** How many soft needles per bundle does eastern white pine have, and the
memory aid?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
<strong>Five</strong> — "W-H-I-T-E" has five letters.
</details>

**Q4.** What two features identify *any* maple before you check the species?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
<strong>Opposite (paired) leaves</strong> and <strong>lobed, hand-shaped leaves
with winged "helicopter" seeds</strong> (samaras).
</details>

**Q5.** Sugar vs. silver maple: give one leaf difference.

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
Sugar maple has smooth <strong>U-shaped</strong> notches and few teeth; silver
maple has <strong>deep, narrow V-shaped</strong> cuts and a silvery-white
underside.
</details>

**Q6.** Roughly how much sap makes 1 L of maple syrup?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
About <strong>40 litres of sap → 1 litre of syrup</strong>.
</details>

**Q7.** Name the single best field mark for oak, birch, aspen, and beech.

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
Oak — lobed leaves + <strong>acorns</strong>; birch — <strong>white peeling
bark</strong>; aspen — leaves that <strong>tremble on flat stalks</strong>; beech —
<strong>smooth grey bark</strong> with tan leaves clinging in winter.
</details>

**Q8.** What is Canada's national tree, and the year it was declared?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
The <strong>maple</strong>, declared in <strong>1996</strong>.
</details>

**Q9.** Name Canada's largest forest region and two signature trees.

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
The <strong>boreal forest</strong>; any two of: white/black spruce, jack pine,
balsam fir, tamarack, aspen, paper birch.
</details>

**Q10.** In what order does an experienced birder read a bird?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
<strong>Size & shape → colour & pattern → behaviour → habitat → sound</strong>
(leading with structure and behaviour, not colour).
</details>

**Q11.** How do you separate a chickadee from a nuthatch by behaviour and face?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
The <strong>nuthatch climbs head-first down the trunk</strong> and has a clean
white face (no bib); the <strong>chickadee</strong> flits/hangs and has a black
<strong>bib</strong> under its cap.
</details>

**Q12.** Why can a northern cardinal fool a beginner?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
Only the <strong>male is red</strong>; the <strong>female is tan</strong>. Identify
by shape (crest, black mask, heavy orange bill), not colour.
</details>

**Q13.** Sort by feeding behaviour: mallard, common loon, great blue heron.

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
Mallard — <strong>dabbles</strong> (tips up at the surface); loon —
<strong>dives</strong> underwater; heron — <strong>stands and stabs</strong> in the
shallows.
</details>

**Q14.** Why does a loon ride low in the water?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
It's a diver with <strong>dense, heavy bones</strong> and legs set far back — great
underwater, so it floats low and needs a long water runway to take off.
</details>

**Q15.** How do you tell an adult bald eagle from a young one?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
Adults have a <strong>white head and tail</strong>; young birds are
<strong>all brown</strong> for ~4–5 years — judge them by size, shape, and habitat.
</details>

**Q16.** Downy vs. pileated woodpecker — the quickest difference?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
<strong>Size</strong>: downy is <strong>sparrow-sized</strong>; pileated is nearly
<strong>crow-sized</strong> with a flaming red crest.
</details>

**Q17.** How do you tell a Canada jay from a blue jay?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
Canada jay is <strong>soft grey with no crest</strong> (tame, in northern/mountain
forest); blue jay is <strong>bright blue with a crest</strong> (towns and parks).
</details>

**Q18.** Does Canada have an official national bird? What was proposed in 2016?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
<strong>No official national bird</strong>; the <strong>Canada jay</strong> was
recommended in a 2016 campaign but never adopted.
</details>

**Q19.** Why do most migrating birds actually leave Canada for winter?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
Their <strong>food disappears</strong> (insects, open water, fruit) — it's about
food, not cold. They return for the summer's long daylight and insect abundance.
</details>

**Q20.** What is an "irruption," with the classic Canadian example?

<details><summary>Show Answer</summary><br>
An unusual mass movement of a species outside its normal range, usually from food
shortage — classically the <strong>snowy owl</strong> pushing south into southern
Canada in some winters.
</details>

## Homework — Final Project

Do **one real "reading walk"** and write it up as a mini field report:
1. The **habitat** and what it led you to expect.
2. **Two trees** identified by the method (say which features).
3. **Two birds** identified by size-shape-behaviour (say which clues).
4. The **season's** evidence.
5. One thing you want to learn next.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong final report reads like naturalist notes, e.g.: "Ravine park, mid-October.
<em>Habitat:</em> mixed forest with a creek → expect hardwoods, conifers, and
forest birds. <em>Trees:</em> sugar maple (opposite lobed leaves, U-notches, orange
colour) and eastern white pine (needles in fives) → Great Lakes–St. Lawrence mixed
forest. <em>Birds:</em> a black-capped chickadee (tiny, black cap+bib, upside-down,
<em>chicka-dee</em>) and a downy woodpecker (sparrow-sized, black-and-white, red
nape spot, drumming). <em>Season:</em> peak leaf colour + geese overhead = autumn.
<em>Next:</em> learn the fall warblers." Full marks for following the reading order,
citing real field marks for each ID, and connecting the trees, birds, and season
into one coherent scene. That's the whole course — congratulations, you can now
read the living landscape of Canada.
</details>
