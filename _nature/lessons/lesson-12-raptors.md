# Lesson 12: Raptors — Birds of Prey

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **raptor** (RAP-tor) — a bird of prey that hunts with a hooked bill and gripping **talons** (hawks, eagles, falcons, owls).
> - **talons** (TAL-unz) — the strong, curved claws a raptor grabs prey with.
> - **eagle** — a very large raptor; the bald eagle is the biggest common one in Canada.
> - **hawk** — a medium raptor; the red-tailed hawk is the one you'll see most.
> - **osprey** (OSS-pray) — a fish-hunting raptor that plunges feet-first into water.
> - **owl** — a mostly nocturnal raptor with a big flat face and forward eyes; the snowy owl is a famous Arctic one.
> - **soar** — to fly in circles on rising warm air without flapping.
> - **raptor "silhouette"** — the shape a bird of prey makes against the sky, key to IDing them high up.

## Concept

Raptors — birds of prey — are thrilling to spot and easier than you'd think,
because you usually identify them by **shape and silhouette** high overhead, not
by fine detail. All share a **hooked bill** and **talons**. Four are worth
knowing first in Canada: the **bald eagle**, the **red-tailed hawk**, the
**osprey**, and the **snowy owl**.

![A bald eagle: white head and tail, dark brown body, yellow hooked bill](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Bald_eagle_about_to_fly_in_Alaska_%282016%29.jpg/960px-Bald_eagle_about_to_fly_in_Alaska_%282016%29.jpg)

***Bald eagle** — huge and unmistakable **once adult**: snow-**white head and
tail**, dark brown body, and a massive yellow hooked bill. (Young birds are all
mottled brown for their first few years — a common source of confusion.) Often
near water, where it fishes and scavenges. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Bald_eagle_about_to_fly_in_Alaska_%282016%29.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### The four raptors

![A red-tailed hawk perched, showing a pale chest and brick-red tail](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Red-tailed_Hawk_%2845812546121%29.jpg/960px-Red-tailed_Hawk_%2845812546121%29.jpg)

***Red-tailed hawk** — the hawk you'll see most, often perched on a highway pole
or **soaring** in wide circles. Broad rounded wings, a pale chest with a darker
"belly band," and (on adults) a brick-**red tail**. Its scream is the classic
"eagle" sound used in movies. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Red-tailed_Hawk_%2845812546121%29.jpg).*

![An osprey perched on a snag, white below with a dark eye-stripe](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Osprey_Perched_Snag_Heislerville.jpg/960px-Osprey_Perched_Snag_Heislerville.jpg)

***Osprey** — the fish hawk: **white underneath**, dark above, with a bold dark
**eye-stripe** through a white head. It hovers over water and **plunges feet-first**
to grab fish, then carries them head-forward like a torpedo. Builds huge stick
nests on poles and platforms. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Osprey_Perched_Snag_Heislerville.jpg).*

![A snowy owl: large, round-headed, white with dark flecks, yellow eyes](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Bubo_scandiacus_%28Linnaeus%2C_1758%29_Male.jpg/960px-Bubo_scandiacus_%28Linnaeus%2C_1758%29_Male.jpg)

***Snowy owl** — a large, round-headed, **white** owl (females and young flecked
with black) with piercing yellow eyes. It breeds on the Arctic tundra and comes
south in winter, hunting by day over open fields and shorelines — a magical winter
sighting. It's the **official bird of Quebec**. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Bubo_scandiacus_%28Linnaeus%2C_1758%29_Male.jpg).*

### Telling them apart in the sky

| Raptor | Size | Silhouette / behaviour | Best mark |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bald eagle** | Huge | Long, plank-flat wings; soars heavily | Adult: **white head + tail** |
| **Red-tailed hawk** | Medium | Broad rounded wings, soars in circles; perches on poles | **Red tail**, pale chest with belly band |
| **Osprey** | Large | Hovers over water, plunges feet-first | **White below** + dark eye-stripe |
| **Snowy owl** | Large | Round head, no "ear" tufts; sits on the ground/posts by day | All **white**, yellow eyes (winter fields) |

Two quick rules of thumb: **near water + white head = eagle** (if adult) or
**near water + white belly + hovering = osprey**; **soaring over a field with a
red tail = red-tailed hawk**; **a big white bird sitting in a snowy field in
winter = snowy owl**.

### The young-eagle trap

The most common raptor mistake: a **young bald eagle** is *all brown* for its
first 4–5 years — no white head yet — so it's easily confused with a golden eagle
or a large hawk. Judge by **size, shape, and habitat** (huge, plank-winged, near
water) rather than expecting the white head. It's Lesson 03's lesson yet again:
plumage changes, structure doesn't.

### Owls hunt where hawks don't

Most owls work the **night shift** (soft-edged feathers for silent flight, huge
forward eyes for low light), which is why you hear them more than see them. The
snowy owl is an exception — an Arctic bird used to the **midnight sun**, it
happily hunts by day, which is part of why winter snowy-owl sightings in southern
fields are so prized.

{: .note }
> **Shape first, colour second (especially for raptors)**
> High overhead you rarely see colour well — you see a **silhouette** and a
> **behaviour**. Huge and plank-winged near water → eagle. Broad-winged, circling,
> pole-sitting → red-tailed hawk. Hovering then plunging into water → osprey.
> Save fine colour marks for when the bird is close.

## Lab — Look & Identify

**Exercise 1 — sky ID.** Name the raptor: (a) hovering over a lake, then diving
feet-first for a fish, white belly flashing; (b) soaring in slow circles over a
field, broad rounded wings, a brick-red tail; (c) enormous, near a river, with a
brilliant white head and tail.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) hover-and-plunge for fish, white below → <strong>osprey</strong>; (b) circling
a field with a red tail → <strong>red-tailed hawk</strong>; (c) huge, riverside,
white head and tail → adult <strong>bald eagle</strong>. Each was identified by
behaviour + silhouette + one mark — no fine detail needed.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the brown eagle.** A friend photographs a huge, all-brown,
plank-winged bird soaring over a lake and says "it can't be a bald eagle — no
white head." Explain in 2–3 sentences.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "It very well could be a bald eagle — a <strong>young</strong> one. Bald
eagles don't get the white head and tail until they're about 4–5 years old; before
that they're mottled brown all over. Judge it by <em>size, shape, and habitat</em>
— huge, long flat wings, near water — rather than expecting the white head." Full
marks for the immature-plumage point and for leaning on structure/habitat over
colour.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — scenario.** In January someone spots "a big white bird just sitting
in a snowy farm field in the middle of the day." They think it's a stray gull or a
plastic bag. What is it, and why is daytime hunting normal for it?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
It's almost certainly a <strong>snowy owl</strong>, which breeds on the Arctic
tundra and moves south into open fields and shorelines in winter. Daytime hunting
is normal because it evolved under the Arctic's <strong>midnight sun</strong>,
where summer has no darkness, so unlike most owls it's comfortable hunting by day.
Its habit of sitting on the ground or a low post in open country is a classic
giveaway. (It's also Quebec's official bird.)
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What two features do all raptors share, and why do we usually identify
them by silhouette rather than colour?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
All raptors share a <strong>hooked bill</strong> and gripping <strong>talons</strong>.
We identify them by <strong>silhouette and behaviour</strong> because they're
usually seen high overhead where colour is hard to judge, but wing shape, size,
and flight style (soaring, hovering, plunging) read clearly against the sky.
</details>

**Q2.** Give the behaviour + best mark for the osprey and for the red-tailed
hawk.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Osprey</strong> — hovers over water and <strong>plunges feet-first</strong>
for fish; mark: <strong>white underneath</strong> with a dark eye-stripe.
<strong>Red-tailed hawk</strong> — <strong>soars in wide circles</strong> and
perches on poles; mark: a brick-<strong>red tail</strong> (adult) and a pale chest
with a dark belly band.
</details>

**Q3.** Why can a bald eagle be mistaken for another bird, and why does the snowy
owl hunt by day when most owls don't?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A <strong>young bald eagle is all brown</strong> (no white head/tail until ~4–5
years old), so it's confused with other large raptors — you must go by size,
shape, and habitat. The <strong>snowy owl</strong> hunts by day because it evolved
on the Arctic tundra under the <strong>midnight sun</strong>, where summers have no
night, so daytime hunting is natural for it (unlike most owls, which are
nocturnal).
</details>
