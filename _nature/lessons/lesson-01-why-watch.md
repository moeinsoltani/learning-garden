# Lesson 01: Why Watch Trees and Birds?

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **species** (SPEE-sheez) — one specific kind of living thing (e.g. "sugar maple" is a species). The word is the same for one or many.
> - **conifer** (KAH-nih-fur) — a tree that makes cones and usually has needles; most stay green all winter ("evergreen").
> - **broadleaf** — a tree with wide, flat leaves (maple, oak, birch); most drop their leaves in autumn.
> - **deciduous** (deh-SIH-joo-us) — a tree that drops all its leaves each fall. Most broadleaf trees are deciduous.
> - **native** — a species that has lived in a place for a very long time on its own, not brought by people.
> - **habitat** (HAB-ih-tat) — the kind of place a species lives (forest, wetland, city park).
> - **field mark** — a single feature you use to identify something (a bird's black cap, a tree's white bark).
> - **birding / birdwatching** — the hobby of finding and identifying wild birds. People who do it are "birders."

## Concept

Canada is one of the greenest, birdiest countries on Earth: about **347 billion
trees** and roughly **450 species of birds** that breed here. But you don't
experience that as a number — you experience it as *the tree outside your
window* and *the bird on the wire*. This course is about turning those anonymous
green and grey shapes into things you can **name and recognise**. Naming changes
how you see: once "some bird" becomes "a chickadee," you start noticing it
everywhere, and a walk stops being scenery and becomes a set of old friends.

![The flag of Canada, a red maple leaf on a white square between two red bars](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Flag_of_Canada_%28Pantone%29.svg/960px-Flag_of_Canada_%28Pantone%29.svg.png)

*Canada put a **tree's leaf** on its flag — the maple leaf. No other G7 country
centres its flag on a plant. Trees and the natural world are woven into how
Canada sees itself. Image: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Flag_of_Canada_%28Pantone%29.svg).*

We split the living world you'll meet into just **two big groups** to start:
**trees** (Phase 2) and **birds** (Phase 3). They're a great pair for a
beginner. Trees hold still and wait for you — perfect for learning a careful
method. Birds move, sing, and reward that method with a genuine thrill when a
tricky one finally clicks. Learn both and almost any outdoor space in Canada
opens up.

![A boreal forest of dark spruce around a still northern lake](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Jack_London_Lake_by_bartosh.jpg/960px-Jack_London_Lake_by_bartosh.jpg)

*The **boreal forest** — spruce, fir, pine, and lakes — is the largest habitat
in Canada, wrapping the whole country in a green band. Much of what you'll learn
lives here, but you don't have to travel to it: city parks hold the same
families of trees and birds. Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Jack_London_Lake_by_bartosh.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### Why these two groups first

You could start with wildflowers, mushrooms, insects, or mammals — but trees and
birds are the beginner's sweet spot for concrete reasons:

- **They're everywhere, all year.** Even a downtown street and a frozen January
  park have trees and birds. Wildflowers vanish in winter; mammals hide.
- **There aren't too many common ones.** You can learn the ~20 trees and ~25
  birds you'll meet 90% of the time in a few weeks. That's a *finishable* goal.
- **They teach a transferable method.** The habit of asking "what shape? what
  colour? what's it doing?" works later for anything else you want to learn.
- **They're the backbone of a habitat.** Know the trees and you know the forest;
  know the birds and you know what the forest is *doing*.

### Native vs. introduced — a gentle warning

Not every tree or bird you see is Canadian by origin. Some of the most common
city birds (the **house sparrow**, the **rock pigeon**, the **European
starling**) were brought from Europe and now thrive here; many city trees
(Norway maple, ginkgo) were planted. That's fine — this course teaches you what
you'll actually *see*, native or not — but we'll flag the famous introduced ones
so you know the difference between "wild Canada" and "what humans added."

{: .note }
> **You already know more than you think**
> If you can tell a Christmas-tree shape (a pointed evergreen) from a big round
> shade tree, you've already made the single most important tree distinction:
> **conifer vs. broadleaf**. If you can tell a duck from a hawk by shape alone,
> you've started **birding**. This course just sharpens instincts you already
> have.

### How to use this course

1. **Read each lesson beside the photos.** Don't skim past the pictures — they
   *are* the lesson. Look at the field marks named in the caption.
2. **Then go outside within a day or two.** Recognition only sticks when you
   match a picture to a living thing. A single park bench for ten minutes is
   enough.
3. **Don't rush to be perfect.** "It's a spruce or a fir" is a great early
   answer. Precision comes with repetition.
4. **Use the checkpoints honestly** — write your answer before revealing the
   model one.

## Lab — Look & Identify

**Exercise 1 — the two-group sort.** Here are eight living things you might see
on a Canadian street. Sort each into **tree** or **bird**, and for the trees say
**conifer** or **broadleaf**: (a) blue jay, (b) sugar maple, (c) white spruce,
(d) Canada goose, (e) paper birch, (f) black-capped chickadee, (g) eastern white
pine, (h) red oak.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Birds: (a) blue jay, (d) Canada goose, (f) black-capped chickadee. Trees —
broadleaf: (b) sugar maple, (e) paper birch, (h) red oak. Trees — conifer:
(c) white spruce, (g) eastern white pine. If you weren't sure which trees are
conifers, remember the shortcut: needles or scales + cones = conifer; wide flat
leaves = broadleaf. You'll meet every one of these species by name later in the
course.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — scenario.** A friend who just moved to Canada says: "I want to
start noticing nature, but there's a whole textbook of species and it feels
hopeless." In 3–4 sentences, tell them why it's more achievable than it looks
and how to begin.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "It feels hopeless because you're imagining you must learn *all* of them —
but you don't. In any one place, the same 20-or-so trees and 25-or-so birds show
up over and over, so a few weeks of paying attention covers almost everything
you'll actually see. Start with one: learn the black-capped chickadee (it's tame,
loud, and everywhere), and let the rest come one at a time. Naming just one thing
is the whole hobby in miniature." Full marks for the core idea: the world of
*common* species is small and finishable, and you build it one recognition at a
time.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What are the two big groups this course starts with, and what's the
single most useful distinction *within* the tree group?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The two groups are <strong>trees</strong> and <strong>birds</strong>. Within
trees, the most useful first distinction is <strong>conifer</strong> (needles or
scales, cones, usually evergreen) vs. <strong>broadleaf</strong> (wide flat
leaves, usually deciduous — dropping their leaves each autumn).
</details>

**Q2.** Roughly how many common bird and tree species do you actually need to
learn to recognise most of what you'll see day to day — and why does that number
matter?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Only a few dozen of each — very roughly ~25 common birds and ~20 common trees
cover the large majority of everyday sightings in a given area. It matters
because it turns an intimidating "hundreds of species" into a small, finishable
goal you can reach in a few weeks of noticing.
</details>

**Q3.** Name one famous city bird in Canada that is *not* native, and explain why
"you'll still learn it here" is not a contradiction.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The <strong>house sparrow</strong>, <strong>rock pigeon</strong>, or
<strong>European starling</strong> — all introduced from Europe. It's not a
contradiction because the course teaches what you will actually *see*: these
birds are now among the most common in Canadian cities, so recognising them is
useful. We simply label them as introduced so you know they aren't part of
"wild, native Canada."
</details>

## Homework

For the next two days, without any app, simply **count how many different kinds
of trees and birds you notice** on your normal routine — you don't have to name
them, just tell them apart ("tall pointy evergreen," "small grey bird with a
black cap," "big round shade tree"). Write down your tally and one sentence on
what surprised you.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
There's no wrong number, but most people are surprised by two things: first, how
many *different* shapes they can already tell apart without knowing a single name
(you might reach 5–8 trees and 4–6 birds on an ordinary block); and second, how
much they'd been walking past without seeing. That surprise is the point of the
exercise — noticing is a switch you can turn on, and the rest of this course
gives names to what you've started to see. If you noticed a small grey bird with
a black cap that let you get close, you've almost certainly already met your
first species: the black-capped chickadee (Lesson 10).
</details>
