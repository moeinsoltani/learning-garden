# Lesson 02: How to Identify a Tree

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **needle** — the thin, narrow "leaf" of most conifers (spruce, pine, fir).
> - **scale-leaf** — tiny flat overlapping leaves pressed to the twig, like on cedar.
> - **cone** — the woody seed-holder of a conifer (a "pine cone"); some are hard, some are papery.
> - **bark** — the outer skin of a trunk; its colour and texture is a great field mark.
> - **lobe** — a rounded or pointed "finger" on a leaf edge (maple and oak leaves are lobed).
> - **simple leaf** — one single leaf blade; **compound leaf** — one leaf split into several leaflets.
> - **crown / canopy** — the leafy top of a tree; its overall shape seen from a distance.
> - **silhouette** (sil-oo-ET) — the tree's outline against the sky.
> - **coniferous** (koh-NIH-fur-us) — cone-bearing; **deciduous** (deh-SIH-joo-us) — leaf-dropping.

## Concept

You identify a tree the way you recognise a friend across the street: **shape
first, then details.** From far away you read the **silhouette** (Christmas-tree
point? round shade tree? white-barked and slender?). Up close you check three
things in order:

1. **Leaves or needles** — broad flat leaf, or needle/scale?
2. **Bark** — colour, and smooth/rough/peeling?
3. **Shape & size** — the overall crown, and how tall.

That's the whole method. Ninety percent of Canadian tree ID is just the first
question — *needle or leaf* — because it instantly halves the tree world into
**conifers** and **broadleaf** trees.

*A **broadleaf** tree (sugar maple): wide flat leaves, a rounded spreading crown,
loses its leaves in autumn. This is the "shade tree" silhouette. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Acer_saccharum_1-jgreenlee_%285098070608%29.jpg).*

![A tall, narrow, pointed white spruce](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Picea_glauca_Fairbanks.jpg/960px-Picea_glauca_Fairbanks.jpg)

*A **conifer** (white spruce): needles instead of flat leaves, cones, a narrow
pointed "Christmas-tree" silhouette, stays green all winter. The pointed shape
sheds snow — an adaptation to the Canadian winter. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Picea_glauca_Fairbanks.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### Step 1 — needle or leaf? (this does most of the work)

| If you see… | It's a… | Examples (Phase 2) |
|---|---|---|
| Narrow **needles** in bundles or all round the twig | **Conifer** | spruce, pine, fir, hemlock, larch |
| Tiny **scale-leaves** flat against the twig | **Conifer** (cedar type) | white cedar, western redcedar |
| Wide, flat **leaves** with veins | **Broadleaf** | maple, oak, birch, aspen, beech |

A quick winter trick: if a tree is **green in January**, it's almost always a
conifer (an evergreen). If it's **bare sticks in January**, it's a broadleaf
(deciduous) tree resting for winter. The main exception is the **larch/tamarack**
— a conifer that *does* drop its needles each fall, turning gold first.

### Step 2 — read the leaf (for broadleaf trees)

- **Lobed** (fingers with gaps): maples have lobes meeting at a point in the
  middle; oaks have rounded or bristle-tipped lobes.
- **Toothed edge, oval leaf:** birch, beech, elm.
- **Simple vs compound:** is it one blade (maple, oak) or one leaf divided into
  many leaflets (ash, walnut, mountain-ash)?
- **Arrangement:** are leaves **opposite** (in pairs) or **alternate** (staggered)?
  A handy memory word is **MAD-Cap-Horse**: **M**aple, **A**sh, **D**ogwood, and
  the **Cap**rifoliaceae/**Horse**-chestnut are the common *opposite*-leaved
  trees; nearly everything else is alternate.

### Step 3 — bark and shape as backups

When leaves are out of reach (or it's winter), **bark** saves you. Some barks
are unmistakable:

![A tall Douglas-fir showing its thick, furrowed grey-brown bark](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Pseudotsuga_menziesii_4584.JPG/960px-Pseudotsuga_menziesii_4584.JPG)

*Bark and crown are strong backup clues (here, a Douglas-fir's furrowed bark and
tall conifer form). When you can't reach the leaves — or in winter — bark colour
and texture often name the tree on their own. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Pseudotsuga_menziesii_4584.JPG).*

Famous "bark tells": **paper birch** (chalk-white, peeling in papery strips),
**trembling aspen** (smooth greenish-white with black scars), **American beech**
(smooth pale grey, like an elephant's leg), **white pine** (deeply furrowed grey
in age), **shagbark hickory** (long peeling plates). We'll point these out as we
meet each tree.

{: .note }
> **The 30-second method (memorise this)**
> 1. **Far:** silhouette — pointed conifer, round broadleaf, or slender white?
> 2. **Near:** needle/scale or flat leaf? (splits the whole tree world in two)
> 3. **Leaf shape:** lobed (maple/oak), toothed-oval (birch/beech), or compound?
> 4. **Bark:** any obvious colour/texture tell?
> Stop as soon as you're confident. Most trees fall in the first two steps.

### Don't over-reach at first

You do **not** need to nail the exact species every time. "That's a spruce (not
a pine or fir)" is a strong, useful answer; separating white spruce from black
spruce can wait. Confidence at the *group* level first, precision later.

the 30-second method, write the two-step call for each: (a) the sugar maple,
(b) the white spruce, (c) the Douglas-fir. Say the silhouette and the
needle-or-leaf result.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) Sugar maple — silhouette: round, spreading shade tree; needle-or-leaf: wide
flat lobed leaf → <strong>broadleaf</strong>. (b) White spruce — silhouette:
narrow pointed Christmas-tree; needle-or-leaf: needles + cones →
<strong>conifer</strong>. (c) Douglas-fir — silhouette: tall pointed conifer
form; needle-or-leaf: needles, thick furrowed bark → <strong>conifer</strong>.
You correctly reached a confident group-level ID for all three without needing

</details>

**Exercise 2 — the winter test.** It's January. You pass two bare-stick trees and
one green one. A friend says "the green one must be healthier — the others are
dead." Correct them in 2–3 sentences using the words *conifer*, *deciduous*, and
*evergreen*.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "They're all alive. The bare ones are <em>deciduous</em> broadleaf trees —
they drop their leaves every autumn and rest through winter, which is normal, not
death. The green one is a <em>conifer</em>, an <em>evergreen</em> that keeps its
needles year-round. In spring the bare ones will leaf out again." Full marks for
naming the reason (deciduous trees shed on purpose) rather than just asserting
they're alive.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — bark alone.** You find a slender tree with bright **white, papery
bark that peels in strips**, and no leaves in reach. What's your best single-word
guess, and what's the method lesson here?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Best guess: <strong>birch</strong> (very likely paper birch — Lesson 07). The
method lesson: <strong>bark can identify a tree by itself</strong> when leaves
are unavailable or it's winter. A few barks — white peeling birch, smooth grey
beech, greenish-white aspen — are so distinctive they skip straight to an answer.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What is the single most useful question to ask when identifying a
Canadian tree, and why does it do so much work?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"<strong>Needle (or scale) versus flat leaf?</strong>" It does so much work
because it instantly splits every tree into <strong>conifer</strong> or
<strong>broadleaf</strong> — halving the possibilities in one glance and pointing
you at the right family before you look at anything else.
</details>

**Q2.** A tree is bare in January. Is it dead? Explain, and name the one common
conifer exception that also goes bare.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
No — it's almost certainly a healthy <strong>deciduous</strong> broadleaf tree
resting for winter; it will re-leaf in spring. The exception is the
<strong>larch / tamarack</strong>, a conifer that turns gold and drops its
needles each autumn, so it too stands bare in winter despite being a
cone-bearing conifer.
</details>

**Q3.** List the 30-second method's steps in order.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
(1) <strong>Silhouette</strong> from far away (pointed conifer / round broadleaf /
slender white). (2) <strong>Needle-or-scale vs. flat leaf</strong> up close
(conifer vs. broadleaf). (3) <strong>Leaf shape</strong> for broadleaf trees
(lobed maple/oak, toothed-oval birch/beech, or compound). (4) <strong>Bark</strong>
colour and texture as a backup — especially in winter. Stop as soon as you're
confident.
</details>
