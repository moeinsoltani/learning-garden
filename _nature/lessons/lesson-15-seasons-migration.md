# Lesson 15: Birds Through the Seasons

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **migration** (my-GRAY-shun) — the seasonal journey many birds make between summer breeding grounds and warmer winter homes.
> - **migrant** — a bird that migrates; **resident** — a bird that stays year-round.
> - **breeding grounds** — where a bird nests and raises young (for many, the Canadian summer).
> - **irruption** (ih-RUP-shun) — an unusual mass movement of a bird south in some winters (e.g. snowy owls) when food is scarce up north.
> - **flyway** — a broad highway in the sky that migrating birds follow.
> - **plumage** (PLOO-mij) — feather colours, which for some birds change between summer and winter.
> - **overwinter** — to survive through the winter in place.

## Concept

Birds are a **calendar**. Canada's huge seasonal swing — rich green summers,
frozen winters — splits its birds into two strategies: **leave** (migrate south to
find food) or **stay** (tough out the winter). Learning who does which turns every
month into a set of expectations: you know roughly *what you'll see* and *when*.

![A Canada goose — geese in V-formation are Canada's clearest migration signal](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Canada_goose.jpg/960px-Canada_goose.jpg)

***Canada goose** — the migration you can't miss. Honking **V-formations** heading
**south** in autumn and **north** in spring are Canada's loudest seasonal clock.
(Some urban geese now stay all winter where food and open water allow — a modern
twist.) Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Canada_goose.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### Why migrate at all?

It's about **food, not temperature**. Many birds could survive the cold, but they
can't find their food — insects vanish, water freezes, fruit runs out. So
insect-eaters and many water birds fly south for the winter and return north in
spring to breed, because the Canadian summer offers something priceless: **long
days and an explosion of insects** to feed hungry chicks. The boreal forest
(Lesson 09) is the summer nursery for **billions** of birds that leave again each
fall.

### Who leaves, who stays

| Strategy | Examples (from this course) | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Migrate south** | Canada goose, common loon, osprey, great blue heron, American robin (many), white-throated sparrow | Food (fish, insects, worms, seeds) disappears under ice and snow |
| **Stay all winter (resident)** | Black-capped chickadee, blue jay, northern cardinal, white-breasted nuthatch, downy & pileated woodpeckers, ruffed grouse, Canada jay | Can switch to seeds, buds, cached food, or bark insects |
| **Comes *from* the north for winter** | Snowy owl (some winters), a few finches | Arctic food shortages push them *south* into Canada's south |

![A black-capped chickadee — a hardy winter resident](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Poecile-atricapilla-001.jpg/960px-Poecile-atricapilla-001.jpg)

***Black-capped chickadee** — a winter champion. It stays all year, switching to
seeds and hidden insect eggs, remembering thousands of food caches, and even
dropping its body temperature at night to save energy. This is why chickadees own
your winter feeder. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Poecile-atricapilla-001.jpg).*

### The snowy owl surprise: winter *arrivals*

Not all winter change is about birds leaving. Some winters, **snowy owls irrupt**
south from the Arctic in numbers (often when Arctic prey like lemmings crash),
appearing in southern fields and along shorelines. So a Canadian winter *subtracts*
the migrants but can *add* northern visitors — winter isn't empty, it's a
different cast.

![A snowy owl — an Arctic bird that can appear in the south in winter](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Bubo_scandiacus_%28Linnaeus%2C_1758%29_Male.jpg/960px-Bubo_scandiacus_%28Linnaeus%2C_1758%29_Male.jpg)

***Snowy owl** — in "irruption" winters, this Arctic hunter shows up in southern
Canadian fields, a prize sighting that only the cold season offers. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Bubo_scandiacus_%28Linnaeus%2C_1758%29_Male.jpg).*

### The birding year at a glance

- **Spring (Mar–May):** the great return — geese and robins first, then a flood of
  migrants; dawn chorus peaks; the best, busiest birding.
- **Summer (Jun–Aug):** nesting season; birds are singing, paired, and feeding
  young; the boreal is packed.
- **Autumn (Sep–Nov):** migration south; geese in V's; leaves turn (Lesson 06);
  last chance for departing species.
- **Winter (Dec–Feb):** residents at feeders (chickadee, cardinal, nuthatch,
  woodpeckers) plus possible northern visitors (snowy owl, winter finches).

### Feeding birds in winter

A winter feeder is the single best way to watch the residents up close. Black-oil
sunflower seed suits most; **suet** draws woodpeckers and nuthatches. Two kindness
rules: **keep feeders clean** (dirty feeders spread disease) and, if you start in
a hard winter, **keep it stocked** — birds come to rely on a reliable source. And
place feeders to avoid window collisions (very close to, or far from, the glass).

{: .note }
> **Read the month, predict the bird**
> Geese overhead + returning robins = **spring**. Singing everywhere, packed
> boreal = **summer**. V's heading south + turning leaves = **autumn**.
> Chickadees, cardinals, and woodpeckers at the feeder, maybe a snowy owl in a
> field = **winter**. The calendar tells you what to look for.

"stays all winter": common loon, black-capped chickadee, osprey, northern
cardinal, great blue heron, downy woodpecker.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Migrate south:</strong> common loon, osprey, great blue heron (their food —
fish and open water — freezes over). <strong>Stay all winter:</strong> black-capped
chickadee, northern cardinal, downy woodpecker (they switch to seeds, buds, cached
food, and bark insects). The dividing line is <em>whether the bird's food survives
the winter</em>, not how cold-hardy it is.

**Exercise 2 — scenario.** In January a friend says "there are no birds in winter,

<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>

in some years <strong>snowy owls irrupt south</strong> from the Arctic into our
fields, a sighting you can <em>only</em> get in the cold months. So winter isn't
birdless — it's a different, and sometimes special, line-up." Full marks for naming
residents and the snowy-owl irruption idea.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — the why.** Explain in 2–3 sentences why so many birds bother to fly
thousands of kilometres to nest in Canada each summer, only to leave again.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "Because the Canadian summer offers something hard to beat for raising
young: <strong>very long daylight and an enormous burst of insects</strong> (and
open water and space), so parents can feed fast-growing chicks. When the food
collapses in autumn, staying would mean starving, so they migrate back south — but
the summer payoff is worth the journey. It's about <em>food and daylight for
breeding</em>, not the temperature itself." Full marks for the food/daylight
breeding advantage plus the food-collapse reason for leaving.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What is the real reason most migrating birds leave Canada for winter, and
what draws them back each spring?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
They leave because their <strong>food disappears</strong> (insects vanish, water
and ground freeze), not simply because it's cold. They return each spring because
the Canadian summer offers <strong>long daylight and abundant insects</strong>
(plus space and water) — ideal conditions for nesting and feeding young.
</details>

**Q2.** Name three birds from this course that stay all winter and one that
typically arrives *from the north* in winter.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Winter residents (any three): black-capped chickadee, northern cardinal, blue jay,
white-breasted nuthatch, downy or pileated woodpecker, ruffed grouse, Canada jay.
A bird that arrives from the north in winter: the <strong>snowy owl</strong> (in
"irruption" years, pushed south by Arctic food shortages).
</details>

**Q3.** What does an "irruption" mean, and give the classic Canadian example.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
An <strong>irruption</strong> is an unusual, larger-than-normal movement of a bird
species outside its usual range, typically driven by food shortages. The classic
Canadian example is the <strong>snowy owl</strong> moving south into southern
Canada (and the northern US) in certain winters when Arctic prey (like lemmings)
crashes.
</details>
