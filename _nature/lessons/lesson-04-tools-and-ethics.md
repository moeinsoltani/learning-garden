# Lesson 04: Tools, Apps & Field Manners

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **binoculars** (bih-NOCK-yoo-lars) — a handheld twin telescope for seeing distant birds close-up. "Binos" for short.
> - **magnification** — how much closer a thing looks; "8×" means eight times closer.
> - **field guide** — a book or app that shows and describes species so you can identify them.
> - **Merlin** — a free bird-ID app from the Cornell Lab; it can name a bird from a photo *or* from its song.
> - **iNaturalist** (eye-NATCH-ur-uh-list) — a free app/website that identifies any wild organism from a photo and logs your sightings.
> - **eBird** — a free app for recording bird sightings; the world's biggest bird database.
> - **playback** — playing a recording of a bird's song outdoors to lure it closer (often discouraged — see ethics).
> - **baiting** — luring an animal with food to photograph it (discouraged for wild raptors especially).

## Concept

You need almost nothing to start — your eyes and attention are 90% of it. But two
kinds of help make an enormous difference for a beginner: **one optical tool**
(binoculars) and **one or two free apps** (a bird-ID app and a "what-is-this" app).
Add a small notebook and a short code of good manners, and you're fully equipped.

![A Canada jay — a soft grey and white bird — perched close on a branch](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Perisoreus_canadensis_mercier2.jpg/960px-Perisoreus_canadensis_mercier2.jpg)

*Some birds, like this **Canada jay**, are so tame they'll land near you — a gift

## Going Deeper

### Binoculars: the one tool worth buying

For birds, binoculars turn "a dot in a tree" into "an eastern bluebird." What to
look for:

- **8×42 or 8×32** is the classic all-round choice. The first number is
  magnification (8× is steady and bright — bigger isn't better, because higher
  magnification shakes more and darkens the view); the second is lens width in mm
  (42 = brighter, 32 = lighter).
- **Close focus** matters more than you'd think — good for butterflies and
  nearby birds.
- You do **not** need to spend a fortune; a modest pair transforms the hobby.
  You also don't need binoculars at all for **trees** — those you can walk up to.

### The two apps that change everything

- **Merlin Bird ID** (free, from the Cornell Lab of Ornithology): answers a few
  questions or reads a **photo** *or* a **song from your phone's microphone** and
  tells you the bird. Sound ID is close to magic for a beginner — point your
  phone at a dawn chorus and watch the names appear.
- **iNaturalist** (free): photograph *any* wild thing — a tree, a bug, a
  wildflower — and it suggests an ID and logs it with a map pin. Great for trees,
  where you can get a clear leaf/bark photo.
- **eBird** (free): once you're hooked, this logs your bird sightings and shows
  you what others are seeing near you — a "what's around right now" map.

Apps are a **teacher, not a crutch**: check the app's suggestion against the
field marks you learned in Lessons 02–03 so you're building real skill, not just
outsourcing it.

### A notebook beats a memory

A pocket notebook (paper or phone) is the oldest birding tool. Jot **date, place,
species, and one detail** ("first robin of spring," "chickadees mobbing an owl").
Over a year this becomes your personal calendar of the natural world — and
re-reading it is how casual noticing becomes lasting knowledge.

### Field manners (ethics)

The one rule behind all the others: **the animal's welfare comes before your
sighting or your photo.**

- **Keep your distance.** If a bird changes its behaviour because of you (stops
  feeding, gives alarm calls, flushes), you're too close. Back off.
- **Never disturb nests.** Don't approach, touch, or photograph nests up close —
  you can lead predators to them or cause a parent to abandon.
- **Go easy on playback.** Playing a bird's song to lure it wastes the bird's
  energy defending a rival that isn't there; avoid it in breeding season and at
  busy sites, and never for rare or stressed birds.
- **Don't bait raptors** (e.g. releasing pet-store mice to get an owl photo) — it
  habituates dangerous behaviours and draws birds to roadsides.
- **Stay on trails; take only pictures.** Protect the habitat you came to enjoy,
  and don't pick or damage plants (that gorgeous trillium is protected in parts
  of Canada).
- **Share sightings responsibly.** For sensitive or rare species, don't broadcast
  exact locations that could draw crowds.

> **A beginner's starter kit**
> 1. Your eyes + the method from Lessons 02–03.
> 2. **Merlin** (birds by photo/sound) and **iNaturalist** (anything by photo) —
>    both free.
> 3. A modest pair of **8×42 binoculars** when you're ready to invest.
> 4. A pocket **notebook**.
> 5. The one ethics rule: **welfare before the shot.**

## Lab — Look & Identify

**Exercise 1 — kit choice.** A friend asks what to buy first to "get into
birding." They have $0 today. What do you tell them to do *before* spending
anything, and what's the first paid item you'd suggest later?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Before spending: install the free apps — <strong>Merlin</strong> (identifies
birds by photo and by song from the phone's mic) and optionally
<strong>iNaturalist</strong> for trees and everything else — and start using the
size/shape/behaviour method on their daily walk. The first paid item later:
a modest pair of <strong>8×42 (or 8×32) binoculars</strong>. Note that trees need
no gear at all — you can walk up to them.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — read the binocular spec.** Your friend sees two pairs: "10×50" and
"8×42." Explain in plain words what those numbers mean and why 8×42 is often the
better beginner choice.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
The first number is <strong>magnification</strong> (how many times closer things
look): 10× vs 8×. The second is the <strong>front lens width in millimetres</strong>
(how much light they gather, i.e. brightness): 50 vs 42. 8×42 is often better for
beginners because lower magnification gives a <strong>steadier, wider, brighter</strong>
view that's easier to hold and to find birds in; 10× shakes more and has a narrower
field. Bigger numbers aren't automatically better.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — ethics scenario.** At a park you meet someone playing a loud owl
call from a speaker in spring to draw an owl out for photos, near where the owl is
nesting. In 3–4 sentences, explain kindly why that's discouraged and suggest a
better approach.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "I get the temptation — but heavy <em>playback</em> in breeding season
makes the owl burn energy defending its territory against a rival that isn't
there, and repeated disturbance near a nest can push a parent off eggs or draw
predators. It can be genuinely harmful for a nesting or rare bird. A kinder path
is to wait quietly at a respectful distance, let the owl behave naturally, and
accept that a wilder, unbaited photo is the more honest one." Full marks for the
principle — <em>the bird's welfare comes before the photo</em> — delivered
warmly rather than as a scolding.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What does "8×42" mean on a pair of binoculars, and why isn't a higher
first number automatically better?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
8× is the <strong>magnification</strong> (things look eight times closer); 42 is
the <strong>front lens diameter in mm</strong> (light-gathering / brightness).
A higher magnification isn't automatically better because it <strong>shakes more,
darkens the image, and narrows the field of view</strong>, making birds harder to
hold and find — which is why 8× is the popular all-round choice.
</details>

**Q2.** Name the two free apps recommended here and what each is best at.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Merlin Bird ID</strong> — identifies <em>birds</em> from a photo or from
their <em>song</em> via the phone's microphone. <strong>iNaturalist</strong> —
identifies <em>any</em> wild organism (trees, insects, plants) from a photo and
logs it on a map. (eBird is a bonus: it records sightings and shows what's being
seen nearby.)
</details>

**Q3.** State the single rule that underlies all birding ethics, and give two
concrete things it tells you not to do.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The rule: <strong>the animal's welfare comes before your sighting or photo.</strong>
Concrete "don'ts" (any two): don't approach or disturb nests; don't over-use
playback (especially in breeding season or for rare/stressed birds); don't bait
raptors with food; keep enough distance that you don't change the bird's
behaviour; stay on trails and don't damage habitat; don't broadcast exact
locations of sensitive species.
</details>

## Homework

Install **one** free app (Merlin is the easiest start) and use it **once** in the
field: point it at a bird's song, or photograph a tree's leaf. Write down what it
told you, and then check that answer against one field mark you learned in Lessons
02–03. Did the app and your own eyes agree?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
There's no single right result — the value is in the habit of *cross-checking*.
A strong answer names what the app suggested and then confirms it with a field
mark you saw yourself, e.g.: "Merlin's Sound ID said <em>black-capped chickadee</em>;
I then saw a small round bird with a black cap and white cheeks, which matches, so
I'm confident." If the app and your eyes ever disagree, that's a learning moment —
look again and note which field mark decides it. Using the app as a *teacher you
check*, not a crutch, is exactly the point.
</details>
