# Lesson 08: Iconic & Provincial Trees

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **arboreal emblem** (ar-BORE-ee-ul) — the official tree symbol of a province, territory, or country. "Arboreal" means "of trees."
> - **provincial / territorial** — belonging to one of Canada's 10 provinces or 3 territories.
> - **national tree** — Canada's official tree, the **maple**, declared in 1996.
> - **lodgepole pine** — a tall, straight western pine (Alberta's tree); "lodgepole" because Indigenous peoples used the poles for dwellings.
> - **tamarack / larch** — a conifer that drops its needles each fall (the NWT's tree).
> - **treeless** — having no trees; most of Nunavut is tundra, north of where trees can grow.

## Concept

Canada loves its trees enough to make them official. The country has a **national
tree** — the **maple** (declared the national arboreal emblem in **1996**) —
behind the leaf on the flag, and **every province and most territories have their
own official tree**, usually one that shaped local life. Learning these is a
delightful shortcut: it ties the species from Lessons 05–07 to the map of Canada,
and half of them are trees you can already identify.

![Canada's flag, centred on the red maple leaf](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Flag_of_Canada_%28Pantone%29.svg/960px-Flag_of_Canada_%28Pantone%29.svg.png)

*The **maple** is Canada's national tree (1996) and the source of the flag's leaf.
No single maple species is named in law, but the **sugar maple** is the popular
face of the emblem. Image:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Flag_of_Canada_%28Pantone%29.svg).*

who's-who of the conifers and broadleaf trees you learned.

### Three worth a closer look

![Tall eastern white pines — Ontario's provincial tree](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Pinus_strobus_trees.jpg/960px-Pinus_strobus_trees.jpg)

***Eastern white pine (Ontario)** — the tallest tree in eastern Canada and the
timber that built the colonial economy (masts for the Royal Navy). Five soft
needles per bundle. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Pinus_strobus_trees.jpg).*

![A giant western redcedar — British Columbia's provincial tree](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Thuja_plicata_Vancouver.jpg/960px-Thuja_plicata_Vancouver.jpg)

***Western redcedar (British Columbia)** — the "tree of life" of the Pacific
Coast, central to First Nations culture (canoes, longhouses, totem poles) and to
BC's forest industry. Scale-leaves in flat sprays. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Thuja_plicata_Vancouver.jpg).*

![Paper birches with white peeling bark — Saskatchewan's provincial tree](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/White_birch_at_Acadia_National_Park,_ME_IMG_2177.JPG?width=960)

***Paper birch (Saskatchewan)** — white peeling bark, the canoe bark of the
northern forest. That a Prairie province picks a birch is a reminder that
Saskatchewan's north is deep boreal forest, not just wheat fields. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:White_birch_at_Acadia_National_Park,_ME_IMG_2177.JPG).*

### Why Nunavut has no tree

Nunavut is **north of the treeline** — the latitude/altitude beyond which trees
can't grow, because the summer is too short and cold and the ground stays frozen
(permafrost). Its landscape is **tundra**: mosses, lichens, dwarf shrubs, and
wildflowers, but no forest. So Canada's newest territory is the one place that
*can't* have an arboreal emblem — a neat illustration of how far north the
country reaches.

{: .note }
> **The trees map onto the land**
> Provincial trees aren't random: BC's giant coastal redcedar, the Prairies'
> hardy spruces and birches, the Maritimes' spruce and fir, Ontario's great white
> pine. Learn the emblem and you've learned something true about the province's
> forests.

Ontario, (c) British Columbia. For each, give one identifying feature from earlier
lessons.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) Canada → the <strong>maple</strong> (opposite lobed leaves + helicopter

**Exercise 2 — scenario.** A friend is surprised that Nunavut has no official
tree. Explain why in 2–3 sentences, using the words *treeline*, *tundra*, and
*permafrost*.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "Nunavut lies mostly north of the <em>treeline</em> — the limit beyond
which trees can't survive because summers are too short and cold and the ground
is locked in <em>permafrost</em>. Instead of forest it's <em>tundra</em>: low
mosses, lichens, and dwarf shrubs. With no trees able to grow, there's simply no
species to name as an arboreal emblem." Full marks for the causal chain: too cold
+ frozen ground → no trees → no tree emblem.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — map logic.** Saskatchewan and Manitoba are "Prairie provinces,"
yet their trees are the paper birch and white spruce (forest trees). What does
that tell you about these provinces?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "That 'Prairie province' only describes the <em>south</em>. The northern
halves of Saskatchewan and Manitoba are deep <strong>boreal forest</strong> —
spruce, birch, pine, and lakes — not grassland. Picking a forest tree as the
emblem reflects that most of each province's *area* is northern forest, even
though most of its *people* live in the southern farm belt." Full marks for the
insight that these provinces are forest in the north and prairie in the south.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What is Canada's national tree, when was it declared, and what everyday
Canadian product and symbol connect to it?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The <strong>maple</strong>, declared Canada's national arboreal emblem in
<strong>1996</strong>. It connects to the <strong>maple leaf on the flag</strong>

redcedar</strong>; Saskatchewan — <strong>paper (white) birch</strong>. (All three

</details>

**Q3.** Why is Nunavut the one region without an official tree?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>

<strong>tundra</strong> with <strong>permafrost</strong> and summers too short and
