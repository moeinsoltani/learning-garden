# Lesson 14: Iconic Canadian Birds

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **emblem** — an official or unofficial symbol standing for a place.
> - **loonie** — the Canadian $1 coin, named for the **loon** pictured on it.
> - **national bird** — a country's official bird; Canada has **no official** one, though the Canada jay was a popular proposal.
> - **provincial bird** — a province's official bird (e.g. snowy owl for Quebec).
> - **whisky-jack** — a folk name for the Canada jay, from the Cree *Wisakedjak*.
> - **iconic** (eye-KON-ik) — widely recognised as a symbol of something.

## Concept

A few birds have become part of how Canada pictures itself — on coins, on
provincial lists, and in the national imagination. You already know all of them
from earlier lessons; this lesson ties them to their symbolism, which makes them
easy to remember and fun to talk about. Meet Canada's four most **iconic** birds:
the **common loon**, the **Canada jay**, the **snowy owl**, and the **Canada
goose**.

![A common loon on calm water, black head and checkerboard back](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Gavia_immer_-Minocqua%2C_Wisconsin%2C_USA_-swimming-8.jpg/960px-Gavia_immer_-Minocqua%2C_Wisconsin%2C_USA_-swimming-8.jpg)

***Common loon** — perhaps *the* sound of Canada: its wild, yodelling call over a
still northern lake. It's pictured on the **$1 coin**, which is why Canadians call
the coin the "**loonie**" (and the $2 coin the "toonie"). It's also the provincial
bird of Ontario. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Gavia_immer_-Minocqua%2C_Wisconsin%2C_USA_-swimming-8.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### The four icons and what they stand for

| Bird | Symbol / status | Where you met it |
|---|---|---|
| **Common loon** | On the **$1 "loonie" coin**; Ontario's provincial bird; the sound of the north | Lesson 11 |
| **Canada jay** | Popular pick for **national bird** (2016 campaign); the tame "whisky-jack" | Lesson 13 |
| **Snowy owl** | **Official bird of Quebec**; Arctic icon | Lesson 12 |
| **Canada goose** | Unofficial national emblem; the bird literally named "Canada" | Lesson 11 |

### Does Canada have a national bird?

**No official one** — and that surprises people. In 2016 a national campaign (led
by *Canadian Geographic*) held a vote and recommended the **Canada jay** as the
national bird, arguing it's found in every province and territory, is smart, tough,
and friendly, and carries deep Indigenous meaning as the whisky-jack. But the
government never made it official, so as of now Canada has a national *tree* (the
maple) and a national *animal* (the **beaver**, plus the horse) but **no national
bird**. It's a great piece of trivia — and a reminder that "iconic" and "official"
aren't the same thing.

![A Canada jay perched close, soft grey with a gentle look](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Perisoreus_canadensis_mercier2.jpg/960px-Perisoreus_canadensis_mercier2.jpg)

***Canada jay** — the bird a national campaign proposed as Canada's official bird:
found coast to coast to coast, hardy enough to nest in late-winter cold, endearing
enough to land on your hand, and rich in Indigenous heritage as the "whisky-jack."
Never made official — but iconic all the same. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Perisoreus_canadensis_mercier2.jpg).*

### The bird named "Canada"

![A Canada goose with its black neck and white chin-strap](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Canada_goose.jpg/960px-Canada_goose.jpg)

***Canada goose** — the only common bird with "Canada" in its name, and an
unofficial national mascot the world over. Its honking **V-formation** overhead is
a seasonal signal Canadians read like a calendar: geese heading south means
winter; geese heading north means spring. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Canada_goose.jpg).*

### The Arctic emblem

![A snowy owl, round-headed and white with dark flecks](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Bubo_scandiacus_%28Linnaeus%2C_1758%29_Male.jpg/960px-Bubo_scandiacus_%28Linnaeus%2C_1758%29_Male.jpg)

***Snowy owl** — the **official bird of Quebec** and an emblem of the Canadian
North: white as the tundra it breeds on, and a thrilling winter visitor to
southern fields. It also once appeared on the Canadian $50 bill. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Bubo_scandiacus_%28Linnaeus%2C_1758%29_Male.jpg).*

{: .note }
> **Official vs. iconic**
> Canada has a national *tree* (maple, 1996) and national *animals* (the beaver
> and the horse) — but **no official national bird**, despite the Canada jay's
> popular 2016 nomination. The loon (loonie coin) and snowy owl (Quebec) are
> official at other levels. "Iconic" is about feeling; "official" is about law.

## Lab — Look & Identify

**Exercise 1 — coin & country.** Why is the Canadian $1 coin called the "loonie,"
and what does that tell you about the loon's place in Canadian culture?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Because the $1 coin depicts a <strong>common loon</strong>, Canadians nicknamed it
the "<strong>loonie</strong>" (and the $2 coin the "toonie"). Putting the loon on
the everyday coin — and the fact the nickname stuck nationwide — shows how deeply
the loon, and its wild call over northern lakes, is woven into Canadian identity.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the trick question.** A friend confidently says "Canada's national
bird is the Canada goose." Gently correct them and give the fuller story in 3–4
sentences.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>

Model: "Actually, Canada has <strong>no official national bird</strong> at all.
There was a big 2016 campaign that recommended the <strong>Canada jay</strong> (the
tame grey 'whisky-jack,' found in every province and territory), but the government
never adopted it, so nothing is official. The Canada <em>goose</em> is a beloved
unofficial mascot and the only bird named 'Canada,' which is probably why people
assume it's the national bird — but officially, the spot is still empty." Full
marks for the no-official-bird fact plus the Canada jay campaign context.
</details>

<details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is the Canadian dollar coin called the "loonie," and what bird gives
it the name?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The $1 coin pictures a <strong>common loon</strong>, so Canadians call it the
"<strong>loonie</strong>" (and the $2 coin, by extension, the "toonie"). The loon —
and its haunting call — is a symbol of Canada's northern lakes.
</details>

**Q2.** Does Canada have an official national bird? Explain, and name the bird a
2016 campaign proposed.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>No</strong> — Canada has no official national bird. A 2016 campaign by
<em>Canadian Geographic</em> recommended the <strong>Canada jay</strong> (the
"whisky-jack"), citing its coast-to-coast range, hardiness, friendliness, and
Indigenous heritage, but the government never made it official. Canada does have an
official national tree (maple) and animals (beaver and horse).
</details>

**Q3.** Which iconic bird is the official bird of Quebec, and which is the only
common bird with "Canada" in its name?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The <strong>snowy owl</strong> is the official bird of <strong>Quebec</strong>. The
<strong>Canada goose</strong> is the only common bird with "Canada" in its name —
an unofficial national mascot known worldwide for its V-formation and honking.
</details>

## Homework

Pick **one** of the four iconic birds and write a short "why it deserves to be a
symbol of Canada" paragraph (4–6 sentences), weaving in at least two facts from
this course (its field marks, its behaviour, its habitat, or its cultural role).
Then say whether *you'd* vote for it as national bird, and why.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong answer blends identification with meaning, e.g. for the loon: "The common
loon is the voice of the Canadian wilderness — its yodelling call over a still
boreal lake is unmistakable. You know it by its checkerboard back and dagger bill,
and by the way it rides low and vanishes underwater to hunt fish, thanks to its
dense diving-adapted body. It's already on the $1 coin, so it's woven into daily
life. I'd vote for it because it captures the vast, watery, northern character of
the country better than any other bird." Full marks for combining at least two
course facts (marks/behaviour/habitat/culture) with a reasoned opinion — there's no
wrong choice, only weak or strong support.
</details>
