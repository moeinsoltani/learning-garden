# Lesson 13: Forest Birds

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **woodpecker** — a bird that clings to trunks and drills wood for insects and to make nest holes.
> - **drumming** — a woodpecker's fast hammering on wood, used like a song to claim territory.
> - **downy** (DOW-nee) — the smallest common woodpecker; "downy" for its soft white back feathers.
> - **pileated** (PIE-lee-ay-tid *or* PILL-ee-ay-tid) — the big crow-sized woodpecker with a red crest.
> - **grouse** (rhymes with "house") — a plump ground-dwelling forest bird, like a wild chicken.
> - **Canada jay** — a soft grey jay of the northern forest, famously tame; also called "whisky-jack."
> - **camouflage** (KAM-oo-flahzh) — colouring that blends into the background (the grouse's specialty).
> - **cache** (kash) — food hidden away to eat later; Canada jays cache thousands of items for winter.

## Concept

Step into the woods and the birds change again — many you'll **hear before you
see**. Four are especially worth knowing: two **woodpeckers** (the tiny downy and
the giant pileated), the plump **ruffed grouse** on the forest floor, and the
tame grey **Canada jay** of the boreal. Each is identified by a distinctive
behaviour or sound as much as by looks.

![A downy woodpecker clinging to a stem: small, black-and-white, checkered wings](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Dryobates_pubescens_UL_03.jpg/960px-Dryobates_pubescens_UL_03.jpg)

***Downy woodpecker** — the smallest and commonest woodpecker, sparrow-sized:
**black-and-white checkered** wings, white back, a small bill, and (on males) a
little **red patch on the back of the head**. Comes to suet feeders and clings to
thin stems. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Dryobates_pubescens_UL_03.jpg).*

## Going Deeper

### The two woodpeckers: same pattern, very different size

![A pileated woodpecker on a trunk: crow-sized, black body, flaming red crest](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/PileatedWoodpeckerFeedingonTree%2C_crop.jpg/960px-PileatedWoodpeckerFeedingonTree%2C_crop.jpg)

***Pileated woodpecker** — the giant: nearly **crow-sized**, black with white neck
stripes and a flaming **red triangular crest** (the "Woody Woodpecker" bird). It
chisels big **rectangular holes** in dead trees hunting carpenter ants, and its
loud ringing call and deep drumming carry through the forest. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:PileatedWoodpeckerFeedingonTree%2C_crop.jpg).*

Downy and pileated share the woodpecker toolkit but sit at opposite ends of the
size scale — **sparrow-sized vs. crow-sized** — so size alone separates them.
Both **drum** on wood (and metal) to claim territory: that fast machine-gun
hammering is a *song*, not feeding.

### The ground bird: ruffed grouse

![A ruffed grouse standing on the forest floor, mottled brown, well camouflaged](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Hey_You%21_%2830559610814%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/960px-Hey_You%21_%2830559610814%29_%28cropped%29.jpg)

***Ruffed grouse** — a plump, chicken-like forest bird, beautifully **camouflaged**
in mottled brown, with a small crest and a fan-shaped tail. You usually meet it by
accident: it **explodes into flight from your feet** with a startling whir of
wings. In spring, males "**drum**" by beating their wings to make a deep, accelerating
*thump…thump…thump-thump-thrrrr* you feel more than hear. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Hey_You%21_%2830559610814%29_%28cropped%29.jpg).*

### The friendly one: Canada jay

![A Canada jay perched close: soft grey and white, no crest, gentle expression](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Perisoreus_canadensis_mercier2.jpg/960px-Perisoreus_canadensis_mercier2.jpg)

***Canada jay** — soft grey above, pale below, with a white face and dark nape;
**no crest** (unlike the blue jay). A bird of the boreal and mountain forests,
famous for being **fearless around people** — it'll take food from your hand,
earning the old name "whisky-jack" (from the Cree *Wisakedjak*). It **caches**
thousands of food items in bark to survive winter, and nests in the cold of late
winter. Photo:
[Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Perisoreus_canadensis_mercier2.jpg).*

### Heard before seen: the forest soundscape

Forest birding leans heavily on the ear:

- **Fast hammering on wood** → a woodpecker **drumming** (territory), not eating.
- **A deep, accelerating thumping** in spring woods → a male **ruffed grouse**
  drumming with its wings.
- **A sudden explosion of wingbeats from the ground** → you just flushed a grouse.
- **A loud, ringing, jungle-like call** deep in the trees → often a **pileated
  woodpecker**.

{: .note }
> **Two jays, one difference**
> **Blue jay** (Lesson 10): blue, crested, loud, in towns and parks.
> **Canada jay** (this lesson): soft grey, **no crest**, silent and tame, in the
> northern/mountain forest. If a "jay" is grey and lets you get close, it's the
> Canada jay.

## Lab — Look & Identify

**Exercise 1 — which woodpecker?** (a) A sparrow-sized black-and-white bird with a
small bill and a red spot on the back of its head, at a suet feeder. (b) A nearly
crow-sized black bird with a flaming red crest, chiselling a rectangular hole in a
dead tree. Name each.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) sparrow-sized, small bill, red nape spot → <strong>downy woodpecker</strong>;
(b) crow-sized, red crest, big rectangular holes → <strong>pileated
woodpecker</strong>. Same black-and-white woodpecker family, but <strong>size</strong>
(sparrow vs. crow) separates them instantly, with the pileated's red crest
clinching it.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — name that sound.** In spring woods you hear a deep, accelerating
*thump… thump… thump-thump-thrrrr* with no visible bird, felt almost in your
chest. What's making it, and how?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A male <strong>ruffed grouse</strong> "<strong>drumming</strong>" — beating its
wings rapidly against the air (not against a log) to create a low, quickening
thumping that carries through the forest as a territorial and courtship signal.
It's a great example of a bird identified entirely by sound and behaviour, since
the drummer is usually hidden.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — scenario.** Hiking in the boreal, a grey bird with no crest lands
right beside your friend and eyes their sandwich. Your friend thinks it's a tame
blue jay. Correct them and add one fascinating fact.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "It's a <strong>Canada jay</strong>, not a blue jay — notice it's soft
<em>grey</em> with <em>no crest</em>, and it's famously fearless, which is why it's
right next to your lunch (old-timers call it 'whisky-jack'). A great fact: to
survive the northern winter it <strong>caches</strong> thousands of food items,
gluing them into bark with sticky saliva, and it even nests in late winter while
snow is still deep." Full marks for the grey/no-crest distinction from the blue
jay plus a real Canada-jay fact (caching or whisky-jack name).
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** How do you tell a downy woodpecker from a pileated woodpecker, and what is
"drumming"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
By <strong>size</strong>: the downy is <strong>sparrow-sized</strong> with a small
bill and a red nape spot (male); the pileated is nearly <strong>crow-sized</strong>
with a flaming red crest and makes big rectangular holes. <strong>Drumming</strong>
is a woodpecker's rapid hammering on a resonant surface — a territorial signal (a
"song"), not feeding.
</details>

**Q2.** Give two ways you'd most likely detect a ruffed grouse in the forest.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
(1) It <strong>explodes into flight from underfoot</strong> with a startling whir
of wings when you nearly step on it. (2) In spring, a male's <strong>wing-drumming</strong>
— a deep, accelerating <em>thump…thump…thump-thump-thrrrr</em> — carries through
the woods. Its camouflage means you rarely spot it sitting still.
</details>

**Q3.** How do you distinguish a Canada jay from a blue jay, and what winter
survival trick is the Canada jay known for?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The <strong>Canada jay is soft grey with no crest</strong> (and very tame, in
northern/mountain forests); the <strong>blue jay is bright blue with a crest</strong>
(in towns and parks). The Canada jay survives winter by <strong>caching</strong>
thousands of food items, sticking them into tree bark with saliva to retrieve in
the cold months — and it even nests in late winter.
</details>

## Homework

Take a walk somewhere with real trees (a woodlot, ravine, park, or trail) and
**listen** as much as look. Note any woodpecker (size?), any sudden burst of wings
from the ground, or any tame grey jay. Write down one bird you identified partly or
wholly **by sound or behaviour** rather than by seeing it clearly.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong answer shows ear-and-behaviour birding, e.g.: "I heard fast hammering and
found a small black-and-white bird with a red nape spot → <strong>downy
woodpecker</strong> (drumming, then confirmed by sight)." Or: "A plump bird burst
from the ground at my feet in a whir of wings → a <strong>ruffed grouse</strong>,
identified purely by that behaviour." Full marks for naming a bird detected first
by sound or behaviour — the core skill of forest birding.
</details>
