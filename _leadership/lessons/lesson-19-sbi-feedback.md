# Lesson 19: Feedback That Lands — SBI

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **SBI** — Situation–Behavior–Impact: the feedback structure this lesson teaches.
> - **behavioral feedback** — feedback about what someone *did*, never about what kind of person they *are*.
> - **observation vs judgment** — what a camera would have recorded vs your interpretation of their character.
> - **verdict** — a judgment pronounced on someone, as if by a court.
> - **to land** (of feedback) — to be actually heard and accepted, not deflected.
> - **raise shields** — to trigger someone's defenses (a Star Trek metaphor).
> - **actionable** — concrete enough that the person knows what to change.
> - **timely** — given soon after the event, while it's fresh.
> - **ambush** — surprising someone with saved-up criticism they never had a chance to fix.
> - **feedback ratio** — the balance of recognition to criticism; if your attention only means criticism, feedback becomes a threat.

## Concept

Feedback is one of a lead's highest-leverage tools — it's how behavior changes, how people
grow, and how a team stays healthy. But most feedback fails: it's either too vague to act on
("be more of a team player"), or it's a judgment that raises shields ("you're careless"). The
skill is **behavioral feedback** — grounded in specific observed behavior and its impact — that
someone can actually hear and act on, rather than a character verdict they defend against.

```
   FEEDBACK THAT RAISES SHIELDS        FEEDBACK THAT LANDS (SBI)
   ────────────────────────────        ─────────────────────────
   "You're careless."                  SITUATION: "In the last two PRs..."
   "You dominate meetings."            BEHAVIOR:  "...they shipped without tests,"
   "You need to be a team player."     IMPACT:    "...and two bugs reached staging."
        ↑ character judgment,               ↑ observed facts + effect,
          vague, invites defense              specific, hard to dispute, actionable
```

The reframe: **describe behavior and its impact, not the person's character.** "You're
careless" is a verdict on who they are — vague, wounding, and impossible to act on. "The last
two PRs shipped without tests, and two bugs reached staging" is an observation of what happened
and its effect — specific, factual, and clearly actionable. SBI (Situation–Behavior–Impact) is
the reliable structure that keeps feedback behavioral, and it's a skill you can practice.

## Going Deeper

### Observation, not judgment

The core distinction: **observation** (what you actually saw — "the last two PRs shipped without
tests") versus **judgment** (your interpretation of their character — "you're careless").
Judgments raise shields (they attack identity, invite defense, and offer nothing to fix);
observations are workable (they're facts, hard to dispute, and point at a specific changeable
behavior). Feedback that lands sticks to observation — describe what happened, not what kind of
person they are.

### The SBI structure

A reliable frame that keeps feedback behavioral:

- **Situation** — anchor it to a specific concrete instance ("in yesterday's design review", "in
  the last two PRs"), not a vague generalization ("you always...").
- **Behavior** — the specific observable behavior, stated as neutral fact ("you interrupted Sana
  twice before she finished", "the PRs went in without tests") — what a camera would have caught,
  not your interpretation.
- **Impact** — the effect it had ("Sana stopped contributing for the rest of the meeting", "two
  bugs reached staging and cost the team a day") — which shows *why* the behavior matters, and
  motivates change by making the consequence concrete.

Then open a dialogue ("what's your view?") — SBI states your observation; the conversation
explores it.

### Timeliness

Give feedback **soon** after the event, while it's fresh and specific — not saved up for a
quarterly review (where it's a stale surprise the person can't connect to a real moment, and
can't act on because it's long past). Timely feedback is actionable (they remember the situation
and can change next time) and fair (no ambushes). The best feedback is close to the behavior.

### Ask before telling

Often the most effective opener isn't your feedback but a question: "how did you feel that
review went?" / "what's your read on the last sprint?" This (a) may surface that they already
see it (so you're coaching, not correcting), (b) gives you their context (which may change your
feedback), and (c) makes it a dialogue from the start. Ask first, then share your observation —
it's more respectful and often more effective than leading with the verdict.

### The feedback ratio

Feedback shouldn't be only critical. A team where the lead only speaks up to criticize learns
that the lead's attention means something's wrong — which is demoralizing and makes critical
feedback feel like an attack. Regular *positive* feedback (Lesson 20) — genuine, specific
recognition — builds the relationship and the trust that makes critical feedback land. The point
isn't a rigid ratio, but that recognition is half the feedback job, not an afterthought.

{: .note }
> **Feedback is behavioral and specific, delivered soon — not a character verdict saved up</br>**
> Most feedback fails by being a vague character judgment ("you're careless", "be more of a team
> player") that raises shields and can't be acted on. Feedback that lands describes specific
> observed <em>behavior</em> and its <em>impact</em> (SBI: Situation–Behavior–Impact) — facts and
> effect, not judgment — which the person can hear without defensiveness and act on because it's
> concrete. Give it soon (fresh and actionable, not a stale quarterly surprise), often ask before
> telling (a question surfaces their view and makes it a dialogue), and balance it with genuine
> recognition (so your attention doesn't only mean criticism). SBI is a reliable, practiceable
> structure — the difference between feedback that changes behavior and feedback that just wounds.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** One of your engineers, Alex, is technically strong but dominates every
meeting — he interrupts people (especially the two junior engineers), talks over others, and
tends to shoot down ideas quickly with "that won't work." In yesterday's design review, he cut
off Sana (a junior) twice before she finished her point, and after the second time she went quiet
and didn't speak again for the rest of the meeting. You've noticed this pattern for a few weeks,
and it's starting to suppress the juniors' participation.

**Write the feedback you'd give Alex, word for word** — the actual conversation opener and your
SBI feedback. Then note what makes it work and the mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong feedback conversation opens by setting a caring frame, often asks before telling, then
delivers specific SBI feedback (behavior + impact, not character), and opens a dialogue. Example:
<br><br>
"Hey Alex, do you have a few minutes? I want to share some feedback, and I want you to know it
comes from a good place — you're one of the strongest engineers on the team and I value your
input a lot. <em>(Caring frame — establishes intent so it's not heard as an attack.)</em>
<br><br>
Before I share my read — how did you feel yesterday's design review went? <em>(Ask before
telling — surfaces his view; he may already sense it, or give context.)</em>
<br><br>
[He responds.] Here's what I noticed: <strong>in yesterday's review (situation)</strong>, when
Sana was walking through her caching idea, <strong>you cut in before she'd finished — twice
(behavior)</strong>. <strong>After the second time, she went quiet and didn't speak again for the
rest of the meeting (impact)</strong>. And honestly, I've seen a version of this a few times
lately — the quick interruptions and fast 'that won't work' — and I'm noticing the juniors are
participating less as a result. <em>(SBI: specific situation, observed behavior stated as fact
not judgment, and the concrete impact — plus the pattern, still described behaviorally.)</em>
<br><br>
I don't think you mean to shut anyone down — I think it's your speed and enthusiasm. But the
effect is that we're losing the juniors' ideas, which we need. <em>(Assumes good intent; separates
the behavior from character; reiterates why it matters.)</em>
<br><br>
How do you see it? And could we figure out a way to keep your sharpness but make more room for
others — maybe letting people finish, or holding your first reaction a beat? <em>(Opens dialogue;
invites him into the solution rather than dictating.)</em>"
<br><br>
<strong>What makes it work:</strong> (1) <strong>Caring frame first</strong> — establishing good
intent and genuine respect up front means the feedback is heard as help, not attack (especially
important with a strong performer whose ego is engaged). (2) <strong>Asks before telling</strong>
— the opening question surfaces his perspective and makes it a dialogue, not a verdict. (3)
<strong>SBI, not judgment</strong> — "you cut off Sana twice and she went quiet" (observed
behavior + impact) rather than "you're domineering" (character verdict); the specific, factual
version is hard to dispute and points at a changeable behavior, where the judgment would raise
his shields and give nothing to fix. (4) <strong>Concrete impact</strong> — "she went quiet",
"we're losing the juniors' ideas" — makes the consequence real, which is what motivates change
(he probably didn't see the effect). (5) <strong>Assumes good intent</strong> — "I don't think
you mean to" separates the behavior from his character, letting him change without feeling
labeled a bad person. (6) <strong>Opens dialogue and co-creates the fix</strong> — inviting his
view and his ideas for the solution builds ownership (he commits to a change he helped shape).
(7) <strong>Timely</strong> — it's the day after, while it's fresh and specific. <strong>Mistakes
to avoid:</strong> (1) leading with a character judgment ("you're dominating meetings") — raises
shields, unactionable; (2) being vague ("be more inclusive") — he won't know what to change; (3)
saving it for a quarterly review — stale, unactionable, feels like an ambush; (4) doing it
publicly (in the meeting or channel) — humiliating, breaks trust (critical feedback is private);
(5) not stating the impact — without "she went quiet / we lose their ideas", he has no reason to
see it as a problem; (6) making it a monologue verdict — no dialogue, so no buy-in and you might
be missing context; (7) piling on every instance ever — the specific recent example + the named
pattern is enough; a list of every past sin overwhelms and feels like an attack.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why does behavioral feedback (SBI) land where character judgments raise shields, and what
are the three parts of SBI?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Behavioral feedback lands where character judgments raise shields because <strong>a person can
hear and act on a description of specific behavior, but a judgment of their character attacks their
identity — which triggers defensiveness and offers nothing to change</strong>. Compare "you're
careless" (judgment) with "the last two PRs shipped without tests and two bugs reached staging"
(behavioral). The judgment fails several ways: (1) it's a <strong>character verdict</strong> — it
says something about who they <em>are</em> (a careless person), which is identity-threatening, so
they defend their character rather than engage with the feedback; (2) it's <strong>vague</strong> —
"careless" doesn't point at any specific action, so even if they accept it, they don't know what
to do differently; (3) it's <strong>disputable</strong> — "careless" is your interpretation, which
they can simply reject ("I'm not careless"), leading to an argument rather than progress; (4) it
<strong>wounds</strong> — being labeled hurts and lingers, damaging the relationship. Behavioral
feedback fixes all of these: it's <strong>an observation, not a judgment</strong> (what you saw,
which is factual and hard to dispute), <strong>specific</strong> (a concrete behavior they can
change), and <strong>not identity-threatening</strong> (about a thing they did, not who they are,
so they stay open). The person can hear "the PRs shipped without tests" without their ego mounting
a defense, and they know exactly what to change. The three parts of SBI: (1) <strong>Situation</strong>
— anchor the feedback to a specific concrete instance ("in yesterday's review", "in the last two
PRs"), not a vague generalization ("you always..."), so it's grounded in a real moment they can
recall; (2) <strong>Behavior</strong> — describe the specific observable behavior as neutral fact
("you interrupted Sana twice before she finished", "the PRs went in without tests") — what a camera
would have recorded, not your interpretation of it; and (3) <strong>Impact</strong> — state the
effect the behavior had ("Sana went quiet and stopped contributing", "two bugs reached staging and
cost a day"), which shows <em>why</em> the behavior matters and motivates change by making the
consequence concrete. SBI works because it keeps feedback anchored in a real situation, factual
about the behavior, and focused on impact — which together make it specific (actionable), factual
(non-disputable and non-attacking), and consequential (the person sees why it matters). After SBI,
you open a dialogue ("how do you see it?") because you may be missing context and because people
commit to changes they help shape. The whole point is that <strong>feedback only works if the
person can receive it and act on it</strong> — and a character judgment, however accurate, usually
triggers the defensiveness that prevents receiving, while SBI's behavioral specificity lets the
feedback land and translate into changed behavior. It's a reliable, practiceable structure that
makes the difference between feedback that changes behavior and feedback that just raises shields.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is "ask before telling" and giving feedback soon (rather than saving it for a review)
more effective?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Ask before telling</strong> is more effective because <strong>opening with a question
surfaces the person's own perspective, makes the feedback a dialogue rather than a verdict, and
often lets them arrive at the insight themselves</strong>. Several benefits: (1) <strong>they may
already see it</strong> — asking "how did you feel that review went?" may reveal they already sense
the issue, in which case you're coaching (helping them act on their own awareness) rather than
correcting (imposing a verdict), which is far more effective and respectful; self-generated insight
sticks better than imposed criticism. (2) <strong>you get their context</strong> — their answer may
reveal information you lacked (a reason for the behavior, a constraint, something going on) that
changes or softens your feedback, so you're not delivering a verdict based on an incomplete picture.
(3) <strong>it makes it a dialogue from the start</strong> — leading with a question frames the
whole conversation as a two-way exploration rather than a one-way judgment, which lowers
defensiveness and increases buy-in (people engage with a conversation they're part of, and defend
against a verdict delivered at them). (4) <strong>it's more respectful</strong> — asking first
treats them as a capable adult with a perspective, rather than a problem to be corrected. So asking
before telling turns feedback from a monologue verdict into a collaborative dialogue, which lands
better and often does half the work for you (they see it themselves). <strong>Giving feedback
soon</strong> (rather than saving it for a review) is more effective because <strong>timely feedback
is fresh, specific, and actionable, while saved-up feedback is stale, vague, and feels like an
ambush</strong>. Several reasons: (1) <strong>it's actionable</strong> — feedback given soon after
the behavior connects to a moment the person clearly remembers, so they can understand exactly what
happened and change it next time; feedback saved for a quarterly review refers to events long past,
which they may barely recall and definitely can't do anything about now (the moment is gone). (2)
<strong>it's specific</strong> — right after the event, you have the concrete details (what exactly
happened, the specific impact); months later, both you and they have lost the specifics, so the
feedback becomes vague ("you've had some communication issues this quarter"), which is unactionable.
(3) <strong>it's fair — no ambush</strong> — timely feedback lets the person address issues as they
arise; saving up months of criticism to dump at a review is an ambush (they had no chance to
course-correct, and now face an accumulated pile), which feels unfair and breeds resentment. A
review should contain <em>no surprises</em> — everything in it should have been raised in real time.
(4) <strong>it lets them improve continuously</strong> — timely feedback means the person can
adjust throughout the year, growing steadily, rather than getting one big batch of stale feedback
they can't act on retroactively. So the principle is that <strong>feedback's value depends on the
person being able to connect it to real behavior and act on it — which requires it to be fresh and
specific, i.e., soon</strong>. Both "ask before telling" and "give it soon" serve the same
underlying goal: making feedback something the person can genuinely receive and act on — asking
makes them a participant (so they engage and buy in), and timeliness keeps it concrete and
actionable (so they can actually change). Together they turn feedback from a stale, one-way verdict
into a timely, two-way, actionable conversation — which is what actually changes behavior.
</details>

## Homework

Think of a piece of critical feedback you need to give someone (or recently gave). Draft it using
SBI: identify the specific Situation, the observable Behavior (as neutral fact, not a character
judgment), and the concrete Impact. Plan a caring opener and an "ask before telling" question. Check
it: is the behavior described as fact (not judgment)? Is the impact concrete? Is it recent enough to
be actionable? Would it be given privately? Then reflect on your feedback habits generally: do you
give feedback soon or save it up? Do you balance critical with genuine recognition? What's one
change that would make your feedback land better?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds the SBI feedback skill and self-awareness about feedback habits. A strong
response drafts real feedback using SBI (specific Situation, observable Behavior stated as fact,
concrete Impact), plans a caring opener and an ask-before-telling question, and checks it against
the criteria (behavioral not judgmental, concrete impact, timely, private). The realizations: (1)
<strong>the hardest part is converting a judgment into an observation</strong> — most people's
instinct is a character verdict ("he's dominating", "she's careless"), and the discipline is
translating that into the specific observed behavior and its impact ("interrupted Sana twice, she
went quiet") — which is what makes the feedback landable; (2) <strong>the impact is what motivates
change</strong> — without the concrete consequence, the behavior seems like just the lead's
preference; the impact ("we lose the juniors' ideas") is what makes the person see it as a real
problem worth changing; (3) <strong>timeliness and privacy matter as much as wording</strong> —
even perfect SBI fails if it's saved for a stale review or delivered publicly. On feedback habits,
people commonly notice: they save up feedback rather than giving it soon (so it's stale and feels
like an ambush), they under-give positive recognition (so their attention only means criticism —
Lesson 20), and they sometimes avoid critical feedback entirely (letting problems fester), or give
it as vague judgments that don't land. The common highest-value change is one of: give feedback
sooner (closer to the behavior, while it's fresh and actionable), convert judgments to behavioral
SBI (so it lands rather than raising shields), or balance critical feedback with genuine specific
recognition (so the relationship and trust are there when criticism is needed). The meta-point:
feedback is one of a lead's highest-leverage tools — it's how behavior changes and people grow —
but most feedback fails by being a vague character judgment given too late; SBI (behavioral,
specific, impact-focused), delivered soon, privately, as a dialogue (ask before telling), balanced
with recognition, is what makes feedback actually work. This is a practiceable skill, and getting
it right is core to leading a team well. It pairs with the English track's Lesson 44 (the language
of feedback); this is the leadership judgment of what makes feedback land. This begins Phase 4
(Feedback & Difficult Conversations). The next lesson covers the other half of feedback —
recognition/praise — which is not an afterthought but half the job, and which most leads under-use.
</details>
