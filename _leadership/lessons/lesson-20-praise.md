# Lesson 20: Praise That Lands

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **recognition** — noticing and naming someone's good work; the positive half of feedback.
> - **calibrated** — adjusted to fit the person and situation (public vs private).
> - **growth mindset** — believing ability grows through effort; praised by recognizing *process*, not just results.
> - **attrition** (ah-TRISH-un) — people gradually leaving an organization.
> - **favoritism** — visibly preferring some people over others.
> - **pass credit up** — telling *your* boss specifically what *your people* did well, by name.
> - **hoarding credit** — letting the team's work look like yours.
> - **unglamorous** — important but unimpressive-looking (data migrations, cleanup).
> - **reflexive pleasantry** — an automatic nice phrase that means nothing ("great job!").
> - **normative** — signalling to everyone what behaviour is valued (public praise is normative).

## Concept

Recognition is half the feedback job — and most leads under-do it or do it badly. "Great job!"
is empty; it recognizes nothing specific and motivates little. Done well, recognition is one of
the cheapest, most powerful tools a lead has: it reinforces the behaviors you want, makes people
feel seen, and is a genuine driver of retention and engagement. The skill is making praise
**specific, deserved, and calibrated** — so it actually lands and means something.

```
   EMPTY PRAISE                     PRAISE THAT LANDS
   ────────────                     ─────────────────
   "Great job!"                     "The way you caught that race condition
   "Nice work team!"                 before it hit prod — digging into the logs
   "You're awesome"                  when the test was flaky instead of retrying —
        ↑ generic, forgettable,       saved us a nasty outage. That kind of
          could be said to anyone      instinct is exactly what we need."
                                          ↑ specific behavior, real impact, deserved
```

The reframe: **specific praise recognizes a person; generic praise recognizes no one.** "Great
job" could be said to anyone about anything — so it lands as a pleasant noise, not real
recognition. Naming the *specific* thing they did and its *impact* ("the way you caught that race
condition... saved us an outage") shows you actually saw and valued what they did — which is what
makes recognition motivating and real. It's SBI (Lesson 19) applied to the positive.

---

## How It Works

### Specific beats generic

The single biggest lever: name the *specific* behavior and its impact, not a generic
compliment. "Great job on the launch" is empty; "the way you kept the migration rollback plan
ready the whole time — that's why we could move fast without fear" recognizes something real.
Specific praise (a) shows you actually noticed (which is what makes it feel genuine), (b)
reinforces the *particular* behavior you want more of, and (c) can't be dismissed as a reflexive
pleasantry. It's SBI for the positive: Situation, Behavior, Impact.

### Praise the process and growth, not just the win

Recognizing *outcomes* only ("nice, the feature shipped") misses the more valuable target:
recognizing the *behaviors and growth* you want to reinforce — the good judgment, the
perseverance, the improved skill ("the way you handled that ambiguity", "you've gotten much
sharper at breaking down big problems"). Praising process and growth (a) reinforces the
controllable behaviors that lead to wins, (b) encourages a growth mindset (effort and learning
are valued, not just innate results), and (c) recognizes people whose great work didn't happen to
produce a visible win.

### Public vs private calibration

Calibrate where you praise. **Public** recognition (in a channel, a meeting) amplifies it and
signals to everyone what's valued — great for reinforcing behaviors team-wide and for people who
appreciate visibility. But calibrate: some people are embarrassed by public praise (praise them
privately or quietly), and public praise must be *fair* (repeatedly praising one person publicly
can breed resentment or look like favoritism). Private recognition is more personal and safe for
the shy; public recognition is amplified and normative. Read the person and the situation.

### Recognition as retention

Feeling genuinely valued is a major driver of engagement and retention — people leave managers
who don't recognize their work far more than they leave for money. Regular, genuine recognition
is thus one of the cheapest, highest-return things a lead does: it costs a sentence and makes
people feel seen, which keeps them engaged and reduces the far more expensive cost of attrition.
Under-recognizing good work is a quiet, common, and costly leadership failure.

### Passing credit upward — accurately

When your team does well, pass the credit up and out — accurately and specifically. Tell your
boss and the wider org *what your people did* ("Priya's rollback design is why the migration was
zero-downtime"), naming names and specifics. This (a) gets your people the visibility and
recognition they deserve (which helps their careers — sponsorship, Lesson 29), (b) builds their
loyalty (a lead who champions them), and (c) reflects well on you (a lead whose team does great
work). Hoarding credit, or vague "the team did great", fails your people; specific, name-named
credit-passing is a core lead behavior.

{: .note }
> **Recognition is half the feedback job — make it specific, and pass credit up accurately</br>**
> Most leads under-do recognition or do it as empty "great job", missing one of the cheapest,

> want), targets <em>process and growth</em> not just wins (reinforcing controllable behaviors and

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** Your team just shipped a significant launch. Three people contributed very
differently: **Priya** did the hard architectural design (the caching layer that made it perform);
**Marcus** did the unglamorous grind (migrating a mountain of legacy data cleanly, working
carefully through a tedious, error-prone task); and **Sana** (a junior) repeatedly unblocked
others — noticing when people were stuck, pairing to help, and keeping the team's momentum. You
want to recognize each of them meaningfully.

**Write the recognition you'd give each of the three** — no copy-paste, each specific to what they
did. Then note what makes recognition land and the mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Strong recognition names the specific behavior and its impact, is tailored to each person (not
copy-pasted), and often recognizes the process/behavior, not just the outcome. Example:
<br><br>
<strong>To Priya (the architecture):</strong> "The caching layer you designed is the reason this
launch actually performs under load — that was genuinely hard design work, and you got the
architecture right. The bit I most appreciated was how you thought through the invalidation
edge cases up front instead of discovering them in prod. That's exactly the kind of judgment that
makes hard systems work." <em>(Specific behavior — the caching design, the up-front edge-case
thinking — and its impact — performs under load; recognizes the judgment/process, not just "it
shipped".)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>To Marcus (the grind):</strong> "I want to specifically call out the data migration —
that was a huge, tedious, error-prone job, and you worked through it so carefully that we had
zero data issues, which almost never happens with a migration that size. That kind of careful,
unglamorous work is exactly what makes a launch actually safe, and I don't want it to go unseen.
Thank you." <em>(Recognizes the unglamorous grind that's easy to overlook — the careful process
and its impact (zero data issues) — and explicitly names that this kind of work is valued and
seen, which matters because grind work often goes unrecognized.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>To Sana (unblocking, a junior):</strong> "Something I noticed and really valued: you kept
the whole team moving. When Alex was stuck on the deploy config, when Marcus hit that schema
question — you jumped in, paired, and unblocked them, again and again. That's not on any ticket,
but it's a big part of why we shipped on time, and it shows real team instincts. For where you are
in your career, that awareness of the whole team's momentum is a genuinely valuable strength —
keep leaning into it." <em>(Recognizes the invisible unblocking work (not on any ticket, easy to
miss), its real impact (shipped on time), and — tailored to a junior — frames it as a career
strength and growth, which is developmental and motivating.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>What makes recognition land:</strong> (1) <strong>Specific to each person's actual
contribution</strong> — each names the particular thing <em>that person</em> did (architecture /
grind / unblocking), so it's real recognition of them, not a copy-pasted "great job team" that
recognizes no one. (2) <strong>Names the behavior AND impact</strong> — SBI for the positive: the
specific behavior (edge-case thinking, careful migration, repeated unblocking) and its impact
(performs under load, zero data issues, shipped on time) — which shows you actually saw it and
makes it feel genuine. (3) <strong>Recognizes process/behavior, not just the win</strong> — the
judgment, the care, the team instinct — the controllable behaviors you want more of, not just
"it shipped". (4) <strong>Tailored to the person</strong> — Marcus's recognition names that grind
work is often unseen (which is exactly what someone doing tedious work needs to hear); Sana's
frames her contribution as a career strength (developmental, right for a junior). (5) <strong>Makes
the invisible visible</strong> — Marcus's grind and Sana's unblocking are the easy-to-overlook
contributions; naming them specifically is high-value because that work usually goes unrecognized.
<strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) copy-paste / generic ("great job everyone!") — recognizes
no one, lands as noise; (2) only recognizing the visible win (the feature) or the obvious
contributor (Priya's architecture) while missing the grind (Marcus) and the invisible glue (Sana)
— which teaches people that only flashy work is seen; (3) recognizing only outcomes, not the
process/behavior/growth (misses the chance to reinforce the controllable behaviors); (4)
one-size-fits-all delivery (not calibrating — e.g., Sana as a junior benefits from the
career-framing that Priya might not need); (5) forgetting to pass this credit upward — you should
also tell your boss/org specifically what each did (Lesson's credit-passing), so they get
visibility beyond the team.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why does specific praise land where generic praise ("great job") doesn't, and why should
you often praise process and growth rather than just the win?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Specific praise lands where generic praise doesn't because <strong>naming the specific behavior and
its impact shows you actually saw and valued what the person did, which is what makes recognition
feel genuine and real — while a generic compliment could be said to anyone and so recognizes no
one</strong>. "Great job!" fails because: (1) it's <strong>generic</strong> — it could be said to
any person about any work, so it carries no information that <em>you noticed this person's specific
contribution</em>; it lands as a pleasant but empty noise, not real recognition. (2) it <strong>shows
no real attention</strong> — precisely because it's generic, it signals you didn't (or don't need
to) engage with what they actually did, so it doesn't make them feel seen. (3) it <strong>reinforces
nothing specific</strong> — it doesn't tell them <em>what</em> to do more of, so it has little
behavioral effect. Specific praise — "the way you caught that race condition by digging into the
logs instead of retrying the flaky test saved us an outage" — fixes all of this: (a) it <strong>shows
you actually saw it</strong> (you can only name the specific behavior and impact if you noticed and
understood it), which is what makes it feel genuine and makes the person feel truly seen; (b) it
<strong>reinforces the exact behavior you want more of</strong> (the person knows precisely what was
valuable — the instinct to investigate rather than paper over — so they'll do more of it); and (c)
it <strong>can't be dismissed as a reflexive pleasantry</strong> (it's clearly deserved and
considered, not automatic). So specific praise recognizes a <em>person</em> and a <em>behavior</em>,
where generic praise recognizes no one and nothing — which is why the specific version motivates and
lands while "great job" evaporates. (It's SBI applied to the positive: Situation, Behavior, Impact.)
Why praise process and growth rather than just the win: <strong>because the process, behaviors, and
growth are what you actually want to reinforce and are within the person's control, whereas outcomes
are partly luck and recognizing only them misses the more valuable and more motivating target</strong>.
Several reasons: (1) <strong>it reinforces controllable behaviors</strong> — the good judgment,
perseverance, and skill that <em>lead to</em> wins are what you want more of, and they're what the
person controls; praising the process ("the way you thought through the edge cases up front")
reinforces the repeatable behavior, where praising only the outcome ("nice, it shipped") reinforces
nothing actionable (and outcomes are partly luck). (2) <strong>it encourages a growth mindset</strong>
— recognizing effort, learning, and improvement ("you've gotten much sharper at breaking down big
problems") signals that growth and effort are valued, which encourages people to stretch and take on
challenges; praising only innate results ("you're so smart") can make people risk-averse (protecting
the image of being talented). (3) <strong>it recognizes valuable work that didn't produce a visible
win</strong> — great judgment, careful work, and real growth sometimes don't happen to produce a
flashy outcome (a project got cancelled for external reasons, the careful work prevented a problem
that's therefore invisible); recognizing only wins misses these, teaching people that only visible
results are seen, whereas recognizing process/behavior captures the valuable work regardless of
outcome. (4) <strong>it's often more meaningful</strong> — being recognized for <em>how</em> you
work (your judgment, your growth) is often more personal and motivating than being recognized for a
result. So praising process and growth reinforces the controllable behaviors that actually drive
success, encourages the growth mindset that builds capability, and recognizes valuable work that
outcome-only praise would miss — which is why it's the more valuable target than just the win.
Together, specific + process-focused recognition is what makes praise a powerful tool: it shows
people they're seen, reinforces exactly the behaviors and growth you want, and does so genuinely —
which motivates, develops, and retains people.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is recognition a driver of retention, and why should a lead pass credit upward
specifically and by name?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Recognition is a driver of retention because <strong>feeling genuinely valued is a fundamental human
need at work, and its absence is a major reason people disengage and leave — often more than
money</strong>. People want their work to be seen and appreciated; when it consistently isn't — when
they work hard and it goes unrecognized, when the lead only speaks up to criticize — they feel
invisible and unvalued, which erodes engagement and motivation over time. This is one of the most
common reasons people leave: not (usually) money, but feeling that their contributions don't matter
to their manager or organization ("people leave managers, not companies"). Conversely, regular
genuine recognition makes people feel seen and valued, which sustains engagement, motivation, and
loyalty — they're far more likely to stay somewhere they feel appreciated. This makes recognition
<strong>one of the cheapest, highest-return tools a lead has</strong>: it costs a sentence (a
specific, genuine acknowledgment) and yields feeling-valued, engagement, and retention — against the
enormous cost of attrition (losing a good person, hiring and ramping a replacement). So
under-recognizing good work isn't a harmless oversight; it's a quiet, common, and costly leadership
failure — the lead is neglecting a nearly-free lever that drives the expensive outcome of retention.
Regular, genuine, specific recognition is thus a high-leverage habit precisely because it's so cheap
relative to its return. Why a lead should pass credit upward specifically and by name: <strong>because
it gets the people who did the work the visibility and recognition they deserve from beyond the team,
which helps their careers, builds their loyalty, and (accurately) reflects well on the lead</strong>.
When your team does well, the wider org and your boss often don't see <em>who</em> did what — they
see the team's output. If you pass credit up vaguely ("the team did great") or hoard it (letting it
reflect on you), the individuals who did the work get no visibility beyond the team. Passing credit
up <em>specifically and by name</em> — "Priya's rollback design is why the migration was
zero-downtime", "Marcus's careful data migration is why we had zero data issues" — changes this: (1)
<strong>it gets your people deserved visibility</strong> — senior people and the org learn what
specific individuals accomplished, which matters for those individuals' reputations, and helps their
careers (recognition, opportunities, promotion cases — this is sponsorship, Lesson 29: using your
platform to get your people seen); (2) <strong>it builds their loyalty and trust</strong> — a lead
who visibly champions their people's work (rather than absorbing the credit) earns deep loyalty;
people work hard for a lead who makes sure they get recognized, and resent one who hoards credit; (3)
<strong>it reflects well on you, accurately</strong> — a lead whose team does great work and who
generously credits them looks like a strong leader (good teams and generous leadership both reflect
well), and it's honest (the credit goes where it's due); hoarding credit, by contrast, may grab
short-term shine but breeds resentment and marks you as someone who takes credit — which erodes trust
and ultimately reflects <em>badly</em>. The "specifically and by name" part is essential: vague
credit ("great team effort") doesn't get any individual seen, so it fails the core purpose (getting
your specific people visibility); naming names and specific contributions is what actually gives each
person recognition beyond the team. So passing credit up by name is a core lead behavior because it
serves your people (visibility, careers, feeling valued → loyalty and retention) while reflecting
honestly and well on you — the opposite of the credit-hoarding that damages both your people and your
reputation. Both recognition-for-retention and credit-passing reflect the same principle: <strong>a
lead's job includes making their people feel valued and get seen, which is both the right thing and
a powerful driver of engagement, loyalty, and retention</strong> — and it's remarkably cheap
(specific recognition and generous credit) relative to its large return.
</details>

## Homework

Practice specific recognition. First, think of three people you work with and, for each, identify a
*specific* recent thing they did well — the behavior and its impact (not "they're great" but "the way
they X, which caused Y"). Draft genuine, specific recognition for each, tailored to them (and
consider process/growth, not just wins). Then reflect on your recognition habits: do you recognize
good work regularly, or mostly stay quiet unless something's wrong? Do you make the invisible work
(grind, unblocking) visible? Do you pass credit upward by name? Identify one recognition habit to
build. Notice, over the coming weeks, the effect of genuine specific recognition on the people around
you.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds the specific-recognition skill and self-awareness about recognition habits — one
of the cheapest, highest-return and most underused leadership tools. A strong response drafts
genuine, specific, tailored recognition for three real people (naming the behavior and impact, often
recognizing process/growth, tailored to each), and honestly audits recognition habits. The
realizations: (1) <strong>specific beats generic</strong> — naming the particular behavior and its
impact ("the way you X, which caused Y") shows you actually saw it and reinforces the exact behavior,
where "great job" recognizes no one; it's SBI for the positive; (2) <strong>make the invisible work
visible</strong> — the grind and the unblocking/glue work are easy to overlook and often go
unrecognized, so specifically recognizing them is high-value (and teaches people that this work is
seen, not just the flashy wins); (3) <strong>recognition drives retention</strong> — feeling valued
keeps people, and its absence is a major reason they leave, making regular genuine recognition a
nearly-free lever on the expensive outcome of attrition; (4) <strong>pass credit up by name</strong>
— getting your people visibility beyond the team helps their careers, builds loyalty, and reflects
well on you. On habits, people commonly find they under-recognize (staying quiet unless something's
wrong — so their attention only means criticism, which is demoralizing and makes critical feedback
land as attack), overlook the invisible work, and forget to pass credit up specifically. The
highest-value habit to build is usually: recognize good work regularly and specifically (not just
when things go wrong), making sure to catch the invisible contributions and to pass credit upward by
name. The meta-point: recognition is <em>half the feedback job</em>, not an afterthought — it
reinforces the behaviors you want, makes people feel seen (a major retention driver), and costs
almost nothing, yet most leads badly under-use it or do it as empty "great job". Making recognition
specific, deserved, process-focused, calibrated, and generously passed upward is a powerful,
high-leverage leadership practice. It pairs with Lesson 19 (critical feedback) — together they're the
two halves of feedback, and the recognition half builds the relationship and trust that make the
critical half land. The next lesson turns to the other direction — <em>receiving</em> feedback as a
lead — where your response determines whether people keep telling you the truth.
</details>
