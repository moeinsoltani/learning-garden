# Lesson 33: Growing Seniors into Leads

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **successor** — the person who could take over your role; **succession** — deliberately preparing them.
> - **multiply yourself** — to create more leadership capacity than one person by growing others.
> - **leadership signals** — the real predictors of leading well: making others better, caring about the team, judgment, communication — *not* coding skill.
> - **aptitude** (AP-tih-tood) — natural ability for something.
> - **acting lead** — someone temporarily doing a lead's job as a bounded trial ("acting" = temporary, as in an acting manager).
> - **bounded** — with clear limits in time and scope.
> - **fail safely** — to make mistakes where the damage is recoverable (Lesson 31's blast radius).
> - **over-protect** — to prevent all mistakes, and with them all learning.
> - **hoarding your role** — clinging to responsibilities out of insecurity.
> - **threatened** — feeling your position is endangered by someone else's ambition (the small, self-defeating reaction).

## Concept

The highest form of developing people is **growing the next generation of leaders** — turning your
strong seniors into leads. It's also how you *multiply yourself*: giving away pieces of your own job
develops others and frees you for higher leverage, and building successors is what lets you (and the
org) grow. But it requires overcoming a specific fear (giving away your own responsibilities) and a
common mistake (thinking the best coder is the natural lead — leadership potential is different).

```
   GROWING LEADS = MULTIPLYING YOURSELF
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ • spot LEAD potential (NOT just the best coder)        │
   │ • give away pieces of YOUR job deliberately            │
   │ • let them lead — and fail safely — with support       │
   │ • "acting lead" trials (lead something real, bounded)  │
   │ • build successors → you can grow, team is resilient   │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
   Leadership potential ≠ coding skill. Give away your job to
   grow people — and to free yourself for higher leverage.
```

The reframe: **grow leaders by deliberately giving away pieces of your own job — and spot potential by
leadership signals, not coding skill.** The instinct is to hold onto your responsibilities (they're
yours, you do them well) and to assume the best engineer is the natural next lead. Both are mistakes:
giving away your job is how you develop successors and free yourself, and leadership potential (drawing
people out, caring about the team, judgment, communication) is different from — and more important than
— being the best coder.

## Going Deeper

### Spot lead potential — it's not the best coder

The most common mistake: assuming the **best engineer** is the natural next lead. Coding skill and
leadership potential are *different things* — the best coder may have no interest in or aptitude for
leading (and promoting them can lose a great engineer and gain a poor lead — the classic error). Look
instead for **leadership signals**: do they make others better (mentoring, drawing people out)? Do they
care about the team, not just their own work? Do they show good judgment on ambiguous, non-technical
problems? Do they communicate well and build trust? Do others already look to them? These — not raw
coding ability — predict leadership. (Some strong coders have them too; the point is that coding skill
alone doesn't.)

### Give away pieces of your own job — deliberately

The core mechanism for growing leads (and multiplying yourself): **deliberately hand over pieces of
your own job** — running a meeting, owning a project's coordination, leading an initiative, mentoring
a junior, representing the team somewhere. This (a) develops the person (they learn leadership by doing
real leadership work), and (b) frees you for higher-leverage work (or your next role). The fear is real
— these are your responsibilities, you do them well, giving them away feels like losing control or
value — but holding onto everything keeps you the bottleneck and develops no successors. Giving away
your job is how you grow people into leaders and how you (and the org) grow.

### Let them lead — and fail safely

You can't grow a leader by only advising — they have to **actually lead**, make decisions, and
sometimes fail. Give them real ownership (a project, an initiative) and let them make the calls — even
when you'd do it differently, and even when it means they'll make mistakes. The key is **failing
safely**: choose leadership stretches where mistakes are recoverable (contained blast radius, Lesson 31)
and support them (available, coaching), so they learn from real leadership experience without a
catastrophic failure. Over-protecting them (stepping in, making the calls for them) prevents the
learning; letting them lead for real (with a safety net) is how leadership develops.

### The "acting lead" trial

A powerful, concrete tool: an **"acting lead" trial** — have the person lead something real and bounded
(cover for you while you're out, lead a sub-project, run the team for a sprint). This gives them (and
you) real information about their leadership — a genuine, bounded trial where they experience leading,
you see how they do, and they learn whether they even want it (many discover leadership isn't what they
imagined). It's low-risk (bounded and temporary) but real (actual leadership, not a simulation), making
it an excellent way to develop and evaluate lead potential.

### When someone wants your actual job

Sometimes a strong person wants to become a lead — possibly *your* role or a lead role. Handle this
generously, not defensively: (1) **don't feel threatened** — someone wanting to grow into leadership is
a good thing (it's your job to develop them, and their growth reflects well on you), not a threat to
your position; (2) **actively develop them** — give them leadership opportunities, sponsor them, help
them get there; (3) **be honest about the path** — what they need to develop, what opportunities exist.
A lead who develops successors (even to their own role) is doing their job well and is more promotable
themselves (you can't move up if no one can replace you). Hoarding your role or feeling threatened by
ambition is small and self-defeating; developing successors is how everyone grows.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** You're going on four weeks of leave (parental leave / extended vacation). You need to
arrange coverage for your team of six. The easy approach is a "coverage rota" — parcel out your tasks so
things don't break while you're gone. But you realize this is also a golden opportunity: instead of just
covering, you could use it as a **growth plan** — deliberately giving people (especially those with lead
potential) real leadership experience while you're out. You have a couple of strong seniors, one of whom
(Priya) has shown real leadership signals (she mentors others, has good judgment, the team already looks
to her).

**Design who covers what — as a growth plan, not just a coverage rota.** Then note the principles and
mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong plan turns coverage into deliberate leadership development — especially an "acting lead" trial
for someone with lead potential. Example:
<br><br>
<strong>Priya → acting lead (the big growth opportunity).</strong> "Priya has real lead potential —
she mentors people, has good judgment, and the team already looks to her. Four weeks of me being out is a
perfect bounded 'acting lead' trial: I'll ask her to be acting lead for the team — running standups,
being the go-to for decisions and unblocking, representing the team. This gives her genuine leadership
experience (not a simulation), lets me and her see how she does, and helps her learn whether she wants
it. I'll set her up: brief her thoroughly, give her the context and authority, and arrange support
(a peer lead or my manager she can escalate to) so she can 'fail safely' if something's hard." <em>(The
acting-lead trial — real, bounded leadership for someone with potential; the core growth move, not just
coverage.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Distribute other pieces of my job as growth, not just tasks.</strong> "Rather than dumping all my
tasks on the seniors, distribute pieces of my role as development: give someone the stakeholder
communication I usually do, someone else the cross-team coordination, someone the release ownership —
each a chance to grow a leadership-adjacent skill. Match to who'd grow from what (and their interest)."
<em>(Giving away pieces of the job as deliberate development, matched to growth.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Set everyone up to succeed (support + safety net).</strong> "Before I leave: brief everyone
thoroughly (context, decisions likely to come up, who owns what), establish clear escalation paths (my
manager, a peer lead) so no one's stranded, document the important things, and make sure Priya
especially has what she needs. The point is they succeed and grow, not that they flounder — 'fail safely'
means a real safety net." <em>(Scaffolding — real ownership WITH support, so it's growth not abandonment.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Plan the debrief.</strong> "When I'm back, debrief with Priya and others: what did they learn,
what was hard, how did they grow, do they want more leadership? Use it to inform their development
(and Priya's path toward a lead role if she wants it)." <em>(Closing the loop — extracting the learning
and informing their growth.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Turn coverage into growth</strong> — the leave is a golden,
bounded opportunity for real leadership experience, not just a rota to prevent breakage. (2) <strong>Acting-
lead trial for lead potential</strong> — Priya gets genuine, bounded leadership (real info for both of
you, and development). (3) <strong>Give away pieces of your job as development</strong> — distribute your
role as growth opportunities, matched to who'd grow. (4) <strong>Fail safely — support and safety
net</strong> — thorough briefing, escalation paths, documentation, so they succeed and grow (not
flounder). (5) <strong>Spot potential by leadership signals</strong> — Priya's mentoring, judgment, and
the team looking to her (not just coding skill) mark her for the acting-lead role. (6) <strong>Debrief to
extract learning</strong>. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>just a coverage rota</strong>
— parceling tasks to prevent breakage, missing the growth opportunity entirely; (2) <strong>no
support/safety net</strong> — throwing Priya into acting-lead with no briefing or escalation path (sets
her up to fail, not "fail safely"); (3) <strong>picking the best coder for acting lead</strong> rather
than the person with leadership signals; (4) <strong>over-controlling from afar</strong> — trying to still
make all the decisions remotely (defeats the growth — let them actually lead); (5) <strong>dumping tasks
without development framing</strong> — giving people your work as burden, not growth; (6) <strong>no
debrief</strong> — missing the chance to extract the learning and inform their development; (7)
<strong>not really letting go</strong> — if you can't step back and let Priya lead (even imperfectly),
neither she nor you grows.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is "the best coder" often not the natural next lead, and what leadership signals should you
look for instead?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"The best coder" is often not the natural next lead because <strong>coding skill and leadership
potential are different capabilities — being excellent at engineering doesn't mean having the interest
or aptitude for leading, which involves a largely different skill set</strong>. The instinct to promote
the best engineer into leadership comes from conflating "most skilled at the current work" with "best
suited for the next role," but they're not the same: leadership is about developing people, building
trust, communicating, exercising judgment on ambiguous non-technical problems, drawing out and enabling
others — skills that are distinct from (though not opposed to) technical excellence. The best coder may:
(1) <strong>have no interest in leading</strong> — they may love the technical work and have no desire to
do the people-and-coordination work of leadership (which is largely <em>not</em> coding); (2) <strong>lack
leadership aptitude</strong> — being brilliant technically doesn't confer the ability to develop people,
communicate, build trust, or navigate the interpersonal and organizational judgment leadership requires;
some excellent engineers are poor at exactly these. Promoting the best coder into leadership on the
assumption they're the natural lead risks the classic double failure: you <strong>lose a great engineer</strong>
(they leave the technical work they excelled at and loved) and <strong>gain a poor lead</strong> (they're
in a role they're not suited for or don't want), which is worse for everyone (and them). This is a
well-known trap. So the reframe is that leadership potential must be assessed on leadership signals, not
extrapolated from coding skill. The leadership signals to look for instead: (1) <strong>do they make
others better?</strong> — mentoring, helping teammates, drawing people out, elevating those around them
(a core leadership trait — leadership is about enabling others); (2) <strong>do they care about the team,
not just their own work?</strong> — investment in the team's success, health, and dynamics, not just
their own tasks and glory; (3) <strong>do they show good judgment on ambiguous, non-technical
problems?</strong> — the ability to navigate people, priorities, and organizational ambiguity, not just
technical problems; (4) <strong>do they communicate well and build trust?</strong> — clear communication
and the ability to earn trust (foundational to leading); (5) <strong>do others already look to them?</strong>
— informal leadership (people naturally turning to them for guidance, respecting their input) is a strong
signal they have leadership presence. These signals — making others better, caring about the team,
judgment, communication, trust, and others looking to them — predict leadership potential, whereas raw
coding ability alone does not. (Some strong coders have these signals too — the point isn't that good
coders can't be leads, but that coding skill alone doesn't indicate it; you look at the leadership
signals specifically.) So a lead spotting future leads should look past "who's the best engineer" to "who
shows these leadership signals" — which is what actually predicts who'll be a good lead, and avoids the
trap of losing a great engineer and gaining a poor lead by promoting on the wrong criterion.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is "giving away pieces of your own job" and "letting people lead and fail safely" how you
grow leaders — and why is handling someone's ambition (even for your role) generously important?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Giving away pieces of your own job</strong> grows leaders because <strong>leadership is learned
by doing real leadership work, and your responsibilities are exactly that work — so handing them over
(with support) is how people develop leadership capability, while it also frees you for higher leverage</strong>.
You can't grow a leader through advice alone; they have to actually do leadership things — run meetings,
own coordination, lead initiatives, represent the team, mentor others. Those things <em>are</em> pieces
of your job, so deliberately handing them over gives people genuine leadership experience (the only way
leadership actually develops), while simultaneously freeing you from being the bottleneck and creating
room for you to do higher-leverage work or grow into your next role. The fear (these are your
responsibilities, you do them well, giving them away feels like losing control or value) is real but
must be overcome: holding onto everything keeps you the bottleneck, develops no successors, and caps both
your growth and the team's. Giving away your job is the core mechanism of both developing leaders and
multiplying yourself. <strong>Letting people lead and fail safely</strong> grows leaders because <strong>real
leadership development requires actually making decisions and sometimes failing — you can't learn
leadership while someone else makes all the calls, so people need genuine ownership (with a safety net so
failures are recoverable), not over-protection</strong>. If you give someone a "leadership" role but keep
making the decisions, stepping in, and protecting them from any mistakes, they don't actually lead (so
they don't learn) — they're just executing your leadership. Growing a leader means letting them make the
calls themselves (even when you'd do it differently), which inevitably means they'll sometimes make
mistakes — and that's essential, because making decisions and learning from the results (including
failures) is how judgment develops. The key qualifier is <em>safely</em>: choose leadership stretches
where failures are recoverable (contained blast radius) and provide support (available, coaching), so
they get real leadership experience and learn from real mistakes <em>without</em> a catastrophic failure.
Over-protecting (stepping in, making the calls for them) prevents the learning; abandoning (real
ownership with no safety net) risks damaging failure — the growth comes from real ownership <em>with</em>
a safety net (the scaffolding idea applied to leadership). Why <strong>handling ambition generously</strong>
(even for your role) is important: <strong>because developing successors is your job and benefits
everyone, so feeling threatened by ambition is self-defeating — a lead who develops people to their own
level is doing well and is more promotable, while one who hoards their role stunts everyone including
themselves</strong>. When someone wants to grow into leadership (even your role), the defensive reaction
(feeling threatened, withholding opportunities to protect your position) is a mistake for several reasons:
(1) <strong>developing them is literally your job</strong> — growing people, including into leadership,
is a core part of leading, so someone wanting that is you succeeding, not being threatened; (2) <strong>it
makes you more promotable, not less</strong> — you generally can't move up if no one can replace you, so
developing a successor is what <em>enables</em> your own advancement (a lead with no successor is stuck);
(3) <strong>hoarding your role is small and self-defeating</strong> — refusing to develop people out of
insecurity stunts their growth, the team's resilience, and your own progression, and it's transparently
self-serving (which damages trust and your reputation); (4) <strong>their growth reflects well on you</strong>
— developing strong leaders is a mark of a great leader. So the generous response — not feeling
threatened, actively developing them (opportunities, sponsorship, honesty about the path) — is both the
right thing (it's your job, and it serves them and the org) and the smart thing (it makes you more
promotable and reflects well on you). All three points reflect the core of growing leaders: it requires
giving away your own responsibilities (overcoming the fear), letting people genuinely lead and learn from
real experience including failure (with a safety net, not over-protection), and embracing rather than
fearing people's ambition (developing successors, even to your own role) — which multiplies you, grows
the team's leadership capacity, and enables everyone (including you) to advance. The through-line is that
developing leaders means genuinely letting go and generously investing in others' growth, which is the
highest form of a lead's development work and how the whole system grows.
</details>

## Homework

Invest in growing future leaders. (1) Look at your team for <em>leadership</em> signals (making others
better, caring about the team, judgment, communication, others looking to them) — not just coding skill.
Who has lead potential you might have overlooked (or wrongly assumed based on coding skill)? (2) Identify
a piece of your own job you could deliberately give away as a growth opportunity for someone with
potential (with support). (3) Look for a chance to give someone real leadership with a safety net (own an
initiative, an "acting lead" trial). (4) If someone has shown leadership ambition, are you developing them
generously or holding back? Reflect: are you actively building successors and giving away your job, or
holding onto everything — and who's one person you could deliberately grow toward leadership?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds growing-leaders — the highest form of developing people and how a lead multiplies
themselves. A strong response looks for leadership signals (not just coding skill) to spot potential,
identifies a piece of their own job to give away as growth, looks for a real leadership opportunity with a
safety net (or an acting-lead trial), and checks whether they're handling ambition generously. The
realizations: (1) <strong>leadership potential ≠ coding skill</strong> — the best coder isn't necessarily
the natural lead (promoting them can lose a great engineer and gain a poor lead); look for leadership
signals (making others better, caring about the team, judgment, communication, others looking to them);
(2) <strong>give away pieces of your own job</strong> — leadership is learned by doing real leadership
work (which is your job), so handing it over (overcoming the fear) develops successors and frees you for
higher leverage; (3) <strong>let people lead and fail safely</strong> — real ownership with a safety net
(not over-protection or abandonment) is how leadership develops, through making real decisions and
learning from real results including failures; (4) <strong>handle ambition generously</strong> —
developing successors (even to your own role) is your job, makes you more promotable, and reflects well
on you; feeling threatened is self-defeating. On reflection, people commonly find they've assessed lead
potential by coding skill (overlooking people with real leadership signals, or wrongly assuming the best
coder is the natural lead), that they hold onto their own responsibilities (not giving away their job out
of the fear), and that they over-protect (not letting people truly lead and fail). The highest-value
change is usually: spot potential by leadership signals, deliberately give away a piece of your job to
someone with potential (with support), and let them genuinely lead. The meta-point: growing the next
generation of leaders is the highest form of developing people and how you multiply yourself — giving away
your job develops others and frees you, building successors lets you and the org grow, and it requires
overcoming the fear of letting go and the mistake of equating coding skill with leadership. A lead who
builds successors (spotting real potential, giving away their job, letting people lead and fail safely,
embracing ambition) is both developing people at the highest level and making themselves more promotable.
It's the culmination of the development work in this phase. The last Phase 6 lesson addresses the flip
side — making sure no one (including you) is irreplaceable — succession and the bus factor, de-risking the
team's dependence on any single person.
</details>
