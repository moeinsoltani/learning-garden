# Lesson 30: The Delegation Ladder

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **delegation** — handing over work *and* the responsibility for it.
> - **micromanage** — to delegate but then control every step; **abdicate** (AB-dih-kate) — the opposite: dump the work with no support ("throw it over the wall").
> - **outcome vs task** — the goal to achieve ("make CI reliable") vs prescribed steps to execute; delegate the former.
> - **rung** — one step of a ladder; here, a level of autonomy.
> - **autonomy** (aw-TON-oh-mee) — freedom to decide how to do the work.
> - **monitoring contract** — the agreed check-in rhythm and escalation rules that replace hovering.
> - **escalate** — to bring a problem up to you when agreed conditions are met.
> - **hover** — to watch over someone's shoulder constantly.
> - **the boomerang** — delegated work that comes flying back to you when the person struggles; don't catch it.
> - **flounder** — to struggle helplessly without support.

## Concept

Delegation is how a lead scales — but most people do it badly: they either **micromanage** (delegate
a task, then hover and control every step) or **abdicate** (throw something over the wall with no
support and hope). The skill is delegating **outcomes, not tasks**, at the **right level** for each
person and situation — and setting up a monitoring contract that catches problems without hovering.
And a key insight: you should delegate the things you're *good at* — that's exactly the point.

```
   THE DELEGATION LADDER (rungs of autonomy):
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ "do exactly this"          (lowest — task, no judgment)│
   │ "do this, check with me"                               │
   │ "propose an approach, then do it"                      │
   │ "handle it, tell me what you did"                      │
   │ "own this area"            (highest — full ownership)  │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
   Match the RUNG to the person's competence + confidence
   for THIS work — and delegate OUTCOMES, not step-by-step tasks.
```

The reframe: **delegate outcomes at the rung that matches the person — not tasks with you still
driving.** Delegating a *task* ("do exactly these steps") keeps you the brain and them the hands (no
growth, and you're still the bottleneck); delegating an *outcome* ("own getting our CI reliable")
gives them the real thing to own. And the *rung* — how much autonomy — should match their competence
and confidence for that specific work, with a monitoring contract that fits.

## Going Deeper

### Delegate outcomes, not tasks

The key shift: hand over the **outcome** (the goal, the thing to achieve — "make our CI reliable")
rather than a **task** (a prescribed set of steps — "run these specific commands"). Delegating tasks
keeps you the decision-maker (they just execute your plan), so they don't grow and you're still doing
the thinking; delegating outcomes gives them the real problem to own — the judgment, the approach,
the responsibility — which is what develops them and actually offloads the work. Say what success
looks like and the constraints; let them own the how.

### The delegation ladder — match the rung to the person

Autonomy comes in levels (rungs), from "do exactly this" up to "own this area entirely." The skill is
matching the rung to the person's **competence and confidence for this specific work**: a highly
capable, confident person on familiar work gets a high rung ("own it, keep me posted"); someone newer
or on unfamiliar territory gets a lower rung ("propose an approach and let's discuss, then do it"). The
same person is at different rungs for different work (senior on backend, novice on frontend). Pitching
the rung right — enough autonomy to own and grow, enough support to not fail — is the core judgment.
Too high a rung for the person = abdication (they flounder); too low = micromanagement (they don't grow,
you're still driving).

### The monitoring contract — check in without hovering

Delegation isn't "assign and disappear" — it's delegation *with* an agreed **monitoring contract**:
how you'll stay informed and catch problems without hovering. Agree up front on check-in cadence
(weekly sync? milestone reviews?), what they'll escalate (when to bring you in), and how you'll know
it's on track — so you have appropriate visibility (a safety net) without controlling every step. This
gives them autonomy *and* catches problems early. The contract should match the rung: higher autonomy =
lighter monitoring; lower rung = more frequent check-ins.

### Delegate what you're good at — that's the point

A counterintuitive but crucial principle: **you should delegate the things you're good at**, not just
the scraps you don't want. The instinct is to keep the work you're best at (you do it faster/better)
and delegate the rest — but that keeps you the bottleneck on exactly the high-value work, and starves
your people of the chance to grow into it. Delegating what you're good at (with support) is how people
develop the valuable skills and how you free yourself for higher leverage. Yes, they'll do it slower
and worse *at first* — that's the cost of growing them, and it's the point of delegation, not a reason
to take it back.

### The boomerang problem — don't take it back

A common failure: you delegate something, the person struggles or brings it back ("can you just do
this part?"), and you *take it back* — which teaches them that struggling returns the work to you (so
they stop trying), and re-establishes you as the bottleneck. Resist the boomerang: when they bring it
back, coach them through it ("what have you tried? what would you do?") and hand it back, rather than
solving it yourself. Taking it back feels efficient now but destroys the delegation (and their growth).
Support them through the struggle; don't absorb the work back.

{: .note }
> **Delegate outcomes at the right rung — including what you're good at — with a monitoring contract</br>**
> Delegation scales you and grows people, but it's done badly as micromanagement (delegate a task, then
> hover) or abdication (throw it over the wall). Do it well: delegate <em>outcomes</em> (the goal to own),
> not tasks (steps to execute); match the <em>rung</em> of autonomy to the person's competence and
> confidence for that work (too high = abdication, too low = micromanagement); set a <em>monitoring
> contract</em> (agreed check-ins and escalation, so you catch problems without hovering); and delegate
> the things you're <em>good at</em> — that's the point, freeing you for higher leverage and growing
> people into valuable work (they'll be slower at first — that's the cost of growth, not a reason to
> take it back). Resist the boomerang (don't re-absorb work people struggle with; coach and hand it
> back). Delegation done well is how a lead multiplies their impact and develops their team.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** Your team's CI is flaky — tests fail randomly, builds are slow, and it's a constant
low-grade drag on everyone. You've been meaning to fix it but never have time, and honestly you *could*
fix it fastest yourself. Instead, you want to delegate it to Maya, a mid-level engineer who's solid,
reasonably confident, but hasn't owned something this broad before. You want to hand her the *outcome*
("own making our CI reliable"), set her up to succeed, and grow her through it.

**Write the actual handoff conversation** — how you'd delegate this, including the outcome, the rung of
autonomy, the safety net, and the check-in plan. Then note the principles and mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong handoff delegates the outcome, pitches the rung appropriately, sets a monitoring contract, and
frames it as a growth opportunity with support. Example:
<br><br>
"Maya, I want to hand you something meaningful: <strong>I'd like you to own making our CI reliable.</strong>
Not a specific task — the whole outcome. Right now it's flaky and slow and it's dragging everyone down,
and I'd like you to own getting it to a good place — reliable, reasonably fast, trustworthy. <em>(Delegates
the outcome, not steps — she owns the goal and the how.)</em>
<br><br>
I'm giving this to you because I think you're ready to own something this broad, and it's a great chance
to grow — you'll get to dig into the whole CI system, make architectural calls, and own a real
improvement everyone will feel. <em>(Frames it as a growth opportunity and a vote of confidence.)</em>
<br><br>
Here's how I'm thinking we work together on it: <strong>you own the approach</strong> — I'd love for you
to investigate, figure out what's causing the flakiness and slowness, and propose a plan. Why don't you
spend a few days digging in, then come show me your diagnosis and plan before you go big on the fixes —
partly so I can be a sounding board, partly because I've seen some of this before and can flag pitfalls.
After that, you run with it. <em>(Pitches the rung: she owns it, but with a 'propose the plan, then
execute' checkpoint appropriate to her not having owned something this broad — enough autonomy to own,
enough support to not go wrong.)</em>
<br><br>
For staying in sync: let's do a quick weekly check-in on how it's going, and — importantly — <strong>pull
me in anytime you're stuck or want to think something through</strong>, that's not a failure, that's what
I'm here for. And if you hit something that'll take longer than expected or affects others, flag it early.
<em>(The monitoring contract: weekly cadence, explicit permission to escalate, what to flag — visibility
without hovering.)</em>
<br><br>
One thing I want to be honest about: you might do some of this slower than I would, and that's completely
fine — the point is for you to own it and grow, not for it to be done the way I'd do it. I've got your
back. Sound good? What questions do you have?" <em>(Sets the expectation that slower/different is fine —
pre-empting the boomerang — and invites her questions.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Delegates the outcome</strong> ("make CI reliable"), not a task
— she owns the problem, judgment, and approach. (2) <strong>Pitches the rung to her level</strong> —
capable but hasn't owned something this broad, so "propose the plan, then execute with autonomy" (not
"do exactly this" = micromanage, not "figure it all out alone" = abdicate). (3) <strong>Monitoring
contract</strong> — weekly check-in + explicit escalation permission + what to flag = visibility and
safety net without hovering. (4) <strong>Delegates something valuable she'll grow from</strong> (and that
the lead could do faster himself — that's the point). (5) <strong>Pre-empts the boomerang</strong> —
"slower is fine, pulling me in isn't failure" sets up support-through-struggle rather than take-backs.
(6) <strong>Frames it as growth + confidence</strong>, motivating. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong>
(1) <strong>delegating the task not the outcome</strong> ("here's my plan, go execute these steps") —
keeps the lead the brain, no growth; (2) <strong>wrong rung</strong> — "figure it out, don't bother me"
(abdication — she may flounder on her first broad ownership) or "check with me on every change"
(micromanagement — no real ownership); (3) <strong>no monitoring contract</strong> — either disappearing
(no safety net) or hovering (no autonomy); (4) <strong>keeping it because he's faster</strong> — the
bottleneck-and-no-growth trap; delegating what you're good at is the point; (5) <strong>setting up the
boomerang</strong> — not signaling that struggle/slower is okay, so she brings it back and he takes it;
(6) <strong>no support / vote of confidence</strong> — handing it over coldly without framing it as
growth or offering backup.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What does "delegate outcomes, not tasks" mean, and why does matching the "rung" of autonomy to
the person matter?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"Delegate outcomes, not tasks" means <strong>handing over the goal to achieve (the outcome — "make our
CI reliable") and letting the person own the approach, rather than handing over a prescribed set of steps
(a task — "run these specific commands") where you've already made the decisions</strong>. The
distinction is about who does the <em>thinking</em>: delegating a task keeps you the decision-maker (you
figured out the approach; they just execute your plan — you're the brain, they're the hands), while
delegating an outcome gives them the real problem to own (the judgment, the approach, the responsibility
for achieving the goal). Why delegating outcomes is better: (1) <strong>it develops the person</strong> —
owning the outcome means they do the thinking (diagnosing, choosing the approach, making judgment calls),
which is how they grow; executing your prescribed task develops nothing (they learn to follow steps, not
to own problems). (2) <strong>it actually offloads the work</strong> — if you delegate a task, you still
did the hard part (the thinking/planning) and remain involved in every decision, so you haven't really
freed yourself; delegating the outcome hands over the whole thing (thinking included), which genuinely
offloads it. (3) <strong>it gives real ownership</strong> — people are more motivated and committed when
they own a meaningful outcome than when they execute someone else's steps. So delegating outcomes (say
what success looks like and the constraints, let them own the how) both develops people and truly scales
you, where delegating tasks does neither. Why matching the "rung" of autonomy to the person matters:
<strong>because the right amount of autonomy depends on the person's competence and confidence for that
specific work — and getting it wrong means either abdication (too much autonomy → they flounder) or
micromanagement (too little → they don't grow and you're still driving)</strong>. Autonomy comes in
levels — from "do exactly this" (lowest) through "propose an approach, then do it" to "own this area"
(highest). The right rung depends on how capable and confident the person is <em>for this particular
work</em>: a highly capable, confident person on familiar territory can handle a high rung ("own it, keep
me posted"), while someone newer or on unfamiliar work needs a lower rung ("propose your approach and
let's discuss, then run with it") — and the same person is at different rungs for different work (senior
on backend, novice on frontend). Matching the rung matters because: (1) <strong>too high a rung for the
person is abdication</strong> — giving someone more autonomy than their competence/confidence supports
means they flounder without adequate support (they may fail, get overwhelmed, or lose confidence — like
throwing someone into their first broad ownership with "figure it out, don't bother me"); (2) <strong>too
low a rung is micromanagement</strong> — giving a capable person less autonomy than they can handle means
they don't get real ownership (so they don't grow and are frustrated) and you're still doing the driving
(so you haven't scaled). The right rung threads between: enough autonomy that the person genuinely owns
it and grows (stretched), enough support/structure that they don't fail (safe) — which is the scaffolding
idea (Lesson 27) applied to delegation. Pitching the rung correctly is the core delegation judgment
because it's what makes delegation both developmental (they own enough to grow) and safe (they have
enough support to succeed) — and it must be calibrated to the specific person and specific work, not
one-size-fits-all. Together, delegating outcomes (not tasks) at the right rung (matched to the person) is
what makes delegation genuinely develop people and scale the lead, rather than micromanaging (task +
low rung) or abdicating (outcome + too-high rung with no support).
</details>

**Q2.** Why should you delegate things you're *good at*, and what is the "boomerang problem"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
You should delegate things you're good at because <strong>keeping the work you're best at makes you the
bottleneck on exactly the high-value work and starves your people of the chance to grow into it — whereas
delegating it (with support) is how people develop valuable skills and how you free yourself for higher
leverage</strong>. The instinct is the opposite: keep the work you're good at (you do it faster and
better) and delegate the scraps you don't want or aren't good at. But this is backwards for several
reasons: (1) <strong>it keeps you the bottleneck on the high-value work</strong> — if you hoard the
important things you're best at, everything in that area routes through you, which doesn't scale (you're
the constraint) and burns you out. (2) <strong>it starves people of growth</strong> — the valuable skills
you have are exactly what your people need to develop to grow, and they can only develop them by doing
that work; keeping it to yourself denies them the growth (they stay junior on the things that matter
most). (3) <strong>it caps the team's capability</strong> — a team where the lead hoards the high-value
skills can never develop more people who can do that work, so the team stays dependent on the lead.
Delegating what you're good at (with appropriate support and rung) fixes all this: it develops people
into the valuable work (growing the team's capability), and it frees you from being the bottleneck to
focus on higher-leverage things only you can do. The catch — and the reason it feels wrong — is that
<strong>they'll do it slower and worse at first</strong>, because they're learning; but that's the cost
of growing them, and it's the entire <em>point</em> of delegation (developing people), not a reason to
take it back. The short-term inefficiency (slower, imperfect) buys the long-term payoff (a capable person
who can now do it, and a freed-up you) — so accepting the temporary cost of them learning is essential.
"Delegate what you're good at" is thus the counterintuitive but crucial principle: the high-value work
you're best at is exactly what you should be growing people into, not hoarding. The <strong>boomerang
problem</strong> is <strong>the common failure where you delegate something, the person struggles or
brings part of it back ("can you just do this bit?"), and you <em>take it back</em> and do it yourself —
which destroys the delegation and their growth</strong>. It happens because taking it back feels
efficient in the moment (you can do it faster, and it relieves their struggle), but it has damaging
effects: (1) <strong>it teaches them that struggling returns the work to you</strong> — so they learn that
when something gets hard, they can bounce it back to you, which means they stop pushing through
difficulty (why struggle if the lead will just take it?) — undermining exactly the growth delegation was
meant to produce; (2) <strong>it re-establishes you as the bottleneck</strong> — the work comes back to
you, so you haven't scaled after all, and the pattern repeats (everything hard boomerangs back); (3)
<strong>it stunts their development</strong> — they don't get to work through the struggle (which is where
the learning is), so they don't grow. The fix is to <strong>resist the boomerang</strong>: when they
bring it back, don't take it — coach them through it instead ("what have you tried? what would you do?
what's the specific blocker?") and hand it back to them, supporting them through the struggle rather than
absorbing the work. This keeps the ownership with them (so they grow and you stay scaled), while giving
them the support to get through the hard part. The discipline is that <strong>your job when someone
struggles with delegated work is to support them through it, not to do it for them</strong> — the struggle
is the growth, and taking it back forfeits both the growth and the scaling. Both points reflect the core
of good delegation: delegate the valuable work (what you're good at) to grow people and scale yourself,
and hold the line when it's hard (don't boomerang) — accepting the short-term cost (slower, struggle) for
the long-term payoff (developed people, a scaled you), which is the entire purpose of delegating.
</details>

## Homework

Examine your delegation. (1) Are you delegating outcomes or tasks? Find something you're delegating as a
task (prescribed steps) and re-frame it as an outcome they own. (2) Are you matching the rung to the
person, or defaulting to one level (micromanaging everyone, or abdicating to everyone)? Pick a delegation
and check the rung fits their competence/confidence for that work. (3) Are you hoarding the work you're
good at? Identify one high-value thing you keep because you're fastest, and plan to delegate it (with
support). (4) Do you take work back when people struggle (the boomerang)? Reflect: what's your default
delegation failure — micromanaging, abdicating, hoarding, or boomeranging — and what's one thing to
delegate better?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds delegation skill and self-awareness about delegation habits. A strong response
examines whether they delegate outcomes or tasks (re-framing a task as an owned outcome), whether they
match the rung to the person, whether they hoard high-value work, and whether they boomerang — and
identifies their default failure mode. The realizations: (1) <strong>delegate outcomes, not tasks</strong>
— handing over the goal to own (with the how) develops people and truly offloads work, where prescribing
steps keeps you the brain and them the hands (no growth, no real scaling); (2) <strong>match the rung to
the person</strong> — autonomy should fit their competence and confidence for that specific work (too
high = abdication/floundering, too low = micromanagement/no growth), with a monitoring contract that
catches problems without hovering; (3) <strong>delegate what you're good at</strong> — hoarding the
high-value work makes you the bottleneck and starves people of growth; delegating it (with support) grows
the team and frees you, accepting that they'll be slower at first (that's the point, not a reason to take
it back); (4) <strong>resist the boomerang</strong> — taking back work people struggle with teaches them
struggle returns it to you and re-establishes you as the bottleneck; coach and hand it back instead. On
reflection, people commonly find a default failure mode: micromanaging (delegating tasks and hovering),
abdicating (throwing things over the wall with no support/contract), hoarding (keeping the high-value work
they're good at — very common), or boomeranging (taking work back when people struggle). The highest-value
change is usually one of: delegate outcomes not tasks, delegate the valuable work you hoard (with support),
or hold the line against the boomerang. The meta-point: delegation is how a lead scales beyond their own
hands and how people grow (the work is the main development tool), but it's done badly as micromanagement
or abdication — done well (outcomes at the right rung, with a monitoring contract, including the work you're
good at, resisting the boomerang), it multiplies your impact and develops your team. It requires accepting
short-term inefficiency (people slower/imperfect at first) for the long-term payoff (capable people, a
scaled you) — which is the entire purpose. This is the engine of growing the team. The next lesson goes
deeper on using the work itself as the development tool — assigning for growth, not just speed, and
countering the trap of always giving the interesting work to the fastest person.
</details>
