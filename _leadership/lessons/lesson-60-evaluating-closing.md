# Lesson 60: Evaluating and Closing Candidates

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **debrief** — the post-interview meeting where interviewers share findings and decide.
> - **evidence before opinions** — presenting what the candidate actually did before anyone states a verdict, to avoid anchoring.
> - **anchor(ing)** — the first stated opinion pulling everyone else toward it.
> - **hire bar** — the absolute standard each hire must clear ("would they raise our average?") — never "better than our weakest."
> - **weak yes** — a hesitant "I guess, probably" — treated as a no, because doubts about hires usually prove right.
> - **false positive / false negative** — hiring someone you shouldn't have (very costly) / passing on someone good (cheap by comparison).
> - **references** — conversations with the candidate's past colleagues; useful only with specific behavioral questions.
> - **lukewarm** — noticeably unenthusiastic; in references, a signal.
> - **the close** — persuading your chosen candidate to accept; decided mostly on **growth, manager, and mission**, not just money.
> - **comp (compensation)** — the full pay package: salary, bonus, equity.

## Concept

After the interviews comes the hard part: **deciding well under uncertainty** (you never have complete
information) and then **actually landing the person** you want. The decision hinges on a good **debrief**
(evidence before opinions, hire-bar not compare-to-team, no weak yeses); the close hinges on understanding
**what candidates actually decide on** (usually growth, manager, and mission — not just money) and handling
**compensation** without games. Both are learnable disciplines that many people do poorly.

```
   DECIDE WELL → LAND THE PERSON
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ THE DEBRIEF:                                           │
   │  • EVIDENCE before opinions ("here's what they did")   │
   │  • HIRE-BAR (above our bar?) not compare-to-the-team    │
   │  • NO WEAK YESES (a hesitant "I guess" = a NO)          │
   │ THE CLOSE:                                             │
   │  • what candidates decide on: GROWTH, MANAGER, MISSION, │
   │    money (often in that order)                         │
   │  • comp conversations WITHOUT games                     │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The reframe: **decide on evidence against the hire-bar (no weak yeses), then close by addressing what the
person actually cares about (usually growth/manager/mission, not just money).** Debriefs go wrong when
opinions come before evidence, when candidates are compared to the team instead of the bar, and when weak
yeses get counted as yeses. Closing goes wrong when you assume it's all about money — most candidates decide
on growth, the manager, and the mission, so the close is about genuinely addressing those.

---

## How It Works

### The debrief — evidence before opinions

Run the hiring debrief so **evidence comes before opinions.** Each interviewer should present **what the
candidate actually did/said** (the specific evidence, against the rubric) <em>before</em> giving their
overall opinion/rating — because opinions stated first anchor everyone (the first strong "hire!" biases the
room) and skip the evidence. Evidence-first means the decision is grounded in what actually happened (the
demonstrated competencies), not in whose opinion is loudest or first. A good debrief is a structured
evidence-sharing (here's what I saw), then a decision built from the evidence — not a vote of gut
impressions. This also surfaces disagreements productively (comparing evidence, not just clashing opinions).

### Hire-bar, not compare-to-the-team

Evaluate against the **hire bar** (is this person above the bar we hire at — will they raise the team?), NOT
**compared to the current team** ("are they better than our weakest?") or to other candidates ("better than
the last one"). Why: comparing to the team (or a low reference) leads to hiring people who are merely "okay
relative to what we have," which lowers the bar over time (you hire people below your best, and the average
drifts down). The bar should be absolute (or aspirational — "would this person raise our average?") — you
want each hire to be strong <em>in absolute terms</em>, ideally raising the team, not just "better than our
weakest current person." Hire above your bar, so the team gets stronger, not weaker, with each hire.

### No weak yeses — the hesitant yes is a no

A crucial discipline: **a weak/hesitant "yes" is a no.** When an interviewer says "I guess... yeah, probably
hire, I'm not sure but..." — that hesitation is significant information: they have doubts, and doubts about a
hire usually prove right. Strong hires generate clear, confident yeses; a hesitant yes usually means
something's off (even if the interviewer can't fully articulate it). So **don't count weak yeses as yeses** —
treat a hesitant, doubt-filled "yes" as a no (or at least dig hard into the doubt). Because a bad hire is so
costly, the bar should be a <em>confident</em> yes; when in real doubt, don't hire (the cost of a false
negative — passing on a good candidate — is much lower than a false positive — a bad hire). "When in doubt,
it's a no."

### References done usefully

**References** (talking to people who've worked with the candidate) are useful if done well: ask specific,
behavioral questions (not "were they good?" — which gets bland positives) — "what was it like to work with
them? what would you have them work on? how did they handle [X]?" — and read between the lines (a hesitant
or lukewarm reference is a signal, like a weak yes). References are most useful for confirming/checking
specific concerns from the interviews and for the collaboration/behavioral dimensions (which interviews
assess less well). Done as a box-tick ("any red flags?"), they're useless; done thoughtfully, they add real
signal.

### The close — what candidates actually decide on

To **land** a candidate you want, understand **what candidates actually decide on** — which is usually
<em>not</em> primarily money: (1) **growth** — will they learn and advance (the biggest factor for many —
the opportunity to grow); (2) **the manager** — will they have a good manager (people join and leave
managers); (3) **the mission/work** — is the work meaningful and interesting; and (4) **money** — comp
matters (it must be competitive/fair), but it's often not the deciding factor (people rarely choose purely on
money if the other things are right, and money rarely compensates for a bad manager or no growth). So the
close is about genuinely addressing what <em>this</em> candidate cares about (understand their priorities —
ask what they're looking for) — sell the growth, the manager relationship, the mission — not just the
salary. Closing is understanding and speaking to what actually drives their decision.

### Compensation without games — and the reneged offer

**Compensation conversations** should be straightforward and fair, not adversarial games (lowballing,
pressure tactics, exploding offers) — which damage trust and the relationship before the person even joins.
Make a fair, competitive offer, be transparent, and treat it as the start of a relationship (not a
negotiation to win). Games (a lowball to "save money," high-pressure tactics) can win a few dollars but cost
goodwill and can lose the candidate. And when an offer is **reneged/declined**, do a **postmortem** — why did
they decline? (a better offer? a concern in the process? something you could have done?) — to learn and
improve (candidate feedback on why they chose elsewhere is valuable data). Handle a decline gracefully (leave
the door open, protect the relationship — they may come back or refer others).

{: .note }
> **Decide on evidence against the hire-bar (no weak yeses); close on growth/manager/mission, not just money</br>**
> After interviews: decide well and land the person. The <em>debrief</em> should put <em>evidence before
> opinions</em> (what they actually did, against the rubric — not gut votes), evaluate against the <em>hire
> bar</em> (above our bar / would they raise the team — not compare-to-the-weakest), and reject <em>weak
> yeses</em> (a hesitant "I guess" is a no — the bar is a confident yes, because a bad hire is costly). Use
> <em>references</em> thoughtfully (behavioral questions, read between the lines). Then <em>close</em> by
> understanding what candidates actually decide on — usually <em>growth, the manager, and the mission</em>
> more than money — and addressing those genuinely, with <em>fair comp conversations, not games</em>. Deciding
> on evidence (no weak yeses) and closing on what really drives the person are the disciplines that turn a good
> interview process into strong hires who actually join.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** You've interviewed a candidate for a senior role, and the panel is **split**: two
interviewers say **strong hire**, two say **no hire** — and there's real evidence on both sides (the
strong-hire interviewers saw genuine strengths; the no-hire interviewers have real concerns). You're the
hiring manager and have to run the debrief and make the call.

**Run the debrief and make the call.** Then note the principles and mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong debrief digs into the evidence on both sides (not just tallying opinions), evaluates against the
hire bar, takes the concerns seriously (no weak yeses / when in doubt), and reaches an evidence-based call.
Example:
<br><br>
<strong>Run the debrief — evidence first, both sides:</strong> "Don't just tally 2-2 or go with the loudest.
Get the <em>evidence</em> from each side: <br>
• Strong-hire interviewers: 'What specifically did they do that impressed you?' — get the concrete evidence
(against the rubric) for the strengths. <br>
• No-hire interviewers: 'What specifically concerned you?' — get the concrete evidence for the concerns
(what did they see — a real gap? a red flag? a rubric miss?). <br>
Put both sets of evidence on the table and examine them together — are the concerns and strengths about
<em>different</em> dimensions (strong technically but concerning on collaboration?) or contradictory reads of
the same thing? Understand what's actually going on, not just who's louder." <em>(Evidence before opinions —
dig into the concrete evidence on both sides, not a vote.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Evaluate against the hire bar and the concerns:</strong> "Key questions: (1) Are the no-hire concerns
<em>serious and real</em> (a genuine gap or red flag) or minor/subjective? Serious concerns from thoughtful
interviewers usually matter — dig into them, don't dismiss them because two others liked the candidate. (2)
Is this a <em>confident</em> hire, or is the split itself a signal of doubt? A genuinely split panel (real
concerns on the table) is often a sign the candidate isn't a clear hire — and the discipline is that when
there's real doubt, especially on a senior hire, lean no (no weak yeses; the cost of a bad senior hire is
high). (3) What do the concerns say against the <em>hire bar</em> — do they mean the person is below the bar
or wouldn't raise the team?"
<br><br>
<strong>The call (lean toward the concerns, unless they're addressable):</strong> "My default on a genuinely
split panel with real concerns is to <strong>lean no</strong> — not because the strengths aren't real, but
because (a) real concerns from thoughtful interviewers usually prove right, (b) a bad senior hire is very
costly (false positive » false negative), and (c) a split panel is itself doubt, and 'when in doubt, it's a
no.' <em>Unless</em>: the concerns are addressable/checkable (e.g., a specific concern I can probe with a
targeted follow-up interview or a reference focused on exactly that concern) — in which case, gather that
evidence before deciding, rather than deciding on the split. If the concerns hold up, no-hire; if they're
resolved by the extra evidence, reconsider." <em>(Lean no on real doubt / serious concerns / a split — no
weak yeses — but resolve addressable concerns with more evidence rather than a coin-flip.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Evidence before opinions</strong> — dig into the concrete evidence
on both sides, don't just tally votes or go with the loudest. (2) <strong>Take concerns seriously</strong> —
real concerns from thoughtful interviewers usually matter; don't dismiss them because others liked the
candidate. (3) <strong>Hire bar</strong> — do the concerns mean below-the-bar / wouldn't-raise-the-team? (4)
<strong>No weak yeses / when in doubt, no</strong> — a genuinely split panel is doubt; lean no on a costly
(senior) hire unless the doubt resolves. (5) <strong>Resolve addressable concerns with evidence</strong> — a
targeted follow-up or focused reference beats deciding on the split. (6) <strong>The asymmetry</strong> — a
bad hire (false positive) costs far more than passing on a good one (false negative), so the bar is a
confident yes. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>tallying votes (2-2 → coin flip)</strong> —
without examining the evidence (the decision is in the evidence, not the count); (2) <strong>going with the
loudest/most-senior interviewer</strong> — rather than the evidence; (3) <strong>dismissing the concerns</strong>
because two people liked the candidate (real concerns usually matter); (4) <strong>counting a split as a weak
yes → hire</strong> — a split with real doubt should lean no (no weak yeses); (5) <strong>hiring to fill the
seat</strong> (pressure to hire someone) — a bad hire is worse than no hire; (6) <strong>not resolving
addressable concerns</strong> — deciding on the split when a targeted follow-up could resolve it; (7)
<strong>comparing to the team</strong> ("better than our weakest") instead of the hire bar.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why should a debrief put "evidence before opinions" and use the "hire bar" (not compare-to-the-team),
and why is a weak yes a no?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A debrief should put <strong>evidence before opinions</strong> because <strong>opinions stated first anchor
the room and skip the evidence, while leading with evidence grounds the decision in what the candidate
actually demonstrated</strong>. If interviewers lead with their opinion/rating ("strong hire!"), that opinion
<em>anchors</em> everyone (the first confident opinion biases the discussion, and people converge on it or
argue opinions), and the specific evidence (what the candidate actually did) gets skipped or backfilled to
justify the opinion. Leading with <strong>evidence</strong> — each interviewer presenting what the candidate
actually did/said (against the rubric) <em>before</em> their overall opinion — grounds the decision in the
demonstrated reality: the room considers the actual evidence (the competencies shown) and builds the decision
from it, rather than from whose opinion was loudest or first. This makes the decision more accurate (based on
what happened, not impressions), surfaces disagreements productively (comparing evidence, not clashing
opinions), and reduces the anchoring/groupthink that opinion-first debriefs produce. Why use the <strong>hire
bar</strong> (not compare-to-the-team): <strong>because comparing candidates to the current team (or to other
candidates) leads to hiring people merely "okay relative to what we have," which lowers the bar over time,
whereas an absolute (or aspirational) bar keeps each hire strong and ideally raises the team</strong>.
Evaluating against the <strong>hire bar</strong> asks "is this person above the bar we hire at — will they
raise the team?" — an absolute standard. Comparing to the team instead ("are they better than our weakest
current person?") or to other candidates ("better than the last one?") uses a <em>relative</em> reference,
which is dangerous: if you hire anyone "better than our weakest," you hire people below your best, so the
team's average drifts <em>down</em> over time (each hire is okay-relative-to-a-declining-reference, and the
bar erodes). An absolute/aspirational bar ("would this person raise our average / are they strong in
absolute terms?") keeps each hire genuinely strong, so the team gets <em>stronger</em> (or at least holds)
with each hire, not weaker. You want to hire people who raise the team, which requires an absolute bar, not a
compare-to-the-weakest one that ratchets the bar down. Why a <strong>weak yes is a no</strong>: <strong>because
a hesitant, doubt-filled yes signals real (if inarticulate) doubts, which usually prove right — and because a
bad hire is so costly, the bar should be a <em>confident</em> yes</strong>. When an interviewer gives a weak
yes ("I guess... probably... I'm not sure but... yeah"), the hesitation is significant information: they have
doubts (even if they can't fully articulate them), and doubts about a candidate — that intuitive "something's
off" — usually turn out to be right (there was a real concern the interviewer sensed). Strong hires generate
<em>clear, confident</em> yeses; a hesitant yes usually means something is off. So counting a weak yes as a
yes ignores real warning signals. The deeper reason is the <strong>asymmetry of hiring errors</strong>: a
<em>false positive</em> (a bad hire) is very costly (they underperform, disrupt the team, are hard to manage
out — a bad hire is worse than no hire), while a <em>false negative</em> (passing on a good candidate) is much
less costly (you miss one person, but can keep looking). Given this asymmetry, the bar should be a
<em>confident</em> yes — you should only hire when you're clearly convinced, and when in real doubt, lean no
(the cost of wrongly passing is much lower than the cost of wrongly hiring). So "a weak yes is a no" (and
"when in doubt, it's a no") is the discipline that keeps the bar high and avoids costly bad hires: treat a
hesitant, doubt-filled yes as a no (or dig hard into the doubt), because the doubt is real information and
the cost of a bad hire justifies requiring a confident yes. All three points make the hiring decision
rigorous: evidence before opinions (grounded in reality, not anchored on impressions), the hire bar (absolute,
so the team stays strong, not a compare-to-the-weakest that erodes it), and no weak yeses (a confident yes
required, because doubts prove right and bad hires are costly) — together producing well-grounded decisions
that reliably hire strong people and avoid the costly bad hires that erode teams.
</details>

**Q2.** What do candidates actually decide on when closing (and why not just money), and why avoid games in
compensation conversations?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Candidates, when deciding whether to join, actually decide primarily on <strong>growth, the manager, and the
mission/work — often more than money</strong>: (1) <strong>growth</strong> — will they learn, develop, and
advance? The opportunity to grow (new skills, bigger scope, career progression) is the biggest factor for
many candidates, especially strong ones who care about their trajectory. (2) <strong>the manager</strong> —
will they have a good manager? People join and leave managers, and a candidate assessing an offer weighs
whether their prospective manager will support, develop, and be good to work for (the manager relationship is
a huge part of job satisfaction). (3) <strong>the mission/work</strong> — is the work meaningful, interesting,
and something they care about? Engaging, purposeful work matters a lot to whether someone wants to join. (4)
<strong>money</strong> — compensation matters (it must be competitive and fair — an insultingly low offer
loses candidates, and comp is real), but it's <em>often not the deciding factor</em>: people rarely choose
purely on money if the other things (growth, manager, mission) are right, and money rarely compensates for a
bad manager, no growth, or meaningless work. So while comp must be fair/competitive, the <em>close</em> —
winning the candidate — is usually about the growth, the manager, and the mission more than the salary. Why
this matters for closing: to land a candidate you want, you must <strong>understand and address what
<em>this</em> candidate actually cares about</strong> — ask what they're looking for (their priorities), and
genuinely speak to those (sell the growth opportunity, the manager relationship, the meaningful work),
rather than assuming it's all about money and just making a bigger salary offer. A candidate deciding on
growth won't be won by money alone; one deciding on the manager needs to feel good about you; so the close is
about genuinely addressing their real decision drivers (which are usually growth/manager/mission), not just
the comp. Why avoid <strong>games in compensation conversations</strong>: <strong>because comp games
(lowballing, pressure tactics, exploding offers) damage trust and the relationship before the person even
joins — a cost that outweighs any small dollar savings, and can lose the candidate</strong>. Compensation
conversations should be straightforward and fair (a competitive, fair offer, transparently communicated),
treated as the <em>start of a relationship</em>, not a negotiation to win. Games — lowballing to "save money,"
high-pressure tactics, exploding offers ("decide by tomorrow or it's gone"), or adversarial haggling — are
counterproductive because: (1) <strong>they damage trust before day one</strong> — a candidate who feels
lowballed or pressured starts the relationship feeling the company tried to take advantage of them, which
poisons the start (and they remember it); (2) <strong>they can lose the candidate</strong> — a strong
candidate with options may walk away from a company that plays games (why join somewhere that treats you
adversarially before you've even started?); (3) <strong>the savings are small and short-sighted</strong> —
squeezing a few dollars via games isn't worth losing goodwill (or the candidate) — the value of a happy,
trusting new hire far exceeds the marginal comp saved; and (4) <strong>it's not who you want to be</strong> —
the negotiation sets the tone for the relationship, and you want it to start with fairness and trust, not
games. So make a fair, competitive offer, be transparent, and treat comp as the start of a relationship
(fair, not a win-lose game) — which builds the trust and goodwill that make the person want to join and start
positive. (And if an offer is declined, do a postmortem to learn why, and handle it gracefully — protecting
the relationship.) Both points reflect closing well: understand and address what candidates actually decide
on (growth, manager, mission — not just money) to win them, and handle comp fairly without games (which
damage trust and can lose the candidate) — together turning a good decision into a strong hire who actually
joins, starting the relationship on trust rather than games. Closing is the often-neglected other half of
hiring: deciding well is worthless if you can't land the person, and landing them requires addressing their
real motivations and treating them fairly.
</details>
