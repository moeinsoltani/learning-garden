# Lesson 26: Coaching vs Mentoring

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **mentoring** — giving your answer and experience; **coaching** — helping someone find *their own* answer through questions (the lesson's core pair).
> - **knowledge transfer** — moving facts from one head to another (what mentoring does fast).
> - **capability / judgment** — the ability to solve *future* problems well (what coaching builds).
> - **GROW model** — a coaching structure: Goal → Reality → Options → Will (way forward).
> - **leading question** — a "question" that's really your answer in disguise ("don't you think you should…?"); fake coaching.
> - **ownership** — feeling that a decision or task is genuinely yours.
> - **dependence** — the state where the team can't decide without you (what always-mentoring creates).
> - **intent-based leadership** — Marquet's model: people bring "I intend to do X" instead of "what should I do?"
> - **onboarding** — the period of learning a new team, codebase, and conventions.
> - **developmental** — done for the person's growth, not just the task's completion.

## Concept

When someone brings you a problem, you have two fundamentally different ways to help: **mentor**
(give them your answer/expertise) or **coach** (help them find their own answer). Both are
valuable, but they serve different purposes — and using the wrong one wastes an opportunity or
creates a bottleneck. The skill is knowing *which* fits the moment: coaching grows the
answer-finder (building their capability), mentoring transfers your knowledge (fast, direct). A
lead who only ever mentors creates dependence; one who develops people knows when to step back and
coach.

```
   MENTORING                        COACHING
   ─────────                        ────────
   YOU give the answer /            YOU help THEM find the answer
   your experience / the pattern     (questions, not answers)
   → fast, direct knowledge          → grows their judgment & ownership
     transfer
   BEST FOR: onboarding, a          BEST FOR: growth, developing
   knowledge gap, a crisis,          judgment, when they can find it
   "just tell me the standard"       themselves, building capability

   Default to COACHING for growth; MENTOR for gaps/crisis/speed.
```

The reframe: **your default should be coaching (grow the answer-finder), reaching for mentoring
when speed or a genuine knowledge gap calls for it.** The instinct — especially for an expert — is
to always mentor (give the answer; it's fast and you know it). But defaulting to mentoring makes
you a bottleneck and keeps people dependent; defaulting to coaching (where appropriate) builds
people who can solve problems themselves — which is how your impact scales.

## How It Works

### The distinction, and why it matters

**Mentoring** = giving your answer, experience, or the pattern ("here's how I'd do it", "the
standard approach is X"). **Coaching** = helping the person find *their own* answer through
questions ("what options have you considered?", "what do you think would happen if...?"). The
difference matters because they produce different things: mentoring transfers *knowledge* (fast,
efficient when there's a real gap); coaching builds *capability and judgment* (slower, but the
person grows and can do it themselves next time). Confusing them — coaching when someone just needs
the answer, or mentoring when they could grow by finding it — wastes the moment.

### When to mentor

Reach for mentoring when: (1) **onboarding / a genuine knowledge gap** — the person simply doesn't
know something factual (a convention, how the system works, the standard approach), and coaching
them to "discover" it would be slow and artificial; just tell them. (2) **a crisis / time
pressure** — when something's on fire and speed matters, give the answer, don't coach through it.
(3) **they explicitly need your expertise** — a genuine "what's the right way to do X?" where your
experience is the value. Mentoring is right when the knowledge transfer itself is what's needed and
coaching would be inefficient or artificial.

### When to coach (the default for growth)

Reach for coaching when: (1) **growth is the goal** — the person could develop by working it out,
and building their judgment matters more than the immediate answer. (2) **they can find it
themselves** — with the right questions, they're capable of reaching a good answer (so coaching
develops them and gives ownership). (3) **it's a judgment call, not a fact** — questions of
approach, trade-offs, and decisions are where coaching builds the judgment that makes someone more
capable. Default to coaching for anything developmental, because it grows the person — which is the
higher-leverage outcome than solving one problem for them.

### The GROW model

A useful coaching structure: **G**oal (what do you want to achieve here?), **R**eality (what's the
current situation? what have you tried?), **O**ptions (what could you do? what else?), **W**ill /
Way forward (what will you do? what's the next step?). GROW gives a simple frame for coaching
someone to their own answer — clarify the goal, explore the reality and options through questions,
and land on their chosen next step. It keeps coaching structured rather than aimless questioning.

### The leading-question anti-pattern

A common coaching failure: **fake coaching** — asking "questions" that are really your answer in
disguise ("don't you think you should use a queue here?"). This isn't coaching (you're not helping
them think; you're steering them to your predetermined answer while pretending to ask), and people
see through it (it's manipulative and slightly insulting). Real coaching means being genuinely open
to their answer (which might differ from yours and be fine, or better). If you've already decided
the answer and just want them to say it, don't fake-coach — either mentor (say it) or genuinely open
the question.

### Intent-based leadership (leader-leader)

A related model (Marquet's *Turn the Ship Around*): move people from "tell me what to do" to "I
intend to...". Instead of giving orders (or answers), cultivate people bringing you their intended
action ("I intend to solve it by doing X") — which you can affirm or adjust. This builds ownership
and judgment (they're deciding, you're guiding) and scales far better than everyone needing your
answer. It's coaching applied to decisions: grow people who bring intentions, not questions.

{: .note }
> **Default to coaching (grow the answer-finder); mentor for gaps, crises, and speed</br>**
> When someone brings a problem, you can mentor (give your answer — fast knowledge transfer, right
> for onboarding, genuine gaps, crises) or coach (help them find their own answer — builds judgment
> and ownership, right for growth and judgment calls they can work through). The default for
> anything developmental should be coaching, because it grows people who can solve problems
> themselves — where always-mentoring makes you a bottleneck and keeps people dependent. Use the
> GROW model to structure coaching, avoid the leading-question anti-pattern (fake coaching that's
> your answer in disguise — genuinely open the question or just mentor), and cultivate "I intend
> to..." over "tell me what to do." Knowing which mode fits the moment — and defaulting to coaching
> for growth — is how you develop people rather than creating dependence.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** Two different engineers bring you the *same* question — "Should we use Kafka or
just a cron job for this?" — but they're in very different places:
- **Sana**, a junior in her second week, genuinely doesn't know the trade-offs and is a bit lost.
- **Marcus**, a senior engineer gunning for a staff role, is entirely capable of reasoning through
  this himself.

**Write how you'd respond to each** — the same question, two different people. Then explain the
principle and the mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
The same question calls for different modes because the two people are in different places — Sana
needs mentoring (a knowledge gap), Marcus needs coaching (growth opportunity).
<br><br>
<strong>To Sana (junior, week 2 — mentor, mostly):</strong> "Good question to be asking. Let me give
you the lay of the land, because this is the kind of trade-off that's worth learning. A cron job is
simpler — it runs on a schedule, easy to set up, good when you just need something to happen
periodically and a little delay is fine. Kafka is a whole event-streaming system — powerful for
high-volume, real-time, decoupled event processing, but it's a lot of complexity to run. For what
we're doing here [context], a cron job is almost certainly right — we don't have the volume or
real-time need that would justify Kafka's complexity. The general principle: don't reach for the
heavy distributed system until the simpler thing genuinely can't do the job. Does that make sense?
Want me to explain any of it more?" <em>(Mentoring — she has a genuine knowledge gap; coaching her
to "discover" the answer would be slow and artificial. Give the answer AND the reasoning/principle,
so she learns the pattern, not just this instance.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>To Marcus (senior, staff-track — coach):</strong> "What's your instinct? Walk me through how
you're thinking about it." [He reasons; you ask coaching questions:] "What's the actual volume and
latency requirement here? ... What would Kafka buy us that a cron job wouldn't, and is that worth the
operational cost? ... What would make you regret each choice in a year?" [Let him reach the answer.]
"Yeah, I think you've got it — sounds like cron is right for now, for the reasons you laid out. One
thing I'd add: [any genuine expertise gap]." <em>(Coaching — Marcus is fully capable of reasoning
this through, and for someone gunning for staff, developing the judgment (and demonstrating he can
make these calls independently) matters far more than getting my answer. The questions build and
surface his judgment; I only add genuine expertise if there's a gap. This also grows him toward the
staff-level autonomy he's aiming for.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>The principle:</strong> Match the mode to the person and situation. Sana has a genuine
knowledge gap (mentor — give the answer + reasoning, so she learns); Marcus has the capability and a
growth goal (coach — help him reach and own the answer, building the judgment he needs for staff).
The <em>same question</em> gets different responses because the goal differs: for Sana, efficient
knowledge transfer; for Marcus, developing judgment and autonomy. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong>
(1) <strong>coaching Sana</strong> — asking a lost beginner "what do you think you should do?" when
she genuinely doesn't know is frustrating and artificial (she can't discover trade-offs she's never
seen); just teach her. (2) <strong>mentoring Marcus</strong> — giving a staff-track senior the answer
robs him of a growth opportunity, keeps him dependent, and signals you don't trust his judgment;
worse for his development and for scaling. (3) <strong>fake-coaching either</strong> — leading
questions ("don't you think cron is better?") that are just your answer in disguise; genuinely coach
Marcus (open to his reasoning) or genuinely mentor Sana (just tell her). (4) <strong>defaulting to
one mode for everyone</strong> — the whole point is reading the person and situation; a lead who
always mentors creates dependence, one who always coaches frustrates people who need answers. (5)
<strong>not giving Sana the principle</strong> — mentoring should transfer the reusable pattern
("don't reach for the heavy system until the simple one fails"), not just the one answer, so she
learns. (6) <strong>rushing Marcus's coaching</strong> — not giving him space to reason (using
silence), or jumping in with the answer if he pauses.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What's the difference between coaching and mentoring, and why should coaching be the default
for developmental situations?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The difference: <strong>mentoring is giving the person your answer, experience, or the pattern (you
provide the knowledge); coaching is helping the person find their own answer through questions (you
develop their thinking)</strong>. When someone brings a problem, mentoring says "here's how I'd do
it / the standard approach is X" — a direct transfer of your knowledge; coaching asks "what options
have you considered? what do you think would happen if...?" — guiding them to reach their own answer.
They produce fundamentally different things: <strong>mentoring transfers knowledge</strong> (fast and
efficient when there's a genuine gap — the person now knows the thing), while <strong>coaching builds
capability and judgment</strong> (slower, but the person develops their own problem-solving and can
do it themselves next time). Neither is universally better — they serve different purposes, and the
skill is matching the mode to the moment. Why coaching should be the default for developmental
situations: <strong>because coaching grows the person into someone who can solve problems themselves,
which is a higher-leverage outcome than solving one problem for them — and defaulting to mentoring
creates dependence and makes you a bottleneck</strong>. Several reasons: (1) <strong>coaching develops
capability, not just solves the instance</strong> — when you coach someone to their own answer, they
build the judgment and problem-solving ability to handle similar situations independently in the
future; mentoring solves this one problem but leaves their capability unchanged (they'll need you
again next time). For anything developmental, growing the capability is worth far more than the single
answer. (2) <strong>coaching builds ownership</strong> — people are more committed to solutions they
reached themselves than ones handed to them, so coached solutions get better buy-in and follow-through.
(3) <strong>always-mentoring creates dependence and a bottleneck</strong> — if you always give the
answer, people learn to bring you every question rather than developing their own judgment, which (a)
keeps them dependent (they don't grow) and (b) makes you a bottleneck (everything routes through you,
which doesn't scale and burns you out). Defaulting to coaching (where appropriate) builds people who
can solve problems without you — which is how your impact scales beyond your own hands (the leverage
theme). (4) <strong>the expert's instinct is to mentor, so coaching requires deliberate default</strong>
— especially for a capable person, the instinct is to just give the answer (it's fast, you know it),
so without a conscious default toward coaching, you'll over-mentor and under-develop. So the default
for developmental situations should be coaching — help them find the answer, building their capability
and ownership — reserving mentoring for when it's genuinely the right tool (Q2: gaps, crises, speed).
The reframe is important because the natural tendency (give the answer) optimizes for the immediate
problem at the expense of the person's growth and your own scalability; defaulting to coaching
optimizes for developing people who can solve problems themselves, which is the higher-leverage goal
and the essence of a lead growing their team. (This doesn't mean never mentor — it means coaching is
the <em>default</em> for growth, with mentoring reached for deliberately when the situation calls for
it.)
</details>

**Q2.** When is mentoring (giving the answer) the right choice, and what is the "fake coaching"
(leading-question) anti-pattern?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Mentoring (giving the answer) is the right choice</strong> when the situation calls for direct
knowledge transfer rather than developing the person's own answer — specifically: (1) <strong>onboarding
or a genuine knowledge gap</strong> — when the person simply doesn't know something factual (a
convention, how the system works, the standard approach, a trade-off they've never encountered), just
tell them; coaching someone to "discover" knowledge they don't have is slow, artificial, and
frustrating (you can't coach a week-two junior to reason out trade-offs she's never seen — she needs
to be taught them). When there's a real gap, the efficient and kind thing is to transfer the knowledge.
(2) <strong>a crisis or time pressure</strong> — when something's on fire and speed matters, give the
answer; coaching through a careful questioning process is the wrong tool when the situation needs a
fast resolution (coach later, in the retro). (3) <strong>they genuinely need your expertise</strong> —
a real "what's the right way to do X?" where your hard-won experience is exactly the value, and the
person would benefit from your direct guidance rather than reinventing it. The common thread:
<strong>mentor when the knowledge transfer itself is what's needed and coaching would be inefficient or
artificial</strong> — a genuine gap, a crisis, or a direct request for your expertise. (Even when
mentoring, transfer the reusable <em>pattern/principle</em>, not just the one answer, so the person
learns.) The <strong>fake coaching / leading-question anti-pattern</strong> is <strong>asking
"questions" that are really your answer in disguise — steering the person to your predetermined
conclusion while pretending to coach</strong>. It looks like coaching (you're asking questions) but
isn't: "don't you think you should use a queue here?" / "wouldn't it be better to...?" are not genuine
open questions — they're your answer phrased as a question, designed to lead the person to say what
you've already decided. Why it's a problem: (1) <strong>it's not actually coaching</strong> — real
coaching helps the person think and reach their own answer (which you're genuinely open to); fake
coaching just manipulates them toward your answer, so it develops nothing (they're not thinking, they're
guessing what you want) — you get neither the speed of honest mentoring nor the development of real
coaching. (2) <strong>people see through it</strong> — leading questions are transparent; the person
realizes you've already decided and are playing a game of "guess what I'm thinking," which is
manipulative and slightly insulting (you're pretending to value their input while not actually being
open to it). (3) <strong>it erodes trust</strong> — the disingenuousness (pretending to ask while
having decided) damages the honest, open dynamic coaching depends on. The fix: <strong>be genuinely
open when you coach, and just mentor when you're not</strong>. If you have a real question and are open
to their answer (which might differ from yours and be fine or better), coach genuinely. If you've
already decided the answer and just want them to arrive at it, don't fake-coach — either mentor
honestly (just say "I think we should use a queue, here's why") or, if you want to develop them,
genuinely open the question and be prepared for a different answer. The anti-pattern comes from wanting
the appearance of coaching (developing people, being collaborative) without the substance (genuine
openness to their answer) — and the honest alternatives (real coaching or honest mentoring) are always
better than the manipulative middle. Both points refine the coach/mentor judgment: mentor when direct
knowledge transfer is genuinely what's needed (gaps, crises, expertise), coach when growth is the goal
and they can work it through — but coach <em>genuinely</em> (open to their answer), never fake-coach
(leading questions), because fake coaching gets the worst of both and people see through it.
</details>

## Homework

Reflect on your coaching/mentoring balance and practice the judgment. (1) Notice your default — do you
tend to always mentor (give the answer — the expert's instinct) or do you coach where you could? (2)
Next time someone brings a problem, consciously choose: is this a genuine gap/crisis (mentor) or a
growth opportunity where they could find it (coach)? Practice coaching where you'd normally have just
given the answer. (3) Catch yourself fake-coaching (leading questions) — either genuinely open the
question or honestly mentor. (4) Try cultivating "I intend to..." — encourage someone to bring you
their intended action, not just a question. Reflect: what's your default, and where would defaulting
more to coaching develop your people?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds the coach/mentor judgment — knowing when to give the answer versus grow the
answer-finder. A strong response notices one's default (usually over-mentoring, the expert's instinct),
consciously chooses the mode per situation (gap/crisis → mentor; growth opportunity → coach), practices
coaching where they'd normally just answer, catches fake-coaching (leading questions), and tries
cultivating "I intend to...". The realizations: (1) <strong>coaching and mentoring serve different
purposes</strong> — mentoring transfers knowledge (fast, for genuine gaps/crises/expertise), coaching
builds capability and judgment (for growth and judgment calls they can work through); (2) <strong>the
default should be coaching for development</strong> — because growing people who can solve problems
themselves is higher-leverage than solving one problem for them, and always-mentoring creates dependence
and makes you a bottleneck (the expert's instinct to always give the answer is exactly what to resist);
(3) <strong>match the mode to the person and situation</strong> — the same question (Kafka vs cron) needs
mentoring for a lost junior (knowledge gap) and coaching for a staff-track senior (growth), because the
goal differs; (4) <strong>coach genuinely, never fake-coach</strong> — leading questions that are your
answer in disguise develop nothing and people see through them; be genuinely open or honestly mentor.
On self-reflection, most capable people find they over-mentor (defaulting to giving the answer because
it's fast and they know it), and that consciously coaching where they could — asking before answering,
being open to the person's reasoning — develops people they'd otherwise keep dependent. The highest-value
shift is usually: default more to coaching for developmental situations (resist the give-the-answer
instinct), reserving mentoring for genuine gaps, crises, and direct expertise needs. The meta-point:
when someone brings a problem, you have two tools (mentor/coach), and knowing which fits — defaulting to
coaching for growth — is how you develop people rather than creating dependence or being a bottleneck.
This is the judgment that makes 1:1s and the developmental relationship effective, built on the
listening/questioning skills of Lesson 25 (coaching <em>is</em> asking powerful questions and listening).
It scales your impact by growing people who can solve problems themselves. The next lesson applies this
to deliberately growing engineers — mentoring juniors and mid-levels with intent (scaffolding, growth
arcs) rather than hoping they develop by osmosis.
</details>
