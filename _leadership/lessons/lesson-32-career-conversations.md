# Lesson 32: Career Development Conversations

## Concept

To develop people well, you need to know **where each person actually wants to go** — and then bend
their work toward it. This is what career development conversations are for: understanding a person's
aspirations (which are often unclear, even to them) and helping them grow toward them. Crucially,
these are *not* performance reviews (backward-looking evaluation) — they're forward-looking, about the
person's growth and future. And the hardest version is the **not-promoted conversation** — telling
someone who expected a promotion that it isn't happening, honestly and constructively.

```
   CAREER CONVERSATION ≠ PERFORMANCE REVIEW
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ PERFORMANCE REVIEW: backward — how did you do?         │
   │ CAREER CONVERSATION: forward — where do you want to go, │
   │   and how do we get you there?                         │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
   "Where do you want to be in ~3 years?" (and 'I don't know'
    is a fine answer) → then bend the work toward it.
   Promotion: legible criteria + the packet you build all year.
```

The reframe: **know where each person wants to go, and bend their work toward it — via forward-looking
career conversations separate from performance reviews.** People grow best when their work connects to
their aspirations, but you can't do that if you don't know their aspirations — which requires
deliberately asking (in a conversation that's about their future, not their past performance). And when
promotion is involved, legible criteria and honest conversations (including the hard not-promoted one)
are what make it fair and developmental.

---

## How It Works

### Career conversations ≠ performance reviews

Keep these separate. A **performance review** is backward-looking evaluation (how did you do against
expectations?) — necessary, but a different thing. A **career conversation** is forward-looking, about
the person's aspirations and growth (where do you want to go, and how do we get you there?). Conflating
them fails both: evaluation makes people defensive (not open about aspirations), and career-thinking
gets crowded out by the assessment. Have dedicated career conversations, framed clearly as about
their future and growth — a space to think about where they're headed, not to be judged.

### "Where do you want to be in ~3 years?" — and "I don't know" is fine

A core career-conversation question: **"where do you want to be in a few years — what kind of work,
what role, what growth?"** It surfaces their aspirations so you can support them. But crucially, **"I
don't know" is a completely valid answer** — many people don't have a clear plan, and pressuring them
to produce one is counterproductive. If they don't know, the conversation becomes exploratory: what do
they enjoy? what energizes them? what do they want to try? You're helping them figure it out, not
demanding a five-year plan. The goal is understanding enough to bend their work in a helpful direction,
not forcing certainty.

### Bend their work toward their goals

Once you know (even roughly) where someone wants to go, **bend their assignments toward it** — give them
work that develops the skills and visibility for their aspiration (Lesson 31's growth assignments,
aimed at their goals). Someone who wants to go deep technical gets stretch technical work; someone
curious about leadership gets a chance to lead something; someone wanting a new domain gets a project in
it. Connecting their day-to-day work to their aspirations is powerfully motivating and developmental —
it's how you help them actually grow toward where they want to go, not just do tickets.

### Legible promotion criteria and the year-long packet

For promotions, two things make them fair and achievable: (1) **legible criteria** — the person should
know clearly what's required for the next level (the ladder/expectations), so it's not a mystery;
vague, unknowable criteria are unfair and demotivating. (2) **The promotion packet you build all
year** — a promotion (at most companies) requires evidence of operating at the next level, gathered
over time; a good lead is building that case throughout the year (noting the person's next-level work,
giving them opportunities to demonstrate it, documenting impact) — not scrambling at review time.
Promotion is a year-long project of growing the person into the level and evidencing it, which you
steward.

### The not-promoted conversation

The hardest version: someone expects a promotion that isn't happening (they're not ready, or it's not
this cycle). Handle it honestly and constructively — like the performance conversation (Lesson 46 of
English track / the feedback lessons): (1) **be honest and clear** (don't be vague or give false hope —
they need to know where they really stand); (2) **be specific about the gap** (what, concretely, is
missing for the next level — behavioral, not vague); (3) **be caring and forward-looking** (frame it as
"here's the path to get there," not just "no"); and (4) **give a concrete plan** (what they need to
demonstrate, by when, with your support). Done well, it's disappointing but fair and motivating (a clear
path); done badly (vague, false hope, or crushing), it demoralizes or loses the person.

{: .note }
> **Know where people want to go and bend their work toward it — and handle promotions honestly</br>**
> To develop people, you must know their aspirations — which requires forward-looking career
> conversations (distinct from backward-looking performance reviews) that ask "where do you want to go?"
> (and accept "I don't know" as valid, becoming exploratory). Then bend their work toward their goals
> (growth assignments aimed at where they want to be) — connecting daily work to aspirations, which is
> deeply motivating and developmental. For promotions, make the criteria legible (so people know what's
> required) and build the evidence packet all year (promotion is a year-long project you steward, not a
> review-time scramble). And handle the hard not-promoted conversation honestly, specifically, caringly,
> and with a concrete forward path — so it's fair and motivating rather than vague, false-hope, or
> crushing. Knowing and supporting where each person wants to go is central to genuinely developing them.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** One of your engineers, Tom, is expecting a promotion to senior this cycle. He's a
good engineer and has been working hard, and he clearly believes it's coming. But honestly, he's not
there yet — his technical work is solid, but he's not yet demonstrating the broader scope, the
independent judgment on ambiguous problems, and the influence on the team that "senior" requires at your
company. The promotion isn't happening this cycle. You need to have the conversation — and give him a
real path.

**Write how you'd have the not-promoted conversation, and outline the 6-month plan that follows.** Then
note the principles and mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong not-promoted conversation is honest and clear (no false hope), specific about the gap, caring
and forward-looking, and paired with a concrete plan. Example:
<br><br>
<strong>The conversation:</strong> "Tom, I want to talk about your promotion, and I want to be honest
with you because I respect you and I'm invested in you getting there. <strong>The promotion to senior
isn't going to happen this cycle.</strong> I know that's disappointing, and I don't say it lightly — you're
a good engineer and you've worked hard, and I want to be clear this is 'not yet,' not 'no.' <em>(Honest
and clear up front — no vagueness or false hope — while caring and framing it as 'not yet.')</em>
<br><br>
Let me be specific about why, because you deserve to know exactly where the gap is. Your technical
execution is genuinely strong — that's not the issue. Senior at our company is about more than strong
execution: it's about <strong>broader scope</strong> (owning larger, more ambiguous problems
end-to-end), <strong>independent judgment</strong> (navigating ambiguity and making the calls yourself,
not checking in for direction), and <strong>influence on the team</strong> (making others better,
driving technical decisions, being someone the team looks to). Those are the areas where I'd want to see
more before I could make a strong case. <em>(Specific, behavioral gap — the concrete things missing for
the next level, not a vague 'you're not ready.')</em>
<br><br>
And here's the thing — <strong>these are all things I think you can develop, and I want to help you get
there.</strong> Can we build a plan together for the next six months to close that gap deliberately?"
<em>(Caring, forward-looking, collaborative — turns it toward a path.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>The 6-month plan:</strong> (1) <strong>Scope</strong> — give Tom a larger, more ambiguous project
to own end-to-end (a stretch assignment aimed at demonstrating senior scope), with support. (2)
<strong>Independent judgment</strong> — deliberately step back and let him make the calls on ambiguous
things (coaching, not directing — Lesson 26), so he practices and demonstrates independent judgment. (3)
<strong>Influence</strong> — create opportunities for him to influence the team: mentor a junior, drive a
technical decision, present a design. (4) <strong>Checkpoints</strong> — regular check-ins on progress
against these specific areas, with honest feedback, so he knows how he's tracking. (5) <strong>Build the
packet</strong> — document the next-level work as he does it, so there's evidence for next cycle. (6)
<strong>Clear target</strong> — "if you demonstrate these things over the next six months, I'll be able to
make a strong case next cycle" (honest — a strong case, not a guarantee).
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Honest and clear, no false hope</strong> — he needs to know
where he really stands; vagueness or "maybe" would be unkind and unfair. (2) <strong>Specific,
behavioral gap</strong> — exactly what's missing (scope, judgment, influence), not "you're not ready,"
so he knows what to work on. (3) <strong>Caring and forward-looking</strong> — "not yet, not no," "I want
to help you get there" — so it's disappointing but motivating, not crushing. (4) <strong>Concrete plan
with a real path</strong> — specific work aimed at the gaps, checkpoints, and a clear (honest) target. (5)
<strong>Legible criteria</strong> — he now clearly understands what senior requires (which he apparently
didn't, since he expected it). <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>vagueness / false hope</strong>
("it's close, maybe next time," "just keep doing what you're doing") — leaves him not knowing where he
stands or what to fix, and sets up worse disappointment later; (2) <strong>a vague gap</strong> ("you're
not senior yet" without specifics) — he can't act on it; (3) <strong>crushing delivery</strong> (blunt,
no care, no path) — demoralizing, may lose him; (4) <strong>no plan</strong> — leaving him with "no" and
no path forward (hopeless); (5) <strong>over-promising</strong> ("do this and you're definitely promoted")
— dishonest if you can't guarantee it; be honest ("a strong case"); (6) <strong>the surprise itself</strong>
— ideally he shouldn't be blindsided; if he expected it and it's not close, that's a sign of earlier
communication failure (the criteria weren't legible, or feedback wasn't given) — a lesson to give ongoing,
honest feedback so promotion readiness isn't a surprise.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why should career conversations be separate from performance reviews, and why is "I don't know"
a valid answer to "where do you want to go?"

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Career conversations should be separate from performance reviews because <strong>they serve different
purposes and mixing them undermines both — a performance review is backward-looking evaluation (which
makes people defensive), while a career conversation is forward-looking exploration of aspirations
(which requires openness)</strong>. A performance review assesses how the person did against
expectations — it's evaluative and backward-looking, and inherently carries some judgment (ratings,
whether they met the bar). A career conversation is about the person's future — where they want to go,
what they want to grow into, how you can help them get there — which is forward-looking and
developmental, not evaluative. Conflating them fails both: (1) <strong>the evaluation makes people
defensive, closing off the career exploration</strong> — when someone is being judged (review mode),
they're guarded and self-protective, which is the opposite of the open, reflective state needed to
honestly explore aspirations (you can't candidly discuss "where do I want to go, what am I unsure about"
while being assessed); (2) <strong>the assessment crowds out the career thinking</strong> — if the
conversation is dominated by the evaluation (the rating, the performance), the forward-looking career
development gets squeezed out or feels secondary; and (3) <strong>the person can't be open about
aspirations tied to leaving or changing</strong> — some career aspirations (wanting a different team,
role, or eventually to move on) are hard to raise when the same conversation determines your rating/pay.
So separating them lets each do its job: the performance review evaluates (honestly, with its own
focus), and the career conversation explores the future (openly, developmentally) — a dedicated space,
clearly framed as about their growth and future (not their assessment), where the person can think
freely about where they're headed. Why "I don't know" is a valid answer to "where do you want to go?":
<strong>because many people genuinely don't have a clear career plan, and pressuring them to produce one
is counterproductive — the goal is to understand and help, not to demand certainty</strong>. Several
reasons: (1) <strong>most people don't have a clear multi-year plan</strong> — career direction is often
genuinely uncertain (people are still figuring out what they enjoy, what they're good at, what they
want), and expecting everyone to have a confident "here's my 5-year plan" is unrealistic; "I don't know"
is honest and common, not a failure. (2) <strong>pressuring for a plan is counterproductive</strong> —
demanding a definite answer when they don't have one creates pressure and can produce a fake plan (they
make something up to satisfy you), which helps no one; it also can feel judgmental (like they <em>should</em>
know), which is discouraging. (3) <strong>"I don't know" makes the conversation exploratory, which is
valuable</strong> — instead of a dead end, "I don't know" opens a genuinely useful exploration: what do
you enjoy? what energizes you? what would you like to try? what are you curious about? — helping the
person <em>figure out</em> their direction (which is exactly what someone who doesn't know needs), rather
than extracting a predetermined answer. (4) <strong>the goal is enough understanding to help, not a
committed plan</strong> — you don't need the person to have a certain five-year plan; you need enough
sense of what they enjoy and want to grow toward to bend their work helpfully, which exploration provides
even without a definite goal. So accepting "I don't know" (and making it exploratory) is both realistic
(most people are uncertain) and more helpful (you help them explore rather than pressuring for false
certainty). The underlying point: career conversations are about genuinely understanding and supporting
where the person wants to grow — which requires a separate, non-evaluative, open space (hence separate
from reviews), and a genuine curiosity that accepts uncertainty and helps explore it (hence "I don't
know" is fine). Both make the conversation what it should be: a supportive exploration of the person's
future, not an evaluation or an interrogation for a plan they may not have.
</details>

**Q2.** Why does "bending work toward people's goals" develop them, and why do legible promotion
criteria and building the packet all year matter?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"Bending work toward people's goals" develops them because <strong>people grow best when their work
connects to their aspirations — it develops the specific skills and visibility they need for where they
want to go, and it's deeply motivating, both of which accelerate their growth</strong>. Once you know
(even roughly) where someone wants to go, giving them work aimed at that direction — technical stretch
work for someone wanting to go deep technical, a leadership opportunity for someone curious about
leading, a project in a new domain for someone wanting to explore it — does two powerful things: (1)
<strong>it builds the relevant skills and visibility</strong> — the work develops exactly the
capabilities and demonstrates exactly the abilities needed for their aspiration (not generic growth, but
growth toward <em>their</em> goal), so it directly moves them toward where they want to be; and (2)
<strong>it's highly motivating</strong> — when someone sees their daily work connecting to their own
aspirations (not just doing tickets, but building toward something they care about), they're far more
engaged and driven, which itself accelerates growth (motivated people grow faster). This is more
effective than generic assignments because it's targeted (developing what they specifically need) and
energizing (connected to what they want) — turning the work into a vehicle for their growth toward their
goals rather than just task completion. It also shows the person you know and care about their
aspirations (you're investing in <em>their</em> future), which builds trust and loyalty. So bending work
toward goals develops people by making the work itself grow them in the direction they want to go, with
the motivation that comes from that connection. Why <strong>legible promotion criteria</strong> matter:
<strong>because people can only work toward a goal they can see — clear, knowable criteria let them
understand and pursue what's required, while vague or mysterious criteria are unfair and demotivating</strong>.
If the requirements for the next level are legible (the person clearly knows what's expected — the
ladder, the specific behaviors and impact required), they can (a) work deliberately toward them (aim
their growth at the actual criteria), (b) assess their own readiness honestly, and (c) trust the process
is fair. If the criteria are vague or unknowable ("we'll know it when we see it," undefined
expectations), the person is left guessing — they can't work toward an unclear target, they may believe
they're ready when they're not (or vice versa), and the process feels arbitrary and unfair (promotions
seem to happen by favoritism or mystery rather than clear merit), which is demotivating and erodes trust.
Legible criteria make promotion a fair, achievable goal rather than a black box. Why <strong>building the
packet all year</strong> matters: <strong>because promotion (at most companies) requires evidence of
operating at the next level, gathered over time — so a good lead builds that case throughout the year
rather than scrambling at review time</strong>. A promotion case needs demonstrated evidence that the
person is already operating at the next level (not just potential) — which accumulates over months of
next-level work. If the lead builds this all year — noting the person's next-level contributions, giving
them opportunities to demonstrate the required abilities, documenting their impact — then at promotion
time there's a strong, well-evidenced case ready. If the lead <em>doesn't</em> (scrambling to assemble a
case at review time), they'll (a) lack the evidence (opportunities to demonstrate next-level work weren't
created, impact wasn't documented), so the case is weak even if the person is deserving, and (b) fail the
person (a deserving person may miss promotion because their lead didn't build the case). So building the
packet all year is how a lead actively stewards someone's promotion: growing them into the level
(creating the next-level opportunities) and evidencing it (documenting the impact) over time, so the case
is strong when the cycle comes. It reframes promotion as a <em>year-long project the lead stewards</em>
(deliberately developing the person toward the level and building the evidence), not a review-time event
— which is what actually gets deserving people promoted. Together, these make promotion fair and
achievable: legible criteria let people know and work toward what's required, and building the packet all
year ensures the lead actively develops and evidences their people's readiness — versus a mysterious,
scramble-at-the-end process that fails deserving people. Both reflect the lead's role in genuinely
developing people toward their goals: know where they want to go, bend their work toward it, make the path
(criteria) clear, and actively steward their advancement (the packet) — rather than leaving growth and
promotion to chance or mystery.
</details>

## Homework

Invest in knowing and supporting where your people want to go. (1) Have a career conversation (separate
from any review) with someone — ask where they want to grow, what energizes them, what they'd like to try
(and accept "I don't know" as a starting point for exploration). (2) For each person, ask: do you know
their aspirations, and is their current work bending toward them? Identify one person whose work you could
better aim at their goals. (3) Are your team's promotion criteria legible to people? Are you building
evidence for deserving people all year, or scrambling at review time? (4) If you have a not-promoted or
hard career conversation coming, plan it (honest, specific gap, caring, concrete path). Reflect: how well
do you know where each person wants to go, and whose growth are you not actively stewarding?

**Your answer:**

<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds career-development conversations and stewarding people's growth toward their goals. A
strong response has a real career conversation (forward-looking, accepting "I don't know" as exploratory),
checks whether they know each person's aspirations and are bending work toward them, examines promotion-
criteria legibility and packet-building, and plans any hard career conversation. The realizations: (1)
<strong>career conversations ≠ performance reviews</strong> — the forward-looking exploration of
aspirations needs a separate, non-evaluative space (evaluation makes people defensive, closing off the
openness career-thinking requires); (2) <strong>"I don't know" is valid and exploratory</strong> — most

work to aspirations develops the relevant skills and is deeply motivating, accelerating growth toward
where they want to go; (4) <strong>legible criteria + year-long packet</strong> — clear promotion
requirements let people work toward them (vague ones are unfair and demotivating), and building the

aimed at their goals, and that they're not systematically building promotion evidence. The highest-value
change is usually: have genuine career conversations to learn where people want to go, then bend their
work toward it and steward their advancement (legible criteria, year-long packet). The meta-point:
developing people well requires knowing where each wants to go (via forward-looking career conversations
distinct from reviews) and bending their work toward it — which is motivating and developmental — plus
making promotion fair and achievable (legible criteria, building the packet all year) and handling hard
career conversations honestly and constructively. Knowing and supporting people's aspirations is central
