# Lesson 44: Negotiation and Expectation Management

## Concept

As a lead, you **negotiate constantly** — scope, timelines, staffing, priorities — with product,
leadership, other teams. It's now a core part of the job, and doing it well (rather than just
accepting whatever's demanded, or flatly refusing) determines whether your team is set up to succeed
or set up to fail. The key frameworks: **interests vs positions** (again — it's everywhere), the **iron
triangle** (scope/time/people — you can't fix all three), and the discipline of **renegotiating early**
rather than apologizing late.

```
   THE IRON TRIANGLE (pick your constraints)
              SCOPE
             /      \
            /        \
        TIME ──────── PEOPLE
   You can't fix all three independently. Want more scope?
   → more time or more people. Fix time AND scope AND people?
   → something gives (usually quality, or it just doesn't happen).

   NEGOTIATE with a TRADE-OFF MENU, not a flat yes or no.
   RENEGOTIATE EARLY (when you see the problem) > apologize LATE.
```

The reframe: **negotiate with a trade-off menu grounded in the iron triangle — and renegotiate early
rather than apologizing late.** When asked for the impossible (full scope, fixed date, no more people),
the answer isn't a doomed "yes" (over-committing, then failing) or a flat "no" (unhelpful) — it's a
**negotiation**: "here are the trade-offs; which do you want?" And when reality diverges from the plan,
raising it *early* (renegotiating the expectation) is far better than staying quiet and apologizing
when you miss.

---

## How It Works

### Interests vs positions — again

The interests-vs-positions frame (Lesson 37) is central to negotiation: people state **positions**
("we need it by the conference, full scope"), but behind them are **interests** (why — "we need
something impressive to show," "the CEO promised it"). Negotiating at the level of positions leads to
win/lose standoffs; digging to interests opens solutions. If leadership's interest is "something
impressive at the conference," maybe a polished subset of the feature (not the full scope) serves that
interest — a solution invisible at the position level ("full scope by the conference"). Always ask what
the person really needs (interest) behind what they're demanding (position) — the negotiation resolves
in the interests.

### The iron triangle — scope, time, people (pick two-ish)

The fundamental constraint of any project: **scope, time, and people (resources) are linked — you can't
independently fix all three.** More scope needs more time or more people; a fixed time and fixed people
caps the scope; wanting more of everything means something gives (usually quality suffers, or it just
doesn't happen). This is the "iron triangle" (or "pick two"). It's a powerful negotiation tool because
it makes the trade-offs explicit and undeniable: when someone demands full scope, fixed date, and no new
people, you can show that's over-constrained — one of them <em>must</em> flex. The iron-triangle
conversation ("we can have any two of these — which two?") turns an impossible demand into a real
choice.

### Negotiate with a trade-off menu, not yes/no

When asked for something that's over-constrained, don't answer with a flat **yes** (over-committing to
the impossible, then failing — the worst outcome) or a flat **no** (unhelpful, adversarial). Negotiate
with a **trade-off menu**: "Here's what's possible — (A) full scope, but we'd need 3 more weeks; (B) the
date, but with reduced scope [specifically X]; (C) the date and full scope, but we'd need 2 more
engineers. Which trade-off works for you?" This: (1) respects the constraint (you're not promising the
impossible), (2) gives them agency (they choose the trade-off that fits their real priority), and (3)
makes you a partner solving the problem (not a blocker or an over-promiser). The trade-off menu turns
"can you do the impossible?" into "which realistic option do you want?"

### BATNA and under-promise calibration

Two supporting concepts: (1) **BATNA** (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement — from *Getting to
Yes*): know your walk-away/alternative, which grounds your negotiating position (e.g., "if we don't get
more people, the alternative is we cut scope — that's my BATNA, so I won't agree to full scope on the
original date and people"). (2) **Under-promise calibration** — calibrate commitments so you can
reliably deliver (better to under-promise and over-deliver than the reverse), building a track record of
reliability (credibility, Lesson 35). Chronic over-promising (agreeing to everything, missing) destroys
credibility; calibrated commitments you keep build it.

### Renegotiate early beats apologizing late

A crucial discipline: when you see that the plan won't work (scope's too big, the date's at risk, a
dependency slipped), **renegotiate the expectation early** — don't stay quiet and hope, then apologize
when you miss. Early renegotiation ("I'm seeing a risk to the date — let's talk about scope or timeline
now") lets everyone adjust while there's still room (replan, cut scope, move the date, add people) and
maintains trust (you flagged it responsibly). Staying quiet and apologizing late (missing the date, then
"sorry") is far worse: everyone's blindsided (no time to adjust), the outcome is worse, and your
credibility is damaged. Renegotiating early is responsible expectation management; late apology is a
failure of it. (This connects to no-surprises, Lesson 41, and delivering bad news early.)

{: .note }
> **Negotiate with a trade-off menu (iron triangle) — and renegotiate early, don't apologize late</br>**
> A lead negotiates constantly (scope, time, staffing), and doing it well sets the team up to succeed. Use
> <em>interests vs positions</em> (dig to what they really need behind what they demand — the resolution is
> in the interests). Use the <em>iron triangle</em> (scope/time/people are linked — you can't fix all three;
> one must flex) to make trade-offs explicit. When asked for the over-constrained, negotiate with a
> <em>trade-off menu</em> ("here's what's possible — which trade-off do you want?") rather than a doomed
> yes (over-commit, then fail) or a flat no (unhelpful). Know your <em>BATNA</em>, and calibrate commitments
> to under-promise/over-deliver (building credibility). Above all, <em>renegotiate early</em> when the plan
> won't work (so everyone can adjust) rather than staying quiet and apologizing late (blindsiding everyone).
> Negotiating scope, time, and staffing well — it's your job now — is how you avoid being set up to fail.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** Leadership wants a significant feature delivered **by an industry conference in 8
weeks** — they want the **full scope** (everything in the original vision), by that **fixed date**, with
**no new people** (your current team). You've assessed it honestly: full scope in 8 weeks with the
current team is not realistic — it's probably a 12-week effort at full scope, or 8 weeks at reduced
scope. Just saying "yes" means over-committing and missing (a disaster at a public conference); just
saying "no" is unhelpful. You need to negotiate.

**Run the negotiation** — including the trade-off menu you'd bring. Then note the principles and mistakes
to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong negotiation digs to the interest, presents the iron-triangle trade-offs as a menu, and helps
leadership choose — rather than over-promising or refusing. Example:
<br><br>
<strong>First — understand the real interest:</strong> "Before the trade-offs, understand what leadership
actually needs at the conference (the interest behind 'full scope by the conference'). Is it something
impressive to demo? A specific capability to announce? A complete product? The real need shapes the best
option — often 'something impressive to show' can be met by a polished subset, not the full scope."
<em>(Interests vs positions — the real need may not require full scope.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Present the iron-triangle reality and a trade-off menu:</strong> "Here's the honest situation:
full scope, in 8 weeks, with the current team isn't realistic — I want to be straight about that rather
than promise it and miss at the conference, which would be far worse. But here's what <em>is</em> possible
— a few options: <br>
• <strong>Option A — hit the conference date with a focused scope</strong>: we deliver a polished, demo-able
version with [the core, impressive capabilities] by the conference, and fast-follow the rest after. Great
for a strong conference demo. <br>
• <strong>Option B — full scope, but ~4 more weeks</strong>: if full scope is essential, we'd need until
[date] — we could show a preview at the conference and launch complete shortly after. <br>
• <strong>Option C — full scope by the conference, with 2 more engineers</strong>: if both the date and
full scope are fixed, we'd need more people — can we get 2 engineers, or borrow from another team? <br>
Which of these best fits what you need for the conference?" <em>(The trade-off menu — makes the iron
triangle explicit and gives leadership a real choice among realistic options, rather than yes/no.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Recommend, based on the interest:</strong> "My recommendation, if the goal is a strong conference
presence, is Option A — a polished focused demo lands better than a rushed full-scope thing that might be
buggy on stage, and we fast-follow the rest. But if full scope is genuinely essential for the announcement,
let's talk B or C." <em>(A recommendation tied to their likely real interest.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>If they still say 'all three, no trade-off':</strong> "If leadership insists on full scope, the
date, and no new people, I'd be honest: 'I want to be a reliable partner, so I have to tell you that's not
achievable with quality — if we try, we risk a buggy demo at the conference, which is the worst outcome.
I'd strongly recommend we pick a trade-off. If we truly must attempt all three, I need us to jointly
acknowledge the quality risk and the likely miss.'" <em>(Hold the line honestly rather than
over-committing to the impossible — under-promise calibration and no false yes.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Dig to the interest</strong> — what leadership really needs at the
conference (often a polished demo, not literally full scope). (2) <strong>Iron triangle explicit</strong> —
show that full scope + fixed date + no people is over-constrained; one must flex. (3) <strong>Trade-off menu,
not yes/no</strong> — present realistic options (A/B/C) and let leadership choose the trade-off that fits
their priority. (4) <strong>Recommend based on the interest</strong> — guide them toward the option that
best serves their real goal. (5) <strong>Don't over-promise</strong> — no false 'yes' to the impossible
(which risks a public failure); under-promise and deliver reliably. (6) <strong>Honest about the risk</strong>
— if they insist on all three, name the quality risk rather than silently accepting a doomed commitment.
<strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>saying yes to the impossible</strong> — the worst outcome
(over-commit, then a buggy/missed demo at a public conference, and destroyed credibility); (2) <strong>flat
no</strong> — unhelpful and adversarial (vs. a constructive trade-off menu); (3) <strong>not digging to the
interest</strong> — negotiating 'full scope' as a fixed position when a polished subset might serve the real
need; (4) <strong>no trade-off menu</strong> — just arguing about feasibility rather than presenting real
options to choose among; (5) <strong>caving under pressure</strong> to a doomed 'yes' (the pressure to
please leadership is exactly when calibrated honesty matters most); (6) <strong>not being honest about the
risk</strong> if they insist — silently accepting an impossible commitment sets everyone up for the public
failure; (7) <strong>waiting to renegotiate</strong> — if you later see it slipping, renegotiate then too
(early), don't wait to apologize at the conference.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What is the "iron triangle," and why negotiate with a "trade-off menu" rather than a flat yes or
no?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The <strong>iron triangle</strong> is the fundamental project constraint that <strong>scope, time, and
people (resources) are linked — you can't independently fix all three; changing one forces a change in
another</strong>. More scope requires more time or more people; a fixed time with fixed people caps the
scope; wanting more of everything means something gives (usually quality suffers, or the project simply
doesn't get done as demanded). It's sometimes called "pick two" — you can fix any two of scope/time/people,
but the third must flex to accommodate them. The iron triangle reflects a real, unavoidable truth about
work: output is bounded by the resources and time available, so you can't get arbitrary scope in arbitrary
time with arbitrary people — the three are in tension. This makes it a powerful negotiation tool because it
<strong>makes the trade-offs explicit and undeniable</strong>: when someone demands full scope, a fixed
date, <em>and</em> no additional people, the iron triangle shows that demand is over-constrained (fixing all
three is impossible without something giving — usually quality, or a missed deadline), so one of them
<em>must</em> flex. Instead of arguing about whether it's possible, you can frame it as "we can have any two
of these — which two?" — turning an impossible demand into a real, structured choice about which constraint
to relax. Why negotiate with a <strong>trade-off menu</strong> rather than a flat yes or no: <strong>because
a flat "yes" over-commits to the impossible (leading to failure), a flat "no" is unhelpful and adversarial,
while a trade-off menu respects the constraint, gives the other party agency, and makes you a partner
solving the problem</strong>. When asked for something over-constrained (full scope, fixed date, no more
people), the two flat answers both fail: (1) <strong>a flat "yes"</strong> is the worst outcome — you
over-commit to something that can't be done, then miss (delivering late, or buggy, or incomplete), which is
a failure that blindsides everyone, produces a bad result (e.g., a broken demo at a public conference), and
destroys your credibility (you promised and failed); saying yes to the impossible to please people in the
moment sets up a much worse outcome later. (2) <strong>a flat "no"</strong> is unhelpful and adversarial —
it just refuses without offering a way forward, positions you as a blocker, and doesn't solve the person's
real need (they still need <em>something</em>). A <strong>trade-off menu</strong> — "here's what's possible:
(A) full scope, +3 weeks; (B) the date, with reduced scope [specifically X]; (C) the date and full scope,
with 2 more engineers — which works for you?" — is far better because it: (1) <strong>respects the
constraint</strong> — you're not promising the impossible (each option is achievable), so you won't
over-commit and fail; (2) <strong>gives the other party agency</strong> — they choose the trade-off that
fits <em>their</em> real priority (if the date matters most, they pick reduced scope; if scope matters most,
they pick more time or people), so the decision reflects what they actually value; and (3) <strong>makes you
a partner solving the problem</strong> — you're not a blocker (flat no) or an over-promiser (flat yes) but
someone presenting real options and helping them get the best achievable outcome, which is constructive and
builds trust. The trade-off menu turns "can you do the impossible?" into "which of these realistic options
do you want?" — a productive negotiation rather than a doomed commitment or an unhelpful refusal. It's the
practical application of the iron triangle: since you can't fix all three constraints, present the real
options (each fixing a different two) and let the other party choose based on their priorities. Together,
the iron triangle (the constraint that makes trade-offs real) and the trade-off menu (the way to negotiate
them) let a lead handle over-constrained demands honestly and constructively — avoiding both the false "yes"
that leads to failure and the unhelpful "no," and instead reaching a realistic commitment the team can
actually deliver (setting the team up to succeed rather than to fail).
</details>

**Q2.** Why does "renegotiating early beat apologizing late," and why is calibrating commitments to
under-promise/over-deliver valuable?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"Renegotiating early beats apologizing late" because <strong>raising a problem while there's still time to
adjust lets everyone respond well and maintains trust, whereas staying quiet and apologizing after you miss
blindsides everyone, produces a worse outcome, and damages credibility</strong>. When you see that the plan
won't work (scope too big, date at risk, a dependency slipped), you have two choices: (1) <strong>renegotiate
early</strong> — flag it as soon as you see it ("I'm seeing a risk to the date — let's talk about scope or
timeline now"), which lets everyone adjust while there's still room: replan, cut scope, move the date, add
people, reset stakeholder expectations — so the situation is handled proactively, the outcome is optimized
given reality, and trust is maintained (you flagged it responsibly, gave people time, acted like a reliable
partner). (2) <strong>stay quiet and apologize late</strong> — hope it works out, say nothing, then miss the
date/scope and apologize ("sorry, we didn't make it") — which is far worse: everyone is <em>blindsided</em>
(they built plans on a commitment that silently failed, with no time to adjust — the conference happens with
no demo, the dependent teams are stuck), the <em>outcome is worse</em> (no chance to mitigate — cut scope,
move the date, get help — because it's now too late), and your <em>credibility is damaged</em> (you knew or
should have known and didn't say, so you can't be trusted to flag problems — the trust cost of Lesson 41's
no-surprises). So early renegotiation is responsible expectation management (proactive, gives everyone room,
maintains trust), while late apology is a failure of it (reactive, blindsides everyone, worse outcome,
damaged trust). The discipline is to renegotiate the expectation the moment you see it won't hold, rather
than staying quiet and hoping — because the problem doesn't go away by ignoring it; it just surfaces later at
a worse time with less room to respond. This connects to no-surprises (Lesson 41) and delivering bad news
early (English Lesson 47): flag problems early, when they can still be managed. Why calibrating commitments
to <strong>under-promise/over-deliver</strong> is valuable: <strong>because reliably delivering what you
committed builds credibility (a track record of trustworthiness), while chronic over-promising and missing
destroys it — and credibility is a core source of your influence and effectiveness</strong>. Calibration
means setting commitments you can reliably meet (erring toward under-promising rather than over-promising),
so that you consistently deliver on (or exceed) what you said. The value: (1) <strong>it builds
credibility</strong> — a track record of "says X, delivers X (or better)" makes you trusted and reliable,
which is a major source of influence (Lesson 35 — people trust and are influenced by those with a track
record of delivery) and makes your future commitments believed; (2) <strong>under-promise/over-deliver is
better than the reverse</strong> — delivering more than promised is a pleasant surprise that builds trust,
while delivering less than promised (over-promise/under-deliver) is a disappointment that erodes it, even if
the absolute output is the same — so the same work calibrated as under-promise lands as reliability, and
calibrated as over-promise lands as failure; (3) <strong>chronic over-promising destroys credibility</strong>
— agreeing to everything (to please people in the moment) and then missing repeatedly makes you unreliable
(your commitments mean nothing), which is deeply damaging (people can't plan around you, stop trusting your
estimates, and your influence erodes). So calibrating to under-promise/over-deliver is how you build the
reliability and credibility that make you trusted and effective — a track record of kept commitments — rather
than the over-promise/miss pattern that destroys it. Both points reflect responsible expectation management:
<strong>manage expectations proactively and honestly — renegotiate early when reality diverges (so everyone
adjusts and trust holds), and calibrate commitments to what you can reliably deliver (building credibility)</strong>
— rather than over-committing and then apologizing (which blindsides people and destroys trust). For a lead,
whose credibility and reliability are central to their influence and their team's success, managing
expectations well (early renegotiation, calibrated commitments) is core to being trusted and to setting the
team up to succeed rather than to fail.
</details>
