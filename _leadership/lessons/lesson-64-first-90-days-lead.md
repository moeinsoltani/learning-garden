# Lesson 64: Your First 90 Days as a Lead

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **the first 90 days** — the standard framing (from Watkins's book) for a role transition's critical opening period.
> - **listen-first period** — roughly 30 days of learning and no big changes.
> - **Chesterton's fence** — don't remove a fence until you know why it was put there; don't change what you don't yet understand.
> - **build your map** — learning the people, systems, and history before acting.
> - **"where the bodies are buried"** — idiom: the hidden problems, old wounds, and political sensitivities nobody mentions.
> - **landmine** — a hidden issue that explodes when accidentally stepped on.
> - **early win** — a small visible improvement that earns trust without imposing.
> - **stepping on toes** — intruding on someone else's territory or authority.
> - **remit** (REE-mit) — the area you're officially responsible for.
> - **renegotiate your IC workload** — explicitly agreeing with your manager how much individual work you drop to make room for leading.

## Concept

Becoming a tech lead deliberately — not just "a senior with extra meetings" — starts with how you spend your
first 90 days. The temptation is to charge in and make changes (prove yourself, fix what you see); the
wisdom is to **listen first, build your map, and earn trust before changing things.** The classic guidance:
a **listen-first period** (roughly 30 days of no big changes), building your understanding of the people,
systems, and history, then early wins that build trust without stepping on toes.

```
   YOUR FIRST 90 DAYS AS A LEAD
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ • LISTEN FIRST (~30 days, no big changes) — understand  │
   │   before you act                                       │
   │ • BUILD YOUR MAP: people, systems, where the bodies are │
   │ • EARLY WINS that build trust (without stepping on toes)│
   │ • RENEGOTIATE your IC workload EXPLICITLY (you can't do │
   │   both jobs fully)                                      │
   │ • BUILD RELATIONSHIPS in week one                       │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The reframe: **listen and build understanding before you change things — earn trust and learn the context
first, rather than charging in.** New leads often rush to make changes (to prove value or fix what they
see), but this backfires: you don't yet understand the context (why things are the way they are), you
haven't earned trust, and you risk breaking things and alienating people. Listening first (understanding the
people, systems, and history), then earning trust with early wins, sets you up far better than a change-first
charge.

---

## How It Works

### Listen first — the ~30-day no-big-changes period

The most important early discipline: **listen and learn before making big changes** — a rough guideline is
no major changes for the first ~30 days. Why: (1) **you don't yet understand the context** — things are
usually the way they are for reasons (some good, some bad), and changing them before understanding why
breaks things you didn't know mattered (Chesterton's fence — don't remove a fence until you know why it's
there); (2) **you haven't earned trust** — charging in with changes before you've earned credibility makes
people defensive and resistant; (3) **listening builds understanding and relationships** — spending the
early period understanding (asking questions, learning) is how you build both the knowledge to change wisely
and the relationships to change successfully. So resist the urge to prove yourself with immediate changes;
invest the first weeks in listening and learning. (Exceptions: a genuine emergency needs action — but the
default is listen-first.)

### Build your map — people, systems, where the bodies are buried

Use the listening period to **build your map**: (1) **people** — who's on the team, their strengths,
aspirations, concerns, dynamics (1:1s with everyone); (2) **systems** — how things work technically (the
architecture, the codebase, the tooling); (3) **the history and the "bodies"** — why things are the way they
are, past decisions, known problems, "where the bodies are buried" (the landmines, the tech debt, the
political sensitivities, the things people don't talk about). This map is what lets you lead well —
understanding the people, the systems, and the context so you can make good decisions and avoid landmines.
Build it deliberately in the early days (it's why the listen-first period is so valuable).

### Early wins that build trust — without stepping on toes

While listening, look for **early wins** — small, visible improvements that build trust and credibility —
but chosen carefully to **not step on toes**: (1) address something the team clearly wants fixed (a pain
point they've been asking about — you're helping, not imposing); (2) something within your clear remit (not
overriding others); (3) something that demonstrates you're here to help, not to disrupt. Good early wins
(fixing an annoyance the team wanted fixed, removing a blocker, a quick helpful improvement) build trust and
show value; bad "early wins" (imposing big changes, overriding people, fixing things that weren't broken to
show off) alienate people. Early wins earn credibility, which you then spend on bigger changes later.

### Renegotiate your IC workload explicitly

A crucial and often-botched transition: **you can't fully do both the IC job and the lead job — so
renegotiate your IC workload explicitly.** New leads often try to keep doing all their old individual work
AND the new lead work, which leads to burnout and doing both badly (the lead work — the higher-leverage part
— often suffers because it's less urgent). Explicitly renegotiate (with your manager) what your IC
contribution will be now (usually significantly reduced), so you have capacity for the lead work. Make this
explicit (don't just silently try to do everything) — the transition to lead means shifting from doing the
work to enabling the team's work (Phase 1), and that requires actually reducing your IC load, agreed with
your manager.

### The relationships to build in week one

Prioritize **building key relationships early** — in week one: (1) **your team** (1:1s with each person —
understand them, start building trust); (2) **your manager** (align on expectations, what success looks
like, how you'll work together — Lesson 41); (3) **peers and key stakeholders** (other leads, the PM, teams
you depend on — the relationships you'll need, built before you need them — Lesson 35). Investing early in
relationships (especially the team and your manager) is high-leverage — these are the relationships through
which you'll lead, so building them from the start (with genuine interest and trust-building) sets you up
well. Relationships are the foundation of leadership, so build them from week one.

{: .note }
> **Start deliberately: listen first, build your map, earn trust — don't charge in with changes</br>**

<em>(Now leading with earned trust and understanding — bigger changes, my leadership rhythm.)</em>

not changing. (2) <strong>Specific and concrete</strong> — real names (who you'll 1:1), real systems (what
you'll learn), real risks (the bodies you know about) — not generic. (3) <strong>Map-building</strong> —
deliberately understanding people, systems, and history. (4) <strong>IC-load renegotiation</strong> — explicit,

relationship-building</strong> (the foundation); (5) <strong>early "wins" that step on toes</strong> (imposing,
overriding, showing off — alienating); (6) <strong>not building the map</strong> (leading without understanding
the context/bodies — hitting landmines); (7) <strong>never moving past listening</strong> (you do eventually
need to lead and change things — the arc goes from listening to acting with earned trust).

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why "listen first" (no big changes for ~30 days) rather than charging in with changes, and why build
your "map" of people, systems, and history?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
You should "listen first" (no big changes for ~30 days) rather than charging in with changes because
<strong>as a new lead you don't yet understand the context or have earned trust — so making changes early
breaks things you didn't understand and alienates people you haven't yet won over</strong>. The temptation is
to charge in with changes (to prove yourself, or fix what you see as obviously broken), but this backfires
for several reasons: (1) <strong>you don't yet understand the context</strong> — things are usually the way
they are for reasons (some good, some bad), and you don't yet know those reasons; changing something before
understanding why it's that way risks breaking things you didn't realize mattered (Chesterton's fence: don't
remove a fence until you understand why it was put there — it might be load-bearing). What looks obviously
wrong to a newcomer often has a reason the newcomer doesn't yet see. (2) <strong>you haven't earned
trust</strong> — as a new lead, you have no credibility yet with the team; charging in with changes (imposing
your views before you've earned the right) makes people defensive and resistant ("who is this person
changing things they don't understand?"), damaging the relationships you need. (3) <strong>listening builds
the understanding and relationships you need</strong> — spending the early period listening and learning
(rather than changing) builds both the <em>knowledge</em> to change wisely (once you understand the context)
and the <em>trust</em> to change successfully (once you've earned credibility). So listening first isn't
passivity — it's the necessary investment in understanding and trust that makes later changes wise and
accepted. The ~30-day guideline (no big changes) forces the discipline to understand before acting. (The
exception is a genuine emergency, which needs action — but the default is listen-first.) Charging in
change-first, by contrast, means changing things you don't understand (breaking stuff) without the trust to
carry it (alienating people) — a bad start. Why <strong>build your map</strong> of people, systems, and
history: <strong>because leading well requires understanding the people, the systems, and the context — so
deliberately building that understanding (the map) is what equips you to make good decisions and avoid
landmines</strong>. The map has three parts: (1) <strong>people</strong> — who's on the team, their
strengths, aspirations, concerns, and the team dynamics (built through 1:1s) — because you lead <em>people</em>,
and leading them well requires understanding each of them (what drives them, what they're good at, what
they're worried about) and the dynamics between them. (2) <strong>systems</strong> — how things work
technically (architecture, codebase, tooling) — because you need to understand the technical reality to make
good technical decisions and lead the work. (3) <strong>the history and the "bodies"</strong> — why things
are the way they are, past decisions, known problems, "where the bodies are buried" (the landmines: the tech
debt, the political sensitivities, the things people don't talk about, the past failures) — because this
context is what lets you avoid stepping on landmines (changing something that's sensitive for reasons you
didn't know, or repeating a past mistake) and understand why the current state exists. Building this map
deliberately (during the listen-first period) equips you to lead well: you make decisions <em>informed</em>
by understanding the people, systems, and context (rather than blindly), and you avoid the landmines a
newcomer without the map would hit. Without the map, you're leading blind — making decisions without
understanding the people you're leading, the systems you're responsible for, or the context and sensitivities
— which leads to bad decisions and hitting landmines. So building the map is the core work of the
listen-first period, and it's what the listening is <em>for</em>: developing the understanding (of people,
systems, history) that good leadership requires. Both points reflect starting deliberately: listen first (to
understand and earn trust before acting — because change-first breaks things and alienates people) and build
your map (the understanding of people, systems, and context that equips you to lead well and avoid
landmines) — together making the first 90 days an investment in understanding and trust that sets up the
whole role, versus a change-first charge that breaks things you don't understand without the trust to carry
it.
</details>

**Q2.** Why must you "renegotiate your IC workload explicitly," and why prioritize early wins that don't
"step on toes"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
You must "renegotiate your IC workload explicitly" because <strong>you can't fully do both the IC job and the
lead job, so without an explicit reduction of your IC load, you'll try to do everything — leading to burnout
and doing both badly, with the higher-leverage lead work usually suffering</strong>. Becoming a lead adds a
whole new job (leading the team — 1:1s, coordination, unblocking, planning, developing people) on top of your
old IC work, but your time didn't expand — so you can't fully do both. New leads often fall into the trap of
trying to keep doing <em>all</em> their old individual contributor work AND the new lead work, which fails:
(1) <strong>it leads to burnout</strong> — two full jobs in one person's time is unsustainable; (2)
<strong>both jobs suffer</strong> — spread across two full jobs, you do neither well; and (3) <strong>the lead
work suffers most</strong> — critically, the lead work is often <em>less urgent</em> (no immediate deadline
for "develop your people" or "improve the team," versus the concrete IC deliverables), so under time pressure
it's the lead work that gets deprioritized — which is exactly backwards (the lead work is the higher-leverage
part of your new role, and the reason you're a lead). So silently trying to do both means the important lead
work gets squeezed out by the urgent IC work, and you fail at the actual job. The fix is to <strong>explicitly
renegotiate</strong> your IC workload (with your manager): agree what your IC contribution will be now
(usually significantly reduced), so you have the capacity for the lead work. "Explicitly" matters because (a)
you need your manager's agreement (they're expecting certain output, and the reduction must be aligned) and
(b) making it explicit (rather than silently trying to do everything) forces the reality — you're shifting
from doing the work to enabling the team's work (Phase 1's transition), which <em>requires</em> actually
reducing your IC load, not just adding lead work on top. The transition to lead is fundamentally a shift in
where your value comes from (from your individual output to the team's output), and that shift requires
explicitly reducing the IC work to make room for the leadership — a renegotiation, not an addition. Why
prioritize <strong>early wins that don't "step on toes"</strong>: <strong>because early wins build the trust
and credibility a new lead needs, but only if they help rather than impose — a "win" that oversteps or
overrides people alienates them, undermining the very trust you're trying to build</strong>. Early wins —
small, visible improvements early in your tenure — are valuable because they build trust and credibility (they
show you add value and are here to help), which you then need to lead effectively and to make bigger changes
later. But <em>how</em> you choose them matters enormously: they must <strong>not step on toes</strong>,
meaning they should help the team (address something they want) rather than impose your will (override people
or change things that weren't broken). Good early wins: (1) address something <em>the team clearly wants
fixed</em> (a pain point they've been asking about — you're helping with something they wanted, building
gratitude and trust); (2) something <em>within your clear remit</em> (not overriding others' areas or
decisions); (3) something that <em>demonstrates you're here to help</em>, not to disrupt. These build trust
because they show you listened, you add value, and you respect the team. Bad "early wins" that step on toes —
imposing big changes, overriding people's decisions, fixing things that weren't broken to show off, changing
things in others' domains — do the opposite: they signal you're here to impose your will (not help),
disrespect the team's existing work and autonomy, and (because you haven't earned trust yet) come across as an
arrogant newcomer overstepping — which <em>alienates</em> people and destroys the trust you need. So the
discipline is to choose early wins that build trust by <em>helping</em> (things the team wanted, within your
remit, demonstrating you're an asset) rather than "wins" that impose and alienate. The logic: as a new lead
you're building trust from zero, and early wins are a chance to build it — but only if they land as "this
person helps and respects us," not "this person is imposing changes they don't have the standing to make."
Both points reflect the deliberate transition: explicitly renegotiate your IC load (so you have capacity for
the higher-leverage lead work, which otherwise gets squeezed out — the real shift to leadership) and choose
early wins that build trust without stepping on toes (helping, not imposing — building the credibility you
need before making bigger changes) — together setting up the new lead role well, versus overloading yourself
(doing both jobs badly) and alienating the team (imposing before earning trust).
</details>
