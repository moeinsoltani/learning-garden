# Lesson 41: Managing Up

## Concept

**Managing up** — working effectively with your own manager — is a skill most people never think to
develop, but it hugely affects your success: your manager controls resources, priorities, air cover,
and your visibility, so making them *effective on your behalf* is high-leverage. The foundation is
understanding what your boss actually needs from you (which is often different from what you assume):
**no surprises**, options rather than problems, and brevity. Above all: **never surprise your
manager** — especially not in front of *their* boss.

```
   WHAT YOUR BOSS ACTUALLY NEEDS FROM YOU
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ • NO SURPRISES (esp. not in front of THEIR boss)       │
   │ • OPTIONS, not just problems ("here's the issue AND     │
   │   two options + my recommendation")                    │
   │ • BREVITY (they're busy — get to the point)             │
   │ • status that answers before they have to ask          │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
   Make your manager effective on your behalf. Manage up
   deliberately — it's not sucking up, it's working well together.
```

The reframe: **managing up isn't sucking up — it's understanding what your manager needs and giving
it to them, so they can be effective on your behalf.** Your manager is a key stakeholder (resources,
priorities, protection, your visibility all flow through them), and working well with them — no
surprises, bringing options, being concise, disagreeing safely — is a legitimate, high-value skill,
not politics. The single most important rule: **no surprises** — bad news, risks, and problems reach
your manager from *you*, early, never as a surprise (especially in front of their boss).

---

## How It Works

### No surprises — the cardinal rule

The most important thing your manager needs: **no surprises.** A manager blindsided by bad news —
especially in front of *their* boss (in an exec review, a leadership meeting) — is put in an
impossible position (they can't respond well to something they didn't know), looks bad to their
boss, and loses trust in you (if they can't rely on you to keep them informed, they can't rely on
you). So problems, risks, slips, and bad news must reach your manager **from you, early** — never as
a surprise, and never first from someone else or in a public forum. "My manager should never be
surprised by something I knew" is the cardinal rule of managing up. This connects to delivering bad
news early (English track Lesson 47).

### Options, not just problems

When you bring your manager a problem, bring **options and a recommendation**, not just the problem.
"The project's going to slip" (problem dumped) is far less useful than "the project's going to slip;
here are two options — cut scope X to hit the date, or move the date two weeks; I recommend cutting
scope because Y." Bringing options (a) shows you've thought it through (you're solving, not just
escalating), (b) makes it easy for your manager to help (they can decide/approve rather than start
from scratch), and (c) demonstrates leadership. Dumping raw problems makes you a problem to manage;
bringing options makes you someone who handles things.

### Brevity — they're busy

Your manager is busy (more so the more senior). **Be concise** — get to the point, lead with what
matters (BLUF — English track Lesson 21), don't make them dig through detail for the ask or the key
info. A crisp "here's the situation, here's what I need/recommend" respects their time and is more
effective than a long ramble. Match the altitude too — give them the level of detail they need
(usually higher-level than you'd give a peer), not every technical detail.

### Status that answers before it's asked

Good managing-up includes **proactive status** — keeping your manager informed (on progress, risks,
what's happening) *before* they have to ask. A manager who has to chase you for status doesn't trust
the work is on track; one who gets proactive, useful updates (the right things, concisely) trusts you
and doesn't need to micromanage. Answer the questions they'd ask (is it on track? any risks? do you
need anything?) before they ask them. This builds trust and buys you autonomy.

### Disagreeing upward — safely

You should disagree with your manager when you think they're wrong — a good manager wants it — but do
it **safely and well**: (1) usually privately (not challenging them publicly); (2) with respect and
framed as a shared goal ("I want us to get this right — can I offer a concern?"); (3) as a
concern/question, not a defiance (English track Lesson 30); and (4) then **disagree-and-commit** (once
they decide, commit — even if you disagreed). Disagreeing well upward makes you valuable (you're not a
yes-person); doing it badly (publicly, defiantly, or never committing) is career-limiting. A good
manager values the person who respectfully pushes back and then commits.

### Using your skip-level, and managing a weak manager

Two situational notes: (1) **The skip-level** (your manager's manager) is a resource — a good
skip-level relationship (through skip-level 1:1s, appropriate visibility) gives you another channel
and perspective; use it appropriately (not to go around your manager, but as a legitimate
relationship). (2) **A weak or overloaded manager** — if your manager is disorganized, overloaded, or
weak, managing up matters *more*: make it easy for them (bring things buttoned-up, remind them of
what you need, fill gaps proactively, don't rely on them to track things). You can be effective even
with an imperfect manager by managing up well (and it's often the reality).

{: .note }
> **Managing up: no surprises, options not problems, brevity — make your manager effective on your behalf</br>**

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** Your project is going to slip about **three weeks** — you've just become confident
of it (a key dependency came in late and testing revealed more work than expected). Here's the
pressure: there's an **exec review this Thursday** where your director (your manager) will report on
this project's status to *their* leadership. If you don't tell your director, they'll find out about
the slip <em>at</em> the exec review — blindsided, in front of their boss, looking like they don't know
their own projects. It's Monday. You need to tell them today.

**Write the message you'd send your director today.** Then note the principles and mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong message tells the director early (no surprise before the exec review), leads with the news,
brings options and a recommendation, and is concise. Example:
<br><br>
"Hi [Director] — flagging something important early, ahead of Thursday's exec review so you're not caught
off guard: <strong>the [project] is going to slip by about three weeks.</strong> <em>(Leads with the bad
news, and explicitly frames it as getting ahead of the exec review — no surprise.)</em>
<br><br>
What happened: the [platform] dependency came in about two weeks late, and testing surfaced more edge-case
work than we'd scoped. I want to own that we under-scoped the testing. <em>(Brief, factual context; owns
it, no blame-shifting.)</em>
<br><br>
Here's where we are and the options: <br>
• <strong>Option A — hold scope, move the date ~3 weeks</strong> (to [date]). Cleanest, ships everything,
but later. <br>
• <strong>Option B — hold the date, cut [specific scope]</strong> — we could hit the original date if we
drop [X], which we could fast-follow. <br>
<strong>My recommendation: Option A</strong> (move the date) — [X] is important enough that shipping it
half-done would hurt, and three weeks is recoverable. But if the date is critical for [business reason],
B is viable. <em>(Options + a clear recommendation with reasoning — not just dumping the problem; makes it
easy for the director to decide.)</em>
<br><br>
For Thursday: I wanted you to have this now so you can present it with the plan rather than be surprised.
Happy to give you a one-line version for the review, or hop on a quick call before then if useful. Let me
know which option you'd like to go with. <em>(Explicitly helps the director look prepared at the review;
offers support; asks for the decision.)</em>"
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>No surprise — tell them today</strong> — the whole point is the
director hears it from you Monday, not at the exec review Thursday (blindsided in front of their boss). (2)
<strong>Lead with the news</strong> — the slip, up front, not buried. (3) <strong>Options + recommendation</strong>
— two concrete options and a reasoned recommendation, so the director can decide/approve, not start from
scratch. (4) <strong>Own it, briefly and factually</strong> — the cause without blame-shifting or excessive
detail. (5) <strong>Help them look prepared</strong> — offer the one-liner / a call, so they present the
slip with a plan (looking on top of it) rather than surprised. (6) <strong>Concise</strong> — respects
their time, gets to the point. (7) <strong>Ask for the decision</strong>. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong>
(1) <strong>not telling them (or waiting)</strong> — the cardinal sin; letting them be blindsided at the
exec review destroys trust; (2) <strong>burying the news</strong> or spinning it (English track Lesson 47);
(3) <strong>dumping the problem with no options</strong> ("we're slipping, what do we do?") — makes the
director do the work and looks like you're not handling it; (4) <strong>blame-shifting</strong> ("the
platform team was late") without owning your part; (5) <strong>too much detail / rambling</strong> — the
busy director needs the crisp version; (6) <strong>not helping with Thursday</strong> — the whole urgency
is the exec review, so explicitly setting them up to present it well is key; (7) <strong>waiting to have a
perfect plan</strong> — early notice with options beats a late perfect plan; tell them now.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is "no surprises" the cardinal rule of managing up, and why bring "options, not just
problems"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"No surprises" is the cardinal rule of managing up because <strong>a blindsided manager can't respond
well, looks bad (especially to their own boss), and loses trust in you — so keeping them informed of
problems early is essential to their effectiveness and your relationship</strong>. When your manager is
surprised by bad news — a slip, a problem, a risk they didn't know about — several bad things happen: (1)
<strong>they can't respond well</strong> — put on the spot with information they didn't have time to
process, they can't react thoughtfully, plan, or present it well; they're caught flat-footed. (2)
<strong>they look bad, especially to their boss</strong> — if the surprise happens in front of <em>their</em>
manager (an exec review, a leadership meeting), your manager looks like they don't know their own
projects/team, which is embarrassing and damaging to <em>them</em> (a manager who's blindsided about their
own area in front of leadership looks incompetent, through no fault of their own except trusting you). (3)
<strong>they lose trust in you</strong> — the most damaging: if you let your manager be surprised by
something you knew, they learn they <em>can't rely on you</em> to keep them informed — which means they
have to start checking up on you, micromanaging, and doubting your other work (if you sat on this, what
else?). Trust, once broken this way, is hard to rebuild. So a surprised manager is a manager who can't
respond, looks bad, and stops trusting you — all serious. The rule is therefore: <strong>problems, risks,
and bad news reach your manager from you, early — never as a surprise, never first from someone else or in
a public forum</strong>. Telling them early (even before you have a full plan) lets them respond well,
prepare, present it competently (looking on top of it, not blindsided), and — crucially — maintains their
trust that you'll keep them informed (which buys you autonomy). "My manager should never be surprised by
something I knew" is the cardinal rule because being blindsided is the thing that most damages your
manager's effectiveness and their trust in you. Why bring <strong>options, not just problems</strong>:
<strong>because bringing options (with a recommendation) shows you've thought it through, makes it easy for
your manager to help, and demonstrates leadership — where dumping a raw problem makes you a problem to
manage rather than someone who handles things</strong>. When you bring your manager a bare problem ("the
project's going to slip — what do we do?"), you're handing them the whole burden of figuring out the
solution, which (a) makes more work for them (they have to analyze it from scratch), (b) signals you're
escalating rather than handling it (you're a problem to manage, not a problem-solver), and (c) doesn't help
them help you (they don't have the context to decide well without doing the analysis themselves). When you
bring <strong>options and a recommendation</strong> ("the project will slip; here are two options — cut
scope X to hold the date, or move the date two weeks; I recommend cutting scope because Y"), you (1)
<strong>show you've thought it through</strong> — you've analyzed the situation and generated solutions, so
you're clearly handling it (solving, not just escalating), which builds confidence in you; (2) <strong>make
it easy for your manager to help</strong> — they can simply decide between your options or approve your
recommendation (a quick, easy action) rather than starting the analysis from scratch (which respects their
time and gets you a faster resolution); and (3) <strong>demonstrate leadership</strong> — bringing solutions
(not just problems) is what leaders do, so it marks you as someone who owns and handles things. The
principle: <strong>don't just surface problems, bring the thinking</strong> — the problem plus options plus
your recommendation, so your manager's job is to decide/support rather than solve. This makes you valuable
(someone who handles things and makes your manager's life easier) rather than a problem to manage (someone
who dumps issues), and it gets problems resolved faster and better. Both rules serve the goal of managing
up — making your manager effective on your behalf: no surprises keeps them informed and trusting (so they
can support you and don't have to micromanage), and options-not-problems makes it easy for them to help and
marks you as a capable handler (so they trust you with more). Together they're the foundation of a working
relationship where your manager can be effective for you and gives you autonomy — which is what good
managing up achieves.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is managing up "not sucking up," and how do you disagree with your manager well?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Managing up is "not sucking up" because <strong>it's about understanding what your manager legitimately
needs to be effective and giving it to them — which serves the work and the relationship — not about
flattery or ingratiation</strong>. "Sucking up" is insincere flattery, telling the boss what they want to
hear, or ingratiating yourself for personal advantage at the expense of honesty — it's self-serving and
fake. Managing up is fundamentally different: it's recognizing that your manager is a key stakeholder
(they control resources, priorities, air cover, and your visibility), that a working relationship with
them requires understanding what they need (no surprises, options, brevity, proactive status), and
providing that — which makes them effective on your behalf and makes the collaboration work. It's the same
skill as managing any important stakeholder relationship well (understanding their needs and working
effectively with them), applied to your boss. It includes <em>honest</em> things that flattery would avoid
— disagreeing with them when they're wrong, delivering bad news early, pushing back respectfully — which
is the opposite of sucking up (a suck-up tells the boss they're right and hides bad news; a good manager-up
tells hard truths and disagrees when warranted, because that's what actually serves the manager and the
work). So managing up is a legitimate, professional skill about effective collaboration with a key
stakeholder, not politics or ingratiation — and doing it well (including the honest, sometimes-difficult
parts) is what makes you and your manager effective together, which benefits everyone (not just you). How
to disagree with your manager well: you <em>should</em> disagree when you think they're wrong (a good
manager wants honest input, not a yes-person), but do it <strong>safely and well</strong>: (1) <strong>usually
privately</strong> — raise the disagreement in private (a 1:1, a direct message), not by challenging them
publicly (which undermines them in front of others and triggers defensiveness); public disagreement with
your boss is risky and usually counterproductive. (2) <strong>with respect and framed as a shared goal</strong>
— "I want us to get this right — can I offer a concern?" — approaching it as jointly trying to reach the
best outcome (not challenging their authority), which keeps it collaborative and non-threatening. (3)
<strong>as a concern or question, not defiance</strong> — "I'm worried that X might happen — have we
considered Y?" (raising the concern for discussion) rather than "you're wrong, we should do Y" (defiant),
using the disagreeing-live skills (English Lesson 30) — framing your view as a concern/question keeps them
open. (4) <strong>then disagree-and-commit</strong> — once you've voiced your disagreement and they make
the decision (even if it goes against your view), commit to it and support it genuinely (don't keep
re-litigating or quietly resist) — because a decision, once made, needs your execution, and being unable
to commit after losing makes you unreliable. Disagreeing well upward — privately, respectfully, as a
concern, then committing — makes you <strong>valuable</strong>: you're not a yes-person (you give honest
input, which good managers prize), and you're reliable (you commit once decided). Doing it badly — publicly
challenging them, being defiant, or never committing (re-litigating decisions) — is career-limiting (it
undermines your manager, damages the relationship, and marks you as difficult). The key is that disagreeing
upward is <em>expected and valued</em> (a good manager wants the pushback), but <em>how</em> you do it
determines whether it's an asset (respectful, private, concern-framed, then committed) or a liability
(public, defiant, uncommitted). Both points reflect that managing up is professional relationship
management with your most important stakeholder: it's honest and substantive (not flattery — including
disagreeing when warranted), and the honesty must be delivered well (disagree privately, respectfully, as
a concern, then commit) — which makes you both effective together and marks you as a valuable, trustworthy
person your manager can rely on and give autonomy to.
</details>
