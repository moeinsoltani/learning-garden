# Lesson 58: Underperformance and PIPs

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **underperformance** — consistently working below the role's expected bar.
> - **PIP (Performance Improvement Plan)** — a formal written plan with specific goals, a deadline, and support; either a genuine chance or "exit paperwork."
> - **exit paperwork** — documentation of a departure already decided.
> - **skill vs will** — can't do it vs won't/doesn't want to; different causes, different responses.
> - **escalation ladder** — the fair sequence: feedback → clear expectations → support → PIP.
> - **the bar** — the expected performance level for a role.
> - **HR** — Human Resources: the department owning formal processes, with legal requirements.
> - **part ways** — the soft phrase for ending employment.
> - **with dignity** — handled so the person keeps their self-respect.
> - **drag on** — to continue unresolved far too long; what avoidance produces.
> - **false hope** — pretending a predetermined PIP is a real chance — the cruel failure (Lesson 32).

## Concept

Handling underperformance is the **hardest management duty** — emotionally difficult, high-stakes, and
often avoided (which makes it worse). Done well, it's early, fair, and gives the person a real chance;
done badly (or avoided), it drags on, demoralizes the team, and ends unfairly. The key disciplines:
**diagnose the cause first** (underperformance has many causes needing different responses), escalate
support fairly (feedback → clear expectations → support → PIP), and understand what a **PIP** really is
(and whether it's a genuine improvement tool or exit paperwork).

```
   HANDLING UNDERPERFORMANCE (early, fair, with dignity)
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ 1. DIAGNOSE FIRST: skill? will? role fit? life? YOU?    │
   │    (different causes → very different responses)       │
   │ 2. ESCALATION LADDER: feedback → clear expectations →   │
   │    support → PIP (fair process, real chance)           │
   │ 3. PIP: honest improvement tool OR exit paperwork —     │
   │    know which, and be honest                           │
   │ • the cost of NOT acting (your best people notice)      │
   │ • part ways with DIGNITY if it comes to that            │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The reframe: **diagnose the cause of underperformance before responding — and act early and fairly,
because avoiding it harms everyone (the person, the team, you).** The instinct is to avoid the hard
conversation (hope it improves) — but that lets it drag on, which is worse for everyone. Handling it well
means diagnosing <em>why</em> (skill, will, role fit, life circumstances, or your own management), then
applying the right response through a fair escalation, giving a real chance to improve, and — if it comes
to parting ways — doing it with dignity.

---

## How It Works

### Diagnose first — why is the person underperforming?

Before responding, **diagnose the cause** — because underperformance has different causes needing very
different responses: (1) **skill** — they lack a capability (response: training, mentoring, support to
build the skill); (2) **will/motivation** — they're capable but disengaged (response: understand why —
motivation issue, Lesson 61 — re-engage, address the demotivator); (3) **role fit** — they're in the wrong
role for their strengths (response: possibly a different role where they'd thrive); (4) **life
circumstances** — something outside work (health, family, personal crisis) is affecting them (response:
support, flexibility, patience — this is temporary and human); (5) **you/the environment** — unclear
expectations, poor management, a bad team situation, lack of support (response: fix <em>your</em>
contribution). Jumping to "they're a bad performer, start a PIP" without diagnosing misidentifies the
problem and applies the wrong response (PIP-ing someone whose issue is a temporary life crisis, or unclear
expectations you never set, is unfair and ineffective). Diagnose first.

### The escalation ladder — feedback → expectations → support → PIP

Handle underperformance through a **fair escalation**, not jumping to the end: (1) **feedback** — first,
give clear feedback that there's a problem (SBI — specific, Lesson 19); often this alone resolves it (the
person didn't realize). (2) **Clear expectations** — make explicit what "meeting the bar" looks like (so
they know the target). (3) **Support** — provide what they need to improve (mentoring, resources, removing
blockers, addressing the diagnosed cause). (4) **PIP** (Performance Improvement Plan) — if it doesn't
improve, a formal plan. This ladder gives the person a fair, escalating chance (feedback and support before
formal measures), which is both right (fair) and effective (many issues resolve at the feedback/support
stage). Skipping to a PIP without prior feedback and support is unfair (they weren't given a real chance)
and often legally problematic.

### PIPs — genuine tool vs exit paperwork

A **PIP** (Performance Improvement Plan) is a formal plan with specific improvement goals, a timeframe, and
support — and the crucial question is whether it's a **genuine improvement tool** (a real chance to improve,
which some people do) or **exit paperwork** (a formality to document the case before letting someone go, with
the outcome effectively predetermined). Both exist, and **you should know which it is and be honest**: (1) a
genuine PIP means you believe the person can improve and you're giving them a real, supported chance (with
achievable goals and genuine support); (2) exit paperwork means the decision to part ways is effectively
made and the PIP documents it. The dishonest failure is a fake "genuine" PIP (pretending it's a real chance
when the outcome's decided) — which is cruel (false hope) and erodes trust. Be honest with yourself and
(appropriately) the person about which it is. Work with **HR** on PIPs (there are legal and process
requirements) — this is where the EM role intersects with formal process.

### The cost of NOT acting

A crucial, often-underweighted point: **not acting on underperformance harms everyone — and your best
people notice.** Avoiding the hard conversation (hoping it improves) feels kinder but is actually harmful:
(1) the underperformer doesn't get the feedback/chance to improve (unfair to them); (2) the team suffers
(carrying the underperformer's slack, and demoralized by it); (3) crucially, **your best people notice and
resent it** — when a strong performer sees an underperformer tolerated (same rewards, no consequences), it's
demoralizing ("why do I work hard if it doesn't matter?") and can drive your best people away. So tolerating
underperformance isn't kind — it's unfair to the individual (no chance to improve), unfair to the team
(carrying the slack), and corrosive to your best people (who lose faith). Acting (fairly, early) is the
kinder and necessary choice.

### Parting ways with dignity

If it comes to parting ways (the improvement doesn't happen, or it's genuinely the wrong fit), do it **with
dignity**: (1) it shouldn't be a surprise (the escalation ladder means they saw it coming); (2) be honest,
clear, and humane; (3) preserve their dignity (it's a hard moment; don't humiliate — it's often a fit
problem, not a worth problem); (4) be generous where you can (a fair transition, a good reference if
deserved, help landing elsewhere). Parting ways is sometimes right (for the person too — they may thrive
elsewhere), and doing it with dignity matters both morally (it's a human being) and practically (the team
watches how you treat people, and word travels). How you handle exits shapes trust as much as anything.

{: .note }
> **Diagnose underperformance first, act early and fairly, and don't tolerate it — your best people notice</br>**
> Underperformance is the hardest management duty, often avoided (which makes it worse). Handle it well:
> <em>diagnose the cause first</em> (skill, will, role fit, life circumstances, or your own management — each
> needs a different response, so don't jump to a PIP without diagnosing). Escalate fairly (<em>feedback →
> clear expectations → support → PIP</em> — a real, escalating chance). Know whether a <em>PIP is a genuine
> improvement tool or exit paperwork</em>, and be honest (a fake-genuine PIP is cruel). Understand the
> <em>cost of NOT acting</em> — tolerating underperformance is unfair to the person, burdens the team, and
> demoralizes your best people (who notice and may leave). And if it comes to it, <em>part ways with
> dignity</em>. Handling underperformance early, fairly, and humanely — diagnosing, escalating, acting — is a
> hard but essential duty that protects the individual, the team, and your credibility.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** One of your engineers, **Priya**, was a strong performer, but for about **two months**
she's been quietly underperforming — her output has dropped, she's missed a couple of commitments, and her
work quality has slipped. It's noticeable but not catastrophic yet. You don't know why (she hasn't said
anything). You need to handle this — plan the arc from the first conversation through an ~8-week resolution,
considering both possible endings (she recovers, or it doesn't work out).

**Plan the arc** — first conversation through 8-week resolution, both endings. Then note the principles and
mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong plan diagnoses first (a previously-strong performer declining suggests a cause), escalates fairly
with support, and handles both endings with dignity. Example:
<br><br>
<strong>First conversation (week 1) — diagnose, with care:</strong> "Priya was strong and has declined —
that pattern strongly suggests a <em>cause</em> (not that she's suddenly a bad engineer). So the first
conversation is diagnostic and caring, not accusatory: 'Priya, I've noticed the last couple of months have
been a bit different — a few missed commitments, and I sense you might not be at your best. I'm not here to
criticize — I care about you and want to understand what's going on. Is everything okay? Is something making
work hard right now, whether it's the work itself or something outside?' Then <em>listen</em> (Lesson 25) —
this often surfaces the real cause: a life circumstance (health, family), disengagement/burnout, a problem
with the work or team, or something I'm doing. The diagnosis shapes everything." <em>(Diagnose first, with
genuine care — a previously-strong performer declining usually has a reason.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Weeks 1-2 — respond to the diagnosis:</strong> "Based on what I learn: <br>
• <strong>Life circumstance</strong> (health, family crisis): support and flexibility — 'let's take pressure
off, adjust your workload, whatever you need — your wellbeing comes first' (this is human and temporary;
support, don't PIP). <br>
• <strong>Disengagement/burnout</strong>: address the demotivator (Lesson 61) — a growth stall, a
meaningless project, overwork — and re-engage. <br>
• <strong>A problem I can fix</strong> (unclear expectations, a team issue, blockers): fix it. <br>
• <strong>Genuine performance issue</strong> (rarer for a previously-strong person, but possible): set clear
expectations and support to improve. <br>
Set clear expectations either way and offer real support."
<br><br>
<strong>Weeks 3-6 — support and monitor:</strong> "Provide the support (flexibility, re-engagement,
mentoring, blocker removal — whatever the cause needs), with regular check-ins (caring, not surveilling) —
how's it going, is the support helping, is she recovering? Give it real time (a couple of months is a real
window). Continue honest feedback (no surprises)."
<br><br>
<strong>Weeks 6-8 — resolution, both endings:</strong> <br>
• <strong>Ending A — she recovers</strong> (most likely if it's a life circumstance or fixable cause): the
support worked, she's back to strong. Great — reinforce, ensure the underlying cause is resolved, and move
on. This is why diagnosing and supporting first matters — you keep a strong performer through a rough patch.
<br>
• <strong>Ending B — it doesn't improve</strong>: if, despite genuine support, it doesn't recover, escalate
the process — a more formal conversation about expectations and, if needed, a PIP (with HR), honestly. But
even here, the fair escalation (feedback → expectations → support → PIP) means she's had a real chance and
it's no surprise. If it ultimately comes to parting ways, do it with dignity (no surprise, humane, generous
where possible)."
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Diagnose first</strong> — a previously-strong performer declining
usually has a cause (life, disengagement, a fixable problem, or you); find it before responding. (2)
<strong>Lead with care</strong> — the first conversation is caring and curious, not accusatory (which also
gets the honest cause). (3) <strong>Respond to the actual cause</strong> — support/flexibility for a life
circumstance, re-engagement for disengagement, fixing your own contribution — not a one-size PIP. (4)
<strong>Fair escalation with real support</strong> — feedback → expectations → support → (only if needed)
PIP, giving a genuine chance. (5) <strong>Both endings handled well</strong> — recovery (the goal, likely
with support) or, if not, a fair process and dignified parting. (6) <strong>No surprises</strong>
throughout. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>jumping to a PIP</strong> without diagnosing
(unfair and wrong if it's a life crisis or something you caused); (2) <strong>an accusatory first
conversation</strong> ("your performance has dropped, shape up") — misses the cause and damages trust; (3)
<strong>not diagnosing</strong> — assuming she's just a bad performer now (a previously-strong person
usually has a reason); (4) <strong>avoiding it</strong> — hoping it improves (harms her, the team, and your
credibility); (5) <strong>no real support</strong> — expecting improvement without helping (unfair); (6)
<strong>surprising her</strong> — if it escalates without prior feedback; (7) <strong>ignoring a life
circumstance</strong> — treating a personal crisis as a performance failure (inhumane and wrong — support
it); (8) <strong>a fake PIP</strong> if it comes to that — pretending it's a real chance when decided
(cruel).
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why must you "diagnose the cause first" before responding to underperformance, and what are the main
causes?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
You must diagnose the cause of underperformance first because <strong>underperformance has many different
causes, each requiring a very different response — so responding without diagnosing (e.g., jumping to a PIP)
misidentifies the problem and applies the wrong, often unfair and ineffective, response</strong>.
Underperformance is a <em>symptom</em>, not a diagnosis — the same visible symptom (dropped output, missed
commitments) can stem from completely different underlying causes, and the right response depends entirely on
<em>which</em> cause it is. If you skip diagnosis and jump to a generic response (typically "they're
underperforming, start a PIP"), you'll often apply the wrong response: PIP-ing someone whose issue is a
temporary life crisis (inhumane and counterproductive), or someone whose issue is unclear expectations you
never set (unfair — it's your failure), or someone who's disengaged for a fixable reason (a PIP addresses the
symptom, not the disengagement). The wrong response is unfair to the person (they're penalized for the wrong
thing, or for something that isn't their fault) and ineffective (it doesn't address the actual cause, so it
doesn't fix the problem). Diagnosing first — understanding <em>why</em> the person is underperforming — lets
you apply the <em>right</em> response, which is both fairer and more effective. The main causes: (1)
<strong>skill</strong> — they lack a needed capability (response: training, mentoring, support to build the
skill — it's a development gap, not a character flaw); (2) <strong>will/motivation</strong> — they're capable
but disengaged (response: understand and address the demotivator — a growth stall, a meaningless project,
overwork — and re-engage, Lesson 61); (3) <strong>role fit</strong> — they're in the wrong role for their
strengths (response: possibly move them to a role where they'd thrive — the issue is fit, not ability); (4)
<strong>life circumstances</strong> — something outside work (health, family, a personal crisis) is affecting
them (response: support, flexibility, patience — this is temporary and human, and deserves compassion, not a
PIP); (5) <strong>you/the environment</strong> — unclear expectations, poor management, a bad team situation,
lack of support, or something you're doing (response: fix <em>your</em> contribution — the underperformance
may be your failure, not theirs). These causes need <em>opposite</em> responses in some cases: a skill gap
needs training, a life crisis needs support and flexibility, disengagement needs re-engagement, a fit problem
needs a role change, and a your-fault problem needs you to fix your management. Applying a PIP (or any single
response) to all of them is wrong for most — you can't fix a skill gap, a life crisis, disengagement, a fit
problem, and your own management failure with the same response. So diagnosing first is essential: it's the
difference between correctly identifying and addressing the real cause (fair and effective) versus applying a
generic response to a misidentified problem (unfair and ineffective). Especially for a <em>previously-strong</em>
performer who's declined, the decline strongly suggests a <em>cause</em> (a life circumstance, disengagement,
a fixable problem, or something you're doing) rather than "they're suddenly a bad engineer" — so diagnosis
(with a caring, curious first conversation that surfaces the real cause) is even more clearly the right first
step. Diagnose first, then respond to the actual cause — which is the foundation of handling underperformance
fairly and effectively.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is not acting on underperformance harmful (and how do your best people factor in), and what's
the difference between a genuine PIP and "exit paperwork"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Not acting on underperformance is harmful because <strong>avoiding it (hoping it improves) — though it feels
kinder and easier — harms everyone: the underperformer, the team, and especially your best people, who notice
and resent tolerated underperformance</strong>. The instinct is to avoid the hard conversation (it's
uncomfortable, and confrontation-averse managers hope it resolves itself). But tolerating underperformance is
harmful on multiple levels: (1) <strong>it's unfair to the underperformer</strong> — without feedback and a
chance to improve, they don't get the opportunity to address the problem (they may not even fully know it's a
problem), so they can't fix it, and it may eventually end worse (a surprise firing) — the "kindness" of
avoiding it actually denies them the chance to improve. (2) <strong>the team suffers</strong> — the team
carries the underperformer's slack (extra load) and is demoralized by working alongside sustained
underperformance. (3) <strong>crucially, your best people notice and resent it</strong> — this is the
often-underweighted point: when a strong performer sees an underperformer <em>tolerated</em> (getting the same
rewards, facing no consequences, while they work hard), it's deeply demoralizing — it signals that
performance doesn't matter ("why should I work hard if underperformance is tolerated equally?"), which
corrodes their motivation and can drive your <em>best</em> people to leave (they go somewhere their
excellence is recognized and underperformance isn't tolerated). So tolerating underperformance doesn't just
fail the underperformer — it risks losing your best people (the opposite of what you want). The reframe:
<strong>tolerating underperformance isn't kind — it's unfair to the individual (no chance to improve), unfair
to the team (carrying the slack), and corrosive to your best people (who lose faith and may leave)</strong>.
Acting on it (fairly, early, with diagnosis and support) is the kinder and necessary choice — for the
individual (a real chance), the team (not carrying it), and your best people (seeing that performance matters).
The cost of <em>not</em> acting is high and often invisible until your best people quietly disengage or leave.
The difference between a <strong>genuine PIP</strong> and <strong>exit paperwork</strong>: a <strong>genuine
PIP</strong> is a real improvement tool — you <em>believe the person can improve</em> and you're giving them a
genuine, supported chance to do so, with achievable goals, real support, and an open outcome (they might
succeed, and some people do). An <strong>exit paperwork PIP</strong> is a formality where the decision to part
ways is <em>effectively already made</em>, and the PIP exists to document the case (for legal/process reasons)
before letting the person go — the outcome is essentially predetermined. Both exist in practice, and the
crucial discipline is to <strong>know which it is and be honest</strong>: (1) if it's a genuine PIP (you
believe they can improve), give them a real, supported chance with achievable goals; (2) if the decision is
effectively made (exit paperwork), be honest with yourself about that (and, appropriately, don't give false
hope). The <em>dishonest failure</em> is a <strong>fake-genuine PIP</strong> — pretending it's a real chance
to improve when the outcome is actually decided — which is cruel (it gives false hope, and puts the person
through a stressful "improvement" process that was never going to change the outcome) and erodes trust (people
learn that PIPs are shams, so the tool loses meaning). So be honest about which kind it is: a genuine PIP
deserves genuine belief and support; if the decision is really made, don't dress it up as a real chance. (And
work with HR on PIPs — there are legal and process requirements, and this is where the EM role intersects with
formal HR process.) The honesty matters both ethically (don't give false hope or run a sham process) and for
trust (fake PIPs corrode faith in the whole system). Both points reflect handling underperformance with
integrity: act on it rather than tolerating it (which harms the person, the team, and your best people), and
be honest about the tools you use (a genuine PIP is a real chance; exit paperwork is a documented exit; a
fake-genuine PIP is a cruel deception) — so that underperformance is handled early, fairly, honestly, and in a
way that protects the individual's dignity, the team's health, and the trust of your best people.
</details>
