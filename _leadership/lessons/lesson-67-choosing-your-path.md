# Lesson 67: Choosing (and Re-choosing) Your Path

## Concept

The final question this course prepares you for: **which path — Lead, EM, or Staff+ (technical) — is right
for you?** This is the capstone. The key insights: make the decision with **real information** about what
each path's <em>daily reality</em> actually is (not the job-description fantasy), distinguish **what
energizes you** from **what merely flatters you**, try before committing where you can, and — crucially —
**keep the decision revisable** (the pendulum between IC and management is normal and healthy; this isn't a
one-way door). You end the course by writing your own decision memo.

```
   CHOOSING YOUR PATH (with real info, revisable)
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ • THE REAL DAILIES (calendar screenshots, not job specs)│
   │ • what ENERGIZES you vs what merely FLATTERS you        │
   │ • TRY before committing (acting roles, internships)     │
   │ • the PENDULUM (IC ⇄ management) is normal & healthy —  │
   │   NOT a one-way door                                   │
   │ • EXIT CRITERIA: how you'll know in a year              │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The reframe: **choose your path based on the real daily reality (what actually energizes you), try it before
fully committing, and keep the choice revisable — it's not a one-way, permanent decision.** People often
choose management for the wrong reasons (status, the "next step," flattery) without knowing the real daily
reality, and treat it as irreversible. The wiser approach: understand what each path's days actually look
like, choose what genuinely energizes you, and know you can re-choose (the pendulum is normal).

---

## How It Works

### The real dailies — calendar screenshots, not job descriptions

The most important input: understand what each path's **daily reality actually is** — not the idealized job
description, but the real day-to-day (a "calendar screenshot" view). The paths differ profoundly in dailies:
(1) **Staff+/technical** — deep technical work, architecture, hard problems, still substantially hands-on,
influence through technical leadership; (2) **EM** — mostly people (1:1s, hiring, performance, coaching),
meetings, coordination, little hands-on coding, success measured through the team; (3) **Tech Lead** — a
blend (some coding, some leadership/coordination). Find out the <em>real</em> dailies (talk to people in each
role, look at their actual calendars/days — "what does your Tuesday actually look like?") — because people
often choose based on the fantasy (the prestige of "manager") without knowing the reality (mostly meetings
and people problems, little coding), and then find the reality doesn't suit them. Choose on the real daily
reality, not the job title or fantasy.

### What energizes you vs what merely flatters you

A crucial distinction: separate **what genuinely energizes you** from **what merely flatters you.** Some
things about a path are <em>flattering</em> (the status of "manager," the sense of "advancing," others'
approval, the title) but don't actually <em>energize</em> you day-to-day (you might find the actual work
draining). Choose based on what <strong>energizes</strong> you — the work that you find genuinely engaging
and fulfilling (do you light up doing people-development and coordination, or deep technical work?) — not
what flatters your ego or looks like the expected "next step." Many people take management because it's
flattering/expected, then are miserable doing work that doesn't energize them. Ask honestly: which path's
<em>actual daily work</em> energizes me (vs. which merely appeals to my ego or others' expectations)? Choose
the energizing one.

### Try before committing

Where possible, **try a path before fully committing** — because you can't fully know if something suits you
until you've done it. Ways to try: an **"acting lead" or "acting manager" role** (a temporary trial —
covering while someone's out, Lesson 33), an **internship/rotation** into a role, a **project** that gives
you a taste (leading something, or a deep technical initiative), or taking on the responsibilities gradually.
Trying gives you real information (does the actual work energize or drain me?) before a full, hard-to-reverse
commitment — much better than committing based on assumption and discovering it doesn't suit you. Try before
you fully commit.

### The pendulum is normal — it's not a one-way door

A liberating and important truth: **moving between IC and management (the "pendulum") is normal and
healthy — it's not a one-way, permanent, irreversible decision.** Many successful people move back and forth
over their careers (IC → manager → back to IC → manager again), and going from management back to IC is
<em>not</em> a failure or a demotion — it's a legitimate, healthy choice (you might prefer the IC work, or
want to go deep again, or find management isn't for you right now). Treating the choice as a one-way door
(once you go management, you can never go back, and going back is failure) is a false and harmful belief that
makes people (a) agonize over the decision as if it's permanent, and (b) stay in a role that doesn't suit
them (afraid that leaving is failure). The reality: it's revisable, the pendulum is normal, and re-choosing
is healthy — which takes the pressure off the decision (you can try management and go back if it's not for
you) and frees you to choose what's right for you now. It's not a permanent identity; it's a choice you can
revisit.

### Exit criteria — how you'll know in a year

When you choose a path, set **exit criteria** — how you'll know, in a year, whether it's working: what would
tell you it's right (you're energized, growing, effective, fulfilled) vs. wrong (drained, miserable, it's not
you)? Setting these criteria in advance (rather than judging in the fraught moment) gives you a clear,
pre-committed way to evaluate the choice — and a built-in re-evaluation point (revisit in a year against the
criteria). This makes the decision revisable in practice (you've planned to re-evaluate) and honest (you'll
assess against pre-set criteria, not rationalize). Combined with the pendulum being normal, exit criteria
make the choice a hypothesis to test (try it, evaluate against criteria, re-choose if needed), not an
irreversible leap.

## Lab — Capstone: Your Decision Memo

**The capstone exercise:** This is the culmination of the course. Write your own **decision memo** about your
path — drawing on everything you've worked through (the 66 scenarios across this course gave you real
exposure to what each path involves).

**Write your decision memo** — including: (1) your **choice** (Lead / EM / Staff+ / undecided-and-here's-how-
I'll-decide); (2) your **evidence** (from this course's scenarios and your own experience — what energized
vs. drained you, what the real dailies revealed); (3) your **12-month plan** (how you'll pursue or test the
choice); and (4) your **re-evaluation date** (when and how you'll revisit it). Make it genuinely yours.

12-month plan</strong> — pursue or test it concretely. (5) <strong>Revisable — re-evaluation date + exit
criteria + pendulum acknowledged</strong> — a hypothesis to test and revisit, not an irreversible leap. (6)

<strong>treating it as irreversible</strong> (a one-way door — agonizing as if permanent, or fearing that
going back is failure); (4) <strong>no testing</strong> (fully committing based on assumption rather than
trying); (5) <strong>no re-evaluation/exit criteria</strong> (no plan to honestly revisit); (6) <strong>vagueness</strong>
(no clear choice or plan). The real value is the honest reflection: which path's actual daily work energizes
<em>you</em>, tested and revisable — a decision made with real information and kept open.

**Q2.** Why is it important that the choice is "revisable" and the IC⇄management pendulum is "normal and
healthy" (not a one-way door)?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
It's important that the choice is revisable and the IC⇄management pendulum is normal and healthy (not a
one-way door) because <strong>treating the decision as permanent and irreversible — with going back seen as
failure — makes people agonize over it unbearably and stay trapped in roles that don't suit them, whereas
recognizing it's revisable takes the pressure off and frees people to choose what's right for them now and
adjust as they learn</strong>. The false but common belief is that the Lead/EM/IC choice is a <em>one-way
door</em>: once you go into management, you can never go back to IC, and going back would be a failure/
demotion. This belief is harmful in two ways: (1) <strong>it makes the decision agonizing</strong> — if the
choice is permanent and irreversible, people treat it as a make-or-break, high-stakes, once-in-a-career
decision, agonizing over it (afraid of making the "wrong" irreversible choice) — which is stressful and can
lead to paralysis or a poorly-made decision under that pressure. (2) <strong>it traps people in roles that
don't suit them</strong> — if going back is seen as failure, people who tried management and found it doesn't
suit them (they miss the technical work, or don't enjoy the people-management) feel they <em>can't</em> go
back (it would be admitting failure), so they stay in a role that makes them unhappy and less effective —
trapped by the fear that leaving is failure, when in fact leaving would be the right choice for them. The
<strong>reality</strong> is the opposite: <strong>the IC⇄management pendulum is normal and healthy</strong> —
many successful people move back and forth over their careers (IC → manager → back to IC → manager again),
and going from management back to IC is <em>not</em> a failure or demotion but a <em>legitimate, healthy
choice</em> (you might prefer the IC work, want to go deep technically again, or find management isn't for you
right now — all valid). The choice is <em>revisable</em>: it's not a permanent identity but a choice you can
revisit and change as you learn what suits you and as your preferences evolve. Recognizing this is
important because it: (1) <strong>takes the pressure off the decision</strong> — if it's revisable (you can
try management and go back if it's not for you), it's no longer a terrifying irreversible leap but a
hypothesis you can test and adjust, so you can make it more calmly and try things without fear; (2) <strong>frees
you to choose what's right for you now</strong> — you can choose the path that suits you at this point in your
life/career, knowing you can re-choose later if things change (rather than trying to make a permanent choice
for all time); (3) <strong>lets you leave a role that doesn't suit you without shame</strong> — if you try
management and it's not for you, going back to IC is a healthy, legitimate choice (not failure), so you're not
trapped by fear of "demotion"; and (4) <strong>reflects reality</strong> — careers are long and preferences
evolve, so moving between paths as you learn and change is natural, not a sign of failure. So the reframe —
revisable, pendulum normal, not a one-way door — is liberating and correct: it de-stresses the decision
(a testable hypothesis, not an irreversible leap), frees you to choose what's right now and adjust later, and
removes the false shame that traps people in unsuitable roles. Combined with exit criteria (how you'll know in
a year) and a re-evaluation date, it makes the choice a <em>hypothesis to test and revisit</em> — choose what
energizes you now, try it, evaluate honestly against criteria, and re-choose if needed — rather than a
permanent, high-stakes, one-way commitment. This is the wiser way to approach the path decision: with real
information (real dailies, what energizes you, trying before committing) AND the freedom of revisability (the
pendulum is normal, going back is fine, re-evaluate in a year) — which lets you choose well, without the false
pressure of permanence, and adjust as you learn what genuinely suits you over a long career. That freedom to
choose and re-choose is what makes the decision manageable and honest, rather than an agonizing, trapping,
irreversible leap.
