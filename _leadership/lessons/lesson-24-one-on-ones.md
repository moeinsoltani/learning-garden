# Lesson 24: 1:1s That Aren't Status Meetings

## Concept

The regular one-on-one is potentially the **highest-leverage 30 minutes of your week** — the
primary channel for building trust, surfacing problems early, developing people, and staying
connected to how each person is really doing. But most 1:1s waste this: they become status
meetings (which a Slack update could replace) or "everything's fine" in five minutes. The skill
is running 1:1s that do what only a 1:1 can — and the key mental shift is that **it's *their*
meeting, not yours.**

```
   THE 1:1 FAILURE MODE          WHAT A 1:1 IS FOR
   ────────────────────          ─────────────────
   status report                 THEIR agenda: blockers, growth,
   ("what did you ship?")          concerns, career, how they're doing
   YOU talk 80%                  YOU listen 80%, ask questions
   skipped when busy             non-negotiable (protects trust)
   "everything's fine" (5 min)   real conversation (trust + depth)
        ↑ a Slack update              ↑ what only a 1:1 can do
          would do this
```

The reframe: **the 1:1 is the report's meeting, for their agenda — not your status update.**
Status ("what did you ship this week?") is a poor use of the 1:1 (get that async); the 1:1's
unique value is the things that need a private, regular, trusting conversation — their blockers,
frustrations, growth, career, and how they're actually doing. Making it their meeting, listening
more than talking, and holding it as non-negotiable is what unlocks its leverage.

---

## How It Works

### It's *their* meeting

The single most important shift: the 1:1 is primarily for the *report*, not the manager — their
agenda, their concerns, their growth, their questions. Your job is largely to listen and help,
not to extract a status report or run through your list. Start by asking what's on *their* mind
("what would be most useful to talk about today?"), and let them drive. A 1:1 dominated by the
manager's agenda (status, tasks) misses the point — those things can go async; the 1:1 is the
space for what *they* need.

### Not a status meeting

The most common failure: the 1:1 becomes a status update ("what did you work on? what's next?").
This wastes the 1:1's unique value — status can be conveyed async (a written update, standup), and
using precious 1:1 time for it crowds out the things that actually need a private conversation
(concerns, growth, how they're doing). If your 1:1s are status reports, reclaim them: move status
async, and use the time for the real stuff.

### Cadence and non-negotiability

Hold 1:1s **regularly** (usually weekly or biweekly) and **protect them** — don't habitually
cancel or reschedule them for "more important" things. Cancelling a 1:1 sends a message ("you're
not a priority"), and skipped 1:1s mean problems fester unsurfaced. The regularity and reliability
are part of the value: a dependable, protected 1:1 signals the person matters and creates the
consistent space where trust builds and issues surface early. Treat them as non-negotiable.

### Questions that open people up

Since it's their meeting and you're listening, good *questions* are your main tool. Beyond "how's
it going?" (which gets "fine"), use questions that open real conversation: "what's frustrating you
right now?", "what would make your work better?", "how are you feeling about your growth?", "what's
something you're stuck on or unsure about?", "what's not being said in the team?". A question bank
helps you go deeper than surface status. (Lesson 25 develops the questioning skill.)

### Track themes across weeks

Keep light notes and track *themes* over time — a concern raised repeatedly, a growth goal, a
brewing frustration, a career aspiration. This lets you (a) follow up (showing you listened and
things don't vanish), (b) notice patterns (a small frustration mentioned three times is a real
issue), and (c) support their growth over time (returning to their development goals). A 1:1 that
picks up threads from previous ones (vs. starting cold each time) is far more valuable and shows
genuine attention.

{: .note }

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** Your 1:1s with one of your engineers, Ravi, have become useless. Every time, he
says "yeah, everything's fine, [brief status], no blockers" and it's over in five minutes. You
sense there's more — he's been a bit disengaged lately, and "fine" doesn't ring true — but the
pattern is set and he's not opening up. You want to break this pattern.

**Design your next three 1:1s with Ravi to break the "everything's fine" pattern** — what you'd do
differently, what you'd ask, and how you'd shift the dynamic. Then note what's likely going on and
the mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Breaking the pattern requires changing the dynamic — making it clearly their meeting, asking better
questions, building safety, and being patient. Example plan:
<br><br>
<strong>Before the next 1:1 — reset the frame:</strong> Recognize the "everything's fine" pattern
usually means the 1:1 has become a status check (so he gives status and leaves) and/or he doesn't
feel it's a space for real things. So explicitly reset it: "Ravi, I want to change how we use our
1:1s. These aren't for status — I can get that async. This is your time, for whatever's useful to
you — what's frustrating, what you want to work on, how you're feeling about things, anything.
Let's make it actually useful."
<br><br>
<strong>1:1 #1 — reset + a real question, and patience:</strong> Open with the reframe above. Then
ask one genuine, specific, open question and <em>give it space</em> (don't fill the silence): "How
are you <em>actually</em> finding the work these days? Not the status — are you enjoying it, is it
challenging you, is anything wearing on you?" Then be quiet and let him answer; if he says "fine,"
gently probe: "You've seemed a bit less energized lately and I care how you're doing — is there
anything going on, even outside work?" Don't force it — but signal genuine care and that this is a
real space. End by asking what <em>he'd</em> like to talk about next time.
<br><br>
<strong>1:1 #2 — go deeper on what surfaced, or build safety:</strong> If #1 opened something,
follow that thread. If he stayed guarded, keep building safety: share a bit yourself (some
vulnerability invites theirs), ask about growth/career ("where do you want to be growing? do you
feel like you are?"), and demonstrate you listened to #1 (follow up on anything he mentioned). The
goal is showing, consistently, that this is a safe, useful space — trust builds over repetition,
not one meeting.
<br><br>
<strong>1:1 #3 — establish the new pattern:</strong> By now, ideally the dynamic is shifting — he's
bringing things. Reinforce it: keep listening more than talking, keep following up on threads, keep
it his agenda. If he's opened up, act on something he raised (close a loop — proving the 1:1 leads
to real things). If still guarded, keep being patient and consistent (some people take longer;
consistency and genuine care eventually work), and consider whether something specific is wrong
(disengagement can signal a deeper issue — a growth stall, a problem, burnout — worth gently
exploring).
<br><br>
<strong>What's likely going on:</strong> "Everything's fine" in five minutes usually means one or
more of: the 1:1 became a status ritual (so he treats it as one), he doesn't feel it's safe or
useful to raise real things (so he doesn't), or he's disengaged/checked-out (the disengagement you
sensed — which is itself the thing to surface). The reset + better questions + patience addresses
all three. <strong>What makes this work:</strong> (1) <strong>explicitly reframes it as his
meeting, not status</strong> — breaking the status-ritual pattern directly; (2) <strong>asks
genuine, specific, open questions</strong> and gives them space (silence) rather than accepting
"fine"; (3) <strong>signals genuine care</strong> ("I care how you're doing", naming the
disengagement gently) — which invites honesty; (4) <strong>builds safety over multiple sessions</strong>
— recognizing trust and openness come from consistent repetition, not one meeting (hence a
three-1:1 arc, not a one-shot fix); (5) <strong>follows up and closes loops</strong> — proving the
1:1 leads to real things, which makes it worth engaging with. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong>
(1) accepting "fine" and letting it stay a five-minute status check (the pattern persists); (2)
interrogating or pushing too hard ("what's REALLY going on??") — pressure makes people close up
more; build safety instead; (3) expecting one good question to fix it (openness builds over time —
be patient and consistent); (4) making it about your agenda still (if you reframe "it's your
meeting" but then run through your list, nothing changes); (5) not following up on what he raises
(if he opens up once and nothing comes of it, he learns it's pointless); (6) ignoring the
disengagement signal (if he's checked out, that's the real issue to gently surface, not paper over);
(7) filling every silence yourself (silence is where people decide to say the real thing — leave
space).
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is "the 1:1 is *their* meeting" the key shift, and why is running it as a status meeting
a waste?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"The 1:1 is <em>their</em> meeting" is the key shift because <strong>it reorients the 1:1 toward the
things that give it its unique value — the report's concerns, growth, and wellbeing — rather than the
manager's agenda, which mostly consists of things that don't need a 1:1</strong>. The 1:1's special
value lies in being a private, regular, trusting conversation — the ideal space for the things that
need exactly that: the person's blockers and frustrations, their growth and career, how they're
really doing, concerns they wouldn't raise in a group, questions they're unsure about. These are
<em>their</em> things, and surfacing them requires the person to feel the meeting is for them and safe
for honesty. So making it their meeting — their agenda, you listening ~80% and asking questions,
starting with "what's on your mind?" — is what unlocks that value: it creates the space where their
real concerns, growth needs, and honest state actually come out, which is what only a 1:1 can do and
what makes it high-leverage (trust, early problem-surfacing, development). If instead you run it as
<em>your</em> meeting (your agenda, your questions, extracting what you want to know), the person is in
respond-mode, not open-up-mode, and the things that need <em>their</em> initiative and safety to
surface don't. Why running it as a status meeting is a waste: <strong>status is the one thing that
<em>doesn't</em> need a 1:1 — it can be conveyed async — so spending the precious 1:1 time on it
crowds out everything the 1:1 uniquely enables</strong>. Status ("what did you ship? what's next?")
can be communicated perfectly well through a written update, a standup, or a tracking tool — it
doesn't require a private synchronous conversation. So using the 1:1 for status (a) wastes the 1:1's
scarce, high-value time on something a Slack message would do, and (b) crowds out the things that
<em>do</em> need the 1:1 (concerns, growth, wellbeing, trust-building) — which then never get the
space they need. A status-meeting 1:1 is thus doubly wasteful: it spends unique time on non-unique
content, and it fails to do the thing 1:1s are for. It also tends to keep the person in report-mode
(delivering status) rather than the open, honest mode where real things surface — reinforcing a
shallow dynamic (like the "everything's fine" pattern). The fix is to move status async (get it
through other channels) and reclaim the 1:1 for what only it can do: their agenda, their concerns,
their growth, and the trust that comes from a regular private conversation focused on them. So the two
points connect: making it <em>their</em> meeting (not the manager's) and getting status <em>out</em>
of it (async) are the same move — clearing the 1:1 of the manager's extractive agenda so it can serve
its real, high-leverage purpose of supporting and developing the person and building the trust and
early-warning that a team's health depends on. The 1:1 is potentially the highest-leverage 30 minutes
of a lead's week precisely because of what a private, regular, their-agenda conversation enables — and
running it as a manager's status check forfeits all of that.
</details>

**Q2.** Why does protecting the 1:1 (not cancelling it) matter, and why are good questions and
tracking themes across weeks important?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Protecting the 1:1 (not habitually cancelling it)</strong> matters because <strong>the
regularity and reliability are themselves part of the value — cancelling sends a message that the
person isn't a priority, and skipped 1:1s mean problems fester unsurfaced</strong>. Several reasons:
(1) <strong>cancelling signals the person doesn't matter</strong> — when you routinely cancel or
reschedule a 1:1 for something "more important," the message received is "you're less important than
this other thing," which erodes the trust and sense of being valued that the 1:1 is supposed to build;
a protected, reliable 1:1 signals the opposite ("you matter enough that this time is sacred"). (2)
<strong>problems fester when 1:1s are skipped</strong> — the 1:1 is the channel where issues,
frustrations, and concerns surface early; if it's frequently skipped, those things don't get surfaced,
so small problems grow into big ones unnoticed (the early-warning value is lost), and the person has
nowhere to raise things. (3) <strong>reliability builds the trust and rhythm</strong> — a dependable,
regular 1:1 creates the consistent space where trust accumulates over time and where the person knows
they'll reliably have the lead's attention; sporadic 1:1s never build that (trust and openness come
from consistent repetition). So protecting the 1:1 — treating it as non-negotiable — is essential
because its value depends on reliability: the regularity is what makes it a trusted, dependable space,
and cancelling undermines both the message (you matter) and the function (surfacing issues early,
building trust). Why <strong>good questions</strong> are important: since the 1:1 is their meeting and
you're mainly listening, questions are your primary tool for opening real conversation — and the
default questions ("how's it going?") get default answers ("fine"). Questions that go deeper —
"what's frustrating you right now?", "how are you feeling about your growth?", "what's not being said
in the team?" — invite the person to surface the real things (concerns, growth, honest state) that
surface-level questions don't reach. Good questions are what get past "everything's fine" to the
substance the 1:1 is for; without them, the 1:1 stays shallow. (Lesson 25 develops this skill.) Why
<strong>tracking themes across weeks</strong> is important: keeping light notes and tracking themes
over time lets you (1) <strong>follow up and close loops</strong> — returning to something the person
raised shows you listened and that things don't vanish, which builds trust and makes them more willing
to raise things (they see it leads somewhere); (2) <strong>notice patterns</strong> — a concern or
frustration mentioned repeatedly across weeks is a real, significant issue (that might seem minor in any
single mention), so tracking reveals patterns a single 1:1 wouldn't; and (3) <strong>support growth
over time</strong> — returning to the person's development goals and aspirations across 1:1s lets you
actually support their growth as an ongoing thread, rather than treating each 1:1 as disconnected.
Tracking themes turns a series of isolated conversations into a continuous, attentive developmental
relationship — a 1:1 that picks up threads from previous ones (vs. starting cold each time) is far
more valuable and shows genuine, sustained attention. Together, these make the 1:1 effective:
protecting it ensures it happens reliably (preserving trust and the early-warning function), good
questions get it past the surface to the real things, and tracking themes makes it a continuous
developmental relationship rather than disconnected check-ins — all serving the 1:1's purpose as the
high-leverage channel for supporting people, surfacing issues early, and building trust.
</details>

## Homework

Assess and improve your 1:1s (or design them, if you're preparing to lead). (1) Are your 1:1s their
meeting or yours? Are they status meetings that could be async? Reclaim one for their agenda. (2)
Build a question bank — write 8–10 questions that open real conversation (beyond "how's it going?"),
to draw out concerns, growth, and honest state. (3) Are you protecting them (regular, not cancelled),
and tracking themes across weeks? Start light notes if not. (4) Pick one 1:1 that's become shallow
and design how you'd deepen it. Reflect: what's your default 1:1 failure mode (status meeting?
talking too much? cancelling? not following up?), and what one change would raise their leverage?
