# Lesson 39: Driving Change Across an Organization

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **resistance is information** — people resist for reasons, often valid; the resistance tells you what to fix.
> - **early adopters** — the willing first users (Lesson 10); the **adoption curve** runs early adopters → early majority → late majority → **laggards** (the last, most reluctant).
> - **beachhead** — a first secured position from which a campaign expands (a military landing metaphor).
> - **paved road** — making the new way the *easiest* way (Lesson 10).
> - **visible wins** — early publicized successes that prove the change works.
> - **momentum** — accumulated motion; success attracting more success.
> - **the honeymoon** — the initial enthusiasm phase after any change, which fades.
> - **revert** — to slide back to the old way once attention moves on.
> - **Kotter's change model** — the classic 8-step organizational-change framework this lesson compresses.
> - **campaign** — a sustained, planned effort over time (not a single announcement).
> - **status quo** (STAT-us KWOH) — the current state of things.

## Concept

The hardest influence challenge is landing a change **bigger than any team you control** — migrating
many teams to a new practice, tool, or way of working. You can't mandate it (no authority over them),
and people resist change (often for good reasons). Success comes from understanding change as a
*process* with predictable dynamics: resistance is information, early adopters are beachheads, making
the new way the *easy* way (paved roads), securing visible wins early, and sustaining past the initial
enthusiasm. This is Kotter's change model, compressed for an engineering org.

```
   DRIVING ORG-WIDE CHANGE
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ • RESISTANCE is information (why do they resist? often  │
   │   valid — address it, don't steamroll)                 │
   │ • EARLY ADOPTERS as beachheads (start with the willing) │
   │ • make the NEW WAY the EASY way (paved roads)           │
   │ • VISIBLE WINS early (proof, momentum)                  │
   │ • SUSTAIN past the honeymoon (change reverts without it)│
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
   You can't mandate a change across teams you don't control —
   you make it easy, prove it works, and build momentum.
```

The reframe: **you drive org-wide change not by mandate but by making the new way easy and proving it
works — and resistance is information, not an obstacle to steamroll.** People resist change for reasons
(often legitimate — real costs, real risks, past burns), so resistance tells you what to address.
Landing a big change is a *campaign*: start with the willing (early adopters), make adoption easy (paved
roads), generate visible wins (momentum), address resistance (it's data), and sustain it past the
honeymoon (or it reverts).

---

## How It Works

### Resistance is information, not an obstacle

The instinct is to see resistance as an obstacle to overcome (push harder). Reframe it: **resistance is
information.** People resist change for reasons — often *legitimate* ones (the new way has real costs,
real risks, disrupts their work, or they've been burned by past changes) — and their resistance tells you
what those reasons are. So instead of steamrolling resistance, *listen to it*: it reveals the real
obstacles to adoption (which you can then address), valid objections (which may improve your plan), and
what people need to get on board. Resistance is feedback about your change; ignoring or overpowering it
means missing the information you need to make the change actually work.

### Early adopters as beachheads

Don't try to convert everyone at once — start with the **early adopters** (the willing, the enthusiastic,
the ones who see the value or like trying new things). They become **beachheads**: (1) they adopt
willingly (easy wins, no fighting resistance), (2) they prove the change works (real examples,
credibility), (3) they generate the visible wins that persuade others, and (4) they become advocates who
help spread it. Starting with the willing and expanding from there (rather than mandating for everyone) is
far more effective — you build momentum from success rather than fighting universal resistance upfront.
(Adoption curve: early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards; work with it.)

### Make the new way the easy way — paved roads

The most powerful lever: **make the new way the path of least resistance** — the "paved road." If the new
way is easier than the old way (better tooling, sane defaults, less friction), people adopt it naturally
because it's genuinely easier for them; if it's harder (more work, more friction), they won't, no matter
how much you advocate. So invest in making adoption easy: good tooling, templates, documentation,
automation, sensible defaults, removing friction. "Make the right thing the easy thing" — people follow
paved roads. This often matters more than any amount of persuasion: a change that's genuinely easier
spreads itself; one that's harder requires constant pushing and reverts.

### Visible wins early

Momentum comes from **visible early wins** — concrete, demonstrated successes that prove the change works
and its value is real. Engineer for early wins: pick initial adoptions likely to succeed visibly, and
publicize them ("Team X adopted this and cut their deploy time in half"). Visible wins (1) provide proof
(evidence beats argument), (2) build momentum (success attracts others — social proof), and (3) create
advocates. Early wins turn a change from a risky proposal into a proven, spreading thing — so getting some
early, visible, and publicized is high-leverage.

### Sustain past the honeymoon — or it reverts

A crucial, often-missed point: **change reverts without sustained effort.** Initial enthusiasm (the
honeymoon) fades, old habits pull people back, and without reinforcement the change quietly unwinds
(people drift back to the old way). So sustaining the change past the initial push is essential:
reinforcing it, making it stick (paved roads help — if the new way stays easier, it sticks), addressing
the friction that emerges, celebrating continued adoption, and not declaring victory too early. Many
change efforts fail not at launch but at sustainment — the change happens, then slowly reverts because no
one kept it going. Plan for the long haul, not just the launch.

### Kotter, compressed

This maps to Kotter's change model (compressed): create urgency (why change — the pain of the status
quo), build a coalition (early adopters, advocates), form a vision (the clear direction — Lesson 38),
communicate it, enable action (remove obstacles — paved roads), generate short-term wins (visible early
wins), sustain and build on them (don't stop early), and anchor the change (make it the new normal). The
through-line: change is a deliberate, sustained campaign — urgency, coalition, easy adoption, visible
wins, and sustainment — not a one-time announcement.

{: .note }
> **Drive org-wide change by making the new way easy and proving it works — resistance is information</br>**
> Landing a change bigger than any team you control can't be done by mandate — it's a campaign. Treat
> <em>resistance as information</em> (people resist for often-legitimate reasons; listen to learn what to
> address, don't steamroll). Start with <em>early adopters as beachheads</em> (the willing prove it works
> and spread it). Above all, <em>make the new way the easy way</em> (paved roads — good tooling/defaults;
> a genuinely easier way spreads itself, a harder one reverts). Generate <em>visible wins early</em>
> (proof and momentum — evidence beats argument). And <em>sustain past the honeymoon</em> (change reverts
> without ongoing reinforcement — many efforts fail at sustainment, not launch). Compressed Kotter:
> urgency, coalition, vision, easy adoption, visible wins, sustainment. You make change happen not by
> pushing everyone at once, but by making it easy, proving it, and building momentum you sustain.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** Your org has 12 teams all using a **shared staging server** that everyone
hates-but-uses — it's a constant source of conflicts (teams stepping on each other's deployments), it's
flaky, and it slows everyone down. You want to migrate all 12 teams to **ephemeral environments**
(on-demand, isolated environments spun up per branch/PR). It's a big, cross-org change; you have no
authority to mandate it. You need a plan to land it over roughly a year.

**Write a one-page year-long campaign plan** to migrate the 12 teams to ephemeral environments. Then note
the principles and mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong plan treats it as a sustained campaign: urgency, early adopters, paved roads, visible wins, and
sustainment. Example one-page plan:
<br><br>
<strong>Q1 — Foundation, urgency, and the first beachhead:</strong>
<br>• <strong>Establish urgency</strong>: quantify the pain of the shared staging server (hours lost to
conflicts, flakiness incidents, developer frustration surveys) — make the cost of the status quo visible
and undeniable (people change when the pain is clear). <br>• <strong>Build the paved road (start)</strong>:
invest in making ephemeral environments genuinely <em>easy</em> — good tooling, one-command spin-up, sane
defaults, docs. This is the highest-leverage work: the new way must be easier than the old. <br>•
<strong>First early adopter</strong>: find one enthusiastic, capable team that hates the shared server most
(a willing beachhead), and help them migrate — with lots of support (make their success likely).
<br><br>
<strong>Q2 — First visible win + second wave:</strong>
<br>• <strong>Publicize the first win</strong>: "Team X moved to ephemeral environments and eliminated
their staging conflicts / cut their cycle time by N" — a concrete, visible, publicized success (proof +
momentum). <br>• <strong>Expand to 2–3 more willing teams</strong> (early adopters), refining the paved
road with each (fix the friction they hit). <br>• <strong>Listen to resistance</strong>: for teams not
adopting, understand <em>why</em> (real obstacles? valid concerns?) and address them (improve tooling,
fill gaps) — resistance is information.
<br><br>
<strong>Q3 — Cross the chasm to the majority:</strong>
<br>• <strong>Leverage the wins and advocates</strong>: with several teams succeeding visibly and
advocating, approach the early majority (now with proof, social proof, and a well-paved road). <br>•
<strong>Make it the default/easy path</strong>: ideally the new way is now clearly easier, and you can
start making it the standard (new projects default to ephemeral; the paved road is the obvious choice).
<br>• <strong>Address the harder cases</strong>: teams with legitimate blockers get targeted help.
<br><br>
<strong>Q4 — Complete and sustain:</strong>
<br>• <strong>Migrate the remaining teams</strong> (late majority/laggards) — now that it's proven,
standard, and easy, and possibly with a deprecation timeline for the shared server (once the new way is
genuinely better and easy, retiring the old removes the fallback). <br>• <strong>Sustain</strong>: keep the
paved road maintained (so it stays easy and doesn't revert), address emerging friction, celebrate
completion, and make ephemeral environments the anchored new normal (not a thing that quietly reverts).
<br><br>
<strong>Throughout</strong>: resistance = information (listen, address); paved roads (keep making it
easier); visible wins (publicize each); advocates (early adopters spread it); don't declare victory early
(sustain past the honeymoon).
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Urgency</strong> — quantify the status-quo pain (the cost of the
hated shared server) so people want to change. (2) <strong>Paved roads (the key lever)</strong> — invest
heavily in making the new way genuinely easier; a change that's easier spreads, one that's harder reverts.
(3) <strong>Early adopters as beachheads</strong> — start with the willing, not a mandate; their success
proves and spreads it. (4) <strong>Visible wins</strong> — publicize concrete successes (proof + momentum
+ social proof). (5) <strong>Resistance is information</strong> — listen to why teams don't adopt and
address it (real obstacles, valid concerns). (6) <strong>Sustain past the honeymoon</strong> — maintain
the paved road and reinforce, so it doesn't revert; don't declare victory early. (7) <strong>Year-long
campaign</strong> — phased (foundation → early adopters → majority → sustain), not a one-time mandate.
<strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>mandating it top-down</strong> for all 12 teams at once (no
authority, and it'll be resisted/fail); (2) <strong>not making the new way easy</strong> — the single
biggest failure; if ephemeral environments are harder than the shared server, no advocacy will make it
stick (paved roads are everything); (3) <strong>ignoring/steamrolling resistance</strong> — missing the
information about real obstacles; (4) <strong>no early wins / not publicizing them</strong> — no proof or
momentum; (5) <strong>trying to convert everyone at once</strong> instead of early-adopter beachheads; (6)
<strong>declaring victory early / not sustaining</strong> — the change reverts as enthusiasm fades and the
paved road decays; (7) <strong>no urgency</strong> — without making the status-quo pain visible, people
don't feel the need to change; (8) <strong>retiring the old way before the new is genuinely better/easy</strong>
— forcing people onto a worse new way breeds resentment and failure.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is "resistance is information" a better stance than steamrolling resistance, and why start with
early adopters as beachheads rather than converting everyone at once?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"Resistance is information" is a better stance than steamrolling because <strong>people resist change for
reasons — often legitimate ones — and their resistance reveals the real obstacles and valid concerns you
need to address to make the change actually work, which steamrolling ignores</strong>. The instinct is to
see resistance as an obstacle to overpower (push harder, mandate, dismiss objections). But resistance is
usually not irrational stubbornness — people resist because the change has real costs to them (more work,
disruption, risk), because they have valid concerns (the new way has genuine problems, or your plan has
gaps), or because they've been burned by past changes (reasonable caution). Their resistance is
<em>feedback</em> about your change: it tells you what the real barriers to adoption are, what objections
need answering, what's missing from your plan, and what people need to get on board. If you <em>listen</em>
to resistance as information, you learn these things and can address them — improving the change (fixing
the real problems), removing the obstacles (making adoption feasible), and winning people over (by
addressing their actual concerns). If you <em>steamroll</em> resistance (ignore or overpower it), you (1)
miss the information (the real obstacles and valid concerns stay unaddressed, so the change fails or
half-works), (2) breed resentment (people forced into a change they had legitimate concerns about resist
harder or comply minimally), and (3) may push through a genuinely flawed change (the resistance was
telling you something was wrong, and you ignored it). So "resistance is information" reframes resistance
from an enemy to overcome into data to use — listen to it, learn what needs addressing, and address it,
which both improves the change and wins people over, versus steamrolling which loses the information and
the people. Why start with <strong>early adopters as beachheads</strong> rather than converting everyone
at once: <strong>because the willing adopt easily and their success proves the change and generates the
momentum, wins, and advocates that persuade everyone else — whereas trying to convert everyone at once
means fighting universal resistance with no proof</strong>. Trying to convert the whole org simultaneously
is hard and often fails: you face the full spectrum of resistance at once (including the skeptics and
laggards), with no demonstrated success to point to (it's still just a proposal), so you're pushing against
maximum resistance with minimum evidence. Starting with early adopters (the willing, enthusiastic ones who
see the value or like trying new things) is far more effective because: (1) <strong>they adopt willingly</strong>
— easy wins, no fighting resistance (you work with the people who want to, not against the ones who don't);
(2) <strong>they prove it works</strong> — real adoptions produce real examples and evidence that the
change is viable and valuable (credibility you didn't have with just a proposal); (3) <strong>they generate
visible wins</strong> — concrete successes that provide proof and social proof (evidence beats argument,
and "Team X succeeded with this" persuades the hesitant); (4) <strong>they become advocates</strong> —
satisfied early adopters spread the change (peer advocacy is powerful) and help others adopt; and (5)
<strong>you refine the change</strong> — early adopters surface the friction and issues (which you fix),
so by the time you reach the majority, the change (and the paved road) is proven and smoothed. This works
with the adoption curve (early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards): you build momentum
from success, using the early adopters' wins to bring along the majority (who follow proof and social
proof), rather than trying to move everyone from a standing start. So starting with beachheads means you
build from success and momentum (easy wins → proof → advocates → majority) rather than fighting universal
resistance upfront — a far more effective way to land a big change. Both points reflect that org-wide
change is a campaign worked <em>with</em> people and their reality (listen to resistance, start with the
willing, build momentum) rather than forced <em>on</em> them (steamroll, mandate everyone) — because you
can't mandate a change across teams you don't control, so you make it work by addressing real concerns and
building from demonstrated success.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is "making the new way the easy way" (paved roads) often the most powerful lever, and why must
change be sustained past the honeymoon?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"Making the new way the easy way" (paved roads) is often the most powerful lever because <strong>people
naturally follow the path of least resistance — so if the new way is genuinely easier than the old, they
adopt it because it's easier for them, whereas if it's harder, no amount of advocacy will make it stick</strong>.
The core insight: behavior follows friction. People (rationally) do what's easiest for them, so the
relative ease of the new way vs. the old way largely determines adoption, often more than any argument: (1)
<strong>if the new way is easier</strong> (better tooling, sane defaults, less friction, automation), people
adopt it naturally — it's the obvious choice because it genuinely makes their work easier, so the change
"spreads itself" with little pushing (people <em>want</em> the easier path); (2) <strong>if the new way is
harder</strong> (more work, more friction, worse ergonomics), people resist it no matter how much you
advocate, because you're asking them to make their own work harder — and even if you force initial adoption,
it reverts (people drift back to the easier old way the moment the pressure's off). So the friction of the
new way vs. the old is the dominant factor, which is why investing in making the new way genuinely easy —
the "paved road" (good tooling, templates, docs, automation, sensible defaults, removing friction) — is the
highest-leverage work in driving change: it makes adoption happen naturally (people follow the paved road)
rather than requiring constant pushing against friction. "Make the right thing the easy thing" often matters
more than any persuasion, because a genuinely easier way is self-sustaining (people adopt and stay because
it's better for them), while a harder way needs perpetual force and reverts. This is why a change effort's
success often hinges on the tooling/ergonomics investment, not the advocacy: you can't persuade people into
permanently doing something harder, but you can make the better way easier so they choose it. Why change
must be <strong>sustained past the honeymoon</strong>: <strong>because change reverts without ongoing
reinforcement — initial enthusiasm fades, old habits pull people back, and without sustained effort the
change quietly unwinds</strong>. Change has a "honeymoon" — initial energy and enthusiasm around the new
thing — but that fades, and several forces pull back toward the old way: (1) <strong>old habits are
sticky</strong> — people revert to familiar patterns when the novelty and attention wear off; (2)
<strong>friction emerges</strong> — real-world use surfaces problems and friction that, if unaddressed,
push people back to the old way; (3) <strong>attention moves on</strong> — the people driving the change
move to other priorities, and without someone sustaining it, it loses momentum; (4) <strong>the paved road
decays</strong> — if the tooling/support that made the new way easy isn't maintained, the new way gets
harder and people drift away. So without sustained effort — reinforcing the change, maintaining the paved
road (keeping the new way easy), addressing emerging friction, celebrating continued adoption, and not
declaring victory too early — the change quietly reverts (the org drifts back to the old way, and the
effort is wasted). This is a crucial, often-missed point: <strong>many change efforts fail not at launch
but at sustainment</strong> — the change happens (initial adoption), then slowly unwinds because no one
kept it going. Declaring victory at initial adoption and moving on is a common fatal mistake; the change
isn't done when it's launched, it's done when it's anchored as the new normal (which takes sustained effort
past the honeymoon). So sustaining past the honeymoon — planning for the long haul, maintaining the paved
road, reinforcing until the change is genuinely the anchored default — is essential, because change that
isn't sustained reverts. Both points reflect that org-wide change is about the durable reality of how
people behave, not a one-time act of persuasion: people follow the easy path (so make the new way easiest —
paved roads), and behavior reverts without reinforcement (so sustain the change past the honeymoon until
it's anchored). A change that's genuinely easier and actively sustained sticks; one that's harder or
un-sustained reverts, regardless of how well it launched. These are why the deepest levers in driving change
are making the new way easy and sustaining it — the unglamorous, durable work that actually makes change
stick, versus the launch-and-advocate that often doesn't.
</details>
