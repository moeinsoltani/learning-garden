# Lesson 29: Sponsorship

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **sponsorship** — spending your own credibility and influence *acting* on someone's behalf (vs mentoring, which only advises).
> - **capital** (social/political) — the accumulated trust and influence you can "spend" on requests and people.
> - **credibility** — being believed and trusted; what you put at risk when you vouch for someone.
> - **"saying their name in the room"** — advocating for someone in the meetings they're not invited to.
> - **visibility** — being seen by decision-makers; work leadership never sees doesn't advance anyone.
> - **promotion calibration** — the leadership meeting where candidates for promotion are compared and decided.
> - **stretch assignment** — a visible, high-stakes task given to grow and showcase someone (Lesson 27).
> - **the safe pick** — the always-chosen, already-proven person; defaulting to them concentrates opportunity.
> - **underrepresented groups** — people whose group is scarce in the field and who face extra bias.
> - **equity lever** — a tool that corrects unfair gaps in opportunity.
> - **self-promote** — to advertise your own accomplishments; those who don't need sponsors most.

## Concept

Mentoring *advises*; sponsorship *acts*. Mentoring is giving someone guidance; **sponsorship is
spending your own credibility and capital on someone else's behalf** — putting them on the visible
project, saying their name in the promotion discussion, advocating for them when they're not in the
room. It's the growth tool nobody tells you about, and it's often what actually moves careers
(advice helps you get better; sponsorship gets you seen and given opportunities). As a lead, you
have credibility and access others don't — sponsorship is spending it on your people.

```
   MENTORING (advises)              SPONSORSHIP (acts)
   ───────────────────              ──────────────────
   "here's how to grow"             puts them ON the visible project
   "here's my advice"               says their name in the promo meeting
   "here's how I'd approach it"      advocates when they're not in the room
        ↓ helps them get better         staffs them on the stretch assignment
                                        ↓ gets them SEEN, given opportunities,
                                          and advanced — spends YOUR capital
```

The reframe: **mentoring makes someone better; sponsorship gets them the opportunities and
visibility that actually advance them — by spending your credibility.** Advice alone often isn't
enough: people need to be *given* the visible work, *advocated for* in the rooms they're not in, and
*put forward* for opportunities — which requires someone with capital to spend it on them. That's
sponsorship, and doing it deliberately (including for the people who don't naturally get picked) is
one of the most powerful and overlooked ways a lead grows people.

---

## Going Deeper

### Sponsorship acts — it doesn't just advise

The core distinction: **mentoring** gives guidance (advice, coaching, teaching — helping someone
improve); **sponsorship** takes *action* on their behalf using your capital — staffing them on a
high-visibility project, recommending them for a promotion, naming them in a room they're not in,
advocating for a stretch opportunity, introducing them to the right people. Mentoring helps someone
get better; sponsorship gets them *seen and advanced*. Both matter, but sponsorship is often what
actually moves a career — because being good isn't enough; you also need the opportunities and
visibility, which sponsorship provides.

### "Saying their name in the room"

A concrete, high-impact form of sponsorship: **advocating for people when they're not present** —
in staffing decisions, promotion calibrations, leadership discussions. "Priya's rollback design is
why the migration was zero-downtime — she's ready for the senior role." Much of what determines
people's opportunities and advancement happens in rooms they're not in, and having someone with
credibility name their accomplishments and advocate for them there is often decisive. As a lead, you
have access to those rooms — using it to champion your people is sponsorship at its most valuable.

### Stretch assignments as sponsorship

Giving someone a **visible, high-stakes assignment** they might not obviously be "ready" for is a
powerful form of sponsorship — it's you spending your credibility to give them an opportunity to
prove and grow (and to be seen doing it). The visible project, the presentation to leadership, the
critical initiative — putting a developing person on these (with support — scaffolding, Lesson 27)
both grows them and gets them visibility. It's a risk you take on their behalf (your credibility is
partly on the line), which is exactly what makes it sponsorship, not just delegation.

### Who you're accidentally *not* sponsoring

The critical equity point: **notice who you default to — and who you're overlooking.** The instinct
is to give the visible opportunities to the "safe" pick (the person who always gets picked, who's
most proven, often the most confident or most similar to you) — which means the same people get
sponsored repeatedly while others (quieter people, those who don't self-promote, people from
underrepresented groups who face bias) get overlooked. This compounds inequity: the already-advantaged
get more opportunities, the overlooked stay overlooked. Deliberately notice who you're *not*
sponsoring — the capable people who don't naturally get picked — and sponsor them intentionally.
Sponsorship is a powerful equity lever precisely because opportunity, not just ability, determines
advancement.

### Track whose career you've moved

A useful discipline: **track whose careers you've actually advanced.** Who have you sponsored — put
on visible work, advocated for, gotten promoted? If you look and find you've sponsored the same one
or two people (or only people like you), that's a signal to broaden. Making sponsorship deliberate
and tracked (rather than defaulting to the usual picks) ensures you're spreading opportunity, not
concentrating it — a mark of a lead who genuinely develops their whole team, not just the obvious
stars.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** A visible, high-profile cross-team project needs an owner — it'll involve
presenting to leadership, working across teams, and it's the kind of high-visibility work that gets
someone noticed and advanced. Your "safe" pick is Marcus — he always gets the big projects, he's
proven, confident, and you know he'd do well. But you also have Sana, a quieter but very capable
engineer who never gets picked for the visible stuff (she doesn't self-promote), and who is more than
ready for a stretch like this with support. You can only pick one owner.

**Decide who you'd pick and why, and describe how you'd handle the person not picked.** Then note the
sponsorship principles and mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
This is a sponsorship-and-equity decision, not just a "who's safest" one. A strong answer recognizes
the pull toward Marcus (the safe pick) and deliberately considers Sana.
<br><br>
<strong>The decision — lean toward sponsoring Sana (with support):</strong> "I'd give it to Sana. Marcus
is the safe pick and always gets these — which is exactly the problem: he keeps getting sponsored while
Sana, who's just as capable, never gets the visible opportunities because she doesn't self-promote. This
is a high-visibility project that could genuinely advance her, and she's ready for the stretch with
support. Defaulting to Marcus again would mean the already-advantaged keeps getting more while the
overlooked stays overlooked. So I'd sponsor Sana — spend my credibility to give her this opportunity and
get her seen — while scaffolding her (staying close, helping with the leadership presentation, unblocking
across teams) so she succeeds. That's using my capital where it actually moves someone's career."
<br><br>
<em>(Caveat: this assumes Sana is genuinely ready for the stretch with support — sponsorship means
taking a considered risk on someone capable, not setting someone up to fail. If she truly weren't ready,
that'd be different — but "she doesn't self-promote" is not the same as "not ready," and that's exactly
the bias to check.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Handling Marcus (not picked):</strong> "I'd talk to Marcus directly and honestly: 'I'm giving
the cross-team project to Sana this time. You're the obvious pick and you'd crush it — and that's part
of my thinking: you've had a lot of the visible projects, and I want to make sure I'm spreading these
opportunities, because Sana's ready and hasn't had the chance. It's not a knock on you at all. And I've
got my eye on [a different growth opportunity / stretch] for you that fits where you're headed — let's
talk about that.' " <em>(Honest about the reasoning, affirms Marcus's value (not a demotion), frames it
as deliberate opportunity-spreading, and gives Marcus his own growth path so he's not just passed over.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Sponsorship principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Notice the default and who's overlooked</strong> —
the instinct to pick the safe/proven/confident person (Marcus) is exactly how the same people get
sponsored repeatedly while capable others (Sana) get overlooked; deliberately countering that is the
core skill. (2) <strong>Sponsorship is spending capital to create opportunity</strong> — giving Sana the
visible project (with support) spends your credibility to get her seen and advanced, which advice alone
wouldn't. (3) <strong>Support the stretch (scaffolding)</strong> — sponsorship isn't throwing someone in
unprepared; it's a considered risk on a capable person, with support so they succeed. (4) <strong>Handle
the not-picked person well</strong> — honestly, affirming their value, with their own growth path, so
it's opportunity-spreading, not a snub. (5) <strong>It's an equity lever</strong> — because opportunity
(not just ability) determines advancement, deliberately sponsoring the overlooked counters the bias that
otherwise compounds. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>defaulting to Marcus unreflectively</strong>
— the "safe pick" reflex that concentrates opportunity in the already-advantaged; (2) <strong>sponsoring
Sana with no support</strong> — setting her up to fail (sponsorship requires scaffolding, especially for
a first big visible thing); (3) <strong>picking Sana as tokenism</strong> — do it because she's genuinely
capable and ready (with support) and deserves the opportunity, not for optics; (4) <strong>handling
Marcus badly</strong> — making him feel demoted or passed over without explanation (affirm him, give him
a path); (5) <strong>confusing "doesn't self-promote" with "not ready"</strong> — the exact bias that
keeps capable quiet people overlooked; (6) <strong>never examining your pattern</strong> — if you always
pick the Marcuses, you're concentrating opportunity; track whose careers you've moved and broaden. (Note:
if Sana genuinely weren't ready even with support, the answer differs — but the discipline is to
seriously consider the overlooked capable person, not default past them.)
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What's the difference between mentoring and sponsorship, and why is sponsorship often what
actually advances a career?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The difference: <strong>mentoring advises (gives guidance to help someone improve); sponsorship acts
(spends your own credibility and capital on someone's behalf to get them opportunities and visibility)</strong>.
Mentoring is giving advice, coaching, teaching — helping someone get <em>better</em> ("here's how I'd
approach this," "here's how to grow"). Sponsorship is taking <em>action</em> using your capital — putting
them on the high-visibility project, saying their name in the promotion room, advocating for them when
they're not present, recommending them for a stretch opportunity, introducing them to the right people.
The crucial distinction is advise-vs-act and who bears the risk: mentoring costs you time and knowledge
but little risk (you're advising); sponsorship spends your <em>credibility</em> (you're putting your
capital on the line for them — if you advocate for someone or staff them on a visible project, your
judgment is partly on the line). Mentoring helps someone become more capable; sponsorship gets them
<em>seen and advanced</em>. Why sponsorship is often what actually advances a career: <strong>because
being good isn't sufficient for advancement — you also need the opportunities and visibility, which
require someone to <em>give</em> them to you, and that's what sponsorship does</strong>. Careers advance
not just through ability but through: getting the visible, high-stakes work (which gets you noticed and
lets you prove yourself), being advocated for in the rooms where opportunities and promotions are decided
(which you're often not in), and being put forward for stretch opportunities. All of these require
someone with capital to <em>act</em> on your behalf — to give you the visible project, to say your name
and vouch for you in the promotion calibration, to recommend you. Advice alone (mentoring) makes you
better, but it doesn't get you into those rooms or give you those opportunities — a highly capable person
who is never given visible work and never advocated for can stagnate, because ability without opportunity
doesn't advance. Sponsorship provides exactly what ability alone doesn't: the opportunities and the
advocacy. Concretely, much of what determines advancement happens in rooms the person isn't in (staffing
decisions, promotion discussions), and having someone credible name their accomplishments and champion
them there is often decisive — no amount of self-improvement substitutes for that advocacy. So while
mentoring is valuable (it makes people better), sponsorship is frequently what actually <em>moves</em>
careers, because it addresses the opportunity-and-visibility half of advancement that ability alone
doesn't. This is why sponsorship is "the growth tool nobody tells you about" — people focus on getting
better (and on giving/receiving mentorship), but the equally-important lever is being sponsored (given
opportunities and advocated for), which requires someone to spend their capital on you. As a lead, you
have credibility and access to rooms that your people don't — and sponsorship is spending that capital to
get them the opportunities and visibility that their ability alone won't guarantee, which is one of the
most powerful (and overlooked) ways you can advance the people you're growing.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is "noticing who you're *not* sponsoring" so important, and how is sponsorship an equity
lever?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"Noticing who you're not sponsoring" is important because <strong>the natural default — giving visible
opportunities to the safe, proven, confident pick — means the same people get sponsored repeatedly while
capable others get systematically overlooked, and only deliberate attention counters that</strong>. When
a high-visibility opportunity arises, the instinct is to give it to the "safe" pick: the person who's
most proven, most confident, who always gets picked, often the one most similar to you or most likely to
self-promote. This feels rational (they're a known quantity, low risk), but it has a compounding problem:
the same already-advantaged people keep getting the visible opportunities (and thus more visibility, more
advancement, becoming even more "proven"), while equally-capable others — quieter people, those who don't
self-promote, people who don't naturally get noticed — keep getting overlooked (and thus stay less
visible, less advanced, never becoming the "obvious" pick). Left to the default, opportunity concentrates
in the few and the overlooked stay overlooked, not because of ability but because of who gets <em>picked</em>.
Since this happens by default (through the unreflective safe-pick instinct), the only way to counter it is
to <em>deliberately notice</em>: who am I defaulting to, and who am I overlooking? Who's capable but never
gets picked for the visible work? Noticing surfaces the capable-but-overlooked people (the quiet Sana who
never gets the big project) so you can sponsor them intentionally, rather than defaulting past them again.
Without this deliberate noticing, you'll unconsciously keep sponsoring the same few. How sponsorship is an
equity lever: <strong>because advancement depends on opportunity (not just ability), and opportunity is
allocated by sponsors — so who gets sponsored determines who advances, which means sponsorship decisions
either counter or compound inequity</strong>. The mechanism: if opportunities systematically go to the
already-advantaged (the confident self-promoters, those similar to the decision-makers, those not facing
bias), then advancement systematically favors them — not because they're more able, but because they got
the opportunities. Groups who face bias, who don't self-promote (which correlates with cultural and
gender norms), or who are simply less similar to the (often homogeneous) decision-makers get overlooked
for sponsorship, so they get fewer opportunities, so they advance less — the inequity compounds through
the opportunity-allocation mechanism. Sponsorship is the lever because <em>it's the point where
opportunity gets allocated</em>: a lead deciding who to sponsor is deciding who gets the career-advancing
opportunities. Used unreflectively (defaulting to the safe/similar pick), sponsorship <em>compounds</em>
inequity (the advantaged get more). Used deliberately (noticing and sponsoring capable overlooked people),
it <em>counters</em> inequity — deliberately giving opportunities to the capable people who wouldn't
otherwise get them, which is exactly what levels the playing field (since ability was never the gap —
opportunity was). This makes sponsorship a powerful equity lever precisely because opportunity, not just
ability, determines advancement, and sponsorship is where opportunity is allocated. So a lead who wants
to develop their whole team fairly (and counter the biases that otherwise operate) must deliberately
notice who they're not sponsoring and sponsor the overlooked capable people — and track whose careers
they've actually moved (to check they're spreading opportunity, not concentrating it). This turns
sponsorship from an unconscious perpetuator of inequity (the default) into a deliberate force for equity
and for developing the whole team's talent — which is both fairer and better (you're developing and
advancing capable people who'd otherwise be wasted/lost). Noticing who you're not sponsoring is the
discipline that makes this possible.
</details>

## Homework

Audit and improve your sponsorship. (1) List whose careers you've actually moved — who have you put on
visible work, advocated for, gotten opportunities? Is it the same one or two people, or people like you?
(2) Notice who you're <em>not</em> sponsoring — capable people who don't naturally get picked (quieter,
less self-promoting). Pick one and think about how you'd sponsor them (a visible opportunity, advocating
for them, saying their name in a room). (3) Next time you allocate a visible opportunity, check your
default pick against who's being overlooked. (4) Distinguish your mentoring from your sponsorship — are
you only advising, or also acting? Reflect: are you spreading opportunity or concentrating it, and who's
one capable person you should sponsor more deliberately?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds deliberate sponsorship — spending your credibility on others' visibility, and doing
it equitably. A strong response audits whose careers they've actually moved (checking for concentration
in the same few or people like them), identifies capable people they're <em>not</em> sponsoring, plans to
sponsor an overlooked person, and distinguishes their mentoring (advising) from sponsorship (acting). The
realizations: (1) <strong>sponsorship acts, mentoring advises</strong> — sponsorship spends your capital
to get someone opportunities and visibility (staffing them on visible work, advocating in rooms they're
not in), which is often what actually advances a career (ability alone isn't enough — you need the
opportunities, which someone must give); (2) <strong>notice who you're NOT sponsoring</strong> — the
default (the safe/proven/confident/similar pick) concentrates opportunity in the already-advantaged while
capable others (quieter, less self-promoting) get overlooked, compounding inequity; deliberately noticing
and sponsoring the overlooked counters it; (3) <strong>sponsorship is an equity lever</strong> — because
opportunity (not just ability) determines advancement, and sponsorship allocates opportunity, so who you
sponsor either compounds or counters inequity; (4) <strong>track whose careers you've moved</strong> — to
check you're spreading opportunity, not concentrating it. On the audit, people commonly find they've
sponsored the same one or two people (or people like themselves), and that there are capable people they've
overlooked (who don't self-promote) — and that they do more mentoring (advising) than sponsorship (acting).
The highest-value change is usually: sponsor deliberately and more broadly — notice the overlooked capable
people and spend your capital on them (visible opportunities, advocacy), not just the default safe picks.
The meta-point: sponsorship is the overlooked growth tool — mentoring makes people better, but sponsorship
gets them the opportunities and visibility that actually advance them, by spending your credibility. And
it's a powerful equity lever: deliberately sponsoring capable overlooked people (rather than defaulting to
the already-advantaged) both develops your whole team's talent and counters the biases that otherwise
compound. As a lead with credibility and access others lack, using it deliberately for your people —
including those who don't naturally get picked — is one of the most powerful and generous things you do.
This completes Phase 5 (1:1s, Coaching & Mentoring): running 1:1s that matter, listening and powerful
questions, coaching vs mentoring, deliberate mentoring, review as teaching, and sponsorship — the ongoing
developmental relationship through which a lead grows their people, which is how your impact scales beyond
your own hands. The next phase (Delegation & Growing the Team) turns to delegation — how to hand off work
in a way that grows people and scales you, rather than either hoarding it or dumping it.
</details>
