# Lesson 61: Motivation

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **autonomy / mastery / purpose** — control over your work / getting better at what matters / work that means something — the three drivers of knowledge-work motivation (Dan Pink's *Drive*).
> - **intrinsic vs extrinsic** — motivation from the work itself vs from external rewards and punishments ("carrots and sticks").
> - **demotivator** — something that kills existing motivation; most are manager-controlled.
> - **churn / thrash** — constant direction changes that waste work (nothing demoralizes like discarded work).
> - **invisible work** — contributions nobody acknowledges (Lesson 20).
> - **burnout** — chronic exhaustion + cynicism + reduced capability from sustained stress (Lesson 05); needs load reduction, not slogans.
> - **coasting** — doing the minimum comfortably; the disengaged-but-present state.
> - **platitude** (PLAT-ih-tood) — an empty comforting phrase ("practice self-care!") substituting for real change.
> - **efficacy** (EF-ih-kah-see) — the sense that your effort actually works.
> - **re-engage** — to reconnect a checked-out person with what once drove them.

## Concept

Understanding what actually motivates engineers — and, crucially, **not accidentally killing it** — is one
of the highest-leverage things a manager does (a motivated team vastly outperforms a demotivated one). The
research is clear: for knowledge work, the real drivers are **autonomy, mastery, and purpose** (not carrots
and sticks). But the key insight is that **motivation is diagnosed per-person, not team-wide** — different
people are driven by different things and demotivated by different things — and many of the biggest
demotivators are ones **you (the manager) control** and can accidentally inflict.

```
   WHAT DRIVES ENGINEERS (autonomy / mastery / purpose)
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ • AUTONOMY: control over how they work                 │
   │ • MASTERY: getting better at something that matters     │
   │ • PURPOSE: work that means something                    │
   │ MOTIVATION is diagnosed PER-PERSON (not one-size)       │
   │ DEMOTIVATORS you control: micromanagement, churn,       │
   │   invisible work, broken promises → STOP killing it     │
   │ • watch BURNOUT signals; re-engage the coasting senior  │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The reframe: **motivation comes from autonomy/mastery/purpose, is diagnosed per-person, and is most often
<em>killed</em> by things the manager controls — so a huge part of "motivating" is not <em>demotivating</em>.**
You rarely need to "add" motivation (people usually start motivated); you need to avoid <em>destroying</em>
it (micromanagement, thrash, ignoring people's work, breaking promises) and address the specific demotivator
for each person. Motivation is largely about removing the demotivators, per-person.

---

## How It Works

### Autonomy, mastery, purpose

The research-backed drivers of motivation for knowledge work (Dan Pink's *Drive*, self-determination
theory): (1) **Autonomy** — having control over how you do your work (versus being micromanaged and
dictated to). Engineers are motivated by owning their work and making their own decisions. (2) **Mastery** —
getting better at something that matters (the satisfaction of developing skill, being challenged, growing).
Engineers are motivated by learning and improving. (3) **Purpose** — doing work that means something (that
matters, serves a purpose, has impact). Engineers are motivated by meaningful work. These <em>intrinsic</em>
motivators drive knowledge work far more than <em>extrinsic</em> carrots-and-sticks (which can even
<em>undermine</em> intrinsic motivation). So to support motivation: give people autonomy (don't
micromanage), opportunities for mastery (growth, challenge), and connection to purpose (why the work
matters).

### Motivation is diagnosed per-person

The crucial insight: **motivation is individual — diagnosed per-person, not team-wide.** Different people
are driven by different things (one by growth, another by autonomy, another by the mission, another by
recognition, another by work-life balance) and demotivated by different things. So a one-size-fits-all
approach fails — you can't "motivate the team" generically; you have to understand what drives (and drains)
<em>each person</em> and respond to them individually. This means knowing your people (via 1:1s — Lesson 24,
and listening — Lesson 25): what does <em>this</em> person care about, what energizes them, what's draining
them? Motivation is a per-person diagnosis, not a team-wide lever.

### The demotivators you control

The most important practical point: **the biggest demotivators are often things the manager controls — and
can accidentally inflict.** People usually start motivated; managers frequently <em>kill</em> that
motivation through: (1) **micromanagement** — removing autonomy (the fastest way to demotivate a capable
person); (2) **churn/thrash** — constantly changing direction, wasted work, chaos (nothing is more
demoralizing than work that gets thrown away); (3) **invisible work** — not recognizing people's
contributions (feeling unseen and unvalued — Lesson 20); (4) **broken promises** — not following through on
commitments to people (destroys trust and motivation); plus unclear priorities, no growth, blocked
autonomy, and pointless process. The key reframe: **a huge part of "motivating" people is <em>not
demotivating</em> them** — removing the demotivators you control (stop micromanaging, reduce churn,
recognize work, keep promises) — because people are usually motivated until something kills it, and that
something is often you (the manager). Look first at what you might be doing to demotivate.

### Burnout signals and real responses

**Burnout** (chronic exhaustion, cynicism, reduced efficacy from sustained stress/overwork) is a serious
motivation-and-health issue. Watch for signals: declining energy/output, disengagement, cynicism,
withdrawal, working long hours unsustainably, irritability, a previously-engaged person going flat. The
<em>real</em> responses (not platitudes): actually reduce the load (fewer things, realistic expectations —
not "practice self-care" while keeping the workload crushing), address the causes (overwork, lack of
control, unfairness, unclear expectations, values mismatch — the drivers of burnout), give real recovery
(time off that's actually off), and take it seriously (burnout is a management/systemic issue, often, not a
personal weakness). Preventing burnout (sustainable pace, sane workloads) is far better than treating it.

### Re-engaging a coasting senior

A common case: a **coasting senior** — a capable, experienced person who's disengaged, doing the minimum,
no longer growing or contributing at their level. Re-engaging them: diagnose <em>why</em> they're coasting
(bored/unchallenged? stalled growth? a past disappointment/resentment? life circumstances? a values
mismatch?), then address that specific cause — often a new challenge, growth opportunity, or bigger scope
(mastery/purpose) re-engages a bored senior; sometimes it's a resentment to address or a role that no longer
fits. Don't just accept the coasting (a disengaged senior is a big loss of capability and a bad signal to
others) — diagnose and re-engage (or, if it's a genuine mismatch, address that honestly). The re-engagement
is per-person (the coasting has a specific cause).

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** Three engineers on your team are disengaged, and from their 1:1 notes, they have **three
different causes**: (1) **Ava** — a strong mid-level who's been doing the same kind of work for a long time
and is clearly **stalled in her growth** (bored, not challenged, not developing); (2) **Ben** — working on a
project he finds **meaningless** (an internal tool nobody seems to use / a deprioritized effort — he doesn't
see the point); (3) **Carlos** — usually engaged, but recently disengaged due to something in his **personal
life** (a family situation weighing on him). You need to diagnose each and plan a response.

**Diagnose each and plan each response.** Then note the principles and mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong response recognizes the three causes need three different responses (motivation is per-person) and
addresses each specifically. Example:
<br><br>
<strong>Ava — stalled growth (mastery):</strong> "Ava's disengagement is a <em>mastery/growth</em> problem —
she's capable but doing the same work, not developing, so she's bored and unchallenged. The response is to
give her <strong>growth and challenge</strong>: a stretch assignment (Lesson 31), a new domain, more scope,
a leadership opportunity, or a hard technical problem — something that re-engages her by letting her grow and
be challenged. Talk with her about where she wants to grow (career conversation, Lesson 32) and aim her work
there. The fix is renewed mastery/growth." <em>(Growth stall → provide challenge/growth.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Ben — meaningless work (purpose):</strong> "Ben's is a <em>purpose</em> problem — he doesn't see the
point of his work (a meaningless/deprioritized project). Two possible responses: (1) if the work <em>does</em>
matter but he doesn't see why, <strong>connect him to the purpose</strong> — explain the real impact/why
(maybe he's missing context, and it's more meaningful than he realizes — share the why, Lesson 40). (2) If
the work genuinely <em>is</em> pointless (a deprioritized effort nobody uses), that's a real problem — <strong>move
him to work that matters</strong> (why is he on a pointless project? re-assign him to meaningful work). Don't
just tell him to care about meaningless work — either surface the real purpose or give him purposeful work."
<em>(Purpose problem → connect to real purpose, or move to meaningful work.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Carlos — personal life (life circumstances):</strong> "Carlos's is a <em>life circumstances</em>
issue — a personal/family situation, not a work motivation problem per se. The response is <strong>support
and flexibility, human care</strong>: 'I've noticed things have been tough — I'm here, and your wellbeing
matters more than any deadline. What do you need — reduced load, flexibility, time off?' Support him through
it with compassion and patience (this is temporary and human — not a performance issue to fix, but a person
to support). Don't push growth or purpose at him — he needs support, not motivation-tactics." <em>(Life
circumstances → support, flexibility, human care — not motivation tactics.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>The key point:</strong> "Three disengaged engineers, three <em>different</em> causes, three <em>different</em>
responses — motivation is diagnosed per-person, not team-wide. The wrong move would be a generic 'motivate
the team' (a team-building event, a pep talk) — which addresses none of their actual, different causes. Each
needs their specific cause addressed: growth for Ava, purpose for Ben, support for Carlos."
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Diagnose per-person</strong> — three causes (stalled growth,
meaningless work, life circumstances), three responses; motivation is individual, not a team-wide lever. (2)
<strong>Match the response to the cause</strong> — mastery/growth for Ava, purpose (connect or move) for Ben,
support for Carlos. (3) <strong>Autonomy/mastery/purpose lens</strong> — Ava's is mastery, Ben's is purpose;
Carlos's is a life issue (not a motivation problem to solve with tactics). (4) <strong>Don't push motivation
tactics at a life-circumstances issue</strong> — Carlos needs human support, not growth/purpose talk. (5)
<strong>Address the real cause</strong> — for Ben, honestly assess whether the work matters (connect to
purpose) or is genuinely pointless (move him). <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>a generic
team-wide "motivation" response</strong> (pep talk, team event) — addresses none of the three specific causes;
(2) <strong>the same response for all three</strong> — motivation is per-person; (3) <strong>pushing growth/
purpose at Carlos</strong> — misreading a life issue as a motivation problem (he needs support); (4)
<strong>telling Ben to just care</strong> about meaningless work (address the purpose — connect or move); (5)
<strong>ignoring Ava's stall</strong> — losing a strong person to boredom (give her growth); (6) <strong>not
diagnosing</strong> — assuming all disengagement is the same or a personal failing rather than a specific,
addressable cause.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What are the real drivers of motivation for knowledge work, and why is motivation "diagnosed
per-person, not team-wide"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The real drivers of motivation for knowledge work are <strong>autonomy, mastery, and purpose</strong> (from
Dan Pink's <em>Drive</em> and self-determination theory) — the <em>intrinsic</em> motivators, not extrinsic
carrots-and-sticks: (1) <strong>Autonomy</strong> — having control over how you do your work (making your own
decisions, owning your work), versus being micromanaged and dictated to; engineers are motivated by owning
their work. (2) <strong>Mastery</strong> — getting better at something that matters (developing skill, being
challenged, growing); engineers are motivated by learning and improving at meaningful work. (3) <strong>Purpose</strong>
— doing work that means something (that has impact, serves a purpose, matters); engineers are motivated by
meaningful work. These intrinsic motivators drive knowledge work far more than extrinsic rewards/punishments
(carrots and sticks) — in fact, extrinsic motivators can <em>undermine</em> intrinsic motivation for creative/
knowledge work (e.g., over-emphasizing bonuses can reduce the intrinsic satisfaction). So supporting
motivation means providing autonomy (don't micromanage — let people own their work), opportunities for mastery
(growth, challenge, skill development), and connection to purpose (why the work matters, its impact) — the
intrinsic drivers — rather than relying on carrots and sticks. Why motivation is <strong>diagnosed per-person,
not team-wide</strong>: <strong>because different people are driven by different things and demotivated by
different things, so a one-size-fits-all approach fails — you have to understand what drives (and drains) each
individual and respond to them specifically</strong>. Although autonomy/mastery/purpose are the general
drivers, the <em>specifics</em> vary hugely by person: one person is most driven by growth (mastery), another
by autonomy, another by the mission (purpose), another by recognition, another by work-life balance, another
by technical challenge, another by impact. And what <em>demotivates</em> them differs too. So you cannot
"motivate the team" with a single generic action (a pep talk, a team event, a blanket policy) — because such
a generic approach addresses no one's <em>specific</em> driver or demotivator (as in the scenario: three
disengaged engineers with three different causes — stalled growth, meaningless work, a life circumstance —
need three different responses; a generic "motivate the team" fixes none of them). Instead, motivation must be
<strong>diagnosed per-person</strong>: understand what drives <em>this</em> individual (what energizes them,
what they care about, what they want) and what's draining <em>them</em> (their specific demotivator or issue),
and respond to that specifically. This requires knowing your people individually (via 1:1s — Lesson 24 — and
listening — Lesson 25): what does this person care about, what's energizing or draining them right now? Then
address <em>their</em> specific situation (growth for the bored person, purpose for the person on meaningless
work, support for the person with a life issue, autonomy for the micromanaged person). The per-person diagnosis
is essential because motivation is individual — the generic team-wide approach is a category error (treating a
per-person thing as a team-wide lever), which is why "motivate the team" efforts (pep talks, events) usually
fail to move the needle (they don't address anyone's actual driver/demotivator). So the two points together:
the general drivers are autonomy/mastery/purpose (intrinsic, for knowledge work — provide these, not carrots/
sticks), but the <em>application</em> is per-person (diagnose each individual's specific drivers and demotivators
and respond specifically) — because motivation is individual, and effective motivation-support requires knowing
and responding to each person, not a one-size-fits-all team-wide approach. This makes supporting motivation a
relational, individualized part of managing (knowing your people and addressing their specific situations),
grounded in the intrinsic drivers but applied person-by-person.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is "a huge part of motivating is NOT demotivating," and what are the demotivators a manager
controls?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"A huge part of motivating is NOT demotivating" because <strong>people usually start out motivated, and the
manager's biggest impact on motivation is often not adding motivation but avoiding <em>destroying</em> the
motivation people already have — much of which is killed by things the manager controls and accidentally
inflicts</strong>. The common assumption is that managers need to "motivate" people — to inject motivation
that isn't there (via pep talks, incentives, motivational tactics). But most people, especially engineers who
chose their field, <em>start motivated</em> — they want to do good work, they care about their craft, they're
intrinsically engaged. So the manager rarely needs to <em>add</em> motivation from scratch; more often, the
issue is that something has <em>killed</em> the motivation people started with — and frequently, that
something is the manager (or the environment the manager controls). So the highest-leverage motivation work
is usually <em>not demotivating</em>: removing or avoiding the things that destroy the motivation people
already have. This reframe is important because it directs attention correctly: instead of asking "how do I
motivate this demotivated person?" (implying you need to add something), ask "what has demotivated them, and
what am I (or the environment) doing to kill their motivation?" (implying you need to remove something) —
which is usually more accurate and more actionable. People are motivated until something breaks it, and that
something is often a manager-controlled demotivator, so a huge part of "motivating" is <em>stopping</em>
demotivating. The demotivators a manager controls (and can accidentally inflict): (1) <strong>micromanagement</strong>
— removing people's autonomy by dictating how they work, second-guessing, controlling every detail — which is
the fastest way to demotivate a capable person (it attacks autonomy, a core driver, and signals distrust). (2)
<strong>churn/thrash</strong> — constantly changing direction, causing wasted work and chaos — nothing is more
demoralizing than doing work that gets thrown away or having priorities whipsaw (it destroys the sense that
work matters, and wastes effort). (3) <strong>invisible work / lack of recognition</strong> — not seeing or
acknowledging people's contributions, so they feel unseen and unvalued (Lesson 20) — feeling that your work
goes unnoticed is deeply demotivating. (4) <strong>broken promises</strong> — not following through on
commitments to people (a promised opportunity, a raise, a change) — which destroys trust and the sense that
effort will be reciprocated. Plus: unclear priorities (people don't know what matters), no growth
opportunities (mastery blocked), blocked autonomy, pointless bureaucracy/process, and unfairness. The crucial
point is that these are things the manager <strong>controls</strong> — micromanaging, thrashing on direction,
failing to recognize work, breaking promises are all manager behaviors — so the manager is often the
<em>source</em> of the demotivation, frequently without realizing it (accidentally inflicting it). So the
practical guidance: <strong>look first at what you might be doing to demotivate</strong> — are you
micromanaging (killing autonomy)? causing churn (wasting people's work)? failing to recognize contributions
(invisible work)? breaking promises (losing trust)? — and stop doing those, because removing the
manager-controlled demotivators is often the highest-leverage motivation work (you're restoring the motivation
people had, which you were inadvertently killing). This reframe — a huge part of motivating is not
demotivating, and the biggest demotivators are manager-controlled — is powerful because it (a) correctly
locates much of the motivation problem in the manager's own behavior (humbling but actionable), (b) shifts the
focus from adding motivation (often futile) to removing demotivators (often effective), and (c) is empowering
(you have direct control over these — you can stop micromanaging, reduce churn, recognize work, keep promises).
Combined with the per-person diagnosis (Q1) and providing autonomy/mastery/purpose, this makes supporting
motivation largely about not killing the motivation people bring — which starts with honestly examining what
you, the manager, might be doing to demotivate them. Motivation is often less about inspiring people than about
getting out of the way and not crushing the motivation they already have.
</details>
