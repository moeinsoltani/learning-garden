# Lesson 02: Lead vs EM vs Staff — the Tracks

## Concept

"Moving up" from senior engineer isn't one path — it's a fork with several
prongs, and knowing which one you're actually on (or aiming for) prevents years
of quiet misalignment. The three broad directions:

```
                        SENIOR ENGINEER
                               │
        ┌──────────────────────┼──────────────────────┐
        ▼                      ▼                      ▼
   TECH LEAD /            ENGINEERING           STAFF / PRINCIPAL
   TEAM LEAD             MANAGER (EM)            (IC track)
   ───────────           ────────────           ─────────────────
   leads a team's        owns PEOPLE: hiring,   deep technical
   technical direction   performance, careers,  leadership WITHOUT
   + delivery; usually   1:1s, org health;      direct reports;
   still codes 30-50%;   codes rarely; success  influence via
   people responsibility  = team health +       architecture,
   is light/shared        delivery              mentorship, and scope
```

Two axes distinguish them: **people responsibility** (do you own others'
careers, reviews, and hiring?) and **technical depth** (how hands-on and
technically deep is the daily work?). A tech lead is high-technical,
light-people. An EM is (increasingly) light-technical, high-people. A
Staff/Principal engineer is high-technical, and influences broadly *without*
managing anyone.

Why this matters now: the "lead" role you're moving into is often ambiguous —
at some companies "team lead" means "senior engineer who also runs standup,"
at others it's a full people-manager, at others a stepping stone to EM. If you
aim at "lead" without knowing which version your company means, you can end up
doing a job you didn't want (drowning in people-work you find draining, or
starved of the technical depth you love). Aiming deliberately — and having the
role-expectations conversation explicitly — is the point of this lesson.

---

## How It Works

### The three roles, concretely

- **Tech Lead / Team Lead**: responsible for a team's technical direction and
  delivery — architecture, breaking down work, unblocking, quality, and shipping
  — while usually retaining significant hands-on coding (30–50%). People
  responsibility is light: you influence and grow the team technically, but a
  separate EM often owns formal careers/reviews/hiring (or it's shared). This is
  frequently the *first* step from senior and the one that keeps you closest to
  the technical work.

- **Engineering Manager (EM)**: owns the *people* — hiring, performance
  management, career development, 1:1s, team health, and process. Coding becomes
  rare (and doing it on the critical path is usually a mistake — Lesson 03). The
  daily work is conversations, planning, and organizational problems. Camille
  Fournier's *The Manager's Path* is the canonical map of this track.

- **Staff / Principal Engineer (IC track)**: the answer to "I want more impact
  and scope but don't want to manage people." You lead technically across teams
  — driving big architectural decisions, setting technical strategy, mentoring
  senior engineers, being the person the org trusts on hard calls — with
  *influence, not authority* (Phase 7). Will Larson's *Staff Engineer* and
  StaffEng.com map this track, which many companies formalized precisely so that
  becoming senior+ doesn't force you into management.

### The dual-track ladder

Good engineering orgs have a **dual ladder**: the management track (Lead → EM →
Director → VP) and the IC track (Senior → Staff → Principal → Distinguished)
run in *parallel*, with equivalent seniority, pay, and respect at each rung.
This exists so that your best engineers don't have to stop being engineers to
advance — a Principal Engineer is as senior as a Director. Know whether your
company has a real dual ladder or just pays lip service to it (a tell: are there
actual Staff/Principal engineers with real influence, or does everyone senior
eventually become a manager?).

### The pendulum is normal

Fournier's insight: many careers *oscillate* between IC and management over
time — you might do a few years as an EM, then swing back to Staff IC, then
back. This isn't failure or indecision; each swing builds skills the other uses
(management makes you a better Staff engineer at influence and communication; a
Staff stint keeps a manager technically credible). Treat "which track" as a
*current* choice, revisable, not a life sentence (Lesson 67 returns to this).

{: .note }
> **What does YOUR company mean by "lead"?**
> The single most useful thing you can do with this lesson: find out what the
> title you're being offered (or aiming for) <em>actually</em> means at your
> company. Ask your manager directly: "In this role, roughly what % is coding
> vs people-work vs technical-direction? Do I own anyone's performance reviews
> and hiring? Is this on the management track or a technical-lead role I could
> stay in?" The answers vary wildly between companies with the same title, and
> assuming is how people end up in the wrong job.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** Map your own organization. For your current company (or the
last one you knew well), identify: who holds each of the three roles (tech lead,
EM, staff/principal IC)? Where do the boundaries blur — e.g., is there a "lead"
who's really doing an EM's job without the title, or an EM who's still coding on
the critical path? And critically: **which role does the "lead" opportunity in
front of you (or that you're aiming for) actually correspond to?**

Write your map and your assessment of where *you* want to aim, with reasons.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
There's no single right map — the value is in doing it accurately and honestly,
because the exercise surfaces the ambiguity that derails people. A strong
response has three parts:
<br><br>
<strong>The map:</strong> name real people (or roles) in each bucket, and be
specific about the <em>blurred</em> cases — those are the informative ones.
Common real-world patterns you might find: a "tech lead" who's quietly become a
de-facto people manager (doing 1:1s, career conversations, hiring input) without
the title, pay, or support of an EM — a bad and common situation (the
"accidental manager"); an EM who still codes on the critical path because they
can't let go (Lesson 03) and consequently does neither job well; a company with
<em>no</em> real Staff/Principal engineers, where the only way "up" is
management — a red flag if you love the technical work; or a healthy dual ladder
where Staff engineers have genuine cross-team influence and equal standing to
managers.
<br><br>
<strong>Which role is "your" opportunity:</strong> this is the load-bearing part.
If you're being offered "team lead," dig into what it means <em>here</em> — the
lesson's questions (% coding, do you own reviews/hiring, is it management-track).
The mistake is assuming; the move is asking your manager explicitly and mapping
the honest answer onto the three roles. If the "lead" role is really an EM role
in disguise (owns people, little coding), and you took it thinking you'd stay
hands-on technical, you'll be miserable — and vice versa.
<br><br>
<strong>Where you want to aim, with reasons:</strong> a good answer connects the
choice to what actually energizes vs drains <em>you</em> (Lesson 67 develops
this) — not to status or default paths. "I want tech lead because I get energy
from technical direction and mentoring but I'd find pure people-management
draining, and I want to stay hands-on enough to keep my technical judgment
sharp" is a deliberate, defensible aim. "I want to be a manager because that's
how you advance here" is the trap — advancing into a job you don't want. The
best answers also acknowledge the pendulum: "I'm aiming at tech lead now, but I
could see trying EM in a few years to build the people skills, then possibly
swinging to Staff" — treating it as a revisable current choice. If your map
reveals your company has no real IC ladder and you love the technical work,
that's a genuine finding: it may mean this isn't the place to grow the way you
want, which is worth knowing now rather than after five years of being pushed
toward management.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Your company offers you a "Team Lead" title. What are the three
most important questions to ask before accepting, and why does each one matter?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
(1) <strong>"What's the rough split between coding, people-work, and technical
direction?"</strong> — because "team lead" ranges from "senior engineer who runs
standup and still codes 60%" to "de-facto manager who codes 10%," and the answer
determines whether you'll be doing work you enjoy or work that drains you. If you
love hands-on technical work and the honest answer is "20% coding," you need to
know before, not after. (2) <strong>"Do I own anyone's performance reviews,
career development, and hiring decisions?"</strong> — this is the sharpest line
between a tech-lead role and a management role, and it carries real weight:
owning people's careers is a serious responsibility with emotional and
time costs, and doing it <em>without</em> the title, training, pay, and support
of a real EM role (the "accidental manager") is a genuinely bad situation to
walk into unknowingly. The answer tells you which of the three roles this
actually is. (3) <strong>"Is this on the management track, or a technical-lead
role I could stay in long-term — and does the company have a real Staff/Principal
IC ladder?"</strong> — because it reveals whether accepting quietly commits you
to eventually managing (if lead is just a stepping stone to EM and there's no IC
ladder, you're being routed toward management whether you want it or not), or
whether you can grow technically without managing. It surfaces whether your
company's advancement paths match what you want. Together these three questions
resolve the ambiguity the lesson warns about — turning a vague title into a
concrete understanding of what job you'd actually be doing and where it leads —
and asking them also signals to your manager that you're thinking about the role
deliberately, which is itself a good look for someone stepping up.
</details>

**Q2.** Explain the "engineer/manager pendulum" and why swinging between the
tracks is described as a strength rather than a lack of commitment.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The pendulum (Charity Majors / Camille Fournier) is the observation that many
strong careers <em>oscillate</em> between individual-contributor and management
roles over time — a few years as an EM, then a swing back to a Staff/Principal
IC role, perhaps back to management later — rather than picking one track at 30
and climbing it forever. It's framed as a strength for two reasons. First,
<strong>each side builds skills the other depends on and that atrophy without
use</strong>: a management stint makes you a dramatically better Staff engineer
at the things Staff work actually requires — influence without authority,
communication, navigating organizational dynamics, understanding what leadership
cares about (all of Phases 3, 7, 8) — while an IC stint keeps a manager
technically credible and grounded in the real work their team does (preventing
the out-of-touch manager who hasn't written code in a decade and makes
disconnected decisions). The person who has done both is more capable in either
role than someone who has only done one. Second, <strong>it prevents the trap of
climbing a ladder you don't actually enjoy</strong>: treating the choice as
permanent pressures people to stay in a role that's draining them (a great
engineer miserable in management, sticking it out because "going back" feels like
failure) — whereas the pendulum framing makes swinging back a normal, even
savvy, career move, not a demotion or an admission of failure. The honest
mechanics also support it: the two roles genuinely require different day-to-day
energy (people-work vs technical depth), and what energizes you can change with
life circumstances, team situations, and your own growth — so a revisable choice
fits reality better than a life sentence. The practical implication (Lesson 67):
hold "which track" as a <em>current</em> decision with an explicit re-evaluation
horizon, not a one-way door — and don't let sunk-cost or status keep you in a
role that's wrong for you now, because moving is a swing of the pendulum, not a
fall off the ladder.
</details>

## Homework

Write two short job descriptions — one for the version of "lead/EM/Staff" you're
*aiming* at, and one for the version you'd actively want to *avoid* — each in
terms of the daily reality (how much coding, how much people-work, what you'd
spend Monday morning doing, what would energize vs drain you). Then compare them
against what you know (or can find out) about the actual role in front of you or
at your company. What's the gap, and what would you need to confirm before
committing?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise forces the specificity that vague aspiration ("I want to be a
lead") hides. A strong response makes the two descriptions concrete and
<em>personal</em> — grounded in your own energy, not generic role definitions.
Example shape: <strong>Aiming at</strong> — "Tech lead of my current team:
~40% hands-on (architecture, hard problems, code review as my main technical
surface — Lesson 28), ~30% technical direction (breaking down work, design
reviews, unblocking), ~30% growing the team technically (mentoring, pairing).
Monday morning: reviewing designs and unblocking, then a focused block on a
gnarly technical problem. Energizes me: solving hard technical problems <em>with</em>
and <em>through</em> people, seeing engineers I mentored level up. I keep enough
hands-on work to stay credible and satisfied." <strong>Avoiding</strong> —
"Full EM: ~10% coding, ~60% people-work (1:1s, performance management, hiring,
conflict, org politics), ~30% process/planning. Monday morning: back-to-back
1:1s and a difficult performance conversation. Drains me: I'd miss the technical
depth, and I find prolonged people-conflict work exhausting rather than
energizing." (Yours might be the reverse — someone who's energized by people-work
and drained by being the technical bottleneck should aim at EM and avoid staying
a hands-on lead. The point is <em>your</em> honest energy, not a value judgment
about the roles.) Then the comparison: map the actual role in front of you onto
these. The valuable outcome is finding the <em>gap</em> and the <em>unknowns</em>
— "the 'team lead' role I'm being offered — I actually don't know if it's closer
to my aiming-at description or my avoiding description, because I don't know the
coding %, whether I'd own reviews/hiring, or if it's management-track." That gap
is your action item: the specific things to confirm with your manager (Q1's
questions) before committing. A weak response stays abstract ("I want more
impact"); a strong one produces a checklist of concrete unknowns to resolve and
a clear-eyed read on whether the available role matches what actually energizes
you — which is the entire point of aiming deliberately rather than defaulting
into whatever "up" means at your company. If doing this reveals the role is
your avoid-description, that's not a disappointment — it's the exercise working,
saving you from a job that would make you miserable, and pointing you toward
either negotiating the role's shape or looking for the version you actually want
(possibly the IC/Staff track if your company has one — Lesson 67).
</details>
