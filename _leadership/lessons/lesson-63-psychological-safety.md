# Lesson 63: Psychological Safety and Culture

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **psychological safety** — the shared belief that it's safe to take *interpersonal risks*: ask, admit mistakes, disagree, raise problems (Amy Edmondson's term).
> - **interpersonal risk** — an act that could make you look bad to colleagues (a "dumb" question, a confessed mistake).
> - **Project Aristotle** — Google's study finding psychological safety the #1 predictor of team effectiveness.
> - **not niceness** — the key correction: safety coexists with high standards; it's honesty without fear, not comfort.
> - **complacency** — comfortable low standards (safety *without* standards).
> - **the flinch** — the small visible negative reaction that teaches people not to bring you truth (Lesson 21).
> - **punishing the messenger** — reacting badly to whoever reports the problem, guaranteeing you stop hearing about problems.
> - **"culture is what you tolerate"** — culture equals the worst behaviour you allow to continue, not the values poster.
> - **micro-behaviors** — the small everyday reactions that build or destroy safety.
> - **safe-to-fail** — designed so failure is survivable and instructive (blameless retros, experiments).

## Concept

**Psychological safety** — the shared belief that it's safe to take interpersonal risks (ask questions,
admit mistakes, disagree, raise problems) — is the single biggest factor in team effectiveness (Google's
Project Aristotle found it the top predictor). It's the foundation for everything: problems surface early,
people do their best work, learning happens. Crucially, **it's not comfort or niceness** — it's the safety
to be honest and take risks. And leaders **destroy it in small ways** (a flinch at bad news, blame, cutting
people off) — so building and protecting it is constant, deliberate work.

```
   PSYCHOLOGICAL SAFETY (the foundation of team effectiveness)
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ = safe to take INTERPERSONAL RISKS (ask, admit, disagree,│
   │   raise problems) — NOT comfort/niceness/low standards │
   │ • top predictor of team effectiveness (Project Aristotle)│
   │ • leaders DESTROY it in small ways: flinches, blame,     │
   │   interrupting, "you should know that"                  │
   │ • "culture is what you TOLERATE"                        │
   │ • rituals compound: blameless retros, demos, safe-to-fail│
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The reframe: **psychological safety is the safety to take interpersonal risks (be honest, admit mistakes,
disagree) — the foundation of team effectiveness — and leaders destroy it in small, everyday ways, so
protecting it is constant deliberate work.** It's not about being comfortable or nice (safety coexists with
high standards — you can be safe AND demanding); it's about people being able to be honest without fear.
And because a single flinch, blame, or dismissal can erode it, building it requires consistent, deliberate
behavior — culture is built (or destroyed) in the small everyday moments.

---

## Going Deeper

### What psychological safety is — and isn't

**Psychological safety** (Amy Edmondson's research) is the shared belief that the team is safe for
interpersonal risk-taking — that you can ask a question, admit a mistake, disagree, raise a concern, or say
"I don't know" <em>without fear</em> of being punished, humiliated, or thought less of. Crucially, it is
**NOT**: (1) comfort (it's not about everyone being comfortable — safety enables the discomfort of honest
disagreement and hard feedback); (2) niceness (it's not superficial politeness — it's the safety to be
honestly critical); (3) low standards (this is the key misconception — psychological safety <em>coexists with
high standards</em>: the best teams are both high-safety AND high-standards — safety is what lets people meet
high standards by surfacing problems, admitting mistakes, and pushing each other honestly). Safety + high
standards = the learning/high-performance zone; safety without standards = comfort/complacency; standards
without safety = anxiety/fear. So psychological safety is about honest risk-taking, not comfort — and it
enables high performance, not excuses low performance.

### Why it's the foundation

Psychological safety is the **top predictor of team effectiveness** (Google's Project Aristotle studied what
makes teams effective and found psychological safety the #1 factor). It's foundational because it enables
everything else: (1) **problems surface early** — people raise concerns, flag risks, and admit mistakes when
it's safe (versus hiding them until they explode — the watermelon status, Lesson 55); (2) **people do their
best work** — they take creative risks, propose bold ideas, and bring their full selves when it's safe
(versus playing it safe and disengaging when it's not); (3) **learning happens** — mistakes and failures
become learning (blameless — Lesson 56) rather than things to hide; (4) **honest collaboration** — people
disagree productively, give real feedback, and ask for help. Without safety, people hide problems, don't ask
questions, don't admit mistakes, and don't take risks — so the team underperforms and problems fester.
Safety is the foundation everything else (honesty, learning, performance) rests on.

### How leaders destroy it — in small ways

The critical insight: **leaders destroy psychological safety in small, everyday ways — often without
realizing.** A single reaction can teach people it's not safe: (1) a **flinch** at bad news or a mistake (a
frown, visible displeasure — teaches "don't bring problems"); (2) **blame** (blaming a person for a
mistake — teaches "hide mistakes"); (3) **interrupting/dismissing** (cutting people off, dismissing ideas —
teaches "don't speak up"); (4) **"you should know that"** (making someone feel stupid for a question —
teaches "don't ask questions"); (5) punishing the messenger, public criticism, reacting badly to
disagreement. These small moments (magnified when they come from the leader, whom everyone watches) teach
people what's safe — and a few bad reactions can shut people down (as in receiving feedback, Lesson 21). So
protecting safety requires <em>consistent</em> good behavior in the small moments: welcome questions, thank
people for raising problems, respond well to mistakes and disagreement, don't flinch/blame/dismiss. Safety is
destroyed or built in the everyday micro-behaviors.

### Culture is what you tolerate

A powerful principle: **"culture is what you tolerate."** Culture isn't the values on the wall or what you
say — it's what actually happens, and specifically what behaviors you <em>tolerate</em> (allow to continue).
If you tolerate a toxic-but-productive person, disrespect, blame, or people being cut down for speaking up,
<em>that</em> is your culture (regardless of stated values). If you don't tolerate those (address them) and
you reinforce the behaviors you want (safety, respect, honesty), that becomes your culture. So culture is
shaped by what you allow and address — the behaviors you tolerate are what you're endorsing. This means
building a safe culture requires <em>not tolerating</em> the behaviors that destroy it (addressing the
person who cuts others down, even if productive) and consistently modeling/reinforcing the behaviors that
build it.

### Rituals that compound, and onboarding as culture transmission

Culture is reinforced by **rituals that compound** over time: blameless retrospectives (safe reflection and
learning), demos (celebrating and sharing work), "failure parties"/blameless postmortems (normalizing that
failure is learning — Lesson 56), norms that make it safe to ask and disagree. These regular practices,
repeated, build and reinforce the culture (each retro that's genuinely blameless teaches that it's safe to
reflect honestly). And **onboarding is culture transmission** — how you bring new people in teaches them the
culture (a new person watching how the team handles a mistake, whether questions are welcomed, how people
treat each other learns the real culture fast). So deliberately transmit the culture through onboarding (and
by modeling it visibly). Culture is built through consistent behavior and reinforcing rituals over time — not
declared once.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** You've taken over a **new team**, and you notice troubling signs: **nobody asks questions
in meetings** (silence when you ask "any questions?"), **people don't admit mistakes** or raise problems
(and consequently **mistakes surface weeks late**, after they've grown), and there's a general sense that
people are guarded and playing it safe. This is a **psychological safety problem** — the team doesn't feel
safe to be honest. You need to diagnose it and build safety.

**Diagnose and write your 90-day culture plan** to build psychological safety. Then note the principles and
mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong plan diagnoses the lack of safety, and builds it through consistent modeling, addressing the causes,
and reinforcing rituals — over time (safety is built through repeated behavior). Example:
<br><br>
<strong>Diagnose (why is it unsafe?):</strong> "The signs — no questions, hidden mistakes, late-surfacing
problems, guardedness — are classic low psychological safety: people don't feel safe to be honest. Understand
why: was the previous leadership punishing (blame, flinches, humiliation)? Is there a history that taught
people it's not safe? Talk to people (1:1s — build trust, ask what it's been like, listen). The team learned
somewhere that honesty is punished; I need to teach the opposite — which takes consistent behavior over
time, not a one-time declaration."
<br><br>
<strong>Days 1-30 — model safety and build trust:</strong> "Start by <em>modeling</em> and <em>building
trust</em>: (1) Respond well every time someone raises a problem, asks a question, or admits a mistake — thank
them, never flinch/blame/dismiss (every reaction teaches what's safe — Lesson 21). (2) Model vulnerability
myself — admit my own mistakes and say 'I don't know' openly (a leader modeling fallibility makes it safe for
others). (3) Explicitly invite input and make it safe — 'I really want to hear questions and concerns, and
there's no such thing as a dumb question here.' (4) 1:1s to build individual trust and understand the history.
Consistency is key — one flinch undoes progress."
<br><br>
<strong>Days 30-60 — reinforce with rituals and address the culture:</strong> "(1) Introduce/strengthen
blameless rituals: blameless retrospectives (safe reflection), blameless postmortems (Lesson 56 — mistakes as
learning, not blame), demos (celebrate work). (2) Actively reward the behaviors I want — publicly appreciate
someone who raises a problem or admits a mistake ('thank you for flagging that early — that's exactly what we
want'). (3) 'Culture is what you tolerate' — address anything that destroys safety (if someone cuts others
down or blames, address it — don't tolerate the safety-destroying behavior). (4) Keep modeling relentlessly."
<br><br>
<strong>Days 60-90 — embed and sustain:</strong> "(1) The rituals and norms should be taking hold — people
starting to ask questions, raise problems earlier, admit mistakes. Reinforce and sustain (safety is fragile —
keep protecting it). (2) Watch for problems surfacing earlier (the key outcome — the late-mistakes problem
improving). (3) Onboard any new people into the safe culture deliberately. (4) Recognize that this is ongoing
— 90 days builds momentum, but safety requires permanent consistent behavior."
<br><br>
<strong>Throughout — the key principles:</strong> "Safety is built through <em>consistent behavior over
time</em> (not a declaration) — every small moment (how I react to a mistake, a question, a disagreement)
teaches what's safe, so relentless consistency matters. And it's not about lowering standards — I want a safe
AND high-standard team (safety is what lets us meet high standards by surfacing problems honestly)."
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Diagnose the cause</strong> — the team learned somewhere that honesty
is punished; understand the history. (2) <strong>Model safety relentlessly</strong> — respond well to every
question/mistake/problem, model vulnerability, never flinch/blame/dismiss (every reaction teaches). (3)
<strong>Build over time, consistently</strong> — safety is built through repeated behavior, not a one-time
declaration; one flinch undoes progress. (4) <strong>Reinforce with rituals</strong> — blameless retros/
postmortems, demos, rewarding the behaviors you want. (5) <strong>"Culture is what you tolerate"</strong> —
address safety-destroying behaviors, reinforce safety-building ones. (6) <strong>Safety + high standards</strong>
— not lowering the bar; safety enables meeting it. (7) <strong>Measure by problems surfacing earlier</strong>.
<strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>declaring safety once</strong> ('this is a safe space!') and
expecting it — safety is built through consistent behavior, not announced; (2) <strong>one bad reaction</strong>
— a single flinch/blame/dismissal undoes progress (relentless consistency needed); (3) <strong>confusing
safety with comfort/low standards</strong> — thinking it means being soft (it's safety to be honest, with high
standards); (4) <strong>not diagnosing the cause</strong> — not understanding why it's unsafe (the history);
(5) <strong>tolerating safety-destroying behavior</strong> — allowing a person who cuts others down (culture
is what you tolerate); (6) <strong>impatience</strong> — expecting instant change (safety is built over time,
carefully); (7) <strong>not modeling vulnerability</strong> — expecting others to be open while being closed
yourself.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What is psychological safety (and what is it NOT), and why is it the foundation of team effectiveness?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Psychological safety</strong> (Amy Edmondson's research) is <strong>the shared belief that the team is
safe for interpersonal risk-taking — that you can ask a question, admit a mistake, disagree, raise a concern,
or say "I don't know" without fear of being punished, humiliated, or thought less of</strong>. It's about
whether people feel safe to take the interpersonal risks that honesty and learning require (speaking up,
admitting fallibility, challenging, surfacing problems) — safe from the social/professional punishment
(embarrassment, blame, being judged) that would otherwise make people stay silent and guarded. What it is
<strong>NOT</strong> (crucial misconceptions): (1) <strong>it's not comfort</strong> — psychological safety
isn't about everyone being comfortable; in fact it <em>enables</em> the discomfort of honest disagreement,
hard feedback, and challenging conversations (a safe team has <em>more</em> uncomfortable honesty, not less —
people can raise hard things); (2) <strong>it's not niceness</strong> — it's not superficial politeness or
everyone being agreeable; it's the safety to be honestly <em>critical</em> and to disagree (a "nice" team
that avoids conflict may actually be <em>unsafe</em> — people don't feel safe to disagree, so they're falsely
nice); (3) <strong>it's not low standards</strong> — this is the most important misconception:
psychological safety <em>coexists with high standards</em>, and the best teams are <em>both</em> high-safety
AND high-standards. Edmondson's framing: safety + high standards = the <strong>learning/high-performance
zone</strong> (people take risks, surface problems, and push each other to meet high standards); safety
without standards = comfort/complacency (nice but low-performing); standards without safety = anxiety/fear
(high pressure but people hide problems); neither = apathy. So safety doesn't mean lowering the bar — it's
what <em>enables</em> meeting a high bar (by making it safe to surface the problems, mistakes, and honest
feedback that high performance requires). Safety and high standards are complementary, not opposed. So
psychological safety is specifically about safe honest risk-taking (not comfort, niceness, or low standards)
— you can be safe AND demanding. Why it's <strong>the foundation of team effectiveness</strong>: <strong>because
it enables everything else that makes a team effective — problems surfacing early, best work, learning, and
honest collaboration — and its absence causes people to hide problems, not ask, not admit mistakes, and not
take risks, which cripples the team</strong>. Google's Project Aristotle (a large study of what makes teams
effective) found psychological safety the <em>top</em> predictor of team effectiveness — above talent,
resources, or other factors — because it's foundational to how a team functions: (1) <strong>problems surface
early</strong> — when it's safe, people raise concerns, flag risks, and admit mistakes early (when they're
cheap to address), versus hiding them until they explode (the watermelon status, late-surfacing mistakes —
the scenario's problem); (2) <strong>people do their best work</strong> — they take creative risks, propose
bold ideas, ask for help, and bring their full engagement when safe, versus playing it safe and disengaging
when unsafe; (3) <strong>learning happens</strong> — mistakes and failures become learning opportunities
(blameless) rather than things to hide, so the team improves; (4) <strong>honest collaboration</strong> —
people disagree productively, give real feedback, and challenge each other's thinking (which produces better
decisions) when safe, versus false harmony or silence when unsafe. Without safety, the opposite happens:
people hide problems (which fester and explode), don't ask questions (so they stay confused or make
avoidable errors), don't admit mistakes (so they recur and aren't learned from), don't disagree (so bad
decisions go unchallenged), and don't take risks (so no innovation) — the team underperforms and problems
accumulate silently. So psychological safety is the foundation because it's the precondition for the honesty,
learning, risk-taking, and collaboration that effective teams need — everything else (talent, process,
strategy) is undermined if people don't feel safe to be honest. It's foundational, not a nice-to-have — the
top predictor of effectiveness — because a team where people can't be honest can't function well regardless
of its other strengths. And correctly understanding it (safe honest risk-taking, coexisting with high
standards — not comfort or low standards) is essential to building it right (a "safe" team with low standards
is complacent, not high-performing; the goal is safe AND demanding — the learning/high-performance zone).
</details>

**Q2.** How do leaders "destroy psychological safety in small ways," and what does "culture is what you
tolerate" mean for building it?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Leaders destroy psychological safety in <strong>small, everyday ways — often without realizing — because a
single reaction can teach people it's not safe, and these micro-behaviors are magnified when they come from
the leader (whom everyone watches)</strong>. Safety is created or destroyed in the small moments of how the
leader responds when someone takes an interpersonal risk, and a few bad reactions teach people to stop.
Examples of the small ways leaders destroy it: (1) a <strong>flinch at bad news or a mistake</strong> — a
frown, a sigh, visible displeasure when someone brings a problem or admits an error — teaches "don't bring
problems / don't admit mistakes" (people learn the leader reacts badly, so they hide things); (2)
<strong>blame</strong> — blaming a person for a mistake (rather than treating it as a systemic learning —
Lesson 56) — teaches "hide mistakes, they'll be blamed on you"; (3) <strong>interrupting or dismissing</strong>
— cutting people off, dismissing ideas or concerns — teaches "don't bother speaking up, you'll be shut down";
(4) <strong>"you should know that"</strong> — making someone feel stupid for asking a question — teaches
"don't ask questions, you'll look dumb"; (5) punishing the messenger, public criticism, reacting defensively
to disagreement — each teaches a specific "don't do this" lesson. These are <em>small</em> (a flinch, a
dismissive comment, a moment of visible displeasure) and often <em>unintentional</em> (the leader doesn't
realize the impact), but they're powerful because: (a) <strong>people watch the leader closely</strong> (the
leader's reactions carry outsized weight — everyone reads them as signals of what's safe, magnified by the
power dynamic — Lesson 21); and (b) <strong>a few bad reactions shut people down</strong> (people are risk-
averse about interpersonal risks — one or two experiences of being flinched at, blamed, dismissed, or made to
feel stupid teaches them it's not safe, and they stop taking the risk — for a long time). So safety is
fragile: it's built slowly through consistent good behavior but can be damaged quickly by small bad
reactions. This means <strong>protecting safety requires consistent good behavior in the small everyday
moments</strong> — welcoming questions (never "you should know that"), thanking people for raising problems
(never flinching), responding well to mistakes (blameless, not blame), engaging with disagreement (not
dismissing) — relentlessly, because every micro-reaction teaches, and one flinch can undo progress. Safety is
built or destroyed in the micro-behaviors, so building it is constant, deliberate attention to how you react
in the small moments. What "<strong>culture is what you tolerate</strong>" means for building it: <strong>culture
is not the stated values or what you say — it's what actually happens, and specifically what behaviors you
<em>tolerate</em> (allow to continue) — so building a safe culture requires <em>not tolerating</em> the
behaviors that destroy safety and consistently reinforcing those that build it</strong>. The principle
captures that culture is defined by behavior, not declarations: you can say "we value psychological safety,"
but if you <em>tolerate</em> a person who cuts others down, blames, disrespects, or shuts people up (allow it
to continue without addressing it), then <em>that</em> — the tolerated behavior — is your actual culture,
regardless of the stated value. What you allow to continue is what you're effectively endorsing, and it
becomes the norm. So for building a safe culture, "culture is what you tolerate" means two things: (1)
<strong>you must NOT tolerate the behaviors that destroy safety</strong> — address the person who cuts others
down, blames, dismisses, or humiliates (even if they're productive — a toxic-but-productive person tolerated
destroys safety and signals that such behavior is acceptable); tolerating safety-destroying behavior makes it
your culture. (2) <strong>you must consistently reinforce the behaviors that build safety</strong> — model
and reward welcoming questions, admitting mistakes, raising problems, respectful disagreement (make those the
norm by consistently doing and endorsing them). So building a safe culture is an active process of addressing
what destroys it (not tolerating those behaviors) and reinforcing what builds it (modeling and rewarding
them) — because culture is the sum of what you tolerate and reinforce, not what you declare. This connects to
the small-moments point: the leader's consistent behavior (modeling safety, addressing violations) in the
everyday moments is what builds the culture — you can't declare a safe culture; you build it by relentlessly
modeling safety, responding well in the micro-moments, and not tolerating the behaviors that destroy it. Both
points show that psychological safety and culture are built (or destroyed) through consistent everyday
behavior: leaders destroy safety in small ways (so protect it in the small moments — never flinch/blame/
dismiss), and culture is what you tolerate (so address safety-destroying behaviors and reinforce
safety-building ones) — making the building of a safe, high-performing culture a matter of constant,
deliberate behavior in the everyday moments, not a one-time declaration, which is why it's demanding,
ongoing leadership work and the foundation everything else rests on.
</details>
