# Lesson 31: Assigning for Growth, Not Speed

## Concept

Who you assign work to is one of your most powerful development levers — because **the work itself is
the main way people grow.** But there's a strong pull to always give important work to whoever will do
it fastest/best (usually the same senior person), which optimizes for short-term speed at the cost of
long-term team growth. Over time, that calcifies the team: the strong get stronger and everyone else
stagnates. The skill is deliberately using assignments to *grow* people — while still delivering — which
means sometimes giving work to the person who'll grow from it, not the one who'll finish it fastest.

```
   THE FASTEST-PERSON TRAP
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ important work → always the fastest/best person       │
   │   → they get stronger, everyone else stagnates         │
   │   → team calcifies; you depend on one person more      │
   │   → short-term speed, long-term fragility & no growth  │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
   ASSIGN FOR GROWTH: sometimes give the stretch work to the
   person who'll GROW from it (with support), not the fastest.
```

The reframe: **assignments are a development tool, not just a delivery mechanism — so assign for growth,
not only speed.** Always assigning to the fastest person is locally optimal (this task ships quickest)
but globally harmful (the team never develops, and you grow dependent on one person). Deliberately
distributing stretch opportunities — giving growth-producing work to people who'll grow from it, with
support — develops the whole team, even though it's slower on any single task. You must balance delivery
and growth; you can't only do one.

---

## How It Works

### The fastest-person trap

The default pull: when important or interesting work comes up, give it to whoever will do it best/fastest
— usually the same proven senior. This feels efficient (it ships quickest, lowest risk), and on any
single task it's locally optimal. But repeated over time it's harmful: (1) the fastest person gets all
the interesting/growth work (getting even stronger), while everyone else gets the routine work (stagnating);
(2) the team **calcifies** — the capability gap widens, fewer people can do the important work, and you
grow ever more dependent on the one person; (3) it's fragile (bus factor — Lesson 34) and demoralizing
(others see the same person always gets the good stuff). The fastest-person trap trades long-term team
growth and resilience for short-term speed.

### Stretch assignments with contained blast radius

The core technique: deliberately give people **stretch assignments** (work beyond their current level,
so they grow — Lesson 27's scaffolding), but with a **contained blast radius** (chosen so that if they
struggle, the downside is limited — not the highest-stakes, can't-fail thing). This lets you grow people
through real stretch work while managing the risk: pick stretches where a stumble is recoverable (with
support and a safety net), so the growth is worth the contained risk. You don't grow people only on
safe, trivial work (no stretch) or only by betting the company on their first stretch (too risky) —
you find real stretches with limited downside.

### Rotate the glue work fairly

**Glue work** — the unglamorous, essential work that holds a team together (fixing flaky tests, writing
docs, onboarding, incident cleanup, coordination) — tends to fall unfairly: often on whoever volunteers,
or disproportionately on certain people (frequently women and underrepresented folks, a known pattern).
Two problems: it's often undervalued (so those doing it don't get credit — see Tanya Reilly's "Being
Glue"), and it's distributed unfairly. As a lead, **rotate glue work fairly** (so no one is stuck doing
all of it), and **make it visible and valued** (recognize it — Lesson 20). Fair distribution of both the
growth work and the glue work is an equity issue.

### Balance delivery pressure and growth — you must do both

The hard truth: you can't only assign for growth (you still have to deliver — some work needs the fastest
person, and delivery pressure is real) *or* only assign for speed (or the team never grows). You must
**balance both**: deliver on commitments while deliberately carving out growth opportunities. Practically:
give the highest-stakes/urgent work to whoever's best when it truly matters, but consciously distribute
the stretch opportunities (not everything has to go to the fastest person — much work is a fine stretch
for someone), and protect some growth even under delivery pressure (or you'll always default to speed and
never develop anyone). It's a both/and, held in tension deliberately.

### Track who's growing stale

A useful discipline: periodically ask, for each person, **are they growing, or going stale?** Someone who's
been doing the same-level work for a long time (not because they can't do more, but because they keep
getting the same assignments) is stagnating — a signal to deliberately give them stretch. Tracking who's
growing vs. stale (rather than just who's delivering) surfaces the people the fastest-person trap has
quietly left behind, so you can course-correct. If someone's been static for months, that's on you to
address through assignments.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** You lead a 5-person team. Looking at the last few sprints, you notice a pattern: the
interesting, high-visibility work almost always goes to **Diego**, your strongest senior — he's fast,
reliable, and it's easiest to just give it to him. Meanwhile: **Amir** (junior) gets only small routine
tickets; **Lena** (mid-level, very capable) does solid work but never gets the stretch stuff; **Priya**
(mid-level) has quietly done most of the glue work (flaky tests, docs, onboarding) for months; and Diego
is starting to get a bit overloaded and possibly bored of being the default. You're planning next sprint's
assignments.

**Plan next sprint's assignments to break the pattern** — who gets what, and why, balancing growth and
delivery. Then note the principles and mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong plan deliberately redistributes growth opportunities and glue work while still delivering.
Example:
<br><br>
<strong>The key interesting/stretch project this sprint → give it to Lena (with support), not Diego.</strong>
"Lena is very capable but never gets the stretch work — she's ready to own something meatier, and this is a
great growth opportunity. I'll give her the high-visibility feature, with a monitoring contract and Diego
available as a sounding board." <em>(Breaks the fastest-person trap — deliberately routing the growth work
to someone who'll grow from it, with a safety net.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Amir (junior) → a real stretch with contained blast radius.</strong> "Instead of only small
tickets, give Amir a slightly bigger, self-contained piece — beyond his current level but where a stumble
is recoverable (not on the critical path), with pairing/support. He needs stretch to grow, not just
routine work." <em>(Stretch + contained blast radius + scaffolding for the junior.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Priya (glue work) → rotate her OFF the glue, give her growth work, and recognize her.</strong>
"Priya's been carrying the glue work for months — that's unfair and she's not growing from it. This
sprint, rotate the glue work (spread it, maybe Diego and I take some), give Priya a growth opportunity,
and explicitly recognize the glue work she's done (to her and up — it's been invisible and undervalued)."
<em>(Fair rotation of glue work + making it visible + giving her growth.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Diego (the default) → lighten his load, and give him a DIFFERENT kind of growth.</strong> "Diego's
overloaded and possibly bored of being the default doer. Take some off him — and give him growth that's
new for <em>him</em>: maybe mentoring Amir or Lena through their stretch work (developing his leadership —
Lesson 33), or a genuinely novel technical challenge, rather than another 'the thing he's already good
at.'" <em>(Even the strong person needs growth — and shifting him toward developing others both grows him
and multiplies the team.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Delivery balance:</strong> "If there's something genuinely urgent/high-stakes this sprint that
truly needs the fastest hands, Diego still takes that — I'm balancing growth with delivery, not ignoring
delivery. But the <em>default</em> of 'everything interesting goes to Diego' is what I'm breaking." <em>(The
both/and — deliver on what's critical, but deliberately distribute growth.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Break the fastest-person trap</strong> — deliberately route
growth work to people who'll grow (Lena, Amir), not always the fastest (Diego). (2) <strong>Stretch with
contained blast radius</strong> — Amir and Lena get real stretch, chosen so a stumble is recoverable, with
support. (3) <strong>Rotate glue work fairly and make it visible</strong> — Priya off the glue, recognized
for it, given growth. (4) <strong>Even the strong person needs growth</strong> — Diego gets a new kind of
challenge (leadership/mentoring or novel work), not more of the same, and gets some load off. (5)
<strong>Balance delivery and growth</strong> — critical work still goes to the best hands, but the default
changes. (6) <strong>Address who's grown stale</strong> — Lena and Priya were quietly stagnating; this
fixes it. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>defaulting to Diego again</strong> — the trap
that calcifies the team; (2) <strong>only assigning for growth, ignoring delivery</strong> — you still
have to ship; balance both; (3) <strong>throwing Amir/Lena into stretch with no support/contained
risk</strong> — setting them up to fail (needs scaffolding and limited blast radius); (4) <strong>leaving
Priya on the glue work indefinitely</strong> — unfair, and she stagnates (and the glue stays invisible);
(5) <strong>ignoring Diego's growth/overload</strong> — the strong person also needs development and can
burn out or get bored as the perpetual default; (6) <strong>not making the glue work visible</strong> —
Priya's months of glue work going unrecognized is both unfair and demoralizing.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What is the "fastest-person trap," and why does always assigning important work to the fastest
person harm the team over time?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The <strong>fastest-person trap</strong> is the pattern of <strong>always giving important or interesting
work to whoever will do it best/fastest — usually the same proven senior — because it's the locally
efficient choice, without regard to the long-term effect on team development</strong>. When work comes up,
the pull is to assign it to the person who'll deliver it quickest and best (lowest risk, fastest ship),
which on any single task is locally optimal. Why it harms the team over time, despite being efficient
per-task: (1) <strong>it concentrates growth in one person and starves everyone else</strong> — since the
work itself is the main way people grow, always giving the interesting/challenging work to the fastest
person means <em>they</em> keep growing (getting even stronger and faster) while everyone else gets only
routine work and stagnates (they never get the stretch that would develop them). The capability gap widens:
the strong get stronger, the rest stay static. (2) <strong>the team calcifies</strong> — over time, fewer
and fewer people can do the important work (because only one person has been developing on it), so the team
becomes rigid and dependent on the one strong person, rather than a growing pool of increasingly capable
people. (3) <strong>it creates fragility (bus factor)</strong> — concentrating the important work and
capability in one person makes the team dangerously dependent on them (if they leave, go on vacation, or
burn out, no one else can do the work — Lesson 34). (4) <strong>it's demoralizing and inequitable</strong>
— the others see the same person always get the interesting/high-visibility work while they get the scraps,
which is demotivating (they have no growth path, no visible opportunities) and unfair (opportunity
concentrated in one person). (5) <strong>even the fastest person suffers</strong> — they get overloaded
(everything routes to them) and can get bored (always the default doer, never new challenges). So the trap
trades <em>short-term speed</em> (each task ships fastest) for <em>long-term harm</em>: a calcified,
fragile, inequitable team where only one person grows and everyone else stagnates, and you become
ever-more-dependent on the one person. The deep problem is that the fastest-person choice optimizes the
wrong thing: it optimizes per-task delivery speed, when a lead should also be optimizing team development
(growing more capable people over time). Locally optimal (this task) is globally harmful (the team's growth
and resilience). The escape is to recognize assignments as a development tool, not just a delivery
mechanism, and to <em>deliberately</em> distribute growth opportunities — sometimes giving work to the
person who'll grow from it rather than the one who'll finish it fastest (with support and contained risk),
accepting the short-term slowdown for the long-term payoff of a growing, resilient, equitable team. This
requires deliberate effort precisely because the default pull (give it to the fastest) is strong and feels
efficient — so without conscious counter-effort, you fall into the trap and the team calcifies. Balancing
delivery (some work does need the fastest person) with growth (deliberately distributing stretch
opportunities) is the skill; always defaulting to speed is the trap.
</details>

**Q2.** Why must a lead balance delivery and growth (rather than only one), and why is rotating glue work
fairly important?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A lead must balance delivery and growth (rather than only one) because <strong>each alone fails: only
optimizing for delivery (speed) calcifies the team and develops no one, while only optimizing for growth
ignores the real obligation to ship — so the lead has to hold both in tension</strong>. If you <strong>only
assign for speed</strong> (always the fastest person), you get the fastest-person trap: the team never
grows, calcifies, becomes fragile and dependent on one person, and others stagnate and are demoralized —
you deliver short-term but destroy long-term team capability and resilience. If you <strong>only assign for
growth</strong> (always give work to whoever will grow from it, regardless of urgency/stakes), you fail
your real delivery obligations — some work is genuinely urgent or high-stakes and needs the best hands, and
a lead who ignores delivery pressure to always prioritize growth will miss commitments and lose credibility
(and the business reality is that delivery matters). So neither extreme works: pure speed sacrifices the
team's future, pure growth sacrifices the present delivery. The lead must <strong>balance both</strong> —
deliver on commitments (giving critical/urgent work to the best hands when it truly matters) while
deliberately carving out growth opportunities (distributing stretch work to develop people, not defaulting
everything to the fastest). This is a genuine tension held deliberately: you can't fully optimize either;
you consciously trade off, protecting some growth even under delivery pressure (or you'll always default to
speed and never develop anyone — the pressure to ship always favors the fastest person, so growth requires
deliberate protection). The both/and is the skill: most work is actually a fine stretch for someone (so you
can distribute growth without sacrificing much delivery), and only the genuinely highest-stakes/urgent work
truly needs the fastest person — so with deliberate attention, you can develop people AND deliver, but it
requires consciously balancing rather than defaulting to speed. Why rotating glue work fairly is important:
<strong>glue work (the essential but unglamorous work that holds a team together) tends to be distributed
unfairly and undervalued — so rotating it fairly and making it visible addresses both an equity problem and
a recognition problem</strong>. Glue work — fixing flaky tests, writing docs, onboarding, incident cleanup,
coordination — is essential (the team falls apart without it) but unglamorous (it's not the shiny feature
work). Two problems arise: (1) <strong>it's distributed unfairly</strong> — it tends to fall on whoever
volunteers or, in a well-documented pattern, disproportionately on certain people (frequently women and
underrepresented folks), so some people get stuck doing all the glue work while others do only the visible
growth work; and (2) <strong>it's undervalued</strong> — because it's unglamorous and often invisible,
those doing it don't get credit or advancement for it (they're doing essential work that doesn't show up in
promotion cases — Tanya Reilly's "Being Glue"). This is harmful and inequitable: the people stuck on glue
work (a) don't grow (glue work is often not developmental), (b) don't get recognized (invisible,
undervalued), and (c) are held back relative to those doing the visible growth work — compounding
inequity, especially since it falls disproportionately on already-disadvantaged groups. Rotating glue work
fairly (so no one is stuck doing all of it — spread it, including the lead taking some) and making it
visible and valued (recognizing it explicitly — Lesson 20) addresses both: fair rotation means no one is
unfairly burdened or held back by it, and making it visible means those who do it get the credit they
deserve. It's an equity issue because unfair glue-work distribution systematically disadvantages certain
people (stuck on undervalued work while others grow visibly), and deliberate fair rotation counters that.
Both points reflect that assignments are a powerful lever with equity and development consequences: a lead
must balance delivery and growth (not sacrifice the team's future for speed, nor delivery for growth), and
must distribute <em>both</em> the growth work and the glue work fairly — so that opportunity and burden are
shared equitably, the whole team develops, and essential-but-unglamorous work is fairly borne and properly
valued. Assigning deliberately (for growth, fairly, balanced with delivery) is how a lead grows a healthy,
equitable, resilient team rather than a calcified, unfair one.
</details>

## Homework

Audit your assignment patterns. (1) Over the last few months, who's gotten the interesting/growth work?
Is it always the same person (the fastest-person trap)? (2) For each team member, are they growing or going
stale? Identify anyone who's been static (getting the same-level work) and needs a stretch. (3) Who's
carrying the glue work? Is it fairly distributed and visible/valued, or falling unfairly on someone? (4)
Plan one assignment change: a stretch opportunity for someone who's been overlooked (with support and
contained risk), or a fairer rotation of glue work. Reflect: are you assigning for growth or defaulting to
speed, and who on your team most needs a growth assignment they're not getting?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds deliberate assignment-for-growth and self-awareness about assignment patterns. A strong
response audits who gets the growth work (checking for the fastest-person trap), who's growing vs. going
stale, and who's carrying the glue work (fairly and visibly, or not) — then plans a concrete change. The
realizations: (1) <strong>the work is the main development tool</strong> — who you assign work to is a
powerful growth lever, so defaulting to the fastest person (the trap) means only one person grows while the
team calcifies; (2) <strong>assign for growth, with contained blast radius</strong> — deliberately give
stretch work to people who'll grow from it (with support and limited downside), not always the fastest —
even though it's slower on that task; (3) <strong>balance delivery and growth</strong> — you can't only do
one (pure speed calcifies the team, pure growth misses delivery), so consciously distribute stretch
opportunities while still delivering critical work with the best hands; (4) <strong>rotate glue work fairly
and make it visible</strong> — the essential unglamorous work tends to fall unfairly and go unvalued
(an equity issue), so distribute it fairly and recognize it; (5) <strong>track who's growing stale</strong>
— someone static for months (getting the same-level work) needs deliberate stretch. On the audit, people
commonly find the interesting work concentrates in one or two people (the trap), that some capable people
have quietly stagnated (never getting stretch), and that glue work falls unfairly on someone (undervalued).
The highest-value change is usually: deliberately route a growth opportunity to an overlooked capable person
(with support), and/or rotate glue work fairly and make it visible. The meta-point: assignments are a
development tool, not just a delivery mechanism, and the fastest-person trap (locally efficient, globally
harmful) calcifies teams — so a lead must deliberately assign for growth (stretch with contained risk),
balanced with delivery, distributing both growth work and glue work fairly. This is how the work itself
grows the whole team rather than just the strongest person, and it's a key equity lever (opportunity and
burden distributed fairly). It builds on delegation (Lesson 30) and scaffolding (Lesson 27) — the work you
delegate and assign is how people grow. The next lesson turns to knowing where each person <em>wants</em> to
go — career development conversations — so you can bend their work toward their aspirations, making the
growth assignments land on what they actually want.
</details>
