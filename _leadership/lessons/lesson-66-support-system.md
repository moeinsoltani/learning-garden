# Lesson 66: Building Your Support System

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **support system** — the deliberately built network of peers, mentors, and resources that keeps a leader learning and sane.
> - **vent** — to release frustration by talking freely; you can't do it to your own team.
> - **peer group** — fellow leads/managers facing the same problems, inside and outside your company.
> - **LeadDev / Rands Leadership Slack** — the two best-known engineering-leadership communities (conference+site / a large Slack community).
> - **mentor one step ahead** — someone who has already done your *next* job, not just your current one.
> - **executive/leadership coach** — a paid professional thinking-partner for your growth; a strength, not a weakness.
> - **stigma** — social shame attached to something (therapy, coaching) that stops people using it.
> - **Manager Tools** — a long-running management podcast and framework.
> - **decision journal** — recording big decisions (what, why, expected outcome, confidence) and reviewing later to improve your judgment.
> - **derail** — to be knocked off course; what isolation does to new leaders.

## Concept

Leadership is lonelier than individual contribution — you can't vent to your team, your problems are more
ambiguous, and you're expected to have answers. Trying to learn it all alone is the **slowest possible way**
(and the loneliest). The antidote is a deliberate **support system**: peers who get it (other leads/
managers), mentors who've done your <em>next</em> job, communities and ongoing learning, and — without stigma
— coaching or therapy. Building this support system is not a luxury; it's how you learn faster, stay sane,
and avoid the isolation that derails many new leaders.

```
   YOUR SUPPORT SYSTEM (stop learning leadership alone)
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ • PEER GROUPS: other leads/managers (inside & outside — │
   │   LeadDev, Rands Leadership Slack)                     │
   │ • MENTORS who've done your NEXT job                    │
   │ • ongoing CURRICULA (StaffEng, Manager Tools, books)   │
   │ • COACHING / THERAPY without stigma                    │
   │ • a DECISION JOURNAL (learn from your own calls)       │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The reframe: **don't learn leadership alone — build a deliberate support system, because peers, mentors, and
communities let you learn far faster (and stay saner) than figuring it all out solo.** Leadership problems
are ambiguous and people-heavy (no Stack Overflow answer), and the isolation of the role is real — so a
support system (people who've faced the same things, mentors ahead of you, communities) is how you get
perspective, advice, and support, dramatically accelerating your learning and sustaining you through the
hard parts.

## Going Deeper

### Peer groups — other leads and managers

The most valuable support: **peers who are also leads/managers** — people facing the same kinds of problems,
who <em>get it</em> in a way your team, your manager, or non-manager friends can't. Build peer groups both
**inside** your company (other leads/EMs — a natural, valuable network) and **outside** it (communities like
**LeadDev**, the **Rands Leadership Slack**, local manager meetups, or a personal circle of manager friends).
Peers provide: a safe place to vent and process (you can't vent to your team), advice from people who've
faced your exact problems, perspective (you're not alone / it's not just you), and shared learning. A peer
group of fellow leaders is one of the highest-value parts of a support system — build it deliberately.

### Mentors who've done your *next* job

Seek **mentors who are a step ahead** — people who've done the job you're growing into (your next role): a
senior EM if you're a new EM, a director if you're a senior EM, a staff engineer if you're going technical.
A mentor who's been where you're going can give you (1) guidance from experience (they've faced what you're
facing and what's coming), (2) perspective on the path (what the next level really involves, how to grow
toward it), and (3) sponsorship (Lesson 29 — they may advocate for you). Find mentors deliberately (many
people are willing to mentor if asked genuinely and respectfully), and choose ones who've done <em>your
next</em> job (so they can guide you forward), not just peers at your level.

### Ongoing curricula — StaffEng, Manager Tools, books

Leadership is a deep field with rich **ongoing learning resources** — treat them as ongoing curricula, not
one-time reads: **books** (*The Manager's Path*, *An Elegant Puzzle*, *Staff Engineer*, *The Making of a
Manager*, *Crucial Conversations*, etc.), **Manager Tools** (the podcast/framework), **StaffEng.com** (for
the technical-leadership path), **LeadDev** (articles, talks, conferences), and more. The point is that
leadership skills can be <em>learned</em> (this whole course is that premise), and the best leaders keep
learning — treating the rich body of leadership knowledge as an ongoing curriculum, not something you master
once. Keep reading, listening, and learning throughout your leadership career.

### Coaching and therapy — without stigma

Two forms of support that leaders often underuse due to stigma: (1) **coaching** — a professional
leadership/executive coach can be enormously valuable (a dedicated thinking partner focused on your growth,
challenges, and blind spots) — many strong leaders have coaches, and it's an investment in your development,
not a sign of weakness. (2) **Therapy** — leadership is stressful, and the emotional load (hard
conversations, others' problems, the loneliness, imposter syndrome) is real; therapy is a healthy,
stigma-free way to process it and stay well. Both coaching and therapy are signs of taking your growth and
wellbeing seriously, not weakness — drop any stigma and use them if helpful. Leaders who invest in their own
support (coaching, therapy) are often the ones who sustain and grow.

### A decision journal — learn from your own calls

A powerful personal practice: keep a **decision journal** — record important decisions (what you decided,
why, what you expected, your confidence) as you make them. Later, review: how did they turn out? was your
reasoning sound? This (1) improves your judgment over time (you learn from your actual decisions, seeing
where your reasoning was good or flawed — vs. hindsight-biased memory), (2) counters hindsight bias (you have
a record of what you actually thought <em>at the time</em>, not a rewritten memory), and (3) builds
self-awareness (patterns in your decision-making). A decision journal turns your own experience into
deliberate learning — a high-value, low-cost practice for developing judgment.

## Lab — Scenario

**The exercise:** This one is about building <em>your</em> support system. A useful frame is a **personal
board of advisors** — a small set of "seats," each filling a different support need, that you deliberately
assemble.

**Design your personal board of advisors** — the four seats (what support need each fills), who could fill
each (real names where you can), and the ask that would get each one. Make it concrete to your situation.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Since this is about your real support system, the "answer" is a strong example structure and the criteria for
a good board. Example four seats (adapt to your situation):
<br><br>
<strong>Seat 1 — The Peer (someone at your level who gets it):</strong> <em>Need:</em> a fellow lead/manager
to vent to, compare notes, and process problems with (someone facing the same things). <em>Who:</em> another
lead/EM in your org, or someone from LeadDev/Rands Slack/your network (name a real person if you can). <em>The
ask:</em> "Would you be up for grabbing coffee/a call regularly to compare notes on the lead/manager stuff?
I'd value having someone facing the same things to talk through it with." (A low-pressure, mutual ask —
peer support is reciprocal.)
<br><br>
<strong>Seat 2 — The Mentor (someone a step ahead):</strong> <em>Need:</em> guidance from someone who's done
your next job (a senior EM/director/staff engineer). <em>Who:</em> a respected leader ahead of you, inside or
outside your company (name someone). <em>The ask:</em> "I really respect how you've [specific thing]. I'm
growing into [role/direction], and I'd value your guidance — could I take you to lunch occasionally to learn
from your experience?" (A genuine, specific, respectful ask — many people say yes to a sincere mentorship
request.)
<br><br>
<strong>Seat 3 — The Coach/Truth-teller (challenges your blind spots):</strong> <em>Need:</em> someone who
will challenge you honestly and help you see your blind spots (a professional coach, or a brutally-honest
trusted colleague/mentor). <em>Who:</em> a leadership coach (if you can invest), or a trusted person who'll
tell you the truth. <em>The ask:</em> (for a coach) engage one; (for a person) "I trust you to be honest with
me — would you be willing to tell me the things I might not see about how I'm leading?" (Someone who gives
you honest, challenging feedback — countering the isolation that lets blind spots grow.)
<br><br>
<strong>Seat 4 — The Support (emotional/wellbeing):</strong> <em>Need:</em> support for the emotional load
and wellbeing (a therapist, a close friend outside work, or a support network). <em>Who:</em> a therapist
(stigma-free), or a trusted friend/partner who supports you through the stress. <em>The ask:</em> (therapy)
seek one; (friend) lean on them and be honest that leadership is stressful and you value their support. (For
the emotional load, loneliness, and stress — sustaining your wellbeing.)
<br><br>
<strong>Plus — ongoing curricula and a decision journal</strong>: subscribe to the learning (books, Manager
Tools, StaffEng, LeadDev), and start a decision journal to learn from your own calls.
<br><br>
<strong>What makes a good board:</strong> (1) <strong>Different seats for different needs</strong> — peer
(process/vent), mentor (guidance from ahead), coach/truth-teller (blind spots), support (wellbeing) — not one
person for everything. (2) <strong>Concrete</strong> — real people (or a real plan to find them) and a real
ask for each. (3) <strong>Deliberate</strong> — you assemble it intentionally, not hope it happens. (4)
<strong>Includes the emotional/wellbeing support</strong> (not just professional) — coaching/therapy without
stigma. (5) <strong>Ongoing learning + decision journal</strong> — the self-development practices. <strong>Common
mistakes:</strong> (1) <strong>no support system</strong> — trying to learn leadership alone (slow, lonely,
blind-spot-ridden); (2) <strong>only one type of support</strong> (e.g., only a mentor, no peers, no
emotional support) — you need the different seats; (3) <strong>not being deliberate</strong> — hoping support
happens rather than assembling it; (4) <strong>stigma about coaching/therapy</strong> — avoiding valuable
support due to a false sense of weakness; (5) <strong>not making the ask</strong> — many people would help if
asked genuinely, but you have to ask; (6) <strong>neglecting the wellbeing seat</strong> — ignoring the real
emotional load of leadership.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is learning leadership alone "the slowest possible way," and why are peer groups (other leads/
managers) so valuable?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Learning leadership alone is "the slowest possible way" because <strong>leadership problems are ambiguous and
people-heavy (no clear answers to look up), and the role is isolating — so without others' experience and
perspective, you're forced to figure everything out from scratch, slowly and blindly, missing the
accelerated learning that others' hard-won experience provides</strong>. Several reasons: (1) <strong>leadership
problems don't have lookup-able answers</strong> — unlike a technical problem (Stack Overflow, docs),
leadership problems are ambiguous, contextual, and people-heavy ("how do I handle this underperformer / this
conflict / this reorg?"), with no single right answer to find; so alone, you're reasoning from scratch on
hard, ambiguous problems. (2) <strong>others have faced your exact problems</strong> — the problems you face
as a leader are ones many leaders have faced before, so others' experience is enormously valuable (they've
tried things, learned what works, and can share it) — learning from their experience (rather than
rediscovering everything yourself) dramatically accelerates your learning; alone, you forgo all that
accumulated wisdom and learn only from your own slow trial-and-error. (3) <strong>you have blind spots</strong>
— alone, you can't see your own blind spots (the things you're doing wrong that you can't perceive); others
(peers, mentors, coaches) can point them out, accelerating your growth, while alone they persist. (4)
<strong>the isolation is real and draining</strong> — leadership is lonelier than IC work (you can't vent to
your team, your problems are ambiguous, you're expected to have answers), and processing it all alone is
draining and can lead to bad decisions (no one to sanity-check with) and burnout. So learning alone is slow
(reasoning from scratch, missing others' experience), blind (unaddressed blind spots), and lonely (draining,
no sanity-checks) — the slowest and hardest path. A support system (peers, mentors, communities) fixes all of
this: you learn from others' experience (faster), get your blind spots pointed out (growth), and have support
and perspective (sane and sustained). Why <strong>peer groups (other leads/managers)</strong> are so
valuable: <strong>because peers facing the same problems "get it" in a way no one else can, providing a safe
place to process, advice from shared experience, perspective (you're not alone), and mutual learning</strong>.
Peers who are also leads/managers are uniquely valuable because they're facing <em>the same kinds of
problems</em> you are, so: (1) <strong>they get it</strong> — they understand the leadership challenges (the
ambiguity, the people problems, the loneliness, the specific situations) in a way your team can't (you can't
vent to your team — you're their boss), your non-manager friends can't (they don't face these), and even your
own manager may not fully (different relationship). Peers are the people who truly understand what you're
going through. (2) <strong>a safe place to vent and process</strong> — you can't vent to your team, but you
can with peers (who face the same things), which is a crucial outlet for processing the hard parts (a hard
conversation, a tough decision, the stress). (3) <strong>advice from shared experience</strong> — peers have
faced your exact problems (a difficult report, a reorg, a conflict) and can share what worked, giving you
practical, relevant advice grounded in the same reality. (4) <strong>perspective — you're not alone</strong>
— hearing that others face the same struggles (the same self-doubt, the same hard situations) is enormously
reassuring (it's not just you, you're not failing — this is normal), countering the isolation and imposter
feelings. (5) <strong>mutual learning</strong> — peer groups are reciprocal (you help each other), building a
shared body of learning. So peer groups provide exactly what the isolated leader lacks: understanding (people
who get it), a safe outlet (vent/process), relevant advice (shared experience), perspective (you're not
alone), and mutual growth — which is why they're one of the highest-value parts of a support system. Build
them deliberately (inside your org — other leads/EMs; and outside — communities like LeadDev, Rands Slack,
manager friends), because the peers-who-get-it are irreplaceable support. Both points reflect that leadership
shouldn't be learned alone: it's slow, blind, and lonely solo (ambiguous problems, missed experience, blind
spots, isolation), and a support system — especially peer groups of fellow leaders — provides the shared
experience, perspective, and support that accelerate your learning and sustain you, which is why building it
deliberately is essential, not a luxury.
</details>

**Q2.** Why seek "mentors who've done your next job," and why are coaching/therapy and a decision journal
valuable (despite stigma or seeming optional)?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
You should seek <strong>mentors who've done your next job</strong> because <strong>someone a step ahead of
you — who's done the role you're growing into — can guide you forward with experience of what you're facing
and what's coming, in a way peers at your level can't</strong>. While peers (at your level) provide shared
experience of your <em>current</em> problems, a mentor who's done your <em>next</em> job provides something
different and complementary: (1) <strong>guidance from experience of what's ahead</strong> — they've been
where you're going, so they can help you navigate not just your current challenges but the path forward (what
the next level really involves, what to develop, what's coming that you can't yet see) — forward-looking
guidance a same-level peer can't give (peers are figuring out the same level as you). (2) <strong>perspective
on the path</strong> — having made the transition you're aiming for, they can advise on how to grow toward it
(what the next role requires, how they made the jump, what matters), helping you develop deliberately toward
your goal. (3) <strong>sponsorship</strong> — a mentor ahead of you may also advocate for you (Lesson 29 —
sponsorship), using their standing to help your advancement. So a mentor who's done your next job guides you
<em>forward</em> (from ahead), where peers support you <em>alongside</em> (at your level) — both valuable, but
the mentor's role is specifically the forward guidance from someone who's traveled the path you're on. That's
why you choose mentors who've done your next job (not just peers): they can see and guide the road ahead.
(Find them deliberately and ask genuinely — many people are willing to mentor.) Why <strong>coaching/therapy</strong>
are valuable (despite stigma): <strong>because they provide dedicated support for your growth (coaching) and
your wellbeing (therapy) that the demands of leadership genuinely require — and the stigma that they're "signs
of weakness" is false; they're signs of taking your development and health seriously</strong>. On
<strong>coaching</strong>: a professional leadership/executive coach is a dedicated thinking partner focused
entirely on <em>your</em> growth — helping you work through challenges, see your blind spots, and develop —
which is enormously valuable (many strong leaders have coaches); it's an investment in your development, not a
remedial fix for weakness. On <strong>therapy</strong>: leadership carries a real emotional load (hard
conversations, carrying others' problems, the loneliness, imposter syndrome, stress), and therapy is a
healthy way to process that load and sustain your wellbeing — which matters both for you personally and for
your effectiveness (a depleted, unwell leader can't lead well). The stigma (that needing coaching or therapy
means you're failing/weak) is false and harmful — it causes leaders to underuse valuable support out of a
misplaced sense that they should handle everything alone; in reality, using coaching and therapy reflects
maturity and self-awareness (taking your growth and wellbeing seriously), and the leaders who invest in their
own support are often the ones who sustain and grow (rather than burning out or stagnating). So drop the
stigma and use them if helpful — they're valuable support, not weakness. Why a <strong>decision journal</strong>
is valuable (despite seeming optional): <strong>because recording your decisions and reasoning as you make
them, then reviewing the outcomes, turns your own experience into deliberate learning — improving your
judgment and countering hindsight bias</strong>. A decision journal (recording important decisions — what you
decided, why, what you expected, your confidence — as you make them) is valuable because: (1) <strong>it
improves your judgment over time</strong> — by later reviewing how decisions turned out against your recorded
reasoning, you learn where your reasoning was sound or flawed, systematically improving your decision-making
(vs. never examining your own calls); (2) <strong>it counters hindsight bias</strong> — you have a record of
what you <em>actually thought at the time</em> (your real reasoning and confidence), not a memory rewritten
by knowing the outcome (hindsight bias makes us misremember our past reasoning as more/less confident than it
was), so you can honestly assess your judgment; and (3) <strong>it builds self-awareness</strong> — patterns
in your decisions and reasoning become visible (your biases, your blind spots, where you're over/under-
confident). It seems optional (it's a small extra practice), but it's a high-value, low-cost way to turn your
own experience into deliberate learning — developing judgment (the core of leadership) from your actual
decisions rather than leaving it to unexamined experience. All three — mentors ahead of you (forward
guidance), coaching/therapy (growth and wellbeing support, stigma-free), and a decision journal (learning
from your own calls) — are parts of a deliberate approach to your own development and support as a leader:
you learn faster (mentors, coaching), stay well (therapy), and improve your judgment (decision journal),
rather than learning alone, neglecting your wellbeing, or leaving your growth to chance. They reflect taking
your own development and sustainability seriously, which is what lets a leader keep growing and sustain the
demanding role over time.
</details>
