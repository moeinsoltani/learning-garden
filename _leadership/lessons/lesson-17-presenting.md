# Lesson 17: Presenting and Demos

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **story shape** — structuring a presentation as situation → tension → resolution instead of a flat list of facts.
> - **demo** — showing the real working thing; a **live demo** runs it in front of the audience (and can break).
> - **a net / fallback** — the backup (recording, screenshots) ready for when the live demo fails ("without a net" = no backup).
> - **narrate** — to talk the audience through what they're seeing, as a story.
> - **flustered** — visibly rattled and losing composure.
> - **credit** (verb) — to publicly name who did the work; the opposite of taking it yourself.
> - **self-promotion** — advertising yourself; making the *team's* work visible is not this.
> - **QBR (quarterly business review)** — the regular meeting where teams present results to leadership.
> - **bluffing** — pretending to know an answer; caught bluffs destroy credibility ("I don't know, I'll follow up" beats it).
> - **deflect** — to dodge a question instead of answering it.

## Concept

A lead regularly has to present — quarterly reviews, demos, proposals to
leadership, all-hands updates. And a critical, under-appreciated part of the job is
making your *team's* work visible: engineers who do great work that leadership
never sees don't get the recognition, resources, or trust their work should earn.
Presenting well isn't self-promotion; it's ensuring the work — yours and your
team's — lands and gets credited.

```
   A presentation's job: make the work LAND and get CREDITED.

   STORY SHAPE (why it works on human attention):
     SITUATION  →  TENSION  →  RESOLUTION
     "here's     "here's the   "here's what we did
      where we    problem/       and the impact"
      were"       challenge"

   SLIDES = evidence, NOT your script (nobody wants to
            watch you read bullet points)

   MAKE THE TEAM VISIBLE: their work, credited, upward
```

Two ideas do most of the work. **Story shape**: human attention is built for
narrative, so structure a presentation as situation → tension → resolution (where
we were, the challenge/problem, what we did and its impact) rather than a flat list
of facts — the tension is what holds attention and makes the resolution land.
**Slides as evidence, not script**: the classic failure is slides dense with text
that the presenter reads aloud (nobody wants that — they can read faster than you
talk); slides should *support* what you say with evidence (a chart, a demo, a key
number, an image), while you deliver the narrative.

---

## How It Works

### Story shape holds attention

A presentation structured as a list of facts ("we did X, then Y, then Z, here are
some metrics") gives the audience no reason to stay engaged — there's no tension
pulling them forward. Story shape creates that pull: **situation** (establish where
things stood — the audience's grounding), **tension** (the problem, challenge, or
stakes — this is what makes them care and want to know the resolution), **resolution**
(what you/the team did and, crucially, the *impact* — the payoff). "Our onboarding
took new users 20 minutes and we were losing 40% of them (situation + tension) — so
we rebuilt the flow, and now it takes 3 minutes and completion is up 60% (resolution
+ impact)" holds attention far better than "we worked on onboarding this quarter,
here are the changes and metrics." The tension-resolution arc is why stories are
memorable and lists are forgettable — use it deliberately.

### Slides as evidence, not script

The cardinal presentation sin is slides full of text that you read aloud — it's
boring (the audience reads ahead and tunes out), redundant (why are you reading
what they can read?), and it makes you a narrator of your slides rather than a
communicator. Instead: **you** deliver the message (the narrative, in your words),
and the **slides provide evidence** for it — a chart showing the impact, a
before/after, a key number, a screenshot, a demo. The slide should make the
audience *believe* or *see* what you're saying, not restate it. A good test: if
your slides make sense without you (all the content is on them), they're doing your
job; if you make sense without your slides (they're evidence you point to), you've
got it right. Fewer words, more evidence.

### Demoing — with and without a net

A live demo is powerful (seeing the real thing beats describing it) and risky (it
can break in front of everyone). Handle both: **narrate the demo as a story** (the
problem → what you're about to show → what they're seeing → the impact — not just
clicking around), and **have a net** — because live demos fail (the wifi drops, the
data's wrong, the one edge case appears), always have a fallback (a recording,
screenshots, the ability to gracefully continue) so a demo failure is a hiccup, not
a disaster. When it does break ("well, it worked this morning" — universal), stay
calm, don't apologize excessively, switch to the fallback, and move on — the
audience forgives a smooth recovery far more than they remember the glitch, but a
flustered meltdown is what they'll remember.

### Making the team's work visible upward

This is the leadership-specific part: a big reason to present is to make your
*team's* work visible to leadership — because work that leadership doesn't see
doesn't earn the recognition, resources, and trust it should. When you present your
team's work: **credit the team** accurately (name contributors, "the team built
X" — not "I built X"; taking credit for the team's work is both wrong and
corrosive — Lesson 20's praise, and it will lose you the team's trust fast), frame
the work in terms of *impact* leadership cares about (Lesson 14 — business terms,
not technical detail), and be the advocate who ensures your engineers' good work is
known upward (part of sponsorship, Lesson 29). A lead who lets their team's work go
unseen fails them; a lead who makes it visible (honestly, crediting the team) earns
the team resources and their engineers recognition.

{: .note }
> **Handling hostile or hard questions</br>**
> Presentations to leadership often include tough questions — skeptical, probing,
> sometimes hostile. The moves: <em>listen fully</em> (don't get defensive or
> interrupt — understand the real question, which is often different from the
> surface one); <em>answer honestly</em> (including "I don't know, I'll follow up"
> — far better than bluffing, which destroys credibility when caught); stay
> <em>calm and non-defensive</em> (a defensive reaction signals weakness; a calm,
> direct answer signals command); and if a question reveals a real gap, acknowledge
> it ("that's a fair concern, here's how we're thinking about it") rather than
> deflecting. Handling hard questions well often does more for your credibility than
> the presentation itself — it shows you know your stuff and can be trusted under
> pressure.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** You have a 5-minute slot in the quarterly business review to
present your team's work to the VP of Engineering and other senior leaders. Last
quarter your team did a lot: they shipped a major performance improvement (cut page
load time in half, which measurably improved user retention), fixed a long-standing
reliability problem (reduced incidents by 70%), and did significant behind-the-
scenes work modernizing the deploy pipeline (not user-visible, but it's why the
team now ships twice as fast). You have 5 minutes and want the work to land and get
credited to your team.

**Prepare it:** the outline (what you'd cover in 5 minutes), your opening line, and
the ONE slide you'd show (describe it). Then note the choices you made and why.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Five minutes is tight, so a strong response is ruthlessly focused (you can't cover
everything well — pick what lands), uses story shape, leads with impact, credits
the team, and frames in terms leadership cares about. Example:
<br><br>
<strong>Opening line:</strong> "This quarter, [team name] made the product
measurably faster and more reliable for users, while doubling our own shipping
speed — I want to show you the impact and give credit to the people who did it." —
This is BLUF (Lesson 13): the impact up front (faster, more reliable, 2x shipping
speed), framed in leadership's terms (user impact + team velocity), and it signals
you're crediting the team (setting the right tone). It gives the audience the whole
gist in one sentence, so even if the next 4:50 blur, they got it.
<br><br>
<strong>Outline (5 minutes):</strong>
<br><strong>0:00–0:30 — Open</strong> with the line above; establish the three
things at a high level.
<br><strong>0:30–2:00 — The headline win (performance), story-shaped:</strong>
"Users were waiting [X seconds] for pages to load, and we were losing [40%] of them
to that friction (situation + tension). The team — [names] — rebuilt [the critical
path], and we cut load time in half, which drove user retention up [measurably]
(resolution + impact)." Lead with the biggest, most business-relevant win, told as
a story with the impact quantified — this is what leadership will remember. Credit
the specific people.
<br><strong>2:00–3:15 — Reliability</strong>, briefly, same shape: "We also had a
long-standing reliability problem — [N] incidents a quarter, each hurting user
trust and eating the team's time. The team drove that down 70%, which means [fewer
outages / more customer trust / more time for features]." Impact-framed, team-
credited, tight.
<br><strong>3:15–4:15 — The invisible win (deploy pipeline), reframed as impact:</strong>
"Behind the scenes, the team modernized how we ship — which isn't user-visible, but
it's why we now deliver twice as fast, meaning everything on our roadmap comes
[sooner]." This is the crucial move: work that <em>isn't</em> user-visible would
normally go uncredited, but framing it by its <em>impact</em> (2x velocity → faster
roadmap delivery — a thing leadership deeply cares about) makes it land. Don't
describe the pipeline technically; describe what it <em>enables</em>.
<br><strong>4:15–5:00 — Close:</strong> "So this quarter the team made the product
faster, more reliable, and set us up to ship faster going forward. Real credit to
[names] — this was their work. Happy to take questions." Recap the three impacts,
credit the team again, invite questions.
<br><br>
<strong>The ONE slide:</strong> A single slide with three clear before/after
numbers, large and visual — e.g., three side-by-side stats: "Page load: [X]s → [X/2]s
(retention +[Y]%)" | "Incidents: [N]/qtr → [N×0.3]/qtr (−70%)" | "Shipping speed:
2x faster." Plus the team members' names at the bottom. This slide is
<em>evidence</em> (the numbers prove the impact you're narrating), not a script
(you're not reading bullets — you're telling the story and pointing to the proof),
it's visual and instantly graspable (three big before/after numbers a busy exec
absorbs in two seconds), and it credits the team (names on it). It follows
"slides as evidence, not script" — the slide makes them <em>believe</em> the impact;
you deliver the narrative.
<br><br>
<strong>Choices and why:</strong> (1) <strong>Ruthless focus</strong> — in 5
minutes you can't cover everything well, so lead with the biggest business-relevant
win (performance/retention) and give the others less time; trying to cover all
three equally would make all three shallow. (2) <strong>Story shape throughout</strong>
— each item is situation → tension → impact, not a flat list, so it holds attention
and lands. (3) <strong>Impact in leadership's terms</strong> (Lesson 14) — retention,
customer trust, roadmap velocity — not technical detail (no mention of <em>how</em>
the performance was improved or what the pipeline modernization technically was);
leadership cares about outcomes, not mechanisms. (4) <strong>The invisible work
reframed by impact</strong> — the deploy pipeline (not user-visible, easily
uncredited) is made visible by framing its <em>impact</em> (2x velocity), which is
the leadership-specific skill: making behind-the-scenes work land by connecting it
to what leadership values. (5) <strong>Team credited repeatedly and specifically</strong>
— names in the opening, in each item, in the close, and on the slide; this is both
right (it's their work) and strategic (it earns your engineers recognition —
sponsorship, Lesson 29 — and builds your team's trust in you; taking the credit
yourself would be corrosive, Lesson 20). (6) <strong>One evidence slide, not many
text slides</strong> — the single before/after-numbers slide is more effective in 5
minutes than a deck of bullet-point slides you'd read; it's evidence for your
narrative, instantly graspable. (7) <strong>BLUF opening</strong> — the whole gist
in the first sentence, so the essence lands even in a distracted room. Common
mistakes: (1) trying to cover all three items equally in 5 minutes (all shallow —
pick the headline); (2) technical detail leadership doesn't care about (how the
performance was improved, rather than the retention impact); (3) letting the
invisible pipeline work go uncredited or described technically (missing the
impact-reframe that makes it land); (4) taking credit personally ("I did X") rather
than crediting the team (wrong and corrosive); (5) dense text slides read aloud
(boring, script-not-evidence); (6) burying the impact (not leading with it).
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why does "story shape" (situation → tension → resolution) hold attention
better than a list of facts, and how do you apply it to presenting technical work?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Story shape holds attention because human cognition is built for narrative — we're
wired to attend to and remember stories far better than lists, and the mechanism is
the <strong>tension</strong>: a story creates an open loop (a problem, a stakes, an
unresolved situation) that the mind wants closed, so the audience stays engaged
<em>to find out how it resolves</em>. A list of facts ("we did X, then Y, here are
some metrics") has no open loop — nothing pulls the audience forward, no reason to
stay engaged, so attention drifts and little is retained (each fact is isolated,
unmemorable, and the audience is passively receiving rather than actively wanting
the next piece). The situation → tension → resolution arc supplies the pull:
<strong>situation</strong> grounds the audience (here's where things stood),
<strong>tension</strong> makes them <em>care</em> (here's the problem/stakes/challenge
— this is what creates the open loop and the engagement), and <strong>resolution</strong>
delivers the payoff (here's what we did and, crucially, the impact — closing the
loop satisfyingly). The tension is doing the work: without it, there's no reason to
care about the resolution; with it, the audience is invested in the outcome.
Applying it to technical work: instead of "we improved the onboarding flow this
quarter, here are the changes and metrics" (a flat list — forgettable), structure
it as: "New users were taking 20 minutes to onboard and we were losing 40% of them
to that friction" (situation + tension — now the audience cares, there's a problem
with stakes) → "so the team rebuilt the flow" → "and now it's 3 minutes and
completion is up 60%" (resolution + impact — the satisfying payoff). The technical
work becomes a story with stakes and a payoff, which lands and is remembered, vs a
recitation of what was done, which doesn't. Concretely for technical presentations:
(1) find the <em>tension</em> — the problem, the stakes, the challenge, the "here's
what was at risk / going wrong / hard" — and lead with establishing it (this is
what most technical presenters skip, jumping straight to "here's what we built,"
which has no tension); (2) frame the work as the <em>resolution</em> of that
tension, not as a list of activities; (3) land the <em>impact</em> as the payoff
(the resolution isn't "we built X," it's "and it produced Y outcome" — the impact
is what makes the resolution satisfying and what leadership remembers). The
discipline is to resist the engineer's instinct to present work as a chronological
list of what was done ("we did A, then B, then C") and instead find the narrative:
what was the problem/stakes, and what changed because of the work. Even a quarterly
review of multiple work items (the lab) applies this per-item (each becomes a mini
situation-tension-impact story) rather than a flat catalog. The meta-point:
presenting is not "transferring the facts of what happened" — it's making the work
<em>land and be remembered</em>, and human memory and attention run on narrative,
so shaping technical work into stories (with real tension and impact-payoffs) is
how you make it stick, versus a comprehensive-but-forgettable list that the audience
tunes out of and doesn't retain. The work is the same; the story shape is the
difference between it landing and it washing over the audience unremembered.
</details>

**Q2.** Making your team's work visible to leadership — while crediting the team,
not yourself — is described as a leadership responsibility, not self-promotion.
Explain why it matters and why taking the credit yourself is corrosive.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Why making the team's work visible matters:</strong> work that leadership
doesn't see effectively doesn't exist for the purposes that matter — recognition,
resources, trust, and the team's reputation. If your team does excellent work but
leadership never learns of it (because nobody presents it, or presents it poorly),
then: your engineers don't get the recognition and career advancement their work
should earn (leadership can't promote or reward what they don't know about); your
team doesn't get the resources and trust that visible impact earns (the team seen
as delivering value gets more headcount, more autonomy, more interesting work); and
the invisible work (like the lab's deploy pipeline — not user-visible) goes entirely
uncredited unless someone makes its impact known. So it's a genuine
<em>responsibility</em> to your team, not optional self-promotion: a lead who lets
their team's good work go unseen is failing the team, depriving their engineers of
earned recognition and the team of earned resources. Advocacy for your team's
visibility is part of the job — you're the connection between their work and the
leadership that rewards and resources it (Phase 8's managing-up, and sponsorship,
Lesson 29). <strong>Why taking the credit yourself is corrosive:</strong> two
reasons. (1) <strong>It's wrong</strong> — the team did the work; presenting it as
"I did X" when your engineers built it is a misrepresentation that steals the
recognition they earned, and it's exactly the opposite of a lead's job (your output
is the team's output — Lesson 1 — but the <em>credit</em> for the work belongs to
those who did it). (2) <strong>It's rapidly self-destructive to your leadership</strong>
— nothing destroys a lead's trust with their team faster than a leader who takes
credit for the team's work; engineers notice immediately (they know who did what),
and a credit-stealing lead loses the team's trust, loyalty, and best effort (why
would they do great work for someone who'll claim it?) — it corrodes the
relationship that a lead's entire effectiveness depends on. It also, ironically,
diminishes you: a lead who credits their team generously is seen (by both the team
and by leadership) as a strong leader who develops and elevates people — which is
what leadership <em>wants</em> in a lead — whereas a credit-grabber is seen as
insecure and untrustworthy. Leadership isn't fooled either; they generally know a
team's output isn't one person's, and a lead who credits the team accurately looks
like a leader, while one who claims it all looks like they're compensating. So the
move — make the team's work visible <em>and</em> credit the team explicitly (names,
"the team built X," Lesson 20's specific praise) — serves everyone: the engineers
get recognition, the team gets resources/trust, and you're seen as the kind of
leader who elevates their people (which is the reputation that actually advances a
lead). It's the opposite of a trade-off between advocating for the team and your own
standing — crediting the team <em>is</em> how a lead builds their standing, because
a lead's job and reputation are about multiplying and elevating others, not about
personal output (Lesson 1). The corrosive alternative (grabbing credit) fails on
every axis: it wrongs the team, destroys their trust, and even makes you look worse
to leadership. The lead's rule: advocate hard for the team's work to be seen, and
give the credit for it entirely to the people who did it — visibility is your job,
credit is theirs.
</details>

## Homework

Prepare a real (or realistic) presentation of your team's recent work — a quarterly
review, a demo, or an update to leadership. Apply this lesson: structure it with
story shape (situation → tension → impact for the key items), lead with a BLUF
opening, design slides as evidence not script (what's the ONE most important slide,
and is it evidence or bullets?), and — deliberately — credit the team specifically
and frame the work (including any invisible work) in terms of impact leadership
cares about. Then reflect: how different is this from how you'd have instinctively
presented (probably a flat list of what was done, technically framed, under-
crediting the team or the invisible work)? What does the gap reveal about making
your team's contributions land?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds the presenting-and-advocacy skill and usually reveals a gap
between the instinctive approach (a chronological list of what the team did,
described technically, with impact and team-credit underdeveloped) and the effective
one (story-shaped, impact-led, team-credited, in leadership's terms). A strong
response shows the transformation: the flat "we did A, B, C" becomes situation-
tension-impact stories that land; the technical framing ("we optimized the query
layer") becomes impact framing ("we cut load time in half, driving retention up");
the buried or missing team credit becomes explicit and specific (names, "the team
built X"); and the invisible work (behind-the-scenes improvements) gets reframed by
its impact so it lands instead of going uncredited. The common realizations: (1)
<strong>The instinct is to present work as activities, not impact</strong> —
engineers naturally list what was <em>done</em> (the technical work) rather than
what <em>changed</em> because of it (the impact leadership cares about), and
reframing everything around impact is both more effective and forces you to
actually articulate the value (sometimes revealing work whose impact you hadn't
crisply identified). (2) <strong>The invisible work is easy to under-present</strong>
— not-user-visible work (infrastructure, tooling, reliability) tends to get skipped
or described technically, and it takes deliberate effort to reframe it by impact so
it gets the credit it deserves; a lead who doesn't do this lets significant team
contributions go unrecognized. (3) <strong>Team credit is easy to underdo</strong>
— even well-meaning leads often present "we/I did X" without naming the specific
people, and being deliberate about specific credit (names, on the slide, repeatedly)
both is right and strengthens your leadership (Q2). The gap reveals the core insight:
<strong>making your team's contributions land is a skill and a responsibility, not
something that happens automatically</strong> — the work being good is necessary but
not sufficient; it has to be <em>presented</em> in a way that lands (story shape,
impact-framing, evidence-slides) and <em>credited</em> to the team, or it fails to
earn the recognition, resources, and trust it should. A lead who presents their
team's work well (compelling, impact-framed, generously credited) advances their
engineers' careers, earns their team resources and standing, and builds the team's
trust — while a lead who presents poorly (flat lists, technical framing, under-
credit) fails their team even when the team did excellent work. Since a lead is the
primary connection between their team's work and the leadership that rewards it,
this presenting-and-advocacy skill directly determines whether their engineers'
good work translates into the recognition and resources it deserves — which is why
it's a leadership responsibility, and why the gap between instinctive and deliberate
presenting is worth closing. If your instinctive presentation was already story-
shaped, impact-led, and generously team-credited — excellent, that's a strong and
somewhat rare skill; the ongoing practice is doing it consistently (every review,
demo, and update is a chance to make the team's work land and be credited) and
resisting the drift back toward flat, technical, under-crediting presentation under
time pressure. This closes the communication-fundamentals for spoken/presented work;
the next lesson covers the written/async channels that are increasingly where
leadership communication actually happens.
</details>
