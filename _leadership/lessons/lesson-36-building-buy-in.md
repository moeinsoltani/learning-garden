# Lesson 36: Building Buy-In for Ideas

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **buy-in** — genuine support for an idea, not just permission (Lesson 10).
> - **socialize (an idea)** — to discuss it informally with key people one at a time, *before* any official meeting.
> - **groundwork** — the preparatory work done before the visible event.
> - **win the room** — to persuade a whole group live in a meeting (the amateur's plan).
> - **blindside** — to surprise someone with something they should have known was coming; never do it to a decision-maker.
> - **ratify** — to formally confirm a decision that's effectively already made.
> - **co-ownership** — others having shaped the idea enough to feel it's partly theirs — and champion it.
> - **pilot / experiment framing** — proposing a small, reversible trial instead of a permanent commitment.
> - **objection inventory** — the per-person list of likely concerns, prepared in advance.
> - **over-selling** — continuing to persuade after you've already won, which re-opens doubt.

## Concept

Getting an idea adopted isn't about making a brilliant pitch in the big meeting — it's about the
**groundwork you do before that meeting**, so that by the time the decision happens, the key people
are already on board. The most common mistake is bringing a fully-formed idea to a group cold and
hoping to win the room; the skilled move is **socializing the idea 1:1 first**, addressing concerns,
building co-ownership, and arriving at the decision meeting with the "yes" already secured.

```
   THE AMATEUR WAY                  THE SKILLED WAY
   ──────────────                   ───────────────
   bring the finished idea to        socialize 1:1 FIRST (get input,
   the big meeting cold →            address concerns, build co-ownership)
   surprise the decision-makers →    → by the meeting, key people are
   face objections in public →         already on board
   often lose                        → the meeting confirms, not decides

   Get to "yes" BEFORE the meeting where "yes" happens.
```

The reframe: **win the "yes" before the meeting, not in it — by socializing the idea 1:1 first.**
Decision meetings are where decisions get <em>ratified</em>, not usually where minds get changed;
people don't like being surprised or pressured to decide publicly, and a cold pitch invites public
objection. Doing the influence work beforehand — talking to key people individually, incorporating
their input, resolving their concerns — means the group meeting confirms a decision that's already
been built, rather than being a high-stakes gamble.

## Going Deeper

### Socialize the idea 1:1 first — never surprise a decision-maker

The core practice: before any group decision, **talk to the key people individually.** Share the
idea, get their input, hear their concerns, and address them — one-on-one, in advance. This works
because: (1) people are more open and honest 1:1 than in a group (they'll tell you their real
concerns); (2) you can address each person's specific objections privately (rather than having them
surface as public opposition); (3) incorporating their input gives them ownership; and (4) **you
never surprise a decision-maker in public** — being blindsided by a proposal in a meeting makes
people defensive and prone to say no (they haven't processed it, and public pressure triggers
caution). By the meeting, the key people have already seen it, shaped it, and agreed — so the meeting
confirms rather than gambles.

### Co-ownership beats ownership

Counterintuitively, an idea is more likely to be adopted if it's **not purely yours.** When you
incorporate people's input and let them shape the idea, they become **co-owners** — invested in its
success because it's partly theirs. An idea people helped build gets their genuine support; an idea
presented as fully-formed and yours ("here's my brilliant plan") invites them to poke holes (it's
yours to defend, not theirs to champion). So loosen your grip: bring a strong direction but let
people shape it, trading sole ownership for the far more valuable broad co-ownership. (Ego cost:
you get less credit for "your" idea; payoff: it actually happens.)

### The pilot / experiment framing

A powerful way to lower resistance: frame the change as a **pilot or experiment**, not a permanent
commitment. "Let's try trunk-based development on one team for a month and see" is far easier to
agree to than "let's switch the whole org to trunk-based development." A pilot: (1) lowers the stakes
(small, reversible — easy to say yes to), (2) is evidence-gathering (you'll have real data, which
persuades better than argument), and (3) de-risks the big decision (prove it small before committing
big). Propose the easy, reversible first step (English track Lesson 42) rather than the scary
all-in.

### The objection inventory

Before pushing an idea, **inventory the likely objections** — for each key person, what will they
worry about? — and prepare to address each. This lets you (a) pre-empt objections (address them
before they're raised, in your 1:1s), (b) genuinely improve the idea (some objections are valid —
incorporate them), and (c) not be caught off guard. Often different people object for *different*
reasons (one worries about cost, another about risk, another about their team's workload), so the
inventory is per-person, and addressing each person's specific concern is what wins them.

### Know when you've won — stop selling

A subtle skill: **recognize when you've got the "yes" and stop selling.** Once someone's on board,
continuing to push and over-justify can backfire — it can re-open doubts ("why are they still
selling? is there a catch?"), annoy them, or seem pushy. When you've won, move to action ("great —
let's do it"), don't keep persuading. Over-selling past the yes is a common mistake; read when the
decision is made and close, rather than continuing to argue a point that's already conceded.

{: .note }
> **Win the "yes" before the meeting — socialize 1:1, build co-ownership, address objections</br>**
> Ideas get adopted through groundwork before the decision meeting, not a brilliant cold pitch in it.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** You want the org to adopt **trunk-based development** (moving away from long-lived
feature branches). You believe it'll improve integration and delivery speed. But three key people are
skeptical, for three *different* reasons: **Raj** (staff engineer) worries it'll cause more broken
builds and instability; **Mia** (a team lead) worries her team isn't disciplined enough with testing
to make it safe; and **the eng director** worries about the disruption/cost of changing everyone's
workflow. You can't mandate this — you need to build buy-in.

**Plan the buy-in campaign** — how you'd build support given these three different objections. Then
note the principles and mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong campaign socializes 1:1, addresses each person's specific objection, uses a pilot framing, and
builds co-ownership — before any org-wide decision. Example:
<br><br>
<strong>Don't start with an all-org pitch — socialize 1:1 first, per objection:</strong>
<br><br>
<strong>Raj (worries about broken builds/instability):</strong> "Meet him 1:1, acknowledge the concern
(it's valid), and address it specifically: trunk-based works <em>with</em> good CI and feature flags —
show how we'd keep builds green (strong CI gates, flags for incomplete work). Better: ask <em>his</em>
input on how to do it safely — make him a co-owner of the technical approach. If Raj (influential —
Lesson 35) is won and helps design the safety, that's huge." <em>(Address his specific technical concern;
turn the influential skeptic into a co-owner of the solution.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Mia (worries her team's testing discipline isn't ready):</strong> "1:1, acknowledge it's a real
risk. Propose: her team doesn't have to go first, or we pair the rollout with improving test coverage,
or we start with a more-ready team and learn. Address <em>her</em> concern (readiness) directly — maybe
her team is the second wave, not the first." <em>(Her objection is different (team readiness), so the
response is different — flexibility on rollout, not the same pitch as Raj.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Eng director (worries about disruption/cost):</strong> "Frame it as a low-cost pilot, not an
org-wide upheaval: 'Let's try it on one willing team for a month, measure the impact, and only expand if
it works.' This addresses the disruption concern (small, contained) and gives evidence for the bigger
decision. Frame the benefit in her terms (delivery speed, quality)." <em>(Pilot framing directly
addresses the disruption/cost worry — low-stakes, reversible, evidence-gathering.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>The sequence:</strong> "(1) Win Raj first (influential, and his safety-design co-ownership
reassures others). (2) Address Mia's readiness concern (flexible rollout). (3) Propose the pilot to the
director (low-risk, with Raj's technical backing and a willing first team). (4) Run the pilot, gather
data. (5) If it works, expand — now with evidence, co-owners (Raj, the pilot team), and the director's
blessing. By any 'decision meeting,' the key people are already on board."
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Socialize 1:1 first, per person</strong> — each key person
individually, addressing their <em>specific</em> objection (Raj's builds, Mia's readiness, director's
disruption — three different concerns, three different responses). (2) <strong>Objection inventory</strong>
— knowing the three different objections lets you address each specifically (not one generic pitch). (3)
<strong>Co-ownership</strong> — make Raj a co-owner of the safety design (his input shapes it, so he
champions it). (4) <strong>Pilot framing</strong> — the low-stakes, reversible, evidence-gathering pilot
addresses the director's disruption concern and de-risks the big decision. (5) <strong>Sequence by
influence</strong> — win the influential skeptic (Raj) first; his buy-in cascades. (6) <strong>Evidence
over argument</strong> — the pilot generates data that persuades better than debate. <strong>Mistakes to
avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>a cold org-wide pitch</strong> — bringing "let's all switch to trunk-based"
to a big meeting, surprising everyone, facing all three objections publicly at once (likely loses); (2)
<strong>one generic pitch for different objections</strong> — the three have different concerns; a single
message addresses none well; (3) <strong>not socializing 1:1</strong> — missing the chance to hear real
concerns and address them privately (they'll surface as public opposition instead); (4) <strong>proposing
the all-in change</strong> instead of a pilot — high-stakes, scary, easy to reject (vs. the easy-yes
pilot); (5) <strong>presenting it as your finished idea</strong> — no co-ownership, so people poke holes
rather than champion it; (6) <strong>ignoring the influence map</strong> — not winning the influential
person (Raj) first, or pitching the director before the groundwork; (7) <strong>over-selling past a
yes</strong> — if someone's convinced, stop pushing.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is socializing an idea 1:1 before the decision meeting more effective than a cold pitch, and
why should you "never surprise a decision-maker in public"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Socializing an idea 1:1 before the decision meeting is more effective than a cold pitch because <strong>it
does the actual persuasion work in the setting where it works (private, individual) and arrives at the
group decision with the "yes" already built, rather than gambling on winning the room in the moment</strong>.
Several reasons: (1) <strong>people are more open and honest 1:1</strong> — individually, people share
their real concerns and objections (which they might not voice, or might voice as public opposition, in a
group), so you learn what actually needs addressing. (2) <strong>you can address each person's specific
objections privately</strong> — in a 1:1 you can understand and resolve each person's particular concern
(and different people have different concerns), whereas in a group those concerns surface as public
objections that are harder to address and can cascade (one person's objection emboldens others). (3)
<strong>incorporating their input builds ownership</strong> — 1:1 conversations let people shape the idea,
making them co-owners who support it, rather than an audience being pitched at. (4) <strong>decisions
aren't usually changed in meetings</strong> — group decision meetings tend to <em>ratify</em> decisions,
not change minds; the real persuasion happens beforehand, in the individual conversations, so doing the
work there (not hoping to win the room cold) is where influence actually operates. So by socializing 1:1
first — talking to each key person, hearing and addressing their concerns, incorporating their input — you
arrive at the decision meeting with the key people already on board and the idea already shaped by their
input, so the meeting <em>confirms</em> a decision that's already been built rather than being a
high-stakes attempt to win people over cold. A cold pitch, by contrast, tries to do all the persuasion in
the one high-pressure group setting where persuasion works worst (public, no time to process, objections
cascade) — a gamble that often loses. Why you should "never surprise a decision-maker in public": <strong>because
being surprised by a proposal in a meeting makes people defensive and prone to say no — they haven't had
time to process it, and public pressure triggers caution</strong>. When you spring a fully-formed proposal
on a decision-maker in a group setting: (1) <strong>they haven't processed it</strong> — they're
encountering it for the first time and being asked to react/decide on the spot, with no time to think it
through, ask questions, or get comfortable — so their safe default is caution or no (people don't commit
to things they haven't digested); (2) <strong>public pressure triggers defensiveness</strong> — being put
on the spot publicly to respond to something new can feel like an ambush or a maneuver (were they set up?),
making them defensive and inclined to resist (or to protect their position by poking holes); (3) <strong>they
may feel disrespected</strong> — springing a decision on someone in public, rather than consulting them
first, can feel like you went around them or are trying to pressure them via the group, which damages the
relationship and their receptiveness; (4) <strong>they can't be seen to cave to pressure</strong> — even if
inclined to agree, a decision-maker put on the spot publicly may resist to avoid looking railroaded. All of
this makes a surprised decision-maker likely to say no (or defer), even to a good idea. The fix is to
<em>never</em> surprise them — talk to them 1:1 first, so by the meeting they've already seen it, processed
it, shaped it, and agreed (or you've addressed their concerns), and the public moment is a comfortable
confirmation rather than an ambush. This is a specific, important application of socializing first:
decision-makers especially must be brought along in advance, never blindsided — because their public "no"
(triggered by surprise) can kill the idea, while their pre-secured "yes" carries it. The overarching
principle is that influence and buy-in are built through private, advance groundwork (where people are open
and can be brought along), not through public cold pitches (where surprise and pressure produce defensive
nos) — so you win the "yes" before the meeting, and never surprise the people whose yes you need.
</details>

**Q2.** Why does "co-ownership beat ownership," and why is the pilot/experiment framing effective for
lowering resistance?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"Co-ownership beats ownership" because <strong>an idea that people helped shape becomes partly theirs, so
they're invested in its success and champion it — whereas an idea presented as fully-formed and yours is
yours alone to defend, inviting others to poke holes rather than support it</strong>. The dynamic: when
you bring a fully-formed idea and present it as your brilliant plan ("here's what I think we should do"),
it's <em>your</em> idea — others have no stake in it, so their natural stance is to evaluate and critique
it (poke holes, raise objections), and even if they agree, they're supporting <em>your</em> thing, not
theirs. But when you bring a direction and let people shape it — incorporate their input, adapt it based
on their concerns, let them contribute to the solution — they become <strong>co-owners</strong>: the idea
is now partly theirs, so they're invested in its success (people support what they helped create) and will
champion it (defend and push it) rather than critique it. The psychology: people are far more committed to
conclusions and plans they helped reach than ones handed to them (the same reason coaching beats telling,
and disagree-and-commit needs people to feel heard). So co-ownership converts people from critics/audience
(evaluating your idea) into champions/co-creators (invested in our idea), which dramatically improves
adoption. The cost is to your ego (you get less credit for "your" idea — it's now "ours," and you loosened
your grip on it), but the payoff is that the idea actually happens (which is the point — you want the
outcome, not the credit). So the skilled move is to bring a strong direction but hold it loosely, letting
people shape it into something co-owned — trading sole ownership (and full credit) for broad co-ownership
(and actual adoption). Why the <strong>pilot/experiment framing</strong> lowers resistance: <strong>because
it makes the change small, reversible, and evidence-based — which is far easier to say yes to than a large,
permanent, all-in commitment</strong>. Framing a change as "let's try it on one team for a month and see"
rather than "let's switch the whole org permanently" lowers resistance in several ways: (1) <strong>it
lowers the stakes</strong> — a small, contained pilot has limited downside (if it fails, only one team for
one month is affected, easily reversed), so people can agree without fear of a big irreversible mistake; a
big permanent commitment is scary (high stakes, hard to undo), so people resist or want extensive analysis
first. (2) <strong>it's reversible</strong> — "we'll try it and stop if it doesn't work" removes the fear
of being stuck with a bad change; people say yes more readily to something they can back out of. (3)
<strong>it's evidence-gathering</strong> — a pilot generates real data on whether it works, which (a)
persuades far better than argument (people believe evidence over claims) for the bigger decision, and (b)
de-risks that bigger decision (you prove it small before committing big). (4) <strong>it turns a
scary all-in decision into an easy first step</strong> — instead of asking people to commit to the whole
change (hard), you ask them to try a small experiment (easy), and let the results make the case for
expansion. So the pilot framing lowers resistance by shrinking the ask to something low-stakes, reversible,
and evidence-generating — the easy yes — rather than the intimidating all-in commitment (this is the
"make the ask easy" persuasion principle, English track Lesson 42). Both co-ownership and the pilot framing
serve building buy-in by reducing the barriers to yes: co-ownership makes people <em>want</em> the idea to
succeed (invested, championing) rather than critiquing it, and the pilot framing makes the <em>decision</em>
easy (low-stakes, reversible, evidence-based) rather than scary. Together with socializing 1:1 and
addressing objections, they're how you build genuine buy-in — getting people invested in and comfortable
with the change — rather than trying to force or cold-pitch it, which produces resistance.
</details>

## Homework

Practice building buy-in for something you want to make happen. (1) Instead of planning a big pitch,
identify the key people and plan to socialize the idea 1:1 with each first — and never surprise a
decision-maker in public. (2) Inventory the objections per person — what will each worry about (likely
different things)? — and plan to address each specifically. (3) Look for co-ownership — how can you let key
people shape the idea so it's partly theirs? (4) Can you frame it as a pilot/experiment (low-stakes,
reversible, evidence-gathering) rather than an all-in commitment? Reflect: do you tend to cold-pitch ideas
in meetings or do the groundwork first — and what's one idea you could build buy-in for the skilled way?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds the buy-in skill — getting to "yes" through groundwork before the decision, not a cold
pitch in it. A strong response plans to socialize an idea 1:1 first (never surprising a decision-maker),
inventories objections per person and addresses each, builds co-ownership, and uses a pilot framing. The
realizations: (1) <strong>win the "yes" before the meeting</strong> — decision meetings ratify, they don't
usually change minds; the real persuasion happens 1:1 beforehand, where people are open, you address
concerns privately, and you never surprise a decision-maker (which makes them defensive and prone to no);
(2) <strong>co-ownership beats ownership</strong> — an idea people helped shape becomes partly theirs, so
they champion it; a fully-formed "your" idea invites hole-poking (loosen your grip, trade credit for
adoption); (3) <strong>the pilot framing lowers resistance</strong> — a small, reversible, evidence-
gathering experiment is an easy yes where an all-in permanent commitment is scary; (4) <strong>objections
are per-person</strong> — different people object for different reasons, so inventory and address each
specifically, not with one generic pitch; (5) <strong>know when you've won — stop selling</strong>
(over-pushing past the yes backfires). On reflection, people commonly find they tend to cold-pitch ideas in

that they present ideas as fully-formed (no co-ownership). The highest-value change is usually: socialize
ideas 1:1 first (address concerns, build co-ownership) before the decision meeting, and frame big changes as
pilots. The meta-point: ideas get adopted through the groundwork before the decision meeting — socializing
1:1, building co-ownership, addressing objections, using pilot framing — so that by the meeting the key
people are already on board and it confirms rather than gambles. The amateur move (a brilliant cold pitch)
usually loses; the skilled move (win the yes beforehand) usually wins. This applies the influence sources
(Lesson 35 — credibility, relationships, framing) to the specific goal of getting an idea adopted. The next
