# Lesson 65: Your First 90 Days as an EM

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **peer-to-boss transition** — becoming the manager of people who were your equals yesterday; every relationship must be renegotiated.
> - **power dynamic** — the asymmetry created when one person influences the other's career (Lesson 21's power gap).
> - **the friend problem** — managing a close friend fairly while the team watches for favoritism.
> - **the disappointed rival** — the person who also wanted your job and now reports to you.
> - **wear the manager hat** — to act in the manager role even when it's personally awkward.
> - **inherit (a team)** — to take over an existing team from a previous manager; **predecessor** — the person who held the role before you.
> - **trash** (verb) — to publicly disparage; never do it to your predecessor.
> - **the real performance picture** — how people are *actually* doing, beneath inherited labels and reputations.
> - **peer group** — as a manager, your true peers are now other managers, not your old teammates (Lesson 66).
> - **false pretense** — pretending nothing changed; the tempting, harmful option.

## Concept

Becoming an Engineering Manager is a bigger jump than tech lead — you now have formal authority over people,
performance, and careers. The hardest version is when you're **managing former peers** (or the team you were
just on) — every relationship has to be renegotiated, including with friends and the person who also wanted
the job. The first 90 days as an EM are about navigating that transition (the peer-to-boss shift), inheriting
the team well (without trashing your predecessor), and getting the real picture (1:1s with everyone, the true
performance state).

```
   YOUR FIRST 90 DAYS AS AN EM
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ • PEER-TO-BOSS: renegotiate EVERY relationship (the     │
   │   friend problem; the person who wanted the job)       │
   │ • INHERITING a team: don't trash the predecessor        │
   │ • 1:1s with EVERYONE (first two weeks) — build trust,   │
   │   get the real picture                                 │
   │ • find the REAL performance picture                     │
   │ • your new PEER GROUP (other managers); STOP old-job    │
   │   habits (you're not the IC anymore)                   │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The reframe: **the EM transition (especially managing former peers) requires renegotiating every
relationship — you're not their peer anymore, you're their manager — and doing it honestly and early.** The
peer-to-boss shift is the hard part: relationships that were peer-to-peer now have a power dynamic, which
must be acknowledged and renavigated (especially with friends and rivals). Handling this transition
directly and honestly — rather than pretending nothing changed — is what makes managing former peers work.

## Going Deeper

### The peer-to-boss transition — renegotiate every relationship

The core challenge of managing former peers: **every relationship changes, and must be renegotiated.** You
were their peer; now you're their manager — which introduces a power dynamic (you influence their
performance reviews, growth, and careers) that changes everything, whether or not anyone acknowledges it.
The mistake is pretending nothing changed ("we're all still the same"); the reality is the relationships
<em>have</em> changed, and pretending otherwise creates confusion and awkwardness. Handle it by
<strong>acknowledging the change directly and honestly</strong> — have the explicit conversation ("our
relationship is changing; I'm now your manager, and I want to navigate that well — here's how I'm thinking
about it") — and renegotiating each relationship consciously. Directness beats pretending.

### The friend problem

A hard specific case: **managing a friend.** You're now the manager of a close work friend — which creates
tension (can you be their manager fairly AND their friend? how do you handle the power imbalance, the
perception of favoritism, the hard conversations you might have to have with them?). Handle it honestly: (1)
**acknowledge it directly** with them ("our relationship is changing — I value our friendship, and I also
have to be a fair manager to you and the whole team"); (2) **be scrupulously fair** (avoid favoritism, and
the <em>perception</em> of it — the team watches); (3) **be clear you'll have to wear the manager hat**
sometimes (including hard feedback or decisions) and that it's not personal. The friendship may change (the
power dynamic makes pure peer-friendship hard), and being honest about that (rather than pretending) is
kinder than false pretense. Many manage friends well, but it requires honesty about the changed dynamic.

### The person who also wanted the job

Another hard case: **managing someone who also wanted (and didn't get) your job** — a senior peer who's
disappointed, possibly resentful, and now reports to you. Handle it directly and generously: (1)
**acknowledge it** (don't pretend it's not there — have the conversation: "I know you were also up for this
role, and I imagine that's hard"); (2) **show respect and value** for them (they're capable — you want them
engaged, not alienated); (3) **invest in their growth and their path** (help them toward <em>their</em>
goals — sponsor them, give them scope — Lesson 33 — so the role isn't a dead end for them); (4) **be secure,
not threatened** (their capability is an asset, not a rivalry). Handled well (acknowledged, respected,
invested in), the disappointed rival can become a strong ally; handled badly (ignored, or treated as a
threat), they become resentful and disengaged (or leave).

### Inheriting a team — don't trash the predecessor

When you inherit an existing team (from a previous manager), a key rule: **don't trash your predecessor.**
Even if the previous manager did things poorly (and you'll hear about it), publicly criticizing them is a
mistake: (1) it's unprofessional and looks bad on you; (2) it makes the team wonder if you'll trash them too;
(3) it poisons the atmosphere. Instead, be respectful (or neutral) about the predecessor, acknowledge what
was good, and focus forward (what you'll do), addressing problems constructively without disparaging the
person. You can fix what was broken without trashing who was there before — and doing so shows class and
builds trust.

### 1:1s with everyone, the real performance picture, and the new peer group

Three more early priorities: (1) **1:1s with everyone in the first two weeks** — meet each person, build
trust, understand them, and hear their perspective (the listen-first principle, Lesson 64). (2) **Find the
real performance picture** — understand the true state of each person's performance (which may differ from
what you assumed as a peer, or from the predecessor's view) — through the 1:1s, the work, and evidence
(you're now responsible for performance, so you need the real picture). (3) **Build your new peer group** —
your peers are now <em>other managers</em> (not the engineers you manage), so build relationships with other
EMs (a crucial support system — Lesson 66) and recognize your peer group has shifted. And **stop old-job
habits** — you're not the IC anymore, so let go of the individual work and the peer-relationships-as-peers,
and step fully into the manager role.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** You've just been promoted to **manage the team you were on** — so you now manage your
former peers. Two relationships are especially hard: (1) **Sam**, your **closest work friend** on the team
(you've been close, socialized together, complained about management together — and now you're their
manager); and (2) **Priya**, a **senior engineer who also wanted the manager job** and didn't get it (she's
capable, was a peer, and is likely disappointed). You need a first-week plan and to handle both hard
conversations.

**Write your first-week plan and both hard conversations (with Sam and Priya).** Then note the principles and
mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong response plans the transition (1:1s with everyone, acknowledging the change) and handles both hard
relationships honestly and directly. Example:
<br><br>
<strong>First-week plan:</strong> "(1) <strong>1:1s with everyone</strong> — meet each person, acknowledge
the transition, listen, start building the manager relationship, and get the real picture. (2)
<strong>Acknowledge the change openly</strong> — with the team and individually: 'Our relationships are
changing — I'm now your manager, and I want to navigate that honestly and well.' (3) <strong>Prioritize the
two hard conversations</strong> (Sam and Priya) early. (4) <strong>Listen-first</strong> (Lesson 64) — no big
changes, understand the team. (5) <strong>Start building my new peer group</strong> (other EMs) and letting
go of IC work."
<br><br>
<strong>The conversation with Sam (the friend):</strong> "Direct and honest about the changed dynamic: 'Sam,
I want to talk about us, because our relationship is changing and I don't want to pretend it's not. I really
value our friendship — that's real and I want to keep it. And I also have to be a fair manager to you and to
the whole team, which means sometimes I'll have to wear the manager hat — give you feedback, make decisions,
be fair to everyone, including not showing favoritism. I want to be upfront that some things will be
different (I can't be your venting buddy about management anymore, for instance), and I'd rather name that
than have it be awkward. How are you feeling about it? I want us to navigate this well together.'" <em>(Acknowledges
the change directly, values the friendship, is honest about the manager role and its limits, invites his
perspective — rather than pretending nothing changed.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>The conversation with Priya (who wanted the job):</strong> "Direct, respectful, and generous: 'Priya,
I want to talk openly — I know you were also up for this role, and I imagine it's disappointing that it went
to me. I want to acknowledge that honestly, and say I have real respect for you and your capabilities. I'd
really like to have you as a partner in leading this team well — your experience and judgment matter to me. I
also want to invest in your growth and your path — let's talk about where you want to go and how I can help
you get there [sponsorship, scope, a lead track if she wants it]. I don't see you as a rival; I see you as
someone I want engaged and growing. How are you feeling, and what would make this work for you?'" <em>(Acknowledges
the disappointment directly, shows respect and values her, invests in her growth/path, is secure not
threatened — turning a potential rival into an ally.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Acknowledge the change directly</strong> — every relationship
changed; name it honestly rather than pretending. (2) <strong>The friend: honest about the changed
dynamic</strong> — value the friendship AND be clear about the manager role and its limits (fairness, no
favoritism, the manager hat); the friendship changes, and honesty is kinder than pretense. (3) <strong>The
rival: acknowledge, respect, invest, be secure</strong> — name the disappointment, value her, invest in her
growth/path, treat her as an ally not a threat. (4) <strong>1:1s with everyone, listen-first</strong>. (5)
<strong>Be scrupulously fair</strong> (especially re: the friend — the team watches for favoritism). (6)
<strong>Directness over pretending</strong> — the hard conversations, had honestly and early, prevent
festering awkwardness/resentment. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>pretending nothing
changed</strong> ('we're all still the same') — the relationships DID change; pretending creates confusion
and awkwardness; (2) <strong>avoiding the hard conversations</strong> with Sam and Priya (letting the
awkwardness/resentment fester); (3) <strong>favoritism toward the friend</strong> (or the perception of it —
the team watches; be scrupulously fair); (4) <strong>treating Priya as a threat</strong> (defensive,
withholding — alienates a capable person who could be an ally; be secure and invest in her); (5) <strong>ignoring
Priya's disappointment</strong> (pretending it's not there — it festers into resentment); (6) <strong>not
being honest with Sam</strong> about the changed dynamic (false pretense that the friendship is unchanged —
which creates problems when you have to wear the manager hat); (7) <strong>charging in with changes</strong>
before understanding the team (listen-first still applies).
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why does the peer-to-boss transition require "renegotiating every relationship," and why is
pretending nothing changed a mistake?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The peer-to-boss transition requires renegotiating every relationship because <strong>becoming the manager of
former peers fundamentally changes the nature of those relationships — a power dynamic now exists that didn't
before — so each relationship must be consciously renavigated to account for the new reality</strong>. When
you were peers, your relationships were peer-to-peer: equal, no formal power over each other, you could vent
together, be candid as equals, socialize as friends. Now that you're their manager, a <strong>power
dynamic</strong> exists: you influence their performance reviews, their growth, their compensation, their
careers — you have formal authority over them. This changes everything about the relationship, whether or not
anyone acknowledges it: (1) they may be more guarded with you (you're now their boss, so they can't be as
candid — they won't complain to you about management the way they did when you were a peer, and they may
watch what they say); (2) there's a fairness/favoritism dimension (you have to be fair to everyone, and both
you and they — and the rest of the team — are aware of how you treat each person); (3) the friendship or
peer-candor dynamic is altered by the power imbalance. So the relationships have genuinely changed (the power
dynamic is real and alters them), which means each must be <em>renegotiated</em> — consciously renavigated to
work under the new reality (what does our relationship look like now that I'm your manager? — for the friend,
the rival, each team member). Renegotiating means having the conversations, acknowledging the change, and
establishing how you'll relate now, rather than assuming the old peer-relationship continues unchanged. Why
<strong>pretending nothing changed</strong> is a mistake: <strong>because the relationships DID change, so
pretending they didn't creates confusion, awkwardness, and problems — the pretense doesn't make the power
dynamic go away; it just leaves it unaddressed and creates a mismatch between the pretense and the
reality</strong>. The tempting move (especially to avoid the awkwardness of the transition) is to act like
"we're all still the same, nothing's changed, I'm still just one of you" — but this fails because: (1)
<strong>the power dynamic exists whether acknowledged or not</strong> — you <em>are</em> now their manager
(with authority over their performance and careers), so pretending you're still a peer doesn't change that
reality; it just leaves the real change unaddressed. (2) <strong>it creates confusion and awkwardness</strong>
— when the pretense ("we're still peers") meets the reality (you have to give feedback, make decisions, be
fair, wear the manager hat), there's a jarring mismatch: the friend who thought nothing changed is surprised/
hurt when you have to manage them; the team is confused about whether you're a peer or a boss. The
unacknowledged change surfaces awkwardly. (3) <strong>it prevents navigating the change well</strong> — you
can only renavigate a relationship you acknowledge has changed; pretending it hasn't means you never have the
honest conversation about how you'll relate now, so the relationship drifts into confusion or resentment
rather than being consciously renegotiated. (4) <strong>it's less honest and ultimately less kind</strong> —
the friend, the rival, and the team deserve honesty about the changed dynamic; pretending is a false comfort
that creates worse problems later than an honest early conversation would. So the mistake of pretending
nothing changed is that it denies the reality (the power dynamic is real), creating a mismatch that surfaces
awkwardly and prevents the honest renegotiation the changed relationships need. The right approach is
<strong>directness</strong>: acknowledge the change openly ("our relationship is changing; I'm now your
manager, and I want to navigate that honestly") and renegotiate each relationship consciously — which is more
honest, prevents the awkward mismatch, and lets you establish how you'll relate under the new reality. Both
points reflect that the peer-to-boss transition is a real change in the relationships (a power dynamic now
exists) that must be handled honestly and directly (renegotiate each relationship, acknowledge the change) —
not pretended away (which denies the reality and creates confusion, awkwardness, and unaddressed problems).
Navigating the transition well means facing the changed relationships directly, not wishing them unchanged.
</details>

**Q2.** How should you handle managing a friend and managing someone who wanted your job, and why not "trash
the predecessor" when inheriting a team?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Managing a friend</strong>: handle it with <em>honesty about the changed dynamic</em>: (1)
<strong>acknowledge it directly</strong> — have the explicit conversation ("our relationship is changing; I
value our friendship, and I also have to be a fair manager to you and the whole team"), rather than
pretending the friendship is unchanged; (2) <strong>be scrupulously fair</strong> — avoid favoritism AND the
<em>perception</em> of it (the team watches how you treat your friend, so any favoritism — real or perceived —
damages your credibility and the team's trust); (3) <strong>be clear you'll wear the manager hat</strong> —
that you'll sometimes have to give hard feedback or make decisions affecting them, and that it's not personal;
and (4) <strong>accept the friendship may change</strong> — the power dynamic makes pure peer-friendship hard
(you can't be their venting-buddy about management anymore, for instance), and being honest about that is
kinder than pretending. The key is honesty: value the friendship, but be clear-eyed and upfront about how the
manager role changes it, rather than pretending nothing's different (which creates problems when you inevitably
have to manage them). Many manage friends well, but it requires this honesty about the changed dynamic and
scrupulous fairness. <strong>Managing someone who wanted your job</strong>: handle it <em>directly and
generously</em>: (1) <strong>acknowledge it</strong> — don't pretend it's not there; name it ("I know you
were also up for this role, and I imagine that's hard"), because the disappointment is real and ignoring it
lets it fester; (2) <strong>show respect and value</strong> for them — they're capable (they were a viable
candidate), and you want them engaged, not alienated; (3) <strong>invest in their growth and their path</strong>
— help them toward <em>their</em> goals (sponsor them, give them scope, support a lead track if they want it —
Lesson 33), so the role isn't a dead end for them; and (4) <strong>be secure, not threatened</strong> — treat
their capability as an asset (a strong person on your team), not a rivalry to suppress. Handled well
(acknowledged, respected, invested in, treated as an ally), the disappointed rival can become a strong ally
and partner; handled badly (ignored, or treated as a threat to be suppressed) they become resentful,
disengaged, or leave (losing a capable person and creating a poisonous dynamic). The key is security and
generosity: acknowledge their disappointment, value and invest in them, and turn a potential rival into an
ally — rather than being defensive or threatened. Why not <strong>trash the predecessor</strong> when
inheriting a team: <strong>because publicly criticizing the previous manager is unprofessional, makes the
team distrust you, and poisons the atmosphere — you can fix what was broken without disparaging who was
there before</strong>. Even if the previous manager did things poorly (and you'll hear the complaints),
trashing them is a mistake for several reasons: (1) <strong>it's unprofessional and reflects badly on
you</strong> — publicly criticizing a predecessor looks petty and classless, and diminishes you (a leader who
badmouths others looks insecure and unprofessional); (2) <strong>it makes the team wonder if you'll trash
them too</strong> — if you openly criticize the previous manager, the team learns that you'll speak badly of
people, so they wonder if you'll do the same to them (behind their backs), which erodes trust and makes them
guarded; (3) <strong>it poisons the atmosphere</strong> — dwelling on and disparaging the past creates
negativity, versus a constructive forward focus. Instead, be <strong>respectful (or at least neutral) about
the predecessor</strong>, acknowledge what was good, and <strong>focus forward</strong> (what you'll do,
addressing problems constructively) — you can fix what was broken (address the real problems, improve things)
without disparaging the person who was there. This shows class, builds trust (the team sees you're
professional and won't badmouth people), and keeps the atmosphere constructive. Fixing problems and trashing
the predecessor are separable — do the former (constructively address what needs fixing) without the latter
(disparaging the person). All three points reflect handling the EM transition with honesty, generosity, and
professionalism: the friend (honest about the changed dynamic, scrupulously fair), the rival (acknowledge,
respect, invest, be secure — turn them into an ally), and the predecessor (don't trash them — fix problems
constructively without disparaging) — together navigating the relational challenges of becoming a manager
(especially over former peers, and inheriting a team) in a way that builds trust and turns potential
conflicts into working relationships, rather than creating resentment, distrust, and a poisoned atmosphere.
</details>
