# Lesson 35: Where Influence Actually Comes From

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **influence vs authority** — getting things done through trust and persuasion vs through a title and reporting line.
> - **credibility** — your track record making people trust your judgment (Lesson 12).
> - **framing** — presenting an idea in terms of what the *listener* cares about.
> - **currency** (metaphor) — something you earn and spend; influence is "the leadership currency."
> - **track record** — your history of delivering and being right.
> - **reciprocity** (ress-ih-PROSS-ih-tee) — the human tendency to help those who have helped you.
> - **trust battery** — Shopify's image: each relationship holds a charge that good interactions fill and letdowns drain.
> - **goodwill** — accumulated positive feeling toward you.
> - **influence audit** — mapping who really decides something and where you stand with each of them.
> - **hit a ceiling** — to stop being able to advance.

## Concept

The higher you go in leadership, the more you get things done through people you *don't* control —
peers, other teams, senior folks — where authority (a title, a reporting line) doesn't work. What
works instead is **influence**, and influence comes from a few real sources: **credibility** (your
track record), **relationships** (built before you need them), and **framing** (how you present
things). Understanding and deliberately building these is investing in the currency you'll spend for
the rest of your leadership career.

```
   THE SOURCES OF INFLUENCE
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ CREDIBILITY   your track record — do people trust your │
   │               judgment and that you deliver?           │
   │ RELATIONSHIPS built BEFORE you need them — trust,       │
   │               reciprocity, knowing people              │
   │ FRAMING       how you present it — in their terms,      │
   │               tied to what they care about             │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
   Authority (title) stops working one level up. Influence
   (credibility + relationships + framing) is what scales.
```

The reframe: **authority stops working as you rise; influence — credibility, relationships, framing —
is what actually gets things done, and it must be built <em>before</em> you need it.** As an IC you
could often just do things or rely on your manager's authority; as a lead, much of your impact is on
people you can't direct, so you need influence. And influence isn't summoned on demand — the
credibility and relationships you'll spend are built over time, in advance, so the work is
continuous.

## Going Deeper

### Credibility — your track record

**Credibility** is whether people trust your judgment and believe you deliver — built on your track
record: have you been right before? do you do what you say? do you know what you're talking about? do
you act with integrity? People are influenced by those they find credible — so a strong track record
of good judgment, reliability, and expertise <em>is</em> influence. You build credibility by
consistently delivering, being right (and honest when wrong), and demonstrating competence over time.
It's slow to build and fast to lose (one broken commitment or bad call dents it), so protecting your
credibility (only committing to what you'll deliver, being honest) is protecting your influence.

### Relationships — built before you need them

**Relationships** are the second source — people are influenced by those they know, trust, and like,
and who've helped them. The crucial insight: **build relationships before you need them.** If you only
reach out to someone when you want something, you have no relationship to draw on (and it's
transparent). Investing in relationships continuously — genuine connection, helping people, being a
good colleague (small talk, Lesson from English track; reciprocity) — means that when you need
influence, there's a real relationship there. The time to build the relationship is long before the
moment you need the favor.

### Framing — presenting in their terms

**Framing** is how you present an idea — and the same idea framed in terms of what the *other person*
cares about is far more influential. Tying your proposal to their goals, their concerns, their
metrics ("this helps your team hit the reliability target you care about") makes it compelling to
them, where framing it in your terms doesn't. Framing is influence through presentation:
understanding what moves the specific person and presenting your idea in that light (audience-first,
Phase 3; persuasion, English track Lesson 42).

### Reciprocity and the trust battery

Two mechanics: (1) **Reciprocity** — people are inclined to help those who've helped them; being
genuinely helpful to others (without keeping score, but the goodwill accrues) builds a reservoir of
influence. Give before you take. (2) **The trust battery** (a Shopify concept) — each relationship
has a "trust battery" that charges with positive interactions (reliability, help, honesty) and
depletes with negative ones (broken commitments, letdowns); your influence with someone tracks their
trust battery for you. Both point to the same practice: build goodwill and trust continuously through
positive, reliable, helpful interactions.

### Why title-based authority stops working one level up

As an IC or junior lead, you could sometimes rely on authority (your manager's, or positional). But
influence-through-authority **stops working as you go up and out**: peers don't report to you, other
teams don't take your orders, senior people aren't moved by your title. The higher and broader your
impact, the more it depends on people you can't direct — so authority is replaced by influence
(credibility, relationships, framing) as the thing that gets things done. Leaders who rely on
authority hit a ceiling; those who build influence keep scaling. This is a core reason influence is
<em>the</em> leadership currency.

### The influence audit

A useful practice: for something you want to make happen, **audit the influence landscape** — who
actually decides? who do they listen to? where do you stand with each (strong relationship? credible
to them? or unknown/negative)? This maps the real influence network (which is often different from
the org chart) and shows you where to invest (build the relationship, establish credibility, find an
advocate). Influence is spent through specific people; mapping them is how you deploy it deliberately.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** There's a change you care about — say, you want the org to adopt a shared design-review
process across teams (you believe it'll raise quality and catch problems early). You don't have authority
over the other teams; getting this to happen depends entirely on influence. Before you do anything, you
want to understand the actual influence landscape — who really decides, who they listen to, and where you
stand with each.

**Map your real influence network for this change** — list who decides, who they listen to, and where you
stand with each, and what that tells you about how to proceed. Then note the principles and mistakes to
avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong influence audit maps the real decision network (not just the org chart) and honestly assesses
where you stand, revealing where to invest. Example:
<br><br>
<strong>Who actually decides?</strong> "For an org-wide process change: probably the engineering
director (formal authority), but in practice also the senior/staff engineers whose opinion carries weight
across teams, and the team leads who'd have to adopt it. Map them: Director Sara (formal decision), Staff
engineer Raj (highly influential — people follow his technical lead), Team leads Mia, Tom, Chen (their
teams would adopt it). The <em>real</em> decision needs the director's blessing AND the influential
engineers' buy-in AND the leads' willingness — not just a top-down order."
<br><br>
<strong>Who do they listen to?</strong> "Sara (director) listens heavily to Raj (staff) on technical
matters and to the team leads on feasibility. Raj is influenced by data and by whether something's
technically sound. The leads listen to each other and to their teams (they won't adopt something their
teams hate). So Raj is a key node — if I win Raj, that heavily influences Sara and gives the leads
technical cover."
<br><br>
<strong>Where do I stand with each?</strong> "Honest assessment: Raj — decent relationship, he respects
my technical work (credible to him) — a strength to build on. Sara — she knows my name but we don't have a
strong relationship — a gap to close. Mia — good relationship (we've worked together). Tom — neutral,
don't know him well — a gap. Chen — slightly negative (we clashed on a past project) — a risk to repair.
So: strong with Raj and Mia, gaps with Sara and Tom, a repair needed with Chen."
<br><br>
<strong>What this tells me:</strong> "Strategy: (1) Start with Raj — I'm credible to him and he's the key
influencer; win him first (with data, technically sound framing), and he influences Sara and gives the
leads cover. (2) Build the relationship with Sara before I need the decision (not cold-pitch her). (3)
Repair with Chen — address the past friction, or at least neutralize his opposition. (4) Use Mia as an
early adopter/advocate. (5) Frame it for each: for Sara, org quality/risk; for Raj, technical soundness;
for the leads, low adoption cost and team benefit. I'll socialize 1:1 before any group decision (Lesson
36)."
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>The real decision network ≠ the org chart</strong> — influential
engineers, advocates, and the people who must adopt it all matter, not just the formal decider. (2)
<strong>Identify who influences the deciders</strong> — winning the key influencer (Raj) can move the
decider (Sara); influence flows through people. (3) <strong>Honestly assess where you stand</strong> —
credibility and relationship with each (strengths, gaps, risks) shows where to invest. (4) <strong>Invest
where there are gaps</strong> — build the Sara relationship, repair Chen, before you need the decision. (5)
<strong>Frame per person</strong> — each cares about different things. (6) <strong>This maps the campaign</strong>
— who to win first, who to repair, who to enlist as advocates. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1)
<strong>only looking at the org chart</strong> — missing the real influencers (Raj) and the adopters whose
buy-in you need; (2) <strong>cold-pitching the decider</strong> without building the relationship or
winning the influencers first (Lesson 36 — never surprise a decision-maker); (3) <strong>ignoring where
you stand</strong> — pitching to people you have no credibility/relationship with, or ignoring a
repairable negative (Chen); (4) <strong>same framing for everyone</strong> — not tailoring to what each
cares about; (5) <strong>trying to build influence only now</strong> — if you had no relationships built
in advance, you're starting from zero (the lesson: build them before you need them); (6) <strong>relying
on authority you don't have</strong> — trying to mandate it top-down without the influence groundwork
(it'll be resisted).
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why does authority stop working as you rise, and what are the three main sources of influence
that replace it?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Authority stops working as you rise because <strong>the higher and broader your impact, the more it
depends on people you don't control — peers, other teams, senior stakeholders — who aren't moved by a
title or reporting line</strong>. As an individual contributor or junior lead, you could often get things
done through authority: your own positional authority over a small scope, or by relying on your manager's
authority to back you. But as your impact grows and broadens, it increasingly involves people you have no
authority over: peers (who don't report to you), other teams (who don't take your orders), and senior
people (who aren't impressed by your title). None of them are obligated to do what you say — a title
grants authority only over those who report to you, and most of the people whose cooperation you need for
bigger impact don't. So authority-based influence hits a ceiling: it works within your direct scope but
fails for the cross-team, peer, and upward influence that broader leadership requires. Leaders who rely on
authority (trying to mandate, order, or pull rank) find it stops working — people outside their authority
resist or ignore it — so they can't scale their impact beyond what they directly control. What replaces
authority is <strong>influence</strong>, which works on people regardless of reporting lines, and it comes
from three main sources: (1) <strong>Credibility</strong> — your track record: whether people trust your
judgment and believe you deliver. People are influenced by those they find credible (have been right
before, do what they say, know what they're talking about, act with integrity), so a strong track record
of good judgment, reliability, and competence <em>is</em> influence — people go along with credible
people. (2) <strong>Relationships</strong> — people are influenced by those they know, trust, like, and
who've helped them; a genuine relationship (built on trust, reciprocity, positive history) makes someone
receptive to your influence, where a stranger or someone you've only ever taken from is not. The key is
these must be built <em>before</em> you need them (you can't summon a relationship on demand). (3)
<strong>Framing</strong> — how you present the idea: the same proposal framed in terms of what the
<em>other person</em> cares about (their goals, concerns, metrics) is far more influential than framed in
your terms. Framing is influence through presentation — making your idea compelling to the specific person
by tying it to what moves them. Together, credibility (they trust you), relationships (they know and like
you), and framing (you present it in their terms) are what actually get things done through people you
don't control — the influence that replaces authority as you rise. Underlying mechanics reinforce them:
reciprocity (people help those who've helped them — give before you take) and the trust battery (charged
by reliable, helpful, honest interactions). The core insight is that <strong>as leadership scales beyond
what you directly control, the currency shifts from authority (which only works on your reports) to
influence (which works on anyone), and influence is built through credibility, relationships, and framing</strong>
— which is why building these deliberately (and in advance) is investing in the currency you'll spend for
the rest of your leadership career. Leaders who understand this build influence continuously and keep
scaling; those who rely on authority hit the ceiling where their title runs out.
</details>

**Q2.** Why must relationships be "built before you need them," and how do reciprocity and the trust
battery work?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Relationships must be "built before you need them" because <strong>a relationship is a reservoir of
trust and goodwill that takes time to build, so it must exist <em>before</em> the moment you need to draw
on it — and reaching out only when you want something is both too late and transparently self-serving</strong>.
The dynamic: influence through relationships works because people help and are receptive to those they
know, trust, like, and have positive history with. But that trust and goodwill accumulate over time
through genuine interaction (working together, helping each other, being a good colleague) — you can't
create them instantly. So if you have no relationship with someone and suddenly need their help or
influence, there's nothing to draw on: you're a stranger asking for a favor, with no trust or goodwill
banked. Worse, if your <em>only</em> contact with people is when you want something, it's transparent and
off-putting — people notice that you only appear when you need them (the person who's never around until
they want a favor), which is transactional and damages rather than builds the relationship (it signals
you see them as a means, not a person). The insight is therefore to invest in relationships
<em>continuously and in advance</em> — genuine connection, helping people, being a good and reliable
colleague — so that a real relationship exists <em>before</em> you need it, and drawing on it feels natural
(you're a trusted colleague asking, not a stranger extracting). The time to build the relationship is long
before the moment you need the favor; by the time you need it, it's too late to start. This is why
relationship-building is ongoing background work, not something you do reactively when a need arises. How
<strong>reciprocity</strong> works: people have a strong inclination to help those who've helped them —
it's a deep social norm (returning favors, not wanting to be in debt). So <strong>being genuinely helpful
to others builds a reservoir of goodwill and reciprocal inclination</strong>: when you help people
(without keeping a ledger, but the goodwill accrues), they're inclined to help you later. The practice is
"give before you take" — be generous with help, support, and value, which both builds relationships and
creates the reciprocal goodwill that becomes influence when you need it. Reciprocity means the helping you
do is also an investment in future influence (though it should be genuine, not calculated — people sense
transactional "help"). How the <strong>trust battery</strong> works (a Shopify concept): each relationship
has a metaphorical "trust battery" representing how much the person trusts you, which <strong>charges with
positive interactions</strong> (you're reliable, you help them, you're honest, you deliver on commitments)
and <strong>depletes with negative ones</strong> (you break commitments, let them down, are dishonest or
unreliable). Your influence with someone tracks their trust battery for you: a well-charged battery (lots
of positive history) means high trust and receptiveness (influence), while a depleted one (broken trust,
letdowns) means low. The trust battery captures that trust (and thus influence) is built and depleted
incrementally through the accumulated pattern of your interactions — so every reliable, helpful, honest
interaction charges it (building influence), and every letdown depletes it (eroding influence). Both
concepts point to the same practice: <strong>build trust and goodwill continuously through positive,
reliable, helpful, honest interactions, in advance of needing them</strong> — reciprocity (give before you
take, building goodwill) and the trust battery (charge it through reliability and help) are the mechanics
by which the relationships and credibility that constitute influence get built over time. The unifying
lesson is that influence is a <em>continuously-built currency</em>, not an on-demand tool: you invest in
relationships, reciprocity, and trust before you need them, so that when you need to influence (get help,
buy-in, cooperation from people you don't control), the reservoir is there. A lead who understands this
invests continuously in relationships and goodwill (and protects their credibility through reliability and
honesty), building the influence they'll spend — rather than scrambling to build relationships at the
moment they need a favor, which is too late.
</details>

## Homework

Invest in your influence. (1) Do an influence audit for something you want to make happen: who decides,
who they listen to, and where you stand with each (credibility, relationship — strengths, gaps, risks)?
(2) Assess your credibility — do people trust your judgment and delivery? Is there anything denting it
(over-committing, being unreliable)? (3) Assess your relationships — are you building them before you need
them, or only reaching out when you want something? Identify a key relationship to invest in <em>now</em>
(before you need it). (4) Practice framing — take a proposal and frame it in terms of what the specific
audience cares about. Reflect: are you building influence continuously (credibility, relationships,
framing) or relying on authority — and where's your biggest influence gap?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds influence-awareness and the deliberate construction of the leadership currency. A
strong response does an influence audit (who decides, who they listen to, where you stand), assesses their
own credibility and relationship-building habits, identifies a key relationship to invest in before
needing it, and practices audience-tailored framing. The realizations: (1) <strong>influence replaces
authority as you rise</strong> — much of a lead's impact is on people they don't control (peers, other
teams, senior folks), where a title doesn't work, so influence (credibility, relationships, framing) is
the currency; (2) <strong>credibility is your track record</strong> — people are influenced by those they
trust to have good judgment and deliver, so protecting your credibility (only committing to what you'll
deliver, being honest) protects your influence; (3) <strong>build relationships before you need them</strong>
— a relationship is a reservoir built over time, so reaching out only when you want something is too late
and transparent; invest continuously; (4) <strong>frame in their terms</strong> — the same idea framed
around what the other person cares about is far more influential; (5) <strong>reciprocity and the trust
battery</strong> — give before you take, and build trust through reliable, helpful, honest interactions.
On reflection, people commonly find they rely more on authority/just-doing-things than they can as they
scale, that they build relationships reactively (only when they need something) rather than in advance,
and that they don't tailor framing to the audience. The highest-value change is usually: invest in key
relationships before you need them, protect/build credibility through reliable delivery, and audit the
influence landscape before pushing a change. The meta-point: as leadership scales beyond what you directly
control, the currency shifts from authority (works only on your reports) to influence (works on anyone),
which comes from credibility, relationships, and framing — built continuously and in advance, not summoned
on demand. Authority stops working one level up, so building influence deliberately is investing in the
currency you'll spend for the rest of your leadership career. This is the foundation of the phase; the next
lessons apply it — building buy-in for ideas, resolving conflict, aligning teams, driving change — all of
which run on the influence (credibility, relationships, framing) this lesson is about. The next lesson
covers building buy-in for an idea — getting to "yes" through the influence groundwork (socializing 1:1,
co-ownership, addressing objections) before the meeting where the decision happens.
</details>
