# Lesson 21: Receiving Feedback as a Lead

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **information supply** — the flow of honest truth reaching you; a lead's decisions depend on it.
> - **the flinch** — your visible first reaction to criticism (a frown, a tightening, a "well, actually…") — the thing everyone watches.
> - **defensiveness** — protecting yourself by explaining and justifying instead of listening.
> - **power gap** — the authority difference that makes criticizing you feel risky to your team.
> - **clarifying question** — a question asked to understand, not to argue ("can you give me an example?").
> - **digest** — to take feedback away and think it over instead of responding on the spot.
> - **close the loop** — to come back later and show what you did with the feedback.
> - **leading blind** — making decisions without the honest information you've silenced.
> - **retro(spective)** — the team meeting reviewing how the last period of work went.
> - **permission-giving question** — a specific ask that makes criticism safe ("what's one thing I should stop doing?").

## Concept

Giving feedback is only half the skill — *receiving* it well is arguably more important for a
lead, because **your information supply depends on it.** Power distorts honesty: people are
naturally reluctant to criticize someone with authority over them, so if you react badly even
once — defend, explain, or visibly flinch — you teach the team it's not safe to tell you the
truth, and the feedback dries up. Then you're leading blind. Making it safe to be told hard
things is a core, and counterintuitive, leadership skill.

```
   THE FEEDBACK-TO-A-LEAD PROBLEM:
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ People fear criticizing someone with power over them   │
   │ → they only tell you if it feels SAFE                   │
   │ → one bad reaction (defend / explain / flinch) teaches  │
   │   "don't tell the lead hard things"                     │
   │ → feedback dries up → you lead blind                    │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

   Your reaction to feedback determines whether you ever get it again.
```

The reframe: **when someone gives you critical feedback, they're taking a risk to help you — so
your job is to make it safe and rewarding, not to respond to the content.** The instinct is to
explain, defend, or justify ("well, the reason I do that is..."). But in the moment, the
<em>content</em> matters far less than the <em>signal your reaction sends</em>: react well
(thank, digest, follow up) and people keep telling you the truth; react defensively and they
stop — permanently.

## How It Works

### Power distorts honesty

Understand the structural problem: people are naturally, rationally reluctant to give critical
feedback to someone who has power over them (their manager, their lead) — it feels risky (could
it hurt my standing, my review, the relationship?). So you will get far *less* honest feedback
than a peer would, and what you do get is offered at some personal risk. This means the honest
critical feedback you receive is precious and fragile — and your job is to protect and encourage
it, not to evaluate it defensively in the moment.

### The flinch — and how it silences teams

The critical moment is your *immediate reaction*. Even a small defensive flinch — a frown, a
"well, actually...", a visible tightening, an explanation of why you did it — sends a loud
signal: *it's not safe to tell this person hard things.* People read your reaction closely
(especially with the power gap), and one bad reaction can silence a team for months (they
conclude feedback isn't welcome and stop offering it). So the discipline is to control your
immediate reaction: no defending, no explaining, no flinching — however much you want to.

### Thank, digest, close the loop

The good pattern when you receive critical feedback: (1) **Thank them** genuinely — "thank you
for telling me, I know that wasn't easy" — which rewards the risk they took and signals it's
welcome. (2) **Digest** rather than react — ask clarifying questions to understand ("can you give
me an example?"), but don't defend or decide on the spot; take it away to consider. (3) **Close
the loop** — come back later with what you did with it ("you were right, I've started X") — which
proves the feedback had effect, making it worth their risk and encouraging more. This pattern
makes giving you feedback safe *and* rewarding.

### Ask for it specifically

Don't wait for feedback — *ask* for it, and make it easy to give. Vague "any feedback for me?"
gets "no, all good" (too broad, too risky). Specific, permission-giving questions work better:
"what's one thing I should stop doing?" / "where am I making your job harder?" / "if you could
change one thing about how I run standup, what would it be?" These lower the risk (you're
inviting it, and narrowing to something specific and safe) and signal you genuinely want it. And
ask repeatedly — a one-time ask isn't enough to overcome the power gap.

### Separate the flinch from the response

You will feel defensive — that's human (feedback stings, especially publicly). The skill isn't
not feeling it; it's *not acting on it in the moment*. Feel the flinch, but respond with thanks
and curiosity, and process the emotional reaction privately later. Over time, genuinely welcoming
feedback (not just performing it) gets easier, and the payoff — a team that tells you the truth —
is enormous.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** You're in a team retrospective. Things are going well until a junior engineer,
Sana, hesitantly says: "Um, this might just be me, but... your late-night Slack messages kind of
stress me out. Even when you say it's not urgent, I feel like I should respond, and it makes it
hard to switch off." The room goes quiet. Everyone is watching your face to see how you react —
this is a test of whether it's safe to be honest with you. (It's also true — you do fire off
messages at night when you think of things.)

**Write exactly what you'd say in response** — your immediate words in the room — and then describe
the follow-up you'd design. Note what your response needs to accomplish and the mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
The immediate response must reward the risk, not defend — because the whole team is reading it as a
signal of whether honesty is safe. Example immediate words:
<br><br>
"Sana — thank you for saying that. I know it wasn't easy to bring up, and I really appreciate it.
And you're completely right: I do fire off messages at night when things pop into my head, and I
hadn't thought enough about how that lands — even 'no rush' still creates pressure to respond. That's
on me, and it's a good thing to fix. Let me think about the best way to handle it — my instinct is
I'll start scheduling those messages to send in the morning, but I'd love to hear if others feel the
same way too. Thank you again for flagging it — this is exactly the kind of thing I want to hear."
<br><br>
<strong>The follow-up:</strong> (1) Actually change the behavior — use scheduled-send for off-hours
messages, so it's fixed, not just acknowledged. (2) Close the loop — in a few days or the next retro,
come back to it: "Following up on what Sana raised — I've switched to scheduling my off-hours
messages for the morning; let me know if it's better, or if I slip." (3) Reinforce the norm — thank
Sana again (maybe privately too), and generally signal that this kind of honesty is valued (so the
one brave act becomes a team norm). (4) Possibly broaden — ask if there are other always-on pressures
to address (making it about the team's health, not just this one behavior).
<br><br>
<strong>What the response needs to accomplish:</strong> (1) <strong>Reward the risk immediately</strong>
— "thank you, I know that wasn't easy" — because Sana took a real risk (criticizing the lead,
publicly, as a junior), and the entire team is watching whether it's rewarded or punished; thanking
her genuinely makes it safe. (2) <strong>Not defend or explain</strong> — crucially, no "well, the
reason I message at night is..." or "you don't have to respond, though" (which shifts blame to her
for feeling pressure); any defense or justification would signal that feedback gets pushback, teaching
the team to stop. (3) <strong>Own it</strong> — "you're right, that's on me" — validating her point
and taking responsibility, which models accountability and confirms her feedback was legitimate. (4)
<strong>Show it'll have effect</strong> — naming a concrete change (scheduling messages) shows the
feedback leads to action, making her risk worthwhile (and the follow-up proves it). (5) <strong>Invite
more</strong> — "I'd love to hear if others feel the same", "exactly the kind of thing I want to hear"
— explicitly signaling that honesty is welcome, converting the moment into a norm. The response's real
audience is the <em>whole team</em>: it's demonstrating, in a high-visibility test moment, that it's
safe to be honest with you. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>Defending or
explaining</strong> ("but I always say no rush", "the reason is...") — the single worst move; it
punishes the feedback and silences the team. (2) <strong>The visible flinch</strong> — a frown, a
defensive tone, going cold — read loudly by everyone as "don't do that again". (3) <strong>Shifting
blame to her</strong> ("you shouldn't feel you have to respond") — makes it her problem for feeling
pressure, which is subtly punishing and misses that the lead's behavior sets the norm. (4)
<strong>Over-promising or being performative</strong> without following through — if you thank her but
don't change or close the loop, the lesson becomes "feedback is theater", which is worse than not
asking. (5) <strong>Only handling it privately later</strong> and letting the public moment pass
awkwardly — the public response matters because the whole team is watching; you need to reward it in
the room. (6) <strong>Making it about you being upset</strong> (even "ouch, okay...") — this can guilt
people out of future honesty; keep the focus on genuine gratitude and ownership.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why does a lead's reaction to feedback matter more (in the moment) than the feedback's
content, and what is the thank-digest-close-the-loop pattern?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A lead's reaction to feedback matters more in the moment than the content because <strong>the
reaction determines whether the person (and the watching team) will ever give you honest feedback
again — and your information supply as a lead depends on continuing to receive it</strong>. The key
is the power dynamic: people are naturally, rationally reluctant to criticize someone with authority
over them (it feels risky to their standing and the relationship), so critical feedback to a lead is
rare, offered at personal risk, and fragile. This means every instance of feedback is also a
<em>test</em>: the person (and often the team, watching) is reading your reaction to learn whether
honesty with you is safe and worthwhile. If you react badly — defend, explain, justify, or even
visibly flinch — you send a loud signal that feedback gets pushback or displeasure, which teaches
people that it's not safe to tell you hard things, and they stop (often permanently, and often the
whole team learns the lesson from one instance). Then the feedback dries up and you lead blind
(making decisions without the honest information about your own impact that you need). If you react
well, people learn it's safe and worthwhile, and keep telling you the truth. So in the moment, the
<em>signal your reaction sends</em> (safe or unsafe) matters far more than engaging with the specific
<em>content</em> of this one piece of feedback — because the reaction determines your entire future
feedback supply, while the single piece of content can be considered later. (You can always digest
and act on the content afterward; you cannot undo a reaction that taught the team to stop being
honest.) The thank-digest-close-the-loop pattern: (1) <strong>Thank them genuinely</strong> — "thank
you for telling me, I know that wasn't easy" — which immediately rewards the risk they took and
signals the feedback is welcome, making it safe (this is the crucial in-the-moment move, especially
because the person and team are watching for exactly this). (2) <strong>Digest, don't react</strong>
— seek to understand (ask clarifying questions, "can you give an example?") but do <em>not</em>
defend, explain, justify, or decide on the spot; take the feedback away to consider it properly.
Digesting rather than reacting both avoids the defensive signal and leads to a better-considered
response than an in-the-moment reaction. (3) <strong>Close the loop</strong> — come back later with
what you did with the feedback ("you were right, I've started doing X"), which proves the feedback
had real effect, making the person's risk worthwhile and strongly encouraging more feedback (people
give feedback to someone who acts on it, and stop giving it to someone who just nods and changes
nothing). This pattern makes giving you feedback both <em>safe</em> (thanking rewards the risk, not
defending avoids punishment) and <em>rewarding</em> (closing the loop shows it matters) — which is
exactly what keeps the honest feedback flowing. The underlying principle: <strong>as a lead, your job
when receiving feedback is not to evaluate the content in the moment but to protect and encourage the
source</strong> — because the honest feedback is precious and fragile (the power gap suppresses it),
and your reaction is what determines whether you keep getting it. Thank, digest, close the loop is the
pattern that makes it safe and rewarding to tell you the truth — which is what a lead's good
decision-making depends on.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is a single defensive "flinch" so costly, and why should a lead ask for feedback
specifically (e.g., "what's one thing I should stop?") rather than generally ("any feedback?")?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A single defensive flinch is so costly because <strong>people read a lead's reaction closely
(magnified by the power gap), and one bad reaction can teach the whole team, for months, that it's
not safe to be honest — silencing the feedback a lead depends on</strong>. Several reasons for the
outsized cost: (1) <strong>the reaction is read as the true signal</strong> — people trust behavior
over words; you might <em>say</em> "I welcome feedback", but if your immediate reaction to actual
criticism is a frown, a "well, actually...", a tightening, or a defensive explanation, that
involuntary reaction is read as your <em>real</em> stance ("feedback is not actually welcome"),
overriding anything you claimed. A single genuine flinch reveals more than a hundred invitations. (2)
<strong>the power gap magnifies it</strong> — because people are already nervous about criticizing
someone with power over them, they're watching your reaction extra-closely and are primed to
retreat at the first sign of displeasure; a small flinch that might be shrugged off between peers is,
from a lead, a strong "stop" signal. (3) <strong>it teaches the whole team, not just the one
person</strong> — feedback often happens with others watching (a retro, a meeting), and even when
private, word spreads; so one bad reaction can teach the entire team the lesson ("don't tell the lead
hard things"), not just the individual. (4) <strong>the effect is durable</strong> — once people
conclude it's not safe, they stop offering feedback, and that silence can last months (they won't
re-test easily), during which you lead blind — and it's hard to rebuild (you have to consistently
prove safety over time to undo one flinch). So the asymmetry is stark: a flinch costs almost nothing
to emit but can shut down your feedback supply for a long time — which is why controlling the
immediate reaction (feel the flinch, but don't act on it) is the core discipline. Why ask for feedback
specifically rather than generally: <strong>a specific, permission-giving question lowers the risk and
effort of giving feedback and signals you genuinely want it, where a vague general ask is too broad
and risky and reliably gets "no, all good"</strong>. "Any feedback for me?" fails because: (1) it's
<strong>too broad</strong> — the person has to figure out what to say across everything, which is hard
and puts the burden on them; (2) it's <strong>too risky</strong> — an open invitation to criticize the
lea, with no safe frame, so the safe answer is "no, all good" (declining the risk); (3) it can read as
<strong>perfunctory</strong> — a general "any feedback?" often sounds like a box-tick, not a genuine
request. A specific question — "what's one thing I should stop doing?", "where am I making your job
harder?", "if you could change one thing about how I run standup, what would it be?" — works better
because: (1) it <strong>lowers the risk</strong> — it's you inviting a specific, bounded critique
(you've pre-authorized it and narrowed it to something concrete and safe), so the person isn't
volunteering an open attack but answering a specific invited question; (2) it <strong>makes it easy</strong>
— a narrow, concrete question is easy to answer (one specific thing) versus the hard open-ended
"anything about me?"; (3) it <strong>presupposes there's something</strong> — "what's <em>one</em>
thing I should stop?" assumes there is one (which there always is), making it socially easier to name
than "is there anything?" (which invites the polite "no"); and (4) it <strong>signals genuine
intent</strong> — a specific, thoughtful question shows you actually want the feedback, not a
box-tick. So specific asks overcome the power-gap reluctance by lowering risk and effort and signaling
sincerity, where general asks let people safely decline. And a lead should ask <em>repeatedly</em> —
one ask isn't enough to overcome the structural reluctance; regularly, specifically inviting feedback
(and then receiving it well — thank, digest, close the loop) is what, over time, builds a team that
actually tells you the truth. Both points serve the core goal: overcoming the power distortion that
suppresses honest feedback to a lead — the flinch discipline protects the feedback you get (don't
punish it), and specific repeated asking actively draws it out (make it safe and easy to give) — so
that you keep receiving the honest information your decisions depend on.
</details>

## Homework

Build your feedback-receiving practice. First, reflect honestly: how do you react when someone
criticizes you? Do you defend, explain, or flinch — or thank and digest? Recall a recent time you
received critical feedback and assess your reaction. Then practice asking: pick a specific,
permission-giving question ("what's one thing I should stop doing?", "where am I making your work
harder?") and actually ask two or three people this week — then receive whatever comes with thanks
and curiosity (no defending), and close the loop later. Notice how it feels to resist the flinch, and
whether asking specifically gets you more than a general ask would. What's one thing that would make
you safer to give feedback to?
