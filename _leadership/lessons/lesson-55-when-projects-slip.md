# Lesson 55: When Projects Slip

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **slip** — to fall behind schedule.
> - **death march** — a doomed period of forced overwork trying to hit an impossible date.
> - **crunch** — sustained long hours under deadline pressure; borrows from the future (burnout, bugs).
> - **watermelon status** — a project reported "green" outside but actually red inside.
> - **velocity** — the measured rate at which work actually completes (the honest signal).
> - **Brooks's Law** — "adding manpower to a late software project makes it later" (onboarding + communication overhead exceed the added capacity).
> - **communication overhead** — coordination cost that grows with team size.
> - **divisible** — able to be split among more people ("nine women can't make a baby in one month").
> - **recovery menu** — the honest options when slipping: cut scope / move date / add people / cut quality — mostly cut scope.
> - **must-haves vs nice-to-haves** — the essential core vs the droppable extras.
> - **false economy** — a "saving" that costs more later (cutting quality).

## Concept

Despite good planning, projects slip — it's normal, not a personal failure. What matters is **recovering
well**: detecting the slip early, choosing the right recovery (which is usually cutting scope, not a death
march), communicating it honestly (no surprises), and preserving your credibility and your team's health.
The two big traps: **detecting slip too late** (status stays "green" until the deadline, then it's a
crisis) and **the death march** (crunching harder, adding people — which usually makes it worse).

```
   RECOVERING A SLIPPING PROJECT (credibility intact, no death march)
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ • DETECT EARLY (velocity, not "status: green" lies)     │
   │ • BROOKS'S LAW: adding people to a late project makes it │
   │   LATER (onboarding cost > added capacity, short-term)  │
   │ • RECOVERY MENU: scope / date / people / quality —      │
   │   mostly CUT SCOPE                                      │
   │ • COMMUNICATE the slip EARLY (no surprises — Lesson 41)  │
   │ • crunch has real costs (burnout, bugs) — rarely worth it│
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The reframe: **projects slip normally — recover by detecting early, cutting scope (not crunching or adding
people), and communicating honestly; protect credibility and the team, no death marches.** The instinct
when slipping is to hide it (status green), then crunch or add people to recover — all of which fail
(late detection makes it a crisis, crunching burns people out and adds bugs, adding people makes it later —
Brooks's Law). The professional recovery is early detection, honest communication (no surprises), and
cutting scope — sustainable and credible.

---

## How It Works

### Detect slip early — velocity, not "status: green" lies

The first key: **detect slippage early**, when you can still recover gracefully — not at the deadline. The
enemy is the "**status: green**" lie — projects that report "on track / green" right up until they
suddenly miss (the "watermelon" status: green outside, red inside). Green-until-it-isn't happens because
people are optimistic, avoid admitting problems, or don't measure real progress. Detect early instead by
watching **real signals**: actual velocity (are we completing work at the rate needed?), whether milestones
(risk-retiring ones — Lesson 50) are actually being hit, and honest status (creating safety for people to
report red — Lesson 21). Early detection (from real velocity/progress signals, not optimistic status) gives
you time to recover with options; late detection (green until the deadline) gives you a crisis with none.

### Brooks's Law — adding people makes it later

A crucial counterintuitive law: **"adding manpower to a late software project makes it later"** (Brooks's
Law). The instinct when behind is to add people — but in the short term it usually makes things <em>worse</em>,
because: (1) new people need <strong>onboarding</strong> (ramp-up time, and they take existing people's time
to onboard — reducing current capacity while they learn); (2) more people means more <strong>communication
overhead</strong> (coordination cost grows with team size); and (3) work isn't always <strong>divisible</strong>
(you can't always parallelize — "nine women can't make a baby in one month"). So adding people to a late
project, especially late in it, typically slows it further (the onboarding and coordination cost exceeds the
added capacity in the timeframe that matters). Adding people can help for the <em>long</em> term (if there's
time to onboard), but as a <em>recovery</em> for a project slipping now, it usually backfires — so it's
rarely the right recovery move.

### The recovery menu — mostly cut scope

When recovering a slipping project, the honest options (the "recovery menu") map to the iron triangle
(Lesson 44): (1) **cut scope** — deliver less (the usually-best option — see below); (2) **move the date** —
take more time (if the date can flex); (3) **add people** — usually backfires short-term (Brooks's Law); (4)
**reduce quality** — cut corners (dangerous — creates debt/bugs, usually a false economy). Of these,
**cutting scope is usually the best recovery**: it's the most controllable (you decide what to drop), it
preserves quality and the date, and there's often scope that's less essential (the walking skeleton /
must-haves vs nice-to-haves). Adding people backfires (Brooks), reducing quality creates problems, moving
the date isn't always possible — so <em>cut scope first</em>. The recovery conversation is usually "what can
we drop to hit the date (or a reasonable date) with quality?"

### Communicate the slip — no surprises

When a project slips, **communicate it early and honestly** — the no-surprises rule (Lesson 41). As soon as
you're confident of the slip (from early detection), tell the stakeholders (your manager, dependent teams,
whoever's planning around it), with the recovery plan/options — <em>before</em> the deadline, never as a
last-minute surprise. Early honest communication (with a recovery plan) lets everyone adjust and preserves
trust (you flagged it responsibly); hiding it until the deadline blindsides everyone and destroys
credibility. This is the same discipline as delivering bad news early (English track Lesson 47) and
renegotiating early (Lesson 44) — the slip is bad news to deliver well.

### The crunch decision and its real costs

The pressure when slipping is to **crunch** (long hours, death march) to recover. Crunch has **real costs**
that usually make it a bad deal: (1) **burnout** — sustained crunch exhausts and demoralizes people, causing
attrition and reduced productivity afterward (you borrow from the future); (2) **more bugs** — tired people
make more mistakes, so crunch often produces lower-quality work (creating problems that cost more later); (3)
**diminishing returns** — productivity per hour drops with excessive hours (working 80 hours doesn't produce
2× the work of 40). So crunch is usually a false economy — it feels like doing something, but the real costs
(burnout, attrition, bugs, diminishing returns) typically exceed the short-term gain, and it's not
sustainable. Occasional short bursts for a genuine crunch moment can be okay (a real deadline, clearly
bounded, with the team's buy-in), but sustained crunch / death marches are a failure of planning and
recovery — cut scope instead. A lead protects the team from unnecessary crunch (it's rarely worth it).

{: .note }
> **Projects slip normally — recover by detecting early, cutting scope, communicating honestly; no death marches</br>**
> Slippage is normal, not failure; recovering well is what matters. <em>Detect early</em> via real velocity
> and honest status (not "green until the deadline" lies), so you have time to recover with options.
> Understand <em>Brooks's Law</em> (adding people to a late project makes it later — onboarding and
> coordination cost, so it rarely recovers a project). Use the <em>recovery menu</em> (scope/date/people/
> quality) but <em>mostly cut scope</em> (the controllable option that preserves quality and date, vs.
> adding people (backfires), reducing quality (creates problems), moving the date (not always possible)).
> <em>Communicate the slip early</em> (no surprises, with a recovery plan — Lesson 41). And resist the
> <em>crunch/death march</em> (real costs — burnout, bugs, diminishing returns — usually a false economy).
> Recovering by early detection, scope-cutting, and honest communication preserves your credibility and your
> team — versus late detection, death marches, and blindsided stakeholders.

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** You're **halfway through** a project (say, week 6 of a planned 12), and you've just
realized — honestly assessing the real progress — that the deadline is **about 40% too optimistic**: at the
current rate, this 12-week project is really going to take ~17 weeks. It's a significant slip. You need to
recover it and communicate it — **today** (you've just realized it; don't sit on it). No death march.

**Write the recovery plan and the stakeholder message — same day.** Then note the principles and mistakes to
avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong response assesses the real situation, builds a scope-cutting recovery plan, and communicates the
slip honestly and early with options. Example:
<br><br>
<strong>The recovery plan (cut scope first):</strong> "First, understand why we're behind (to inform
recovery and future estimates — probably underestimation + unknown unknowns). Then the recovery — and the
lever is <strong>scope</strong>, not crunch or adding people: <br>
• <strong>Identify the must-haves vs nice-to-haves</strong> in the remaining scope. What's the core that
delivers the main value (the walking skeleton), and what's less essential? <br>
• <strong>Cut scope to hit the date (or a reasonable date)</strong>: if we drop [nice-to-have features X, Y,
Z], can we deliver the core by the original date (or close)? Aim to protect the date by cutting scope, or
propose a smaller date move with reduced scope. <br>
• <strong>NOT: add people</strong> (Brooks's Law — at week 6, onboarding new people would slow us further),
NOT crunch/death march (burnout, bugs — false economy), NOT cut quality (creates worse problems). <br>
• <strong>Present options</strong>: (A) original date, cut to core scope [specify]; (B) full scope, +5 weeks;
(C) a middle — some scope cut, some date move. <br>
Recommend based on what matters most (date vs scope)."
<br><br>
<strong>The stakeholder message (today — no surprises):</strong> "Hi [stakeholders] — I need to flag
something important today, as soon as I'm confident of it. <strong>Having honestly assessed our progress at
the halfway point, I believe [project] is going to take longer than planned — realistically ~5 weeks more
than the original date if we hold full scope.</strong> I want to get ahead of this now, not surprise anyone
later. <br>
What happened: we underestimated [X] and hit [unknowns] — I own the estimation miss. <br>
Here's the recovery — and I want to do it sustainably, no death march: the main lever is scope. Options: <br>
• <strong>Option A</strong>: hit the original date by delivering the core [must-haves] and dropping [nice-to-
haves X, Y] (fast-follow later). <br>
• <strong>Option B</strong>: full scope, ~5 weeks later. <br>
• <strong>Option C</strong>: [middle]. <br>
My recommendation is [A — protect the date with core scope], because [the core delivers the main value and
the date matters for X]. I'd rather deliver the core well and on time than everything late or rushed. Which
would you prefer? Happy to talk it through." <em>(Early, honest, owns it, recovery menu with scope-cutting
recommendation, sustainable — no crunch.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Communicate today (no surprises)</strong> — flag the slip the day
you're confident, not at the deadline (early detection enables this — Lesson 41/47). (2) <strong>Cut scope,
not crunch or add people</strong> — scope is the controllable lever; crunch (burnout/bugs) and adding people
(Brooks) backfire. (3) <strong>Recovery menu with a recommendation</strong> — options (date/scope trade-offs)
so stakeholders can choose, with your reasoned recommendation. (4) <strong>Own it honestly</strong> — the
estimation miss, without blame-shifting or excessive excuse. (5) <strong>Protect the team</strong> — no death
march; sustainable recovery. (6) <strong>Must-haves vs nice-to-haves</strong> — cut the less essential,
protect the core value. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1) <strong>sitting on it / status green</strong>
— hiding the slip and hoping, then surprising everyone at the deadline (the watermelon lie — destroys
credibility); (2) <strong>death march / crunch</strong> to try to hit the date at full scope (burnout, bugs,
false economy, unsustainable); (3) <strong>adding people</strong> at week 6 (Brooks's Law — makes it later);
(4) <strong>cutting quality</strong> to hit the date (creates worse problems/debt); (5) <strong>no options /
just "we're late"</strong> — dumping the slip without a recovery plan (Lesson 41's options-not-problems); (6)
<strong>blame-shifting</strong> — blaming others rather than owning the estimation miss; (7) <strong>promising
to still hit the date at full scope</strong> — over-committing again (the honest move is the slip is real,
recover via scope).
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is detecting slip early (vs. "status: green" until the deadline) so important, and why does
Brooks's Law mean adding people to a late project usually makes it later?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Detecting slip early is important because <strong>early detection gives you time to recover with options,
while late detection (status green until the deadline) gives you a crisis with none</strong>. When you detect
a slip early — while there's still substantial time before the deadline — you have room to respond: you can
cut scope, renegotiate the date, adjust the plan, and communicate the slip to stakeholders with time for
everyone to adapt (Lesson 41's no-surprises, Lesson 44's renegotiate-early). Recovery options are available
because there's time to exercise them. When you detect a slip <em>late</em> — at or near the deadline —
you're in a crisis: there's no time to recover gracefully (the deadline is here and the work isn't done), so
your only "options" are bad (miss the date with no warning, or death-march to try to salvage it), and
stakeholders are blindsided (they planned around a commitment that silently failed, with no time to adjust).
So the timing of detection determines whether you have a manageable situation (early — options and time) or a
crisis (late — no options, blindsided everyone, damaged credibility). The enemy of early detection is the
<strong>"status: green" lie</strong> (the "watermelon" — green outside, red inside): projects that report
"on track" right up until they suddenly miss. This happens because people are optimistic, avoid admitting
problems (it's uncomfortable, or feels like failure), or don't measure real progress (so they genuinely don't
know they're behind). Green-until-it-isn't is dangerous because it hides the slip until it's too late to
recover — the status looks fine, so no one acts, until the deadline reveals the slip as a sudden crisis. To
detect early instead, you watch <strong>real signals</strong> rather than optimistic status: actual velocity
(are we completing work at the rate needed to finish on time?), whether the risk-retiring milestones are
actually being hit (Lesson 50), and honest status (creating psychological safety for people to report "red"
— Lesson 21, so problems surface rather than being hidden under green). Early detection from real
progress/velocity signals (not optimistic self-reported "green") is what gives you the time to recover with
options rather than facing a deadline crisis — which is why it's so important. Why <strong>Brooks's Law</strong>
(adding people to a late project makes it later) holds: <strong>because in the short term, the costs of adding
people (onboarding time, communication overhead) exceed the capacity they add, and much work isn't
divisible</strong>. The instinct when behind is to add people (more hands = faster, intuitively). But for a
project that's <em>already late</em>, adding people typically makes it <em>later</em> in the timeframe that
matters, for three reasons: (1) <strong>onboarding cost</strong> — new people need to ramp up (learn the
codebase, the project, the context), which takes time before they're productive, AND they take <em>existing</em>
people's time to onboard (the current team members stop doing project work to bring the new people up to
speed) — so in the short term, adding people <em>reduces</em> the current team's effective capacity (existing
people are onboarding instead of building) while the new people aren't yet productive. (2) <strong>communication
overhead</strong> — more people means more coordination and communication paths (which grow faster than
linearly with team size), so a larger team spends more time coordinating, reducing the efficiency gain. (3)
<strong>work isn't always divisible</strong> — you can't always parallelize the remaining work ("nine women
can't make a baby in one month") — some work is inherently sequential or can't be split among more people, so
adding people doesn't help with the indivisible parts. The net effect: for a project slipping <em>now</em>
(where the relevant timeframe is short), the onboarding + coordination costs of adding people exceed the
capacity they add in that timeframe, so the project goes <em>slower</em>, not faster — adding people makes it
later. (Adding people can help the <em>long</em> term, if there's enough time for the new people to onboard
and become net-productive — but as a <em>recovery</em> for a project that's late now, it usually backfires.)
So Brooks's Law means adding people is rarely the right recovery move for a slipping project — it's a
counterintuitive trap (feels like it should help, actually hurts short-term). Both points reflect recovering
slips well: detect early (real signals, not green lies — so you have time and options, not a crisis), and
don't add people to recover (Brooks's Law — it makes it later; cut scope instead) — together enabling a
graceful, credible recovery (early detection + scope-cutting) rather than a late-detected crisis "recovered"
by counterproductive people-adding or death marches.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is cutting scope usually the best recovery (vs. crunching or reducing quality), and why
communicate the slip early?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Cutting scope is usually the best recovery</strong> because <strong>it's the most controllable lever
and it preserves quality and (often) the date, whereas the alternatives — crunching, adding people, reducing
quality — each have serious downsides that usually make them worse</strong>. When a project is slipping, the
recovery options (the "recovery menu," mapping to the iron triangle) are: cut scope, move the date, add
people, or reduce quality. Comparing them: (1) <strong>adding people</strong> — backfires short-term (Brooks's
Law — onboarding and coordination costs make a late project later), so it's rarely a good recovery. (2)
<strong>reducing quality</strong> (cutting corners, skipping testing) — dangerous and usually a false economy:
it creates tech debt and bugs that cost more later (and can cause incidents, rework, and damage), so you're
trading a short-term schedule gain for larger future costs — rarely worth it. (3) <strong>moving the date</strong>
— valid if the date can flex, but often it can't (there's a real external deadline — a conference, a
commitment), so it's not always available. (4) <strong>cutting scope</strong> — delivering less — is usually
the best because: (a) it's the <em>most controllable</em> — you directly decide what to drop (versus hoping
crunch or added people work out), so you have real control over the recovery; (b) it <em>preserves quality
and the date</em> — by delivering less (but well) you can often hit the date without cutting quality or
burning people out; (c) there's <em>usually less-essential scope to cut</em> — most projects have must-haves
and nice-to-haves (the walking skeleton vs. the extras), so you can cut the nice-to-haves and deliver the
core value (often the core delivers most of the value anyway — the 80/20); and (d) it's <em>sustainable</em>
— no burnout, no debt, no counterproductive people-adding. So cutting scope lets you recover by delivering
the essential core well and on time (dropping the less-essential), which is controllable, quality-preserving,
and sustainable — versus crunching (burnout, bugs, false economy), adding people (Brooks — makes it later),
or reducing quality (creates worse problems). Hence the recovery conversation is usually "what can we drop to
hit the date (or a reasonable date) with quality?" — cut scope first. Why <strong>communicate the slip
early</strong>: <strong>because early honest communication (with a recovery plan) lets everyone adjust and
preserves trust, while hiding the slip until the deadline blindsides everyone and destroys credibility</strong>
— the no-surprises rule (Lesson 41) applied to slips. When you communicate a slip early (as soon as you're
confident of it, from early detection), you: (1) <strong>let stakeholders adjust</strong> — they can replan,
reset expectations (with customers, their own leadership), adjust dependent work, and make decisions with the
new reality, because they have time (versus being told at the deadline, when it's too late to adjust); (2)
<strong>preserve trust</strong> — flagging the slip responsibly (early, with a recovery plan) shows you're on
top of it and honest, maintaining credibility (you're a reliable partner who surfaces problems), whereas
hiding it until the deadline destroys trust (you're the person who let everyone be blindsided by something
you knew); (3) <strong>enable a better outcome</strong> — early communication + recovery options (scope-
cutting) means the situation is handled proactively (adjust scope/date/plans with time), producing the best
achievable outcome, versus a last-minute crisis with no options. Hiding the slip (status green until the
deadline) does the opposite: everyone is blindsided (no time to adjust), the outcome is worse (no time to
recover), and your credibility is destroyed (you knew and didn't say — the trust cost). So communicating the
slip early — honestly, with the recovery plan/options, the day you're confident — is essential to recovering
well: it's the same discipline as delivering bad news early (English Lesson 47) and renegotiating early
(Lesson 44), because a slip is bad news that must be delivered well (early, owned, with a plan) to preserve
trust and enable adjustment. Both points reflect professional slip recovery: cut scope (the controllable,
quality-preserving, sustainable lever — not crunch/add-people/cut-quality) and communicate early (no
surprises, with a recovery plan — so everyone adjusts and trust holds) — together recovering a slipping
project with credibility intact and the team's health protected, versus death marches, quality-cutting, and
blindsided stakeholders that damage both.
</details>
