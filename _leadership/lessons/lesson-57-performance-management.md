# Lesson 57: Performance Management

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions for this lesson's jargon)*
> - **performance management** — the formal cycle of evaluating work: reviews, ratings, expectations.
> - **rating** — the score assigned in a review cycle ("meets / exceeds expectations").
> - **review-season scramble** — trying to reconstruct a whole year's performance from memory in one week.
> - **halo effect** — one strong (or weak) trait unfairly colouring your judgment of everything else about a person.
> - **recency effect / recency-biased** — over-weighting the last few weeks over the whole period.
> - **salience** (SAY-lee-ence) — how much something stands out in memory (dramatic ≠ representative).
> - **calibration (meeting)** — managers comparing ratings across teams so standards stay consistent; you defend yours with evidence.
> - **blindside** — to surprise someone with a bad rating they never saw coming (a management failure — Lesson 41).
> - **evidence-based** — grounded in documented specifics (SBI at scale — Lesson 19), not impressions.
> - **developmental** — aimed at helping the person grow, not only judging them.

## Concept

Performance management — evaluating people's work, writing reviews, and assigning ratings — is a core
EM duty, and doing it well means **evaluating fairly, documenting honestly, and never letting a review
surprise anyone.** The cardinal rule: a performance review should contain **no surprises** — everything
in it should have been said in real-time feedback throughout the period (a bad rating that blindsides
someone is a management failure). Done well, performance management is fair, evidence-based, and
developmental; done badly, it's an arbitrary, recency-biased, surprise-laden ritual.

```
   PERFORMANCE MANAGEMENT (fair, honest, no surprises)
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ • CONTINUOUS evaluation (not a review-season scramble)  │
   │ • NO SURPRISES (the review reflects year-round feedback)│
   │ • EVIDENCE-BASED (SBI at scale — specific, documented)  │
   │ • beware HALO & RECENCY effects (one trait/recent month)│
   │ • CALIBRATION: ratings are set relative to peers, in a  │
   │   room — defend yours with evidence                    │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The reframe: **performance management is continuous and evidence-based, not a review-season scramble —
and a review should never surprise anyone.** The failure mode is treating reviews as a once-a-year event:
scrambling to remember what happened (recency-biased), writing vague assessments, and delivering ratings
that blindside people (because real feedback wasn't given throughout). Good performance management
evaluates continuously (documenting evidence year-round), so reviews are fair, specific, and contain no
surprises — just a summary of feedback already given.

## Going Deeper

### Continuous evaluation, not a review-season scramble

Performance management should be **continuous** — observing, giving feedback, and documenting evidence
throughout the period — not a scramble at review season (trying to remember what someone did all year).
The scramble approach fails: you can't accurately recall a year of work (so you rely on recent/memorable
things — recency and salience bias), the feedback comes too late to help (a year's issues dumped at
once), and it's a stressful, low-quality process. Continuous evaluation (ongoing feedback — Lesson 19 —
and light documentation of notable work/issues as they happen) means that at review time you have a real,
evidence-based picture, the person has been getting feedback all along (no surprises), and the review is a
summary rather than a reconstruction.

### No surprises — the cardinal rule

The most important principle: **a performance review should contain no surprises.** Everything in it —
especially any criticism or a lower-than-expected rating — should have been communicated as real-time
feedback during the period. If someone is surprised by their review (a bad rating they didn't see coming),
it's a **management failure**: you didn't give them the feedback that would have let them address it. A
review should be a summary of things already discussed, not a reveal. This connects to timely feedback
(Lesson 19) — give feedback throughout, so the review confirms rather than shocks. A surprised employee at
review time means you failed to manage their performance along the way.

### Evidence-based reviews — SBI at scale

Write reviews grounded in **specific evidence** — SBI (Lesson 19) at scale: specific situations,
behaviors, and impacts, not vague characterizations. "Consistently delivered high-quality work,
including [specific examples with impact]" beats "is a good engineer"; "struggled with collaboration, e.g.,
[specific instances and their effect]" beats "has attitude problems." Evidence-based reviews are (1) fair
(grounded in what actually happened, not impressions), (2) actionable (specific enough to learn from), (3)
credible (defensible in calibration and to the person), and (4) less biased (evidence checks the halo/
recency effects). Document evidence throughout (continuous evaluation) so you have it at review time.

### Halo and recency effects — the biases to guard against

Two biases that distort reviews: (1) **halo effect** — letting one strong (or weak) trait color the whole
assessment (a great communicator rated high on everything, or a person with one flaw rated low overall) —
rather than assessing each dimension on its own evidence. (2) **recency effect** — over-weighting recent
events (the last month or two) versus the whole period (a strong year undone by a recent stumble, or a
weak year rescued by a recent win). Both distort fairness. Guard against them by (a) evaluating each
dimension on its specific evidence (halo), and (b) reviewing the whole period's documented evidence, not
just recent memory (recency) — which is why continuous documentation matters (you have the year's evidence,
not just recent salient events).

### Calibration — how ratings really happen

An important reality: ratings are usually set in **calibration meetings** — where managers compare their
people against each other (and against level expectations) to ensure ratings are consistent across the org
(so one manager's "exceeds" means the same as another's). This means: (1) ratings are <em>relative and
calibrated</em>, not just your individual call; (2) you must **defend your ratings with evidence** in the
room (a rating without evidence loses to one with evidence); and (3) you're advocating for your people
(a manager who can't make the evidence-based case for their person's rating fails them). Understanding
that ratings happen through calibration (and preparing evidence to defend them) is key to both fair
ratings and advocating for your team.

{: .note }
> **Performance management is continuous and evidence-based — and a review never surprises anyone</br>**
> Evaluating people fairly means continuous evaluation (documenting evidence and giving feedback year-round),
> not a review-season scramble (which is recency-biased and too late). The cardinal rule: <em>no surprises</em>
> — everything in a review, especially criticism or a low rating, should have been said as real-time
> feedback; a surprised employee is a management failure. Write <em>evidence-based</em> reviews (SBI at
> scale — specific situations/behaviors/impacts, not vague characterizations), guarding against the
> <em>halo</em> (one trait coloring all) and <em>recency</em> (recent events overweighted) effects. And

## Lab — Scenario

**The situation:** You're writing the annual review for one of your engineers, **Jordan**. The file: Jordan
produces **strong technical output** (ships a lot, good quality work, technically capable) — but has
**corrosive collaboration** (dismissive in code reviews, interrupts and talks over people, creates tension,
and a couple of junior engineers have quietly said they find him hard to work with). So: strong individual
output, but a negative effect on the team. You need to write the review, assign a rating, and be able to
defend it in calibration.

**Write the review's key content, assign a rating, and defend it for calibration.** Then note the principles
and mistakes to avoid.

**Your response:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong review is evidence-based, addresses both the strong output AND the corrosive collaboration
honestly (collaboration is part of performance), and assigns a rating that reflects the whole picture.
Example:
<br><br>
<strong>The review content (evidence-based, both dimensions):</strong> <br>
"<strong>Technical execution (strong):</strong> Jordan is a highly capable engineer who consistently ships
high-quality work — e.g., [specific projects delivered, their impact]. His technical output is a real
strength. <br>
<strong>Collaboration and teamwork (needs significant improvement):</strong> However, Jordan's impact on
the team has been negative in ways that matter. Specifically [SBI]: in code reviews, his comments have
often been dismissive (e.g., [examples]); in meetings, he frequently interrupts and talks over others
(e.g., [instances]); and this has created tension and, notably, made some junior engineers uncomfortable
working with him (which suppresses their contribution and growth). Collaboration is a core part of
performance at this level — an engineer's job includes making the team effective, not just their own
output — so this significantly offsets his strong technical work. <br>
<strong>Overall:</strong> Strong individual output, materially undercut by collaboration problems that hurt
the team. To grow, Jordan needs to bring his collaboration up to match his technical skill."
<br><br>
<strong>The rating:</strong> "A <em>middling</em> rating (e.g., 'meets' — not 'exceeds,' despite the strong
output, and not the lowest either). The strong technical output alone might suggest 'exceeds,' but the
corrosive collaboration — which actively harms the team (suppressing juniors, creating tension) —
materially offsets it. Performance isn't just individual output; it's total contribution, and Jordan's net
contribution is dragged down by his negative team impact. So the rating reflects both: capable, but not
exceeding because the collaboration problems are a real, significant deficiency."
<br><br>
<strong>Defending it in calibration:</strong> "I'd defend it with evidence: 'Jordan's technical output is
genuinely strong [evidence]. But I'm not rating him "exceeds" because his collaboration is corrosive
[specific evidence: dismissive reviews, interrupting, junior engineers uncomfortable] — which actively
harms the team, and collaboration is part of performance at this level. An engineer whose output is high
but who makes the team worse isn't exceeding overall. I've given him this feedback throughout the year, so
this rating won't surprise him.'" <em>(Evidence-based, defends the offset of output by collaboration,
notes the feedback was given throughout — no surprise.)</em>
<br><br>
<strong>Crucial — no surprise:</strong> "This review must NOT be the first time Jordan hears about the
collaboration problem. If I've done my job, I've been giving him this feedback (SBI, Lesson 19) throughout
the year, so the review summarizes what he's already heard. If he'd be surprised, that's my failure — and
I should have been addressing it all along."
<br><br>
<strong>Principles:</strong> (1) <strong>Collaboration is part of performance</strong> — strong output
doesn't excuse corrosive collaboration; total contribution (including team impact) is what's evaluated. (2)
<strong>Evidence-based, both dimensions</strong> — specific SBI evidence for both the strong output and the
collaboration problems. (3) <strong>The rating reflects the whole</strong> — the negative team impact
offsets the strong output (not "exceeds"). (4) <strong>Defend with evidence in calibration</strong> — the
specific evidence makes the case. (5) <strong>No surprise</strong> — the collaboration feedback should have
been given all year (a review is a summary, not a reveal). (6) <strong>Developmental</strong> — clear that
Jordan needs to grow his collaboration to match his technical skill. <strong>Mistakes to avoid:</strong> (1)
<strong>rating him "exceeds" on output alone</strong> — ignoring the corrosive collaboration (which harms
the team and signals that toxic-but-productive is fine — demoralizing to everyone else); (2) <strong>vague
characterizations</strong> ("attitude problems") instead of specific SBI evidence; (3) <strong>surprising
him</strong> — if the collaboration feedback wasn't given throughout the year (a management failure — the
review shouldn't be the first he hears of it); (4) <strong>only addressing output</strong> — omitting the
team-impact problem (which is the real issue); (5) <strong>halo effect</strong> — letting the strong
technical output color the whole assessment positively; (6) <strong>not being able to defend it</strong> —
going to calibration without the specific evidence; (7) <strong>ignoring the juniors' experience</strong> —
the suppression of junior engineers is a serious impact that matters.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why should a performance review "contain no surprises," and why is continuous, evidence-based
evaluation better than a review-season scramble?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A performance review should contain no surprises because <strong>a surprising review — especially a bad
rating or criticism the person didn't see coming — means you failed to give them the real-time feedback
that would have let them understand and address it, which is a management failure</strong>. The purpose of
feedback is to help people improve, which requires giving it <em>when there's time to act on it</em>
(throughout the period — Lesson 19's timely feedback). If a criticism or a lower-than-expected rating first
appears in the annual review, it means the person went the whole period without knowing there was a problem
— so they had no chance to address it, and the review blindsides them with an accumulated judgment they
couldn't have prevented. This is unfair (they were never told), unkind (a surprise negative judgment is a
shock), and counterproductive (the feedback comes too late to help — the period is over). And it's a
<em>management failure</em>: managing performance means giving feedback along the way so people can improve;
if the review surprises someone, you didn't do that — you sat on the feedback until it was too late. So the
rule is that a review should be a <strong>summary of feedback already given</strong> — everything in it,
especially any criticism, should have been communicated in real-time throughout the period, so the review
confirms and consolidates what the person already knows, rather than revealing new judgments. A person should
be able to predict their review because they've been getting the feedback all along. "No surprises" is thus
both a fairness principle (people deserve to know about problems in time to address them) and a marker of
good management (you've been giving feedback continuously). Why <strong>continuous, evidence-based
evaluation</strong> is better than a review-season scramble: <strong>because continuous evaluation produces
a fair, accurate, evidence-based, surprise-free review, while the scramble approach is recency-biased,
inaccurate, too late, and surprise-laden</strong>. The <strong>review-season scramble</strong> (treating
reviews as a once-a-year event, trying to reconstruct a year of performance at review time) fails multiple
ways: (1) <strong>it's recency/salience biased</strong> — you can't accurately recall a whole year, so you
rely on recent or memorable events (the recency effect), giving a distorted picture (a strong year undone by
a recent stumble, or vice versa); (2) <strong>it's inaccurate</strong> — reconstructed-from-memory
assessments lack the specific evidence of what actually happened across the period; (3) <strong>the feedback
is too late</strong> — a year's worth of issues surfaced at once, at review time, when it's too late to act
on them (and often a surprise); (4) <strong>it's stressful and low-quality</strong> — a scramble to remember
and write produces rushed, vague reviews. <strong>Continuous evaluation</strong> (observing, giving feedback,
and documenting evidence throughout the period) fixes all of these: (1) <strong>it's evidence-based and
accurate</strong> — you have documented evidence from across the whole period (not just recent memory), so
the review reflects the real, full picture (and guards against recency/halo bias); (2) <strong>the person
has been getting feedback all along</strong> — so the review contains no surprises (it summarizes what
they've already heard) and they've had the chance to improve; (3) <strong>it's fair and specific</strong> —
grounded in documented evidence (SBI at scale) rather than impressions; and (4) <strong>the review is a
summary, not a reconstruction</strong> — a lower-stress, higher-quality process (you're consolidating known
information, not reconstructing a year). So continuous evaluation produces reviews that are fair (evidence-
based, whole-period), accurate (documented, not recency-biased), timely (feedback given throughout), and
surprise-free (a summary of ongoing feedback) — versus the scramble's biased, inaccurate, too-late,
surprising reviews. The connection: continuous evaluation is what <em>enables</em> the no-surprises rule
(you give feedback and document evidence throughout, so the review confirms rather than reveals) — they're
two aspects of the same good practice: manage performance continuously (feedback + evidence year-round) so
that the review is a fair, evidence-based summary of what the person already knows, rather than a
recency-biased, surprise-laden once-a-year reveal. Both make performance management fair and developmental
rather than an arbitrary, blindsiding ritual.
</details>

**Q2.** What are the halo and recency effects, and why must you understand that ratings happen through
"calibration"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The <strong>halo effect</strong> and <strong>recency effect</strong> are two cognitive biases that distort
performance evaluations. The <strong>halo effect</strong> is letting <em>one</em> strong (or weak) trait
color the <em>whole</em> assessment: a person who's excellent at one salient thing (say, a great
communicator, or a strong technical output) gets rated highly on <em>everything</em> (their other
dimensions benefit from the "halo" of the one strength), or conversely a person with one notable flaw gets
rated low overall — rather than each dimension being assessed on its own evidence. It distorts fairness
because it lets a single trait override the actual, dimension-by-dimension reality (e.g., rating a
strong-output-but-corrosive-collaboration engineer high overall because the output halo colors everything,
missing the collaboration problem). The <strong>recency effect</strong> is over-weighting <em>recent</em>
events (the last month or two before the review) relative to the whole period: a strong year can be undone
by a recent stumble, or a weak year rescued by a recent win, because the recent events are freshest in
memory and loom largest — rather than the whole period being weighted fairly. It distorts fairness because
the review should reflect the entire period, not just what happened recently. Both biases make reviews
unfair and inaccurate (assessments driven by one trait or recent memory rather than the full, dimension-by-
dimension evidence). You guard against them by: (halo) evaluating <em>each dimension on its own specific
evidence</em> (so one strength/weakness doesn't color the others), and (recency) reviewing <em>the whole
period's documented evidence</em> (not just recent memory) — which is why continuous documentation matters
(you have the year's evidence across all dimensions, not just recent salient events). Being aware of these
biases and deliberately checking against them (evidence per dimension, whole-period) makes reviews fairer.
Why you must understand that ratings happen through <strong>calibration</strong>: <strong>because ratings
are usually set in calibration meetings where managers compare their people against each other and against
level expectations — so ratings are relative and calibrated (not just your individual call), you must defend
yours with evidence, and you're advocating for your people</strong>. Calibration is the process where
managers across the org come together to compare their people and ensure ratings are <em>consistent</em>
(so one manager's "exceeds" means the same as another's — preventing rating inflation by lenient managers or
deflation by harsh ones, and ensuring fairness across the org). Understanding this reality has three
important implications: (1) <strong>ratings are relative and calibrated</strong> — your rating of your
person isn't just your individual assessment; it's set relative to peers across the org in the calibration
room, so you need to understand where your person genuinely stands relative to others (not just your own
impression). (2) <strong>you must defend your ratings with evidence</strong> — in calibration, a rating
backed by specific evidence (concrete situations, behaviors, impacts) wins over one based on vague
impressions; if you can't make the evidence-based case for your person's rating, it won't hold (it'll get
calibrated down, or you'll lose the argument) — so you must come prepared with the evidence (which continuous
documentation provides). (3) <strong>you're advocating for your people</strong> — calibration is where your
people's ratings are decided, so it's where you advocate for them (make the strong evidence-based case for
the rating they deserve); a manager who can't or doesn't advocate effectively (no evidence, weak case) fails
their people (they get under-rated). So understanding that ratings happen through calibration means: know
where your people genuinely stand relative to peers, prepare specific evidence to defend their ratings, and
advocate for them effectively in the room — because the rating isn't just your private call, it's decided
(calibrated) in a room where evidence and advocacy determine the outcome. This is crucial for both fairness
(calibration ensures consistency) and for serving your people (you must advocate with evidence, or they're
under-rated). Both the bias-awareness (halo/recency) and the calibration understanding make performance
management fair and effective: guard against the biases that distort individual assessments (evaluate per
dimension, whole-period), and understand and prepare for the calibration process that sets ratings (know
your people's relative standing, defend with evidence, advocate) — together producing fair, defensible,
well-advocated ratings rather than biased individual impressions that don't hold up.
</details>
