# Lesson 30: Newborn Health & Warning Signs

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice. This lesson names red flags so you act
> fast — but it is **not** a diagnosis tool. For a young baby, **trust your
> instincts and don't wait**; a fever in a baby under 3 months is an emergency.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **well-baby visit** — a routine checkup to track growth, development, and health.
> - **immunisation / vaccine** — protection against serious infections; the schedule starts in the first months.
> - **jaundice** (JAWN-diss) — yellowing of the skin/eyes from bilirubin; common in newborns, sometimes needs treatment.
> - **rectal temperature** — the most accurate way to take a young baby's temperature.
> - **lethargy** — being unusually drowsy, floppy, or hard to wake.
> - **dehydration** — not enough fluids; watch wet-diaper counts.

## Concept

Most newborn health is about **routine care** — well-baby visits, vaccines, and
growth tracking — punctuated by a few **common, usually-manageable issues** (like
jaundice) and a short list of **genuine red flags** that mean *act now*. Knowing the
red flags cold is the goal of this lesson: for a young baby, some symptoms are true
emergencies, and hesitating "not to overreact" is the wrong instinct. **When in
doubt, call — or go in.**

## Going Deeper

### Routine care

- **Well-baby visits** track weight, length, head size, feeding, and development,
  and are your chance to ask questions.
- **Immunisations** begin in the first months on a schedule — they protect against
  serious diseases; keep to the schedule.
- **Growth** is followed on charts; steady growth along a curve matters more than a
  single number.

### Common issues

- **Jaundice:** yellowing of the skin/whites of the eyes, from **bilirubin**, is
  **very common** in the first days and often mild. But it can occasionally need
  treatment (light therapy), so **have it checked** — especially if it appears in
  the first 24 hours, spreads/deepens, or the baby is sleepy and feeding poorly.
- **Feeding and weight** worries, **cord** issues (Lesson 28), and mild reflux/
  spit-up are common — ask if unsure.

### The newborn red flags — act now

{: .warning }
> **Get urgent medical help / go to emergency if your baby:**
> - Has a **fever of 38 °C (100.4 °F) or higher and is under 3 months old** —
>   **this is an emergency; go now.**
> - Has **trouble breathing** (fast, laboured, grunting, pauses, or blue lips/skin).
> - Is **very hard to wake, floppy, or unusually unresponsive** (lethargy).
> - **Won't feed** or is feeding very poorly, or has **far fewer wet diapers**
>   (possible dehydration).
> - Has **persistent or forceful vomiting**, especially **green** vomit, or **blood
>   in stool/vomit**.
> - Is **inconsolable** in a way that feels different and wrong, or has a **rash that
>   doesn't fade** when pressed.
> - Has a **seizure**, or you simply feel **something is seriously wrong** — trust it.

### Taking a temperature, and trusting yourself

Learn to take your baby's temperature (a **rectal** reading is most accurate for a
young infant; your provider can show you). And remember the golden rule: **you know
your baby.** Parental instinct that "something is off" is a valid reason to seek
care. Health professionals would always rather see a well baby than miss a sick one.

{: .note }
> **The one number to burn into memory**
> **Fever ≥ 38 °C (100.4 °F) in a baby under 3 months = emergency, go now.** A young
> baby's immune system is immature, so a fever can signal a serious infection and
> needs urgent assessment — never "wait and see" with a feverish newborn.
