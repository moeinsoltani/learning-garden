# Lesson 13: Pregnancy Complications

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice. This lesson names common complications so
> you understand your prenatal care — it is **not** a diagnosis tool. Follow your
> provider, and see Lesson 14 for the "call now" warning signs.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **gestational diabetes** (jes-TAY-shun-al) — high blood sugar that develops in pregnancy and usually resolves after birth.
> - **preeclampsia** (pree-ee-KLAMP-see-ah) — a serious condition of high blood pressure plus signs of organ stress.
> - **Rh factor** — a blood-type marker; a mismatch between mother and baby (Rh-negative mother) is managed with an injection.
> - **placenta previa** (PREH-vee-ah) — when the placenta covers the cervix; can cause bleeding and may require a C-section.
> - **preterm labour** — labour starting before 37 weeks.
> - **anemia** (uh-NEE-mee-ah) — low iron / low red blood cells, causing tiredness and paleness.

## Concept

Most pregnancies are healthy — and the whole point of **regular prenatal care** is
to catch the minority of problems **early**, when they're most manageable. This
lesson gives you a working knowledge of the common complications so that a term
like "gestational diabetes" or "preeclampsia" isn't frightening jargon, and so you
understand *why* they check your blood pressure, urine, and blood sugar at every
visit.

## Going Deeper

### The common complications

| Complication | What it is / signs | How it's managed |
|---|---|---|
| **Gestational diabetes** | High blood sugar in pregnancy; usually **no symptoms** (found by a screening test ~24–28 wks) | Diet, monitoring, exercise; sometimes medication/insulin. Matters because it can make the baby large and affect delivery |
| **Preeclampsia** | High blood pressure + organ stress; signs: **severe headache, vision changes, sudden swelling, upper-belly pain** | Close monitoring of BP and urine; sometimes early delivery — the cure is birth. **Report the signs urgently** |
| **High blood pressure** | Can exist before or arise in pregnancy | Monitoring, sometimes medication (pregnancy-safe) |
| **Anemia** | Low iron; tiredness, paleness | Iron-rich diet and supplements |
| **Rh incompatibility** | Rh-negative mother, Rh-positive baby | An injection (anti-D / "RhoGAM") prevents problems |
| **Placenta problems** (previa, abruption) | Can cause **bleeding**; abruption also pain | Monitoring; may require a planned C-section or urgent care |
| **Preterm labour** | Labour before 37 weeks | Care to delay/manage; report early signs |

### Why prenatal visits are the hero here

Notice how many of these have **no obvious symptoms** early (gestational diabetes,
rising blood pressure, anemia). They're caught by the **routine checks** — the
blood pressure cuff, the urine dip, the blood-sugar test — that can feel repetitive
but are precisely how problems are found before they become serious. That's the
best reason never to skip a prenatal appointment.

### Keep it in proportion

Reading a list of complications can be scary. Remember: these are the reasons for
monitoring, not predictions. **The large majority of pregnancies proceed without
serious complications**, and those that arise are usually caught early and managed
well. Knowledge here is for *understanding your care*, not for worry.

{: .warning }
> **Preeclampsia — know these signs and report them fast:**
> a **severe or persistent headache**, **changes in vision** (blurring, spots,
> flashing), **sudden swelling** of the face/hands, **pain just below the ribs**,
> or feeling suddenly very unwell. Preeclampsia can be serious for mother and
> baby — don't wait to report these.

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What is gestational diabetes, when is it found, and why does it matter?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
It's <strong>high blood sugar that develops in pregnancy</strong> (usually
resolving after birth), typically with <strong>no symptoms</strong>, found by a
screening test around <strong>24–28 weeks</strong>. It matters because it can make
the baby large and affect delivery and later health; it's managed with diet,
monitoring, exercise, and sometimes insulin.
</details>

**Q2.** What are the warning signs of preeclampsia?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A <strong>severe/persistent headache</strong>, <strong>vision changes</strong>
(blurring, spots, flashing lights), <strong>sudden swelling</strong> of the face
and hands, <strong>pain just below the ribs</strong>, and feeling suddenly very
unwell. These should be reported <strong>urgently</strong> — preeclampsia is
serious.
</details>

**Q3.** Why are routine prenatal checks so important for catching complications?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because many complications — rising blood pressure/preeclampsia, gestational
diabetes, anemia — have <strong>no obvious early symptoms</strong>. The routine
blood-pressure, urine, and blood tests at each visit are how they're detected
<strong>early</strong>, before they become serious. That's why skipping visits is
risky.
</details>
