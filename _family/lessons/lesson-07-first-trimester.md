# Lesson 07: The First Trimester

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice — follow your own provider. When in doubt, call.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **trimester** — one of the three ~13-week thirds of pregnancy; the first is weeks 1–13.
> - **embryo → fetus** — the developing baby is called an *embryo* until ~8 weeks, then a *fetus*.
> - **morning sickness** — nausea (with or without vomiting) that can strike at *any* time of day.
> - **hyperemesis gravidarum** (hy-per-EM-eh-sis) — severe, persistent pregnancy vomiting that needs medical treatment.
> - **food aversion** — a sudden strong dislike of foods or smells you used to like.
> - **hormones** — chemical messengers (like hCG, progesterone) that cause many early symptoms.

## Concept

The first trimester is a paradox: **the least to show, the most going on.** From
the outside, nothing; on the inside, the baby's brain, heart, spine, and organs
are all forming, and the mother's hormones surge — which is why this is often the
**most physically rough** trimester (nausea, bone-deep fatigue) even though there's
no bump yet. It usually eases as the second trimester begins.

## Going Deeper

### What's happening inside

- By **~6 weeks**, a heartbeat can often be detected.
- By the **end of week 12**, all the major organs have formed and the fetus is
  fully recognisable — just tiny (about the size of a plum). The rest of pregnancy
  is mostly growth and maturing.

This is why the early weeks are so sensitive to folic acid, alcohol, smoking, and
medications — the foundations are being laid.

### Common symptoms (all normal)

| Symptom | What helps |
|---|---|
| **Nausea / "morning" sickness** | Small, frequent bland snacks; eat before getting up; ginger; stay hydrated; ask about vitamin B6 |
| **Exhaustion** | Rest without guilt — this is real; nap, go to bed early, accept help |
| **Sore, swollen breasts** | A supportive bra; it settles |
| **Peeing often, constipation, bloating** | Fluids and fibre; keep moving gently |
| **Food aversions / heightened smell** | Eat what stays down; it usually passes by the second trimester |
| **Big emotions / mood swings** | Normal hormonal shifts — be gentle with yourself and each other |

### When "normal" needs a call

Most first-trimester misery is normal, but contact your provider for:

- **Vomiting so severe** you can't keep fluids or food down, or you're losing
  weight — this may be **hyperemesis gravidarum**, which is treatable (don't just
  tough it out).
- **Vaginal bleeding or severe/one-sided abdominal pain** (see Lesson 10 on early
  loss and ectopic pregnancy — some of these are urgent).
- A **fever**, or feeling very unwell.

### The emotional side, and when to tell people

Excitement, anxiety, and "is this really happening?" all coexist — normal. Many
couples wait until after ~12 weeks (when miscarriage risk drops) to share widely,
but there's **no right answer**; some tell a few trusted people early precisely so
they'd have support if something went wrong. Decide together.

{: .note }
> **Be kind to the first-trimester body**
> Nausea and fatigue are not weakness — they're the body doing enormous hidden
> work. Lower your expectations of yourself, let chores slide, eat whatever stays
> down (a "safe foods only" week is fine), and lean on your partner. It almost
> always gets easier in the second trimester.

## Lab — Plan Together

**Exercise 1 — the nausea toolkit.** Build a five-item plan for coping with
morning sickness that the non-carrying partner can help stock and support.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong toolkit: (1) plain crackers/dry toast by the bed to eat <em>before</em>
getting up; (2) small snacks every 1–2 hours to avoid an empty stomach; (3) ginger
(tea, candies) and cold water sipped often; (4) keeping strong-smelling foods out
of the way; (5) asking the provider about vitamin B6. The partner's role: keep the
snacks stocked, take over cooking smells, and never dismiss it as "just a bit of
nausea." Flag it if she can't keep fluids down — that's a medical issue, not a
toughness test.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — normal or call?** Sort: (a) tired and queasy most mornings at 8
weeks; (b) vomiting everything for 2 days, can't keep water down; (c) mild breast
soreness; (d) heavy bleeding with cramping.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) <strong>Normal</strong> — classic early symptoms. (b) <strong>Call</strong> —
possible hyperemesis; dehydration needs treatment. (c) <strong>Normal</strong>.
(d) <strong>Call/seek care</strong> — bleeding with cramping needs assessment
(Lesson 10). The rule: fatigue, nausea, and tenderness are expected; inability to
keep fluids down, bleeding, or severe pain warrant a call.

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is the first trimester often the hardest even though there's no bump?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because enormous hidden work is happening — the baby's major organs are forming
and the mother's <strong>hormones surge</strong> — which causes nausea, deep
fatigue, breast tenderness, and mood swings, even before any visible bump. It
usually eases in the second trimester.
</details>

**Q2.** By the end of week 12, what has happened to the baby's development, and why
does that make the early weeks so sensitive?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
By ~12 weeks all the <strong>major organs have formed</strong> and the fetus is
fully recognisable (just tiny); the rest of pregnancy is mostly growth. Because the
foundations are laid this early, the first weeks are especially sensitive to folic
acid, alcohol, smoking, and medications.
</details>

**Q3.** When does first-trimester nausea/vomiting warrant a call to the provider?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
When vomiting is so <strong>severe and persistent</strong> that you <strong>can't
keep fluids or food down</strong> or you're losing weight — this may be
<strong>hyperemesis gravidarum</strong>, which is treatable and shouldn't just be
endured. (Also call for bleeding, severe/one-sided pain, or fever.)
</details>
