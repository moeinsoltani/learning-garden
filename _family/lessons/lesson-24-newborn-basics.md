# Lesson 24: Newborn Basics

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice. For a young baby, trust your instincts —
> when in doubt, call your provider. Lesson 30 covers the true red flags.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **newborn / neonate** — a baby in its first ~4 weeks.
> - **fontanelles** (fon-tah-NELZ) — the soft spots on a baby's head where the skull bones haven't fused yet.
> - **vernix** (VER-nix) — the white, creamy coating some babies are born with.
> - **meconium** (meh-KOH-nee-um) — the sticky, dark-green first poops.
> - **reflex** — an automatic movement (rooting, sucking, grasping, startling).
> - **feeding cues** — early signs a baby is hungry (rooting, hands to mouth) before crying.

## Concept

A newborn is not a tiny adult — they're a brand-new little creature with their own
normal. Much of what alarms first-time parents (odd head shape, blotchy skin,
sneezing, irregular breathing, weird noises) is completely normal. The core truth:
newborns have **simple, relentless needs** — to be **fed, kept warm and clean, and
held close** — and communicate the only way they can, by **crying**.

## Going Deeper

### What newborns are like

- **Appearance:** a temporarily **cone-shaped head** (from birth), **vernix**,
  blotchy or peeling skin, puffy eyes, sometimes swollen genitals or breast buds
  (from mum's hormones), a drying **umbilical cord stump**, and soft spots
  (**fontanelles**) on the head — all normal.
- **Reflexes:** **rooting** (turning toward a touch on the cheek, looking to feed),
  **sucking**, **grasping** your finger, and the **Moro/startle** (flinging arms out
  at a sudden noise). These fade over the early months.
- **Senses:** they see best at about **20–30 cm** — the distance to your face
  during a feed — hear well, and know your voice and smell.
- **Sleep:** a lot (roughly **16 hours a day**) but in **short bursts** around the
  clock; newborns don't know day from night yet.

### What they need (and their "output")

Feed, sleep, warmth, a clean diaper, and closeness — that's the whole job, on
repeat. Watch **feeding cues** (rooting, hands to mouth, mouthing) and feed
*before* the crying stage. Their diapers tell you a lot: the first poops are dark
**meconium**, then colour changes; and enough **wet diapers** (building to ~6+ a
day after the first week) is a key "getting enough milk" sign (Lessons 26–27).

### Normal things that scare new parents

Hiccups, sneezing (clearing the nose, not a cold), occasional **irregular breathing**
with brief pauses, grunts and squeaks, **spit-up**, crossing eyes, and losing a
little weight in the first days then regaining it by ~2 weeks — all typically
normal. Knowing this saves a lot of 2 a.m. worry. (Lesson 30 covers what *isn't*
normal.)

{: .note }
> **The newborn job description**
> Feed them, keep them warm and clean, hold them close, and let them sleep — over
> and over. You cannot "spoil" a newborn by holding or responding to them; meeting
> their needs quickly builds security. Everything else is details.

## Lab — Plan Together

**Exercise 1 — normal or call?** Sort: (a) a cone-shaped head and peeling skin on
day 2; (b) hiccups and sneezing; (c) losing 6% of birth weight by day 3; (d) a
fever and refusing to feed. *(Lesson 30 has the red flags.)*

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Normal:</strong> (a) cone head and peeling skin settle in days/weeks; (b)
hiccups and sneezing are normal; (c) a small weight loss in the first days is normal,
with regain by ~2 weeks. <strong>Call/urgent:</strong> (d) a fever with refusing to
feed in a newborn is a red flag (see Lesson 30 — fever in a baby under 3 months is
an emergency). Most odd newborn things are normal; fever, poor feeding, and
breathing trouble are not.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — match the reflex.** Match: (a) turns head toward a cheek touch,
searching; (b) flings arms out at a loud noise; (c) grips your finger tightly.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) → <strong>rooting reflex</strong> (a feeding cue — the baby is looking for the
breast/bottle); (b) → <strong>Moro / startle reflex</strong>; (c) → <strong>grasp
reflex</strong>. These automatic behaviours are signs of a healthy newborn nervous
system and fade over the first months.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What are a newborn's core needs, and how do they communicate?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
To be <strong>fed, kept warm and clean, and held close</strong> (plus lots of
sleep). They communicate mainly by <strong>crying</strong>, and give earlier
<strong>feeding cues</strong> (rooting, hands to mouth) before they cry.
</details>

**Q2.** Name three normal newborn things that often worry new parents.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Any three: a <strong>cone-shaped head</strong>, <strong>peeling/blotchy skin</strong>,
<strong>hiccups and sneezing</strong>, <strong>irregular breathing</strong> with
brief pauses, <strong>spit-up</strong>, and a small <strong>weight loss</strong> in
the first days (regained by ~2 weeks). All are typically normal.
</details>

**Q3.** Can you "spoil" a newborn by holding them too much?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
No. You <strong>cannot spoil a newborn</strong> by holding or responding to them —
meeting their needs quickly and offering closeness builds security. Newborns need
comfort and contact as much as feeding.
</details>
