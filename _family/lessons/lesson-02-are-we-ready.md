# Lesson 02: Are We Ready?

{: .important }
> General education, not medical or financial advice. Use it to talk and plan
> together; decisions about your family are yours. (See the [learning plan]({{ '/family/learning-plan.html' | relative_url }}).)

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **readiness** — feeling prepared enough (never *perfectly* ready) to take a big step.
> - **mental load** — the invisible work of *remembering and managing* everything (appointments, supplies, planning), on top of the physical tasks.
> - **values** — the beliefs that will guide how you raise a child (discipline, faith, screen time, education).
> - **support network / village** — the family and friends who can help you (the "it takes a village" idea).
> - **parental leave** — paid or unpaid time off work after a baby arrives.

## Concept

Here is the truth almost every parent will tell you: **you are never 100% ready,
and that's normal.** If you wait to feel completely prepared, financially secure,
and calm, you may wait forever. "Ready enough" means something more realistic:
you are **committed as a couple**, you've had the **honest conversations**, and
you have a **plan and some support** — not that every box is ticked.

Readiness has three parts, and it helps to check each one:

```
  RELATIONSHIP        PRACTICAL           EMOTIONAL
  are we a team?      can we manage?      do we both want this?
  shared vision,      money, time,        genuinely, not from
  communication,      housing, leave,     pressure — the
  handling conflict   support network     "two-yes" rule
```

The single most important thing you can do before a baby is **strengthen your
relationship and get on the same page** — because a newborn *amplifies* whatever
is already there. A strong, communicating couple gets stronger under pressure; an
unspoken tension gets louder.

## Going Deeper

### The "two-yes" rule

Both partners should genuinely *want* this — not agree out of pressure, guilt, or
a deadline. A reluctant yes tends to surface later as resentment. If one of you is
unsure, that's not a failure; it's a conversation to have openly (and sometimes
with a counsellor). Wanting it *together* is the foundation everything else rests
on.

### The honest conversations to have first

Don't assume you agree — actually talk about:

- **Money:** rough costs, whose income changes, how you'll budget. (Aim for "we
  can manage," not "we can afford everything perfectly" — nobody can.)
- **Division of labour:** who does nights, feeds, chores, appointments? Who
  carries the **mental load**? Say it out loud *before*, not at 3 a.m.
- **Careers:** whose job flexes, who takes leave, how you protect each other's
  ambitions.
- **Values & parenting style:** discipline, faith or culture, education,
  screens, closeness to extended family.
- **The big numbers:** do you both imagine one child or several? Roughly when?
- **The "what ifs":** how you'd handle a difficult pregnancy, a loss, or a child
  with extra needs.

You won't resolve all of these perfectly — the point is to become a team that can
*talk* about hard things, which is the real skill parenting demands.

### The money question, kept in proportion

Children are expensive, and it's wise to look at costs, your parental-leave
entitlements, and a basic budget. But don't let a spreadsheet decide everything:
most families adjust, and there is no income at which a baby feels "affordable" in
the abstract. Prepare sensibly — an emergency cushion, understanding your leave —
then accept that you'll figure a lot out as you go.

### Build your village *before* you need it

Parenting alone is brutally hard. Notice now who your **support network** is —
family nearby, friends with kids, a community or faith group, neighbours. If your
circle is thin (for example, if you've moved to a new country), start building it
early: prenatal classes, parent groups, and online communities all become
lifelines.

{: .note }
> **Myth vs. reality**
> - *"We'll try when we're completely ready."* → You'll never feel completely
>   ready; aim for "ready enough" and committed.
> - *"A baby will fix / bond our relationship."* → A baby **amplifies** what's
>   already there. Strengthen the relationship first.
> - *"We must be able to afford everything first."* → Aim to *manage*, not to
>   afford perfection.

## Lab — Plan Together

**Exercise 1 — the readiness check.** As a couple, rate yourselves 1–5 on each:
relationship/teamwork, communication, finances-manageable, time/career plan,
support network, and "we both genuinely want this." Which is your **lowest**, and
what's one thing you could do about it this month?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
There's no passing score — the value is honesty and a next step. A strong answer
picks the lowest area and names a concrete action, e.g.: "Support network is our
weakest (we're new to the city) → we'll join a prenatal class and reconnect with
two local friends." Or: "Division of labour worries me → we'll write down who does
what in a typical week." The insight to reach: you don't need all 5s; you need a
plan for your weakest area and a genuine shared yes.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the mental-load talk.** List five recurring "invisible" tasks a
baby will add (e.g. tracking vaccine appointments, restocking diapers, noticing
when formula is low). Decide, for each, **who will own it** — not just help with
it.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Strong answers assign genuine ownership, e.g.: "Vaccine/checkup scheduling → me;
diaper &amp; formula restock → you; laundry cycle → me; thank-you notes &amp;
visitors → you; the baby's 'what's next' calendar → shared weekly check-in." The
key idea: sharing the <em>mental load</em> means one person truly <em>owns</em> a
task (remembers it without being reminded), not just does it when asked. Splitting
this before the baby arrives prevents a very common source of resentment.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is "we'll try when we're completely ready" a misleading standard?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because <strong>no one ever feels completely ready</strong> — waiting for perfect
finances, calm, and certainty means waiting forever. A healthier standard is
"<strong>ready enough</strong>": committed as a couple, having had the honest
conversations, with a basic plan and some support. Preparation matters; perfection
isn't available.
</details>

**Q2.** What does it mean that a baby "amplifies" a relationship, and what does
that imply you should do first?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A newborn's stress and exhaustion <strong>magnify whatever dynamic already
exists</strong> — a strong, communicating couple grows closer; unspoken tensions
grow louder. The implication: <strong>strengthen the relationship and your
communication before</strong> the baby arrives, rather than expecting the baby to
fix or bond you.
</details>

**Q3.** Name three "honest conversations" a couple should have before trying.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Any three of: <strong>money/budget and leave</strong>; <strong>division of labour
and the mental load</strong>; <strong>careers</strong> (whose flexes, who takes
leave); <strong>parenting values</strong> (discipline, faith/culture, screens,
education); <strong>how many children and roughly when</strong>; and the
<strong>"what ifs"</strong> (a hard pregnancy, a loss, a child with extra needs).
</details>

## Homework

Have **one** unhurried conversation this week from the "honest conversations"
list — money, division of labour, careers, or values. Write down what you agreed,
what you disagreed on, and one thing you learned about your partner.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Completing the conversation is the win. A strong reflection notes a point of
agreement, an honest point of difference (differences are normal and fine to
surface early), and something new you learned — because the real goal isn't to
agree on everything, it's to prove to yourselves that you can <em>talk about hard
things as a team</em>. That skill matters more than any single answer.
</details>
