# Lesson 09: Early Screening & Tests

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice. These tests are **optional** and personal;
> a provider or genetic counsellor should guide your choices.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **screening test** — estimates the *chance* of a condition; safe and non-invasive, but not a yes/no answer.
> - **diagnostic test** — gives a definitive answer, but may be invasive with a small risk.
> - **NIPT / cell-free DNA** — a blood test from ~10 weeks that screens for certain chromosome conditions.
> - **nuchal translucency (NT)** — an ultrasound measurement (~11–14 weeks) used in first-trimester screening.
> - **chromosome condition** — e.g. Down syndrome (trisomy 21), caused by an extra chromosome.
> - **amniocentesis / CVS** — diagnostic tests that sample fluid or placenta; definitive, with a small miscarriage risk.

## Concept

Early pregnancy comes with a menu of **optional** tests. The key distinction to
understand is **screening vs. diagnostic**:

- **Screening** tells you the *chance* (e.g. "1 in 1,500") of a condition. It's
  **safe and non-invasive** (blood test + ultrasound) but never a definite answer.
- **Diagnostic** gives a *definite* answer but usually means an invasive procedure
  (**amniocentesis** or **CVS**) with a small miscarriage risk.

Most people start with screening; only if a screen shows higher risk (or for other
reasons) is diagnostic testing considered. **All of it is your choice** — including
choosing none.

## Going Deeper

### The common early tests

- **Dating ultrasound** — confirms the pregnancy, dates it, and checks for more
  than one baby.
- **First-trimester combined screening** (~11–14 weeks) — an ultrasound
  **nuchal translucency (NT)** measurement plus a blood test, giving a risk
  estimate for chromosome conditions.
- **NIPT / cell-free DNA** (from ~10 weeks) — a **blood test** that screens for
  the common trisomies (like Down syndrome) with high accuracy; it can often also
  reveal the **baby's sex**. Still a *screen*, not a diagnosis.
- **Diagnostic tests** — **CVS** (~11–14 weeks) or **amniocentesis** (~15+ weeks)
  sample cells for a definitive answer; offered when a screen is higher-risk or on
  request, with counselling about the small risk.
- **Carrier screening** — a blood test (ideally preconception) checking whether
  you both carry the same recessive condition.
- **Routine bloods** — blood type and **Rh**, anemia, immunity, and infections.

### Deciding what's right for you

Screening isn't automatically "the responsible thing" or "unnecessary worry" — it
depends on **what the information would mean to you**. Helpful questions to discuss:

- Would a result **change any decision** we'd make, or how we'd prepare?
- Would knowing **reduce or increase** our anxiety?
- Are we comfortable with a *risk estimate*, or would we want a definite answer
  (and its small procedure risk)?

There are no wrong answers — some couples want all the information, some want none,
many want the safe screens but not invasive tests. A **genetic counsellor** can
help you think it through, especially with a family history.

{: .note }
> **Screen ≠ diagnosis**
> A "higher-risk" screening result does **not** mean the baby has the condition —
> it means further testing could clarify. Many higher-risk screens are followed by
> completely reassuring diagnostic results. Try not to panic at a screening number
> before you've spoken with your provider or a genetic counsellor.

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What's the difference between a screening test and a diagnostic test?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A <strong>screening</strong> test estimates the <em>chance</em> of a condition and
is safe/non-invasive (blood test + ultrasound), but isn't a definite answer. A
<strong>diagnostic</strong> test (like amniocentesis or CVS) gives a
<em>definite</em> answer but is usually invasive with a small miscarriage risk.
</details>

**Q2.** Does a "higher-risk" screening result mean the baby has the condition?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
No. A higher-risk screen only means the <em>chance</em> is increased and that
further (diagnostic) testing could clarify — many higher-risk screens are followed
by reassuring diagnostic results. Speak with your provider or a genetic counsellor
before assuming anything.
</details>

**Q3.** What's a good question a couple should ask themselves before choosing
tests?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Chiefly: <strong>"Would this information change a decision we'd make, or help us
prepare — and would knowing ease or increase our anxiety?"</strong> The right
choice depends on what the results would <em>mean</em> to you; declining is a valid
option, as is wanting the safe screens but not invasive tests.
</details>
