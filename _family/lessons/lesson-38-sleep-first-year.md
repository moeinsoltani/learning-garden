# Lesson 38: Sleep in the First Year

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice. Keep **safe-sleep rules (Lesson 25)** for
> the whole first year. Sleep approaches are personal — do what fits your family and
> ask your provider with concerns.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **circadian rhythm** (sir-KAY-dee-an) — the internal day/night body clock, which develops over the first months.
> - **sleep cycle** — the repeating stages of sleep; babies wake between cycles (normal).
> - **drowsy but awake** — putting a baby down sleepy but not fully asleep, to help them learn to settle.
> - **sleep regression** — a temporary stretch of worse sleep (often around developmental leaps or teething).
> - **sleep training** — various methods to help a baby fall asleep/resettle independently (a personal choice).
> - **self-settling** — a baby's ability to fall back asleep on their own.

## Concept

Baby sleep is the topic new parents obsess over — and the biggest gift here is
**realistic expectations.** Newborns have **no day/night rhythm** and wake often;
over the first year, sleep gradually **consolidates** into longer night stretches
and more predictable naps. **Night wakings are normal**, "sleeping through" varies
hugely, and there's **no single right method.** Gentle routines and patience do most
of the work.

## Going Deeper

### How sleep develops

- **Newborn (0–3 months):** sleeps a lot, in short bursts, no rhythm; wakes to feed
  around the clock.
- **~2–4 months:** a **circadian rhythm** develops — night stretches lengthen, and
  a rough day/night pattern emerges.
- **Later in the first year:** naps consolidate (often down to ~2 a day), and many
  (not all) babies sleep longer stretches at night — with plenty of normal
  variation and disruptions.

### Realistic expectations

**Night wakings are normal and common well into the first year** — babies wake
between sleep cycles and often need help resettling, and many still wake to feed.
"Sleeping through the night" (a marketing-ish phrase) happens on very different
timelines and isn't a measure of good parenting. Comparison here causes needless
misery.

### Building healthy sleep habits (gently)

- **Day/night distinction:** bright, active, chatty days; calm, dim, boring nights.
- **A consistent, calming bedtime routine** (bath, feed, book, song, bed) cues
  sleep — start it whenever you like; babies love predictability.
- **"Drowsy but awake"** (when developmentally ready) helps a baby learn to
  **self-settle**, so they can resettle between cycles.
- **Watch for tired cues** (yawning, eye-rubbing, fussiness) and avoid
  overtiredness, which paradoxically makes settling harder.

### Sleep training — a personal choice

From around **4–6 months**, some families use **sleep-training** methods (ranging
from very gentle/gradual to brief cry-based approaches) to help a baby learn to fall
asleep independently. It's **optional, personal, and sometimes debated** — there's
no single "right" answer, and gentle, responsive approaches are just as valid as
more structured ones. Do what fits your baby and your values; it's not required.

### Disruptions are normal

**Sleep regressions** (temporary worse patches, often around developmental leaps),
**teething**, illness, and travel all disrupt sleep — usually temporarily. And mind
the **parents' sleep** too: shifts, naps, and shared nights (Lesson 35) keep you
functioning.

{: .note }
> **Lower the pressure**
> There's a whole industry built on new-parent sleep anxiety. The reassuring truth:
> frequent waking is normal, it improves over the first year, and a loving, rested-
> enough family beats a "perfect sleeper" achieved through stress. Keep safe sleep,
> build gentle routines, be patient, and take comparison off the table.

## Lab — Plan Together

**Exercise 1 — realistic or a myth?** True or false: (a) a healthy 4-month-old
"should" sleep 12 hours straight; (b) night wakings are normal in the first year;
(c) a consistent bedtime routine helps signal sleep; (d) a baby who wakes at night
means the parents did something wrong.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) <strong>False/myth</strong> — many 4-month-olds wake overnight; expecting 12
straight hours sets you up for disappointment. (b) <strong>True</strong> — night
wakings are normal well into the first year. (c) <strong>True</strong> — routines
cue sleep. (d) <strong>False</strong> — normal waking isn't a parenting failure. The
theme: expect wakings, use gentle routines, and drop the guilt and comparison.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — design a bedtime routine.** Sketch a simple, calming ~30-minute
bedtime routine for an older baby, and say why each step helps.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A good routine is short, consistent, and calming, e.g.: <strong>warm bath</strong>
(winds down) → <strong>into pajamas + dim lights</strong> (signals night) →
<strong>feed</strong> (comfort, not necessarily to sleep) → <strong>book or song in
a quiet voice</strong> (predictable cue) → <strong>into the crib drowsy but awake</strong>
(learning to settle). It works because babies thrive on <strong>predictable cues</strong>
that say "sleep is coming," and calm/dim steps distinguish night from day. Consistency
matters more than the exact steps.
</details>

**Your answer:**

<strong>circadian rhythm develops around 2–4 months</strong>, night stretches

</details>

**Q2.** Name two ways to gently build healthy sleep habits.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>

dim nights); keep a <strong>consistent calming bedtime routine</strong>; put the baby
down <strong>"drowsy but awake"</strong> when ready (to learn self-settling); and
