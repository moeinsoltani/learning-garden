# Lesson 28: Daily Care

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice. When something about your baby's skin,
> cord, or care worries you, ask your provider — and see Lesson 30 for red flags.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **umbilical cord stump** — the small remainder of the cord; it dries and falls off in ~1–3 weeks.
> - **diaper rash** — sore, red skin in the diaper area; usually preventable and treatable.
> - **sponge bath** — washing with a damp cloth (used until the cord falls off).
> - **cradle cap** — flaky, scaly patches on the scalp; harmless and common.
> - **baby acne** — small bumps on a newborn's face; harmless and passes.
> - **fontanelle** — a soft spot on the head; handle gently, but it's tougher than it looks.

## Concept

The daily-care basics — **diapering, bathing, cord care, dressing, and handling** —
are quickly learned and soon become second nature. Most of it is simpler than new
parents fear, and many "problems" (dry skin, baby acne, cradle cap) are **normal and
self-resolving**. This lesson gives you the confidence to handle the everyday
looking-after without second-guessing every step.

## Going Deeper

### Diapering

Change diapers **often** (whenever wet or soiled — newborns need many changes a
day). Wipe front-to-back (especially for girls), let the skin dry, and use a barrier
cream if needed. **Prevent diaper rash** with frequent changes, gentle cleaning, and
some nappy-free air time; most rashes clear with these steps, but ask if it's severe,
blistering, or not improving. The **cord stump** should be kept outside the diaper
(fold the diaper down) to stay dry.

### Bathing

Newborns don't need daily baths — **2–3 times a week** is plenty (more drying is
worse for their skin). Until the **cord stump falls off**, stick to **sponge baths**.
Then:

- Use **warm (not hot) water** — test with your elbow/wrist — and support the head
  and neck at all times.
- **Never leave a baby alone** in or near water, even for a second.
- Keep baths short; pat dry, especially the skin folds.

### Umbilical cord care

Keep the stump **clean and dry**; it falls off on its own in about **1–3 weeks**. A
little dried blood when it separates is normal. **Contact your provider** if you see
signs of infection: **redness spreading** around the base, **pus/oozing**, a bad
smell, or if the baby has a fever.

### Dressing, nails, and skin

- **Dress** the baby in about **one more layer than you'd wear** — comfortable, not
  overheated (overheating is a safe-sleep concern, Lesson 25).
- **Nails** grow fast and babies scratch themselves — file or carefully trim them
  (easiest when the baby is asleep).
- **Skin:** dry/peeling skin, **baby acne**, and **cradle cap** are all common and
  harmless and pass on their own; you don't need to treat them aggressively.

### Handling

Always **support the head and neck** (newborns can't yet). Handle the **soft spots**
gently — but they're covered by a tough membrane and are more robust than they look.
Skin-to-skin, gentle rocking, and talking are all part of care, not just cleaning.

{: .note }
> **Most "problems" are normal**
> Cradle cap, baby acne, dry peeling skin, occasional spit-up, and mild diaper
> redness are usually normal and self-limiting. Save your worry (and your calls) for
> the things that actually signal trouble — spreading redness or pus at the cord, a
> severe or blistering rash, or the Lesson 30 red flags.

**Q1.** How often do newborns need baths, and what's the rule until the cord falls
off?

**Your answer:**

## Homework

Practise the daily-care basics before or early on — a diaper change, supporting the
head, a sponge bath routine — so both partners are comfortable and can **share** the
tasks. Learn the cord-infection signs together, and agree how you'll split daily
care so it doesn't all fall on one person.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The win is two confident, capable caregivers who can each diaper, bathe, and dress
the baby, know the harmless-vs-worrying skin/cord signs, and split the work. Shared
competence here matters enormously — it prevents one parent becoming the sole
"expert" and lets the other genuinely rest.
</details>
