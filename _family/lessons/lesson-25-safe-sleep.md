# Lesson 25: Safe Sleep

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice. Safe-sleep guidance is important and
> evidence-based — follow current official guidance and your provider.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **SIDS** — Sudden Infant Death Syndrome: the sudden, unexplained death of a baby, usually in sleep. Safe-sleep habits lower the risk.
> - **room-sharing** — baby sleeps in the parents' room, in their *own* sleep space (recommended). Not the same as bed-sharing.
> - **bed-sharing / co-sleeping in an adult bed** — sleeping with the baby *in* an adult bed; carries higher risk.
> - **swaddle** — snug wrapping that can soothe a young baby (stop once they can roll).
> - **tummy time** — supervised play on the tummy while **awake** (not for sleep).

## Concept

Safe sleep is the single most important safety habit of the newborn phase, because
consistent safe-sleep practices dramatically **reduce the risk of SIDS** and sleep
accidents. It boils down to three words: **Alone, Back, Crib** — and to keeping the
sleep space **empty and firm**. This lesson is worth learning cold, because a tired
brain at 3 a.m. needs the rule to be automatic.

## Going Deeper

### The safe-sleep rules

- **Back to sleep — every sleep, every time** (naps and night). Once a baby can
  roll both ways on their own, they can find their own position, but you still
  *place* them on the back.
- **Alone in their own sleep space:** a **crib or bassinet** with a **firm, flat
  mattress** and a fitted sheet — and **nothing else** in it (no pillows, bumpers,
  loose blankets, quilts, toys, or positioners). Use a sleep sack instead of loose
  blankets for warmth.
- **In your room (room-share)** for the early months (often ~6 months), but in the
  baby's *own* space — **not** in your bed.
- **Avoid overheating** — dress lightly, keep the room comfortable, don't over-bundle.
- **Smoke-free** environment (in pregnancy and after) lowers the risk.
- **Breastfeeding** and offering a **pacifier** at sleep are each linked to lower
  risk.

### The dangerous shortcuts to avoid

The riskiest situations are **bed-sharing in an adult bed** (especially with soft
bedding, or if a parent has smoked, drunk alcohol, taken sedating medication, or is
very tired) and — worst of all — **falling asleep with the baby on a couch or
armchair**. If you're feeding at night and worry you might doze off, feed in a
**chair you won't fall asleep in**, or in bed with pillows/duvet away and then put
the baby back in their own space before you sleep.

### Swaddling and tummy time

- **Swaddling** can soothe a young baby, but keep it snug at the chest and loose at
  the hips, don't overheat, and **stop swaddling once the baby shows signs of
  rolling** (a swaddled baby who rolls to their front is at risk).
- **Tummy time** is important — but only while **awake and supervised**. It builds
  neck and shoulder strength and prevents flat spots. It is *never* a sleep position.

{: .note }
> **The three-word rule: Alone, Back, Crib**
> **A**lone (own space, nothing in it), on the **B**ack, in a **C**rib/bassinet in
> your room. Firm flat mattress, no soft bedding, no overheating, smoke-free. Memorise
> this — it's the highest-value safety habit of the newborn months.

## Lab — Plan Together

**Exercise 1 — safe-sleep audit.** What's unsafe in each? (a) Baby asleep on their
tummy for the night; (b) a soft blanket and a stuffed toy in the crib; (c) baby
sleeping in the parents' bed under the duvet; (d) parent dozing with baby on the
sofa.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) <strong>Back to sleep</strong> — place the baby on their back, not their tummy.
(b) The crib must be <strong>empty</strong> — remove the blanket and toy (use a
sleep sack for warmth). (c) <strong>Bed-sharing under a duvet</strong> is high-risk —
baby should be in their own space in the room. (d) <strong>Sleeping with baby on a
sofa/armchair is the most dangerous</strong> of all — never do it. Each fix moves
toward "Alone, Back, Crib."
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the night-feed trap.** It's 4 a.m., you're exhausted, and it's
tempting to feed the baby lying down in bed and drift off. What's the safer plan?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Feed somewhere you're <strong>less likely to fall asleep</strong> — or if feeding in
bed, clear away pillows and duvet and <strong>put the baby back in their own crib/
bassinet before you sleep</strong>. Crucially, <strong>never finish up asleep on a
couch or armchair with the baby</strong> — that's the highest-risk scenario. Planning
this in advance (a firm chair, a light on, your partner taking a turn) prevents the
dangerous "just this once" doze.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What is the three-word safe-sleep rule, and what does each part mean?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Alone, Back, Crib.</strong> <em>Alone</em> = the baby's own sleep space with
<strong>nothing in it</strong> (no pillows, blankets, bumpers, toys). <em>Back</em> =
placed on the back for every sleep. <em>Crib</em> = a firm, flat crib/bassinet, ideally
in the parents' room (room-sharing, not bed-sharing).

## Homework

Set up (or double-check) the baby's sleep space so it passes the audit — **firm flat
mattress, fitted sheet, nothing else, in your room** — and both of you memorise
"**Alone, Back, Crib**." Agree a **safe night-feed plan** so no one is tempted into a
sofa doze.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The deliverable is a genuinely empty, firm crib/bassinet in your room, both partners
able to recite the rule, and a night-feeding plan that avoids sofas and beds-with-

automatic — and planning the tempting 4 a.m. feed in advance — is what keeps it real.
</details>
