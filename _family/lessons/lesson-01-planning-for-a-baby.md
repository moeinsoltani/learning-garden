# Lesson 01: Planning for a Baby — Before, During & After Pregnancy

{: .important }
> **Read this first — this lesson is a map, not a doctor.**
> Everything here is **general, evidence-based education** to help you ask good
> questions and feel prepared. It is **not** medical advice for your specific
> situation. The single most valuable action you can take is to **book a
> preconception visit** with a family doctor, obstetrician, or midwife *before*
> trying — bring this lesson's checklists with you. Always follow your own
> healthcare provider over anything written here.

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **preconception** (pree-kon-SEP-shun) — the time *before* you get pregnant, when you prepare your body and life.
> - **conception** — the moment a sperm fertilises an egg; the start of pregnancy.
> - **prenatal** (pree-NAY-tal) — "before birth"; the care and checkups during pregnancy. Also called *antenatal*.
> - **postpartum** (post-PAR-tum) — the period *after* delivery, for both baby and recovering mother. Often called the "fourth trimester" (first ~3 months).
> - **trimester** (try-MESS-ter) — one of the three ~13-week thirds of pregnancy.
> - **folic acid / folate** (FOH-lik / FOH-late) — a B-vitamin taken before and in early pregnancy to help prevent serious birth defects of the brain and spine.
> - **ovulation** (ov-yoo-LAY-shun) — when an ovary releases an egg; the few days around it are the "fertile window."
> - **obstetrician / OB** (ob-steh-TRISH-un) — a doctor specialising in pregnancy and birth; a **midwife** is a trained professional who supports low-risk pregnancy and birth.
> - **preeclampsia** (pree-ee-KLAMP-see-ah) — a serious pregnancy condition of high blood pressure; a key reason for the warning signs below.
> - **SIDS** — Sudden Infant Death Syndrome; "safe sleep" habits lower the risk.

## Concept

Having a baby is one journey in **three stages**, and each has a different job:

```
   BEFORE                 DURING                  AFTER
 (preconception)        (pregnancy)            (postpartum)
      │                     │                       │
  prepare bodies       grow the baby           recover + care for
  & life; fix          safely; monitor         newborn; protect
  risks early          mother & baby           mother's body & mind
      │                     │                       │
   ~3 months            ~40 weeks              first weeks–months
   ahead of time        (3 trimesters)         ("4th trimester")
```

The most important idea for a couple *thinking* about kids: **the work starts
before pregnancy, not at the positive test.** Folic acid, vaccines, quitting
smoking or alcohol, and getting chronic conditions under control all matter most
in the first weeks — often *before* a woman even knows she's pregnant. Preparing
early is the highest-leverage thing you can do.

The second most important idea: **this is a two-person project.** The pregnant
partner carries it physically, but the other partner's habits (before), support
(during), and hands-on care (after) hugely affect how it goes. This lesson is
written for **both of you**.

## Going Deeper

### Stage 1 — Before pregnancy (preconception)

Think of this as "getting the ground ready." Ideally start **about three months
ahead**.

**Both partners:**
- **Book a preconception checkup.** Ask about your health, family history, and
  any medications. This one visit covers most of what follows.
- **Stop smoking, vaping, recreational drugs, and alcohol.** These affect
  fertility, sperm, egg, and early development — for *both* of you.
- **Get to a healthy weight and move regularly.** Being significantly under- or
  over-weight can make conceiving harder and pregnancy riskier.
- **Eat well** (vegetables, whole grains, protein, less ultra-processed food)
  and **cut caffeine down** (aim under ~200 mg/day, about one small coffee).
- **Review family & genetic history.** Ask about inherited conditions; your
  doctor may offer **genetic carrier screening** (a simple test to see if you
  both carry the same recessive condition).
- **Check your finances, leave, and support system** — parental leave, childcare,
  who can help. Talk honestly about readiness and expectations.

**The partner who will carry the baby, especially:**
- **Start folic acid now.** Most people need **0.4 mg (400 mcg) daily**, ideally
  from **2–3 months before** conception through at least the first trimester. Some
  (previous affected pregnancy, diabetes, certain medications, higher weight) need
  a **higher dose — only on medical advice.** A prenatal multivitamin usually
  includes it.
- **Update vaccinations *before* pregnancy** — e.g. **MMR (rubella)** and
  **chickenpox (varicella)** are *live* vaccines that must be given before, not
  during. Flu and COVID vaccines are safe and recommended.
- **Get chronic conditions well-controlled** — diabetes, thyroid, high blood
  pressure, epilepsy, and **mental-health conditions**. Some medications need to
  be switched to pregnancy-safe versions *before* conceiving — never stop a
  prescription on your own; ask first.
- **See a dentist** — gum health matters and some treatment is easier done before.
- **Learn your cycle.** Ovulation is usually ~14 days before the next period; the
  **fertile window** is roughly the 5 days before, plus the day of, ovulation.

**The other partner, for sperm health:** healthy diet, no smoking/excess alcohol,
avoid excessive heat (hot tubs, laptop on lap), manage weight and stress. Sperm
take ~3 months to mature, so changes now pay off later.

{: .note }
> **How long does it take — and when to ask for help?**
> Many healthy couples take several months; about **8 in 10 conceive within a
> year** of trying. See a doctor about fertility if you're **under 35 and haven't
> conceived after 12 months**, **35 or older after 6 months**, or sooner if you
> already know of a relevant condition. Struggling to conceive is common and
> often treatable — it is not a failure.

### Stage 2 — During pregnancy

Pregnancy is about **40 weeks**, counted from the first day of the last period,
split into three trimesters.

| Trimester | Weeks | What's happening | Your focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| **First** | 1–13 | Major organs form; often nausea & fatigue; miscarriage risk highest early | Book first prenatal visit (~8–10 wks), keep taking folic acid, avoid harms |
| **Second** | 14–27 | Often the "easier" trimester; bump grows; you feel movement | Anatomy scan (~18–22 wks), gestational-diabetes test (~24–28 wks) |
| **Third** | 28–40 | Baby gains weight; more discomfort; body prepares for birth | Count movements, birth plan, classes, GBS swab (~35–37 wks) |

**Prenatal care (don't skip visits).** A typical schedule: first visit around
8–10 weeks, then roughly **every 4 weeks until ~28 weeks, every 2 weeks until
~36, then weekly** until birth. These visits catch problems early — that's their
whole point.

**Eating for pregnancy:**
- Keep taking a **prenatal vitamin** (folic acid, iron, and often vitamin D and
  DHA). Ask your provider about iron and vitamin D.
- **Foods to avoid** (infection or mercury risk): raw/undercooked meat, poultry,
  eggs, and fish; **high-mercury fish** (shark, swordfish, king mackerel, marlin —
  limit tuna); **unpasteurised** milk/cheese; deli meats and pâté unless heated;
  raw sprouts. **Alcohol: none — there is no known safe amount.** Caffeine: keep
  under ~200 mg/day.
- Gentle, regular **exercise** is good for most pregnancies (walking, swimming,
  prenatal yoga) — check with your provider, especially for anything intense.

**Tests & vaccines you'll be offered:** a dating ultrasound; **genetic
screening** (e.g. a blood test called NIPT from ~10 weeks) which is *optional*;
the ~20-week **anatomy scan**; a **gestational diabetes** test at 24–28 weeks; a
**Group B Strep (GBS)** swab near the end; and vaccines **during** pregnancy —
**Tdap (whooping cough)**, flu, COVID, and (newer) RSV protection — to protect
the newborn. Ask what each test is *for* and that it's your choice.

{: .warning }
> **Call your provider or seek urgent care during pregnancy if you have:**
> - **Vaginal bleeding** or fluid gushing/leaking
> - **Severe or constant abdominal pain**
> - A **severe headache, vision changes, or sudden swelling** of face/hands *(possible preeclampsia)*
> - **Your baby moving much less than usual** (in later pregnancy)
> - **Fever**, persistent vomiting, or pain/burning when urinating
>
> When in doubt, **call** — providers would always rather hear from you.

**Look after the mind, too.** Anxiety and depression can happen *during*
pregnancy, not only after. It's common and treatable — tell your provider.

**Partner's job this stage:** go to appointments, learn alongside her, take on
more of the housework and mental load, protect her rest, and help avoid the
harms above. Consider prenatal/birth classes together and start a simple **birth
plan** (preferences for labour, pain relief, who's present) — held loosely, since
birth is unpredictable.

### Stage 3 — After delivery (postpartum)

The "fourth trimester" is two recoveries at once: a **newborn** learning to live
outside, and a **mother** healing. Both need care.

**Caring for a newborn — the basics:**
- **Feeding.** Breast milk is recommended where possible (immune and bonding
  benefits), but **fed is what matters** — formula and combination feeding raise
  healthy babies too. Newborns feed **8–12 times per 24 hours**. A good sign
  feeding is working: by about day 5–6, **6+ wet diapers a day** and steady weight
  gain (babies regain their birth weight by ~2 weeks).
- **Safe sleep (lowers SIDS risk):** always place baby **on their back**, on a
  **firm flat surface**, in their **own crib/bassinet in your room** (room-share,
  don't bed-share), with **no pillows, bumpers, loose blankets, or toys**, and
  don't let them overheat. A pacifier at sleep and breastfeeding may lower risk.
- **Well-baby visits and vaccines** keep the baby on track — go to all of them.
- **Cord, jaundice, feeding cues:** keep the cord stump clean and dry; watch for
  yellowing skin (jaundice) and ask if unsure; learn hunger cues (rooting,
  hands to mouth) rather than waiting for crying.

{: .warning }
> **Newborn — seek care urgently if your baby has:**
> a **fever of 38 °C (100.4 °F) or higher in a baby under 3 months** *(go to
> emergency immediately)*, trouble breathing, is very hard to wake or floppy,
> won't feed, has far fewer wet diapers, persistent vomiting, or turns blue.
> For a young baby, trust your instinct and don't wait.

**Mother's recovery:**
- Expect **bleeding (lochia)** for a few weeks, **afterpains**, and soreness
  (perineal stitches or a **C-section incision** to care for). Rest, hydrate, and
  don't rush.
- There's usually a **6-week checkup**, but recovery takes longer — be patient
  with your body.
- **Contraception & spacing:** breastfeeding is **not** reliable birth control;
  ask about contraception, and providers often suggest waiting ~18–24 months
  before the next pregnancy.

{: .warning }
> **Mother — postpartum warning signs (can occur for up to a year):** call for
> help or go to emergency for **heavy bleeding** (soaking more than one pad an
> hour), **fever**, a **severe headache or vision changes**, **chest pain or
> trouble breathing** *(possible blood clot)*, **pain/redness/swelling in one
> leg**, a very painful or hot belly/incision, or **any thoughts of harming
> yourself or the baby.**

**Emotional health — take this seriously.** The **"baby blues"** (tearful, up-
and-down) are very common in the first ~2 weeks. If low mood, anxiety, or
hopelessness **lasts beyond two weeks or feels severe**, that may be
**postpartum depression or anxiety** — it is common, **treatable**, and **not a
weakness or a failure.** Partners can experience depression too. Get help early.

**Partner's job this stage:** this is where you carry a huge share. Take
**parental leave** if you can, do night feeds/diapers/chores, guard her sleep and
meals, manage visitors, and **watch for the mood warning signs** above — she may
not notice them in herself. Accept and ask for help; you don't have to do it
alone.

{: .note }
> **The one-line version of each stage**
> **Before:** prepare early — folic acid, vaccines, quit harms, control health,
> see a doctor. **During:** go to every checkup, eat and avoid wisely, know the
> warning signs. **After:** safe sleep + feeding for baby, rest + recovery +
> mental health for mother, hands-on partner throughout.

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>

severe or you can't keep fluids down. (b) <strong>Seek care now</strong> — headache

(c) <strong>Call now</strong> — a marked drop in fetal movement in late pregnancy
needs prompt checking. (d) <strong>Emergency now</strong> — a fever ≥38 °C in a baby
under 3 months is a medical emergency. Full marks for knowing that (b), (c), and
(d) are "don't wait" situations.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — divide the load.** Imagine the first month after birth. Write down
**three specific jobs the non-birthing partner will own** so the mother can rest
and recover. (Be concrete.)

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Strong, concrete examples: "I take all diaper changes and burping overnight so she

"I manage visitors and thank-yous so she isn't 'on'"; "I book and drive to the

Full marks for jobs that are <em>specific</em> and genuinely protect her sleep,

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why does so much of the preparation belong *before* pregnancy rather than
after the positive test?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because the earliest weeks — when the baby's brain, spine, and organs are forming —
often happen <strong>before a woman even knows she's pregnant</strong>. Folic acid
(to prevent neural-tube defects), avoiding alcohol/smoking, updating live vaccines
that can't be given during pregnancy, and getting chronic conditions and
medications sorted all have their biggest effect in that early window, so starting
~3 months ahead is the highest-leverage move.
</details>

**Q2.** What is folic acid for, roughly how much is the usual dose, and when
should it be taken?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Folic acid helps prevent serious <strong>neural-tube birth defects</strong> of the
brain and spine. The usual dose is <strong>0.4 mg (400 mcg) daily</strong> for most
people, taken from <strong>2–3 months before conception through at least the first
trimester</strong>. Higher-risk individuals may need a larger dose — but only on
medical advice.
</details>

**Q3.** Name two warning signs in pregnancy and two in a newborn that mean "seek
care now, don't wait."

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Pregnancy (any two):</strong> vaginal bleeding or leaking fluid; severe
headache with vision changes/swelling (preeclampsia); a big drop in the baby's
movement late in pregnancy; severe abdominal pain; fever. <strong>Newborn (any
two):</strong> a fever ≥38 °C in a baby under 3 months; trouble breathing; very
hard to wake / floppy; won't feed; far fewer wet diapers; turning blue.
</details>

**Q4.** What's the difference between the "baby blues" and postpartum depression,
and what should you do about the latter?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The <strong>baby blues</strong> are common, mild ups-and-downs in the first ~2
weeks that pass on their own. <strong>Postpartum depression/anxiety</strong> is
more intense and <strong>lasts beyond two weeks</strong> (persistent low mood,
hopelessness, severe anxiety). It is common, <strong>treatable, and not a
weakness</strong> — the right response is to <strong>seek help early</strong> from
a provider. Partners should watch for the signs too (and can experience depression
themselves).
</details>
