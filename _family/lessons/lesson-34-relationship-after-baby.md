# Lesson 34: Your Relationship After Baby

{: .important }
> General education, not therapy. If conflict becomes constant or you feel stuck,
> couples counselling is a healthy, normal tool — not a last resort.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **fourth trimester** — the intense first ~3 months after birth.
> - **resentment** — slow-building anger from feeling unseen or unfairly loaded.
> - **repair** — reconnecting after a conflict (apologising, understanding, moving on).
> - **libido** (lih-BEE-doh) — sex drive, which commonly dips after birth.
> - **check-in** — a short, regular conversation to stay connected.
> - **bid for connection** — a small attempt to get attention/affection; noticing and responding to these keeps couples close.

## Concept

A baby is one of the most joyful — and most **stressful** — things that can happen
to a couple. Sleep, time, energy, and sex all drop while chores and pressure rise,
so it's completely normal for the relationship to feel **strained**. This isn't a
sign you chose wrong; it's a **predictable phase** that couples get through with
communication, fairness, and patience. Knowing it's coming is half the battle.

## Going Deeper

### Why it gets hard

Less sleep and less couple-time, an unequal-feeling division of labour, big
identity shifts, and raw exhaustion are a recipe for **short tempers and
resentment**. Most new-parent conflict is really about **feeling unseen, unequally
loaded, or unsupported** — not about the socks or the dishes on the surface.

### The tools that protect a couple

- **Communicate needs directly.** Exhausted people can't mind-read. "I need 30
  minutes to nap" beats silent resentment.
- **Share fairly, without scorekeeping.** Aim for a division that *feels* fair to
  both (Lesson 33), and assume good intent rather than tallying points.
- **Appreciate out loud.** Notice and thank each other for the invisible work —
  small appreciation buffers a lot of stress.
- **Do tiny reconnections.** A hug, a coffee together, a two-minute check-in,
  responding to each other's small **bids for connection** — these keep you a
  team, not just co-managers of a baby.
- **Repair after conflict.** Fights will happen on no sleep; what matters is
  **repairing** — a genuine "I'm sorry, I was fried, I love you" — not never
  fighting.

### Intimacy will change — and that's normal

Physical intimacy usually pauses while the mother heals (often until after the
~6-week check and when *she* feels ready — there's no deadline), and **low libido**
is common for both, from exhaustion, hormones, and pain. Keep **emotional
intimacy** alive (affection, talking, closeness) and let physical intimacy return
**gradually and without pressure**. And remember: **breastfeeding is not reliable
contraception** — sort out birth control (Lesson 31 area / your provider) if you're
not ready for another pregnancy.

### It's a phase — protect the long game

The intensity eases. Couples who come through well tend to **treat each other as
teammates**, keep talking, lower their standards for a while, and remember they're
on the **same side against the problem** — not on opposite sides against each other.

{: .note }
> **Assume you're on the same team**
> When you're both exhausted, it's easy to see your partner as the obstacle. Try to
> remember the real opponent is the *hard phase*, not each other. A quick check-in
> ("How are we doing? What do you need?") and a bit of grace go a long way. This is
> temporary — the teamwork you build now lasts.

## Lab — Plan Together

**Exercise 1 — spot the real issue.** One partner snaps: "You never do anything
around here!" after a sleepless night. What's likely the *real* message underneath,
and a better way to say it?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
The surface accusation usually masks "<strong>I feel overwhelmed, unseen, and
unsupported, and I need help and acknowledgment.</strong>" A better version: "I'm
running on empty and I feel like I'm carrying most of this — can we look at how we're
splitting things, and can you take X tonight?" Full marks for reading conflict as an
unmet-need signal and reframing it as a direct, non-blaming request. (And the
listener's job: hear the need, not just the words.)
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the weekly check-in.** Design a short, regular couple check-in for
the newborn months. What would you ask, and why keep it small?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A good check-in is brief and kind, e.g. weekly (even 10 minutes): "How are you doing,
really?", "What felt hard this week?", "What do you need more/less of from me?", "One
thing I appreciated about you this week is…". Keep it <strong>small</strong> because
exhausted new parents can't manage long summits — a short, regular ritual keeps you
connected and catches resentment early, before it builds. Consistency beats intensity.
</details>

## Homework

Start a small **weekly check-in** ritual now (even before the baby), practise stating
needs directly and appreciating each other out loud, and agree a shared reminder for
the hard days: **"same team, this is a phase."** Sort out your plan for contraception
after birth too.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The win is a lightweight, sustainable connection habit plus a shared mindset ("same
team; temporary phase") and a practical contraception plan. Couples who keep talking,
share fairly, repair after fights, and protect small moments of closeness weather the
newborn storm best — and the teamwork you build now outlasts the exhaustion.
</details>
