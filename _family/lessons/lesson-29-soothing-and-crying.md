# Lesson 29: Soothing & Crying

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice. This lesson includes a vital safety rule:
> **never shake a baby.** If you feel overwhelmed, use the safe plan below and
> reach out for help.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **soothing** — calming a distressed baby.
> - **colic** (KOL-ik) — long bouts of crying in an otherwise healthy, well-fed baby; it passes.
> - **the 5 S's** — a popular set of soothing techniques (swaddle, side/stomach hold, shush, swing, suck).
> - **overstimulation** — too much noise/light/activity, which can make a baby cry.
> - **abusive head trauma / shaken baby** — severe brain injury from shaking a baby; **never shake a baby**.
> - **cry-it-out vs. responding** — for *newborns*, respond to cries; you cannot spoil a newborn.

## Concept

Crying is a newborn's **language**, not a verdict on your parenting. Babies cry for
many reasons — and sometimes for no findable reason at all. You'll build a
**soothing toolkit** and get better at reading your baby, but some crying is normal
and unavoidable, peaking around **6–8 weeks** and then easing. The most important
thing in this whole lesson is a safety rule for the *parent's* hardest moments:
**if you're overwhelmed, it is always okay to put the baby down safely and step
away — and never, ever shake a baby.**

## Going Deeper

### Why babies cry, and how to soothe

Run through the usual causes: **hungry, tired, wet/dirty diaper, too hot/cold,
uncomfortable, overstimulated, or just wanting contact.** Then reach for soothing
techniques — the **5 S's** are a handy set:

- **Swaddle** (snug wrapping)
- **Side/stomach hold** (in your arms — not for sleep)
- **Shush** (a loud "shhh" or white noise, like the womb)
- **Swing** (gentle rhythmic motion, rocking)
- **Suck** (breast, clean finger, or pacifier)

Also: feeding, skin-to-skin, a walk or a car ride, a warm calm environment, and
simply holding them. **You cannot spoil a newborn** by responding — comfort builds
security.

### Colic — the "healthy baby who won't stop crying"

Some babies have **colic**: long crying bouts (often evenings), in a baby who is
**healthy, fed, and growing**. A rough guide is the "rule of 3s" — crying **3+
hours a day, 3+ days a week, for 3+ weeks**. It's exhausting and not your fault,
there's often no clear cause, and — crucially — **it passes**, usually by 3–4
months. If crying seems different (high-pitched, with fever, poor feeding, or
you're worried), get it checked (Lesson 30).

### The most important safety rule

Relentless crying can push any loving parent to the edge — this is **normal and
human**, and admitting it is a strength. But shaking a baby, even briefly, can
cause **catastrophic brain injury or death** (**abusive head trauma**). So the rule
is absolute:

{: .warning }
> **If you feel overwhelmed or angry:**
> 1. **Put the baby down safely** on their back in the crib.
> 2. **Walk away**, close the door, and take a few minutes to breathe.
> 3. A crying baby in a safe crib is **fine** for a few minutes while you calm down.
> 4. **Call someone** — your partner, a friend, family, or a helpline.
> **Never shake a baby.** Stepping away is not failing; it is exactly the right,
> responsible thing to do.

Swap with your partner, take shifts, and treat "I need a break" as a normal,
respected request — not a weakness.

{: .note }
> **Normal crying has a peak**
> Crying tends to increase in the first weeks, **peak around 6–8 weeks**, then
> gradually ease. Knowing there's a peak — and that it gets better — helps you
> ride out the hardest stretch. Some crying you simply hold them through; it's not
> a sign you're doing anything wrong.
