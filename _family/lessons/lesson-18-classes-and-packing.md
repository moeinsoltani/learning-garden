# Lesson 18: Classes & Packing

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice. Follow your provider's and hospital's
> specific guidance on what to bring and when.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **prenatal / childbirth class** — a course teaching labour, birth, and newborn basics (sometimes "antenatal class").
> - **infant CPR** — emergency resuscitation for a baby; often taught in classes.
> - **hospital bag** — the packed bag you grab when labour starts.
> - **colostrum** (koh-LOSS-trum) — the first thick milk; a class will mention early feeding.
> - **maternity pad** — a large absorbent pad for the bleeding after birth.

## Concept

The last weeks are for **learning and packing**. **Prenatal classes** turn "I hope
I'll cope" into "I know roughly what happens and what to do," for *both* partners.
And a **packed hospital bag** by ~36 weeks means that when labour starts, you grab
it and go instead of scrambling. Neither is glamorous, but both hugely reduce
last-minute stress.

## Going Deeper

### Prenatal / childbirth classes

Usually taken in the third trimester, classes cover:

- The **stages of labour** and what to expect; coping and breathing techniques.
- **Pain-relief** options and how to make decisions in the moment.
- **The partner's role** — how to actively support, not just spectate.
- **Newborn basics:** feeding (including breastfeeding), diapering, soothing, and
  often **safe sleep** and **infant CPR/first aid**.

They're valuable for the birthing person *and* the partner — a prepared partner is
a far better support — and they're a great way to meet other expecting parents (the
start of your "village," Lesson 35).

### The hospital bag (pack by ~36 weeks)

| For the birthing parent | For the baby | For the partner |
|---|---|---|
| ID, health card, paperwork, birth plan | Going-home outfit + a spare | Snacks, water, phone + **charger** |
| Comfy loose clothes, robe, warm socks, slippers | A couple of sleepers/onesies | Change of clothes, toiletries |
| Toiletries, lip balm, hair tie | Swaddle/blanket | Cash/change, a pillow |
| **Maternity pads**, nursing bra | (Hospital usually provides diapers; check) | List of people to call/text |
| Going-home outfit (still bump-sized), snacks | **Car seat installed in the car** | Camera; patience and encouragement |

Keep the bag by the door. Install and check the **car seat** in advance — many
hospitals won't discharge you without a properly installed one (Lesson 19).

### The logistics plan

Sort the boring-but-vital details now: the **route to the hospital**, **who
drives**, a **backup driver**, and a plan for **pets or older children** while
you're away. Save the provider and hospital **numbers** in both phones.

{: .note }
> **Why the partner should take classes seriously**
> In labour, a calm, informed partner who knows the stages, comfort techniques,
> and the birth plan is worth an enormous amount. Classes transform the partner
> from an anxious bystander into an effective support person and advocate — one of
> the best gifts you can give the birthing parent.

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What do prenatal classes cover, and why should the partner attend too?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
They cover the <strong>stages of labour</strong>, coping/breathing techniques,
<strong>pain-relief</strong> options, the <strong>partner's supporting role</strong>,
and <strong>newborn basics</strong> (feeding, soothing, safe sleep, often infant
CPR). The partner should attend because a <strong>calm, informed partner</strong> is
a far more effective support and advocate during labour.
</details>

**Q2.** By when should the hospital bag be packed, and name two commonly forgotten
essentials.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
By about <strong>36 weeks</strong> (labour can start earlier than the due date). Two
commonly forgotten essentials: the <strong>phone charger</strong> and the
<strong>car seat installed in the car</strong> (often required before discharge).
</details>

**Q3.** What logistics should you sort before labour begins?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The <strong>route to hospital</strong>, <strong>who drives</strong> (plus a
<strong>backup driver</strong>), where the <strong>bag</strong> is, care for
<strong>pets or older children</strong>, and <strong>saved provider/hospital
numbers</strong> in both phones.
</details>

## Homework

Two tasks: **sign up for a prenatal class** (if you haven't) and **pack the hospital
bag** using the checklist, leaving it by the door. While you're at it, install the
**car seat** and confirm your labour-day logistics plan.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The win is tangible: a class booked, a bag packed and waiting, the car seat
installed, and a logistics plan agreed. These are the tasks that feel optional until
labour starts at 3 a.m. — doing them by ~36 weeks means the birth itself can be
about welcoming your baby, not last-minute panic.
</details>
