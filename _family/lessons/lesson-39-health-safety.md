# Lesson 39: Health, Safety & Baby-Proofing

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice. For any fever, illness, or injury that
> worries you, follow your provider — and re-read the newborn red flags in Lesson
> 30. **Never give aspirin to a baby.**

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **baby-proofing / childproofing** — making a home safe for a mobile baby.
> - **febrile** (FEB-rile) — relating to fever.
> - **dehydration** — not enough fluids (fewer wet diapers, dry mouth, lethargy).
> - **anchoring** — securing furniture/TVs to the wall so they can't tip onto a child.
> - **infant CPR / choking response** — emergency first aid for a baby; learn it in a class.
> - **poison control** — a helpline for suspected poisoning; save the number.

## Concept

As babies get mobile, two safety fronts open up: **health** (they catch a lot of
minor illnesses — normal — with a few real emergencies to recognise) and
**physical safety** (a crawling, grabbing, mouthing baby turns your home into an
obstacle course). This lesson equips you to **handle common illness calmly, know the
emergencies, learn basic first aid, and baby-proof** before your baby is on the
move.

## Going Deeper

### Common illness — normal, but know the red flags

Babies catch frequent **colds and viruses** (especially in childcare) — that's
normal immune-system training. Comfort measures, fluids, and rest handle most. But
**know when to act**:

- **Fever:** for a baby **under 3 months, a fever ≥38 °C (100.4 °F) is an emergency
  — go now** (Lesson 30). For older babies, fever is common with viruses; focus on
  how the baby *looks and acts*, keep fluids up, and ask your provider about
  suitable fever medicine and dose for the age. **Never give aspirin to a child.**
- **Watch for dehydration** (far fewer wet diapers, no tears, lethargy, dry mouth).
- **Emergencies** (recap): trouble breathing, very hard to wake/floppy, a
  **non-fading rash**, repeated/green vomiting, a seizure, or your gut saying
  something's badly wrong.
- **Keep vaccines on schedule** — they prevent serious diseases.

### Learn infant first aid (this is worth a class)

Take an **infant CPR and choking course**. Knowing the **choking response** for a
baby and basic CPR is one of the highest-value things new parents can do — you hope
never to use it, but it can save a life. Also save your local **poison control** and
emergency numbers.

### Baby-proofing (before they're mobile)

A baby goes from still to everywhere fast — proof the home *ahead* of crawling:

- **Anchor furniture and TVs** to the wall (tip-overs are a leading injury) and pad
  sharp corners.
- **Cover electrical outlets**, and keep **cords** (appliance and **blind/curtain
  cords** — a strangulation risk) out of reach.
- **Gate stairs** (top and bottom) and block unsafe rooms.
- **Lock away** cleaning products, medicines, and chemicals; keep small objects,
  button batteries, and magnets away (choking/serious harm).
- **Kitchen/bath:** turn pot handles inward, keep **hot drinks** away from the edge,
  set the water heater to a safe temp, and **never leave a baby alone near water**
  (baths, buckets, pools) — babies can drown in very little water.
- **Car seat:** keep them **rear-facing as long as possible** per the seat's limits.

{: .note }
> **Do the "baby's-eye view" crawl**
> Before your baby is mobile, get down on your hands and knees and crawl through
> each room. You'll spot the hazards a standing adult misses — dangling cords,
> reachable outlets, wobbly furniture, small objects, gaps at the stairs. Fix what
> you find. It's the fastest, cheapest baby-proofing audit there is.

## Lab — Plan Together

**Exercise 1 — call, emergency, or home care?** Sort: (a) a 6-month-old with a mild
cold and a low fever, feeding and playing fairly normally; (b) a 2-month-old with a
temperature of 38.3 °C; (c) a baby struggling to breathe with a non-fading rash;
(d) a baby with a runny nose but otherwise happy.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) <strong>Home care / call if worried</strong> — a common viral cold; comfort,
fluids, watch how they act. (b) <strong>Emergency — go now</strong>: fever ≥38 °C in
a baby under 3 months. (c) <strong>Emergency — go now</strong>: breathing trouble +
non-fading rash. (d) <strong>Home care</strong> — a happy baby with a runny nose is
usually fine. Two are true emergencies (young-baby fever; breathing/rash); the others
are ordinary illness.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — baby-proofing audit.** Do the "baby's-eye view" crawl in your mind
for a living room and kitchen. List five hazards you'd fix.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Strong finds: <strong>anchor the TV/bookshelf</strong>; <strong>cover outlets</strong>
and tuck away <strong>cords</strong> (including blind cords); <strong>gate the
stairs</strong>; <strong>lock away chemicals/medicines</strong> and remove small
