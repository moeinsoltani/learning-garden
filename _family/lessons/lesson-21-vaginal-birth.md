# Lesson 21: Vaginal Birth

{: .important }
> General education, not medical advice. Your birth team guides every step in the
> moment — this lesson just removes the fear of the unknown.

{: .note }
> **Words to know**
> - **second stage** — the pushing phase, ending with the baby's birth.
> - **crowning** — when the baby's head becomes visible and stays visible at the opening.
> - **perineum** (pair-ih-NEE-um) — the area between the vagina and anus, which stretches during birth.
> - **tear / episiotomy** (eh-piz-ee-OT-oh-mee) — a natural tear, or a small cut, sometimes needed to help the baby out; repaired afterward.
> - **assisted delivery** — using **forceps** or a **vacuum (ventouse)** to help deliver the baby.
> - **crowning-to-birth** — the final moments as the head, then body, are born.

## Concept

A vaginal birth is the body doing what it's built to do, with a skilled team
supporting. It follows the stages from Lesson 20, with the dramatic part —
**pushing and the baby's arrival** — in the **second stage**. Every birth is
different in length and detail, but the shape is predictable, and knowing it helps
both partners stay calm and work *with* the process.

## Going Deeper

### The pushing stage

Once the cervix is fully open (~10 cm), the urge to push arrives. The birthing
person pushes with contractions, often trying different **positions** (upright,
side-lying, hands-and-knees, squatting) — being mobile can help. The team coaches
the timing. The head **crowns** (becomes visible), there's often a stinging,
stretching sensation, and then — with a few more pushes — the head, then the body,
are born. First births usually take longer at this stage than later ones.

### Tears, and a little help if needed

The **perineum** stretches a lot; small **tears** are common and are stitched and
heal well, and occasionally a small cut (**episiotomy**) is made to help. If the
baby needs help through the final stretch, an **assisted delivery** with **forceps**
or a **vacuum (ventouse)** may be used. None of this means anything went "wrong" —
they're normal tools to bring the baby safely into the world.

### Right after

The baby is usually placed straight onto the mother's chest for **skin-to-skin**
(Lesson 23), the cord is clamped and cut (often after a short delay), and the
**placenta** is delivered (third stage). The team checks the baby and the mother.
The overwhelming feeling most describe is relief and awe.

### The partner's role

You are not a spectator. Practical support: offer **calm encouragement**, help with
**breathing and positions**, provide **physical comfort** (counter-pressure on the
back, a cool cloth, sips of water), **advocate** for the birth plan while staying
flexible, and simply **be present and steady**. A grounded partner genuinely changes
the experience.

{: .note }
> **Every birth is different**
> Length, sensations, positions, and details vary hugely — there's no single "right"
> way it should go. Tears, needing help with forceps or a vacuum, changing your pain
> plan mid-way: all common and normal. Trust your team, and remember the goal is a
> healthy mother and baby, however the birth unfolds.

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What happens in the second stage of labour?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The cervix is fully open, the birthing person <strong>pushes with contractions</strong>
(often trying different positions), the baby's head <strong>crowns</strong> (becomes
visible), and then the head and body are <strong>born</strong>. It ends with the
baby's birth (the placenta follows in the third stage).
</details>

**Q2.** Are tears or an assisted delivery (forceps/vacuum) signs something went
wrong?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
No. Small <strong>perineal tears</strong> are common and heal well after stitching,
and <strong>forceps or a vacuum</strong> are normal tools to help the baby safely
through the final stretch when needed. They're routine parts of birth, not failures.
</details>

**Q3.** Give two ways the partner actively supports during birth.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Any two: <strong>calm encouragement</strong>; helping with <strong>breathing and
positions</strong>; <strong>physical comfort</strong> (back counter-pressure, cool
cloth, water); and <strong>advocating</strong> for her wishes while staying flexible.
Steady presence matters a great deal.
</details>
