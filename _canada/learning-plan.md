# Canada: History & Civics Learning Plan

A curriculum for understanding Canada as a whole country: its history from the
First Peoples to today, its geography, its institutions of government, its
economy, its civic life, and the question of what "Canadian identity" actually
means. The track is built so that by the end you can follow Canadian news,
understand how a federal election or a Supreme Court ruling works, place any
province or historical event in context, and hold your own in a conversation
about Quebec, reconciliation, or the Canada–US relationship.

**Lab format:** this track has no terminal. Each lab is a **source & scenario
exercise** — you work with material included right in the lesson: a short
primary-source excerpt (a treaty clause, a Charter section, a famous speech),
a map or data table, or a realistic scenario ("explain to a newcomer friend why
Quebec has a civil code", "you receive a jury summons — what happens next?").
You write your answer in a `**Your answer:**` field, then reveal a hidden model
answer with the reasoning. Checkpoints double as **citizenship-test-style
questions** — short, factual, and cumulative — so the track also serves anyone
preparing for the Canadian citizenship test.

**Core references** (cited throughout the Further Reading tables):
*Discover Canada* (the official citizenship study guide), The Canadian
Encyclopedia, the official Parliament of Canada and Government of Canada sites,
the Truth and Reconciliation Commission reports, and Wikipedia for deeper
dives.

## Phase 1: The Land — Geography

Where things are, before what happened there. Every later phase leans on this
mental map.

### Lesson 1: Canada at a glance
**Goal:** Hold the whole country in your head: how big, who lives there, what the pieces are.
**Topics:** Second-largest country on Earth; ten provinces and three territories with capitals; the three oceans; the world's longest land border; ~40 million people; Ottawa vs Toronto (capital vs largest city).
**Lab:** Blank-map drill — place all 13 provinces/territories and their capitals; then a scenario: a friend thinks Toronto is the capital — write your two-sentence correction with the *why*.

### Lesson 2: Physical regions
**Goal:** See Canada as seven physical regions, not one undifferentiated landmass.
**Topics:** Canadian Shield, Great Lakes–St. Lawrence lowlands, Appalachians, Interior Plains (Prairies), Western Cordillera, Arctic, Hudson Bay lowlands; why each region shaped what humans could do there.
**Lab:** Match each region to its economic consequence (Shield → mining, Prairies → wheat…), then explain why almost nobody lives in the region that covers half the country.

### Lesson 3: Climate and the North
**Goal:** Understand the climate bands and why "the North" is a distinct idea in Canadian life.
**Topics:** Climate zones from Windsor to Alert; permafrost; the treeline; the territories vs "northern parts of provinces"; Arctic sovereignty; climate change hitting the North fastest.
**Lab:** Rank five cities by January temperature (the answers surprise most people), then a scenario on why Canada cares so much about Arctic sovereignty.

### Lesson 4: Where the people are
**Goal:** Grasp the single most important geographic fact: Canadians live in a thin ribbon.
**Topics:** 80%+ within ~160 km of the US border; the Quebec City–Windsor corridor; major metros (Toronto, Montreal, Vancouver, Calgary, Edmonton, Ottawa); urban vs rural; why distance shapes politics, prices, and identity.
**Lab:** A population-density data table — find the pattern, then explain to a European friend why a "short domestic flight" in Canada is four hours.

### Lesson 5: Natural resources
**Goal:** Connect the map to the wealth: what comes out of the ground, water, and forest, and where.
**Topics:** Oil sands (Alberta), hydro (Quebec, BC, Manitoba), potash (Saskatchewan), forestry (BC), fisheries (Atlantic and Pacific), mining in the Shield and North; "hewers of wood and drawers of water" as a national anxiety.
**Lab:** Match resource → province → dependent city; scenario: why a change in oil prices moves Alberta's budget more than Ontario's.

## Phase 2: First Peoples & the Colonial Era

The deep history: who was here, who arrived, and the two empires whose collision
still structures the country.

### Lesson 6: The First Peoples
**Goal:** Know who lived here for millennia before contact — as distinct nations, not a single group.
**Topics:** First Nations, Inuit, and Métis as three distinct groups; major cultural areas (Pacific coast, Plains, Eastern Woodlands, Arctic); the Haudenosaunee Confederacy; oral tradition, trade networks, and governance before contact.
**Lab:** A primary-source excerpt on the Haudenosaunee Great Law of Peace; scenario: a colleague says "Indigenous people" as if it's one culture — write a gentle, accurate correction.

### Lesson 7: European contact
**Goal:** Sort out who "found" what, when, and what contact actually meant.
**Topics:** Norse at L'Anse aux Meadows (~1000 CE); Cabot (1497) and the cod fishery; Cartier (1534) and "Kanata"; early exchange, alliance, and epidemic disease; why "discovery" is the wrong word.
**Lab:** Timeline-ordering drill; then analyze Cartier's own journal excerpt about erecting the cross at Gaspé — what did each side think was happening?

### Lesson 8: New France
**Goal:** Understand the French society on the St. Lawrence whose descendants are a quarter of Canada today.
**Topics:** Champlain and Quebec (1608); the seigneurial system; habitants, coureurs des bois, and the Catholic Church; the Filles du Roi; alliances with the Wendat and wars with the Haudenosaunee; Acadia.
**Lab:** Scenario: explain why French is spoken in North America at all, in five sentences, to someone who knows nothing; source work on a seigneurial land contract.

### Lesson 9: The fur trade
**Goal:** See the fur trade as the economic engine — and the first genuinely joint Indigenous-European enterprise — that mapped the continent.
**Topics:** The beaver hat economy; Hudson's Bay Company (1670) vs the North West Company; voyageurs and canoe routes; Indigenous peoples as partners, provisioners, and power brokers; the birth of the Métis Nation.
**Lab:** Trace a fur's journey from trapline to London on a route map; scenario: why did the HBC "own" a third of the continent, and what did ownership even mean?

### Lesson 10: The Conquest and its bargain
**Goal:** Understand the 1759–1774 hinge: Britain wins, but French Canada survives — and that bargain defines Canada.
**Topics:** Seven Years' War; the Plains of Abraham (1759); the Royal Proclamation of 1763 (also the foundation of Indigenous land rights); the Quebec Act of 1774 — keeping French civil law, Catholic religion, seigneurial tenure; why Britain accommodated instead of assimilating.
**Lab:** Primary source: the Royal Proclamation's Indigenous-lands clause — read it and state what it promises; scenario: connect the Quebec Act directly to something visible in Canada today.

### Lesson 11: Loyalists and the War of 1812
**Goal:** See how the American Revolution created English Canada, and 1812 kept it separate.
**Topics:** ~50,000 Loyalist refugees; Black Loyalists in Nova Scotia; the split into Upper and Lower Canada (1791); the War of 1812 — Brock, Tecumseh, Laura Secord, the burning of York and Washington; "not American" as a founding identity.
**Lab:** Scenario: an American friend says Canada is "basically the US" — use 1776 and 1812 to explain, warmly, why Canadians bristle; map drill on Upper vs Lower Canada.

## Phase 3: Confederation & Expansion

How four colonies became a transcontinental country — and at whose expense.

### Lesson 12: Rebellion and responsible government
**Goal:** Understand the 1830s–40s struggle that produced Canada's actual system of government.
**Topics:** The Family Compact and Château Clique; the 1837–38 rebellions (Mackenzie, Papineau); Lord Durham's Report and its two recommendations; responsible government won peacefully by 1848 (Baldwin, LaFontaine, Howe); why this matters more than any battle.
**Lab:** Define "responsible government" in one sentence a teenager would understand (harder than it sounds); source excerpt from Durham's Report — find the famous insult and the useful idea.

### Lesson 13: Confederation, 1867
**Goal:** Know why Confederation happened, who made it, and what the deal was.
**Topics:** The pressures: American threat, railway debt, political deadlock; Charlottetown and Quebec conferences; the British North America Act; Macdonald, Cartier, Brown; the original four provinces; federalism as the compromise that let French and English share a country; July 1 — Dominion Day to Canada Day.
**Lab:** Citizenship-test-style rapid round on Confederation facts; scenario: why did Quebec's leaders *want* a federal union rather than independence or the status quo?

### Lesson 14: The railway and the West
**Goal:** Follow the nation-building project — a railway to the Pacific and provinces strung along it.
**Topics:** Manitoba (1870), BC (1871, on a railway promise), PEI (1873); the Canadian Pacific Railway — the Pacific Scandal, the last spike (1885); Chinese railway workers and the head tax; the North-West Mounted Police; provinces to 1949 (Newfoundland) and 1999 (Nunavut).
**Lab:** Order the provinces by entry date from memory, then check; source: a head-tax certificate — write what it tells you about who built the railway and how they were treated.

### Lesson 15: Riel and the Métis resistances
**Goal:** Understand Louis Riel — traitor to some, father of Manitoba and hero to others — and why he still divides.
**Topics:** The Red River Resistance (1869–70) and the Manitoba Act; the North-West Resistance (1885); Riel's trial and execution; the fallout in Quebec vs Ontario; Riel's rehabilitation in modern memory.
**Lab:** Riel's final courtroom speech (excerpt) — what is he actually arguing?; scenario: write two one-paragraph obituaries for Riel, one as an 1885 Ontario paper, one as a Manitoba Métis community today.

### Lesson 16: Peopling the Prairies
**Goal:** See how immigration policy filled the West — and who it welcomed vs excluded.
**Topics:** Clifford Sifton's "stalwart peasants"; Ukrainian, German, Scandinavian settlement; the Dominion Lands Act; exclusion on the other hand — the head tax, the *Komagata Maru*, "None is too many"; the pattern: Canada's immigration generosity has always been selective.
**Lab:** Analyze a 1900s immigration poster (what it promises, what it hides); scenario: connect Sifton's policy to the surnames in any Prairie phone book today.

### Lesson 17: Treaties, the Indian Act, and residential schools
**Goal:** Face the other side of nation-building: the legal machinery applied to Indigenous peoples.
**Topics:** The Numbered Treaties (1871–1921) — two understandings of the same documents; the Indian Act (1876): status, reserves, bans on ceremony, enfranchisement; residential schools begin; the pass system; why "the treaties" are live legal documents, not history.
**Lab:** Read Treaty 6's text against a Cree oral account of the same negotiation — list the differences; checkpoint questions on what the Indian Act still governs today.

## Phase 4: The World Wars to Modern Canada

The century in which Canada became fully independent, fully urban, and had to
decide — twice — whether it would stay one country.

### Lesson 18: The First World War
**Goal:** Understand WWI as Canada's brutal coming-of-age.
**Topics:** Automatic entry via Britain, 1914; Ypres, the Somme, Passchendaele; Vimy Ridge (1917) and the "birth of a nation" story — and its critics; the conscription crisis of 1917 splitting French and English Canada; 60,000 dead from 8 million people; women's suffrage arriving alongside.
**Lab:** In Flanders Fields + a conscription-crisis editorial from a Quebec paper — hold both at once; citizenship-round on Vimy, Remembrance Day, the poppy.

### Lesson 19: Between the wars
**Goal:** Track the 1920s–30s: new autonomy, new rights, deep depression.
**Topics:** The Statute of Westminster (1931) — legislative independence; the Persons Case (1929) and the Famous Five; the Winnipeg General Strike; the Great Depression on the Prairies; On-to-Ottawa Trek; the birth of new parties (CCF, Social Credit).
**Lab:** The Persons Case scenario: reconstruct the argument that women were not "persons" — then demolish it as the Privy Council did; source photo analysis of a Depression-era relief camp.

### Lesson 20: The Second World War
**Goal:** Know Canada's WWII: enormous contribution abroad, hard shadows at home.
**Topics:** Canada declares war *itself* (a week after Britain — the point is the week); the Battle of the Atlantic; Dieppe; D-Day and Juno Beach; the liberation of the Netherlands (and the tulips); the home front and women in war industry; Japanese Canadian internment; the *St. Louis* and Jewish refugees.
**Lab:** Scenario: why do the Dutch send Ottawa tulips every year?; source work on an internment order — what was taken, and when was it apologized for?

### Lesson 21: The postwar boom
**Goal:** See the 1945–1970 transformation into the Canada recognizable today.
**Topics:** Newfoundland joins (1949); mass immigration and the suburbs; the St. Lawrence Seaway; Tommy Douglas, Saskatchewan, and the birth of medicare; Pearson: peacekeeping (Suez, 1956), the flag debate (1965), the Order of Canada; the Auto Pact; Expo 67.
**Lab:** Scenario: explain to an American why Canadians treat healthcare as identity, not just policy — using the Douglas story; rank the 1960s changes by how visible they are in daily life today.

### Lesson 22: Quebec — the Quiet Revolution to the referendums
**Goal:** Understand the Quebec question well enough to discuss it respectfully with anyone.
**Topics:** Duplessis and the Grande Noirceur; the Quiet Revolution (1960s): "Maîtres chez nous"; the FLQ and the October Crisis (1970); the Parti Québécois and Bill 101; the 1980 referendum (60–40) and the 1995 referendum (50.6–49.4); sovereignty today.
**Lab:** The 1995 referendum question, verbatim — is it clear? What would you have voted and why?; scenario: a Toronto colleague calls Bill 101 "language police nonsense" — steelman the Quebec view before giving your own.

### Lesson 23: The Constitution comes home
**Goal:** Understand 1982 — patriation, the Charter, and the wound that never closed.
**Topics:** Trudeau Sr. and patriation; the "kitchen accord" and the Night of the Long Knives (Quebec's view); the Constitution Act 1982 and the Charter of Rights and Freedoms; the amending formula; Meech Lake and Charlottetown failures; why Quebec has never signed — and why everything still works anyway.
**Lab:** Read Charter sections 1, 2, and 33 in the original — explain the architecture (rights, limits, override) in your own words; scenario: why can a constitution function when a quarter of the country never signed it?

### Lesson 24: Reconciliation and the Canada of today
**Goal:** Bring the story to the present: the reckoning with residential schools and the country's current shape.
**Topics:** The last residential school closes (1996); the 2008 apology; the Truth and Reconciliation Commission and its 94 Calls to Action; unmarked graves (2021) and Orange Shirt Day; UNDRIP; modern treaties and self-government (Nunavut, Nisga'a); Canada's demographics today — one in four born abroad.
**Lab:** Read three of the 94 Calls to Action and assess their status honestly; scenario: a newcomer asks "should I feel guilty about this history?" — write the answer that is honest about the past and clear about responsibility vs guilt.

## Phase 5: Government & Institutions

How the machine actually works — the phase that turns news from noise into
signal.

### Lesson 25: The Crown and the constitutional monarchy
**Goal:** Understand why Canada has a King, what he actually does (almost nothing), and why it works.
**Topics:** Constitutional monarchy vs absolute; the King, the Governor General, and lieutenant governors; reserve powers and the King–Byng affair; royal assent as ceremony; the republican debate and why change is constitutionally near-impossible.
**Lab:** Scenario: an American asks "so the King can overrule your Parliament?" — give the accurate answer; sort a list of powers into "theoretical" vs "actually exercised".

### Lesson 26: Parliament — Commons and Senate
**Goal:** Know the two chambers and follow a bill from idea to law.
**Topics:** The House of Commons: 343 MPs, ridings, Question Period; the Senate: appointed, "sober second thought", its real role; how a bill becomes law (readings, committee, both chambers, royal assent); Hansard; the Speaker.
**Lab:** Trace a real recent bill through LEGISinfo's stages; scenario: your MP votes against their party — what happens to them, and is that good or bad for democracy?

### Lesson 27: The Prime Minister and Cabinet
**Goal:** See where power really sits: confidence, cabinet, and the PMO.
**Topics:** The PM is not elected as PM — confidence of the House; forming government; majority vs minority governments; cabinet and cabinet solidarity; the PMO's growth; how governments fall (confidence votes) and prorogation controversies.
**Lab:** Scenario walk-through: election night produces a hung parliament — who becomes PM? Work the rules, not the intuition; citizenship-round on PM basics.

### Lesson 28: Elections and voting
**Goal:** Understand how Canadians vote and what first-past-the-post does to the results.
**Topics:** Ridings and FPTP; the major parties and their ideological range; Elections Canada (non-partisan by design); fixed-date elections that aren't; how seats can diverge from vote share; the eternal electoral-reform debate; who can vote and how.
**Lab:** Given real vote-share numbers, compute who wins under FPTP vs proportional — feel the difference; scenario: it's your first federal election as a voter — walk through registration to ballot.

### Lesson 29: Federalism — who does what
**Goal:** Know which government to blame (or thank) for any given issue.
**Topics:** Sections 91/92 — federal vs provincial powers; healthcare and education are *provincial* (the perennial surprise); shared and contested areas (environment, immigration); equalization payments and the fights about them; municipalities as "creatures of the provinces"; the territories' different status.
**Lab:** Sort 15 real issues (passports, potholes, hospitals, daycare, pipelines…) to the right level of government; scenario: why is the PM blamed for healthcare he doesn't run?

### Lesson 30: Courts and the law
**Goal:** Understand the court system and Canada's two legal traditions.
**Topics:** The court pyramid up to the Supreme Court; common law everywhere except Quebec's civil code (the Quebec Act, still echoing); how judges are appointed (no elections, minimal circus); the presumption of innocence; Indigenous law as a third tradition gaining recognition.
**Lab:** Scenario: explain to a friend from the US why Canadian judicial appointments are boring — and why that's the point; map a dispute from small claims to the SCC.

### Lesson 31: The Charter in action
**Goal:** Know your rights — and the two clauses (1 and 33) that make Canadian rights different.
**Topics:** Fundamental freedoms, democratic, mobility, legal, and equality rights; language rights; Section 1 (reasonable limits) and the Oakes test in plain words; Section 33 — the notwithstanding clause, its history and its recent normalization; landmark cases in one line each.
**Lab:** Apply Section 1 reasoning to a scenario (a law limits speech — walk the test); source: read Section 33 and answer honestly whether you find it reassuring or alarming.

## Phase 6: Civic Life

Citizenship as it's actually lived: rights, duties, services, and money.

### Lesson 32: Citizenship — rights and responsibilities
**Goal:** Know what citizenship formally means and how it's acquired.
**Topics:** Rights and responsibilities of citizens (Discover Canada's list, annotated honestly); birthright and naturalization; the citizenship test and oath; dual citizenship; permanent residence vs citizenship — what differs in practice.
**Lab:** A full citizenship-test-style mock round (20 questions) drawing on Phases 1–5; scenario: the oath includes the King — a friend objects; explain what the oath is actually promising.

### Lesson 33: The justice system in practice
**Goal:** Know what happens when the law meets daily life.
**Topics:** Police (RCMP's odd double role, provincial and municipal forces); your rights on arrest; criminal vs civil; jury duty; legal aid; small claims court as the system most people actually touch.
**Lab:** Scenario: you receive a jury summons — walk through what happens, what excuses count, and why serving matters; a "rights on arrest" quick-drill.

### Lesson 34: The social safety net
**Goal:** Map the programs Canadians lean on from birth to retirement.
**Topics:** Medicare — what's covered and what isn't (drugs, dental, eyes: the gaps); provincial delivery, federal money (the Canada Health Act's five principles); EI; CPP/OAS/GIS; the Canada Child Benefit; public education and student aid; how it compares to the US and Europe (neither caricature is right).
**Lab:** Scenario set: a job loss, a birth, and a retirement — name the program that catches each; explain the difference between "free healthcare" and what Canada actually has.

### Lesson 35: Taxes and public money
**Goal:** Understand where the money comes from and where it goes — without ideology.
**Topics:** Income tax (progressive brackets — marginal rates explained properly), GST/HST, provincial taxes; the annual tax return ritual; RRSP and TFSA in one paragraph each; federal budget basics; equalization revisited with numbers.
**Lab:** Compute the actual tax on a given salary using real brackets (killing the "raise pushed me into a higher bracket so I earn less" myth); scenario: a newcomer's first tax season — what do they need to know?

### Lesson 36: Taking part
**Goal:** Turn civic knowledge into civic participation.
**Topics:** Voting at three levels; contacting your MP/MPP/councillor (it works more than people think); public consultations; volunteering (Canada's high-volunteering culture); protests and their legal protection; jury duty and census as duties.
**Lab:** Find your actual three representatives (federal, provincial, municipal) and one live issue each is handling; scenario: something in your city annoys you — write the email to the right official, to the right level of government.

## Phase 7: The Economy

What Canada does for a living, and why the US relationship dominates
everything.

### Lesson 37: The economy at a glance
**Goal:** Hold the shape of the Canadian economy in your head.
**Topics:** A G7 economy of ~40M people; services vs resources vs manufacturing (the real proportions); regional economies — Alberta ≠ Ontario ≠ Newfoundland; the branch-plant legacy; productivity anxiety as the perennial national worry.
**Lab:** A GDP-by-sector table — find three things that contradict Canada's own self-image; scenario: why does a country this rich constantly worry about productivity?

### Lesson 38: The elephant next door
**Goal:** Understand the Canada–US economic relationship — the single most important fact about Canada's economy.
**Topics:** ~75% of exports go to one customer; Free Trade (1988 — an election fought over it), NAFTA, USMCA/CUSMA; supply management (dairy) as the perpetual irritant; softwood lumber, the forever war; energy exports; "sleeping next to an elephant" (Trudeau Sr.'s line) as economic reality.
**Lab:** Scenario: a US tariff hits one Canadian industry — trace the consequences to a specific town; source: the actual "elephant" quote in context.

### Lesson 39: What Canada makes and sells
**Goal:** Know the flagship industries and where they live.
**Topics:** Energy (oil sands, hydro, the pipeline wars); mining and critical minerals; agriculture (wheat, canola, supply-managed dairy); autos in Ontario; aerospace in Montreal; banks; tech corridors (Toronto–Waterloo, Montreal AI); film and games; whatever happened to Nortel and BlackBerry (a cautionary tale Canadians retell).
**Lab:** Match ten companies/industries to their home regions; scenario: why do pipelines produce Canada's fiercest political fights — name all three governments involved and each one's stake.

### Lesson 40: Money and banking
**Goal:** Understand the loonie, the Bank of Canada, and the banking system's deliberate boringness.
**Topics:** The Bank of Canada: inflation targeting, rate announcements and why the news cares; the Big Five banks and why Canada skipped the 2008 banking collapse; the loonie as a petro-currency (why it tracks oil); mortgages and the eternal housing conversation.
**Lab:** Read a real Bank of Canada rate announcement and translate it to plain English; scenario: oil prices jump — walk through what happens to the dollar and who wins/loses.

### Lesson 41: Working in Canada
**Goal:** Understand the labour market a newcomer or citizen actually navigates.
**Topics:** Employment standards vs the US model (vacation, parental leave, notice); unions' historical role (Winnipeg 1919 echo) and current reality; the immigration–labour pipeline (Express Entry, credential recognition and its frustrations); minimum wages by province; the "Canadian experience" catch-22.
**Lab:** Scenario: a newly arrived engineer can't get their credentials recognized — explain the system honestly and name the workarounds; compare a job offer's terms against employment-standards minimums.

## Phase 8: Culture & Identity

The capstone question: what, if anything, is a Canadian?

### Lesson 42: Two languages
**Goal:** Understand official bilingualism — the policy, the reality, the point.
**Topics:** The Official Languages Act (1969); what bilingualism actually requires (federal services, not individuals); francophones outside Quebec (Acadians, Franco-Ontarians, St. Boniface); anglophones in Quebec; French immersion as an English-Canadian institution; is bilingualism a success? (an honest scorecard).
**Lab:** Scenario: "Why do my cereal boxes have French on them?" — answer with the history and the principle; map the francophone communities outside Quebec.

### Lesson 43: Multiculturalism
**Goal:** Understand Canada's official multiculturalism — the world's first — and the debates around it.
**Topics:** The 1971 policy and the 1988 Act; mosaic vs melting pot (and how much that contrast is real); points-based immigration since 1967; today's demographics (Toronto and Vancouver among the most diverse cities on Earth); the critiques from left, right, and Quebec (interculturalism); does it work? — the evidence.
**Lab:** Scenario: defend and critique multiculturalism in two honest paragraphs each — no strawmen; analyze one census chart of Canada's changing origins.

### Lesson 44: Indigenous Canada today
**Goal:** Complete the arc from Phase 2: Indigenous peoples as present-tense nations, not historical chapters.
**Topics:** 1.8M+ people, the fastest-growing population; the resurgence — language revitalization, art (Kent Monkman, Tanya Tagaq), literature; economic power (development corporations, resource partnerships); land acknowledgements — origin and debate; self-government in practice (Nunavut at 25); what "unceded" means in Vancouver.
**Lab:** Read your local land acknowledgement and research what it actually refers to; scenario: a visitor asks whether land acknowledgements are meaningful or performative — give the honest both-sides answer and your own view.

### Lesson 45: Symbols, holidays, and rituals
**Goal:** Decode the national furniture — flag, anthem, beaver, and the calendar.
**Topics:** The flag (only since 1965 — and the debate was ferocious); O Canada in two languages (the lyrics differ!); the beaver, the loon, the maple leaf; national holidays and what each marks (Canada Day, Victoria Day, St-Jean-Baptiste, Truth and Reconciliation Day, Remembrance Day); Mounties as symbol vs reality; Tim Hortons as folk religion and its ironies.
**Lab:** Citizenship-round on symbols and holidays; scenario: plan the "most Canadian day possible" and justify each element — then note which choices a Québécois or Indigenous friend might make differently.

### Lesson 46: Arts, sports, and the shared culture
**Goal:** Know the cultural touchstones that come up in any Canadian conversation.
**Topics:** Hockey — 1972 Summit Series, Gretzky, the Habs–Leafs divide, women's Olympic dominance; the CBC/Radio-Canada institution; CanCon rules (why the radio plays The Tragically Hip); literature (Atwood, Munro's Nobel, Richler); music from Cohen and Joni to Drake and Céline; comedy as a Canadian export; "sorry", toques, and the vocabulary.
**Lab:** A cultural-literacy match-up (name → why they matter); scenario: your team's Slack goes silent during a Canada–US Olympic hockey final — explain to a confused colleague abroad what is happening and why it matters this much.

### Lesson 47: What is a Canadian? (capstone)
**Goal:** Synthesize the whole track into your own answer to the identity question.
**Topics:** The classic candidates: "not American", the mosaic, peace-order-good-government vs life-liberty-happiness, survivance (Quebec), the North, politeness-as-myth; regional identities (Newfoundland, Alberta, BC, the North) as counterweights; hyphenated Canadians; the honest answer: a country held together by argument, accommodation, and choice.
**Lab:** Capstone essay: "What is a Canadian?" — 500 words, drawing on at least five earlier lessons, written *before* revealing the model essay; final cumulative citizenship-style test covering all eight phases.

## Phase 9: Living Canadian

The post-capstone phase: the culture you'd have absorbed by growing up there —
written for someone not born or raised in Canada. The exam first (Lesson 47),
then the life.

### Lesson 48: Growing up Canadian
**Goal:** Borrow the shared childhood — school, seasons, and rituals — so native reminiscence lands.
**Topics:** Public school's texture (grade 9 not 9th grade, no uniforms, pizza day, the anthem mumble); the annual rituals (Terry Fox Run, Remembrance assemblies, snow days); French immersion and the Catholic-board bargain; summer as sacred (cottage/cabin/camp, day camps, provincial parks); safety-net childhood; what differs from the US movie version.
**Lab:** Decode a colleague's childhood reminiscence line by line; advise a newcomer parent on school choice; build the month-by-month childhood year for Winnipeg vs Vancouver.

### Lesson 49: The unwritten rules
**Goal:** Run the Canadian social operating system — the politeness stack and its kernel.
**Topics:** The sorry economy (punctuation, not confession); thank the bus driver, the yield-wave, queue theology, shoes off; understatement decoded ("not bad" = great, "small concerns" = objections, "yeah, no" = no); small-talk topics and taboos; the limits of nice (slow friendship, passive-aggressive shadow, politeness ≠ agreement).
**Lab:** Translate a triple-softened meeting objection; plan a dinner-party evening without fouls; diagnose and repair a bluntness incident with a withdrawn colleague.

### Lesson 50: Everyday life
**Goal:** Master the errand layer — money, measurements, and retail nationalism.
**Topics:** Tax added at the till (GST/HST/PST by province); tipping norms; loonie/toonie, the dead penny, e-transfer; the metric-imperial mash-up and its rule; Tim's/Canadian Tire/the Bay/LCBO field guide; bag milk; "hydro"; health card first; winter-car liturgy.
**Lab:** Receipt forensics on an $18 burger across provinces; the newcomer's ten first purchases; write natural sentences in the correct measurement system per domain.

### Lesson 51: Winter as a way of life
**Goal:** Convert winter from siege to season — the physical curriculum and the emotional calendar.
**Topics:** Layering, windchill grammar, winter driving, the shoveling covenant and the windrow; the emotional year (November greying, the February wall, false spring, patio-season mania); played winter vs endured winter (rinks, hills, carnivals, the backyard rink); honest footnotes (SAD, the March escape, Vancouver's asterisk, the North's magnitude).
**Lab:** Read an Environment Canada warning operationally; write the February rescue text to a struggling newcomer; decode four winter rituals (the push, April shorts, the backyard rink, the windrow curse).

### Lesson 52: Food and drink
**Goal:** Hold both true answers to "what is Canadian food?" — the folk canon and the multicultural table.
**Topics:** Poutine (and its custody), butter tarts and the raisin war, Nanaimo bars, maple and the sugar shack, KD, the Caesar; the regional table from Jiggs' dinner to donair to perogies; the immigrant table as the actual table; potluck/BBQ/double-double as social technologies; drinking culture and provincial retail.
**Lab:** Build a 48-hour food tour holding both answers; potluck strategy as cultural capital; decode four food fights (raisins, poutine authenticity, Montreal bagels, "Canadian bacon").

### Lesson 53: Hockey nation
**Goal:** Install the ambient religion — pipeline, scripture, vocabulary, and honest footnotes.
**Topics:** Timbits-to-shinny pipeline, road hockey's "CAR!" protocol; the sacred moments (Summit Series '72, Gretzky, the 2002 loonie, the 2010 Golden Goal, the women's dynasty, "no Cup since '93," the Leafs); HNIC liturgy and the word kit; cost, scandals, and the Raptors' new belonging; Terry Fox atop the pantheon.
**Lab:** Read a playoff-season office and script newcomer participation; explain the Golden Goal's tears to a colleague abroad; run Lesson 45's symbol audit on hockey itself.

### Lesson 54: Speaking Canadian
**Goal:** Speak the dialect and the references — the phase's graduation.
**Topics:** The lexicon (toque, hydro, klick, two-four, keener, bunny hug…); the spelling truce (British ink, American stuff) and "aboot"; "eh" engineered; the reference canon (Heritage Minutes, Mr. Dressup, Log Driver's Waltz, The Hockey Sweater, the Tragically Hip, Bob & Doug); deployment etiquette and the earned-teasing rule.
**Lab:** Translate a fluent-Canadian paragraph; draft the en-CA style-guide memo; triage the reference canon into a weekend syllabus (and name what you must never fake).

## How the phases fit together

```
Phase 1  GEOGRAPHY      the stage
Phase 2  FIRST PEOPLES  ┐
Phase 3  CONFEDERATION  ├─ the story (chronological)
Phase 4  MODERN CANADA  ┘
Phase 5  GOVERNMENT     ┐
Phase 6  CIVIC LIFE     ├─ the systems (how it works now)
Phase 7  ECONOMY        ┘
Phase 8  IDENTITY       the synthesis
Phase 9  LIVING IT      the lived culture (post-capstone)
```

History (Phases 2–4) is taught chronologically and *before* institutions
(Phase 5), because every Canadian institution — the monarchy, federalism,
bilingualism, the Charter's notwithstanding clause — is an artifact of a
historical bargain, and it only makes sense once you know the bargain. Phase 8
deliberately comes last: the identity question is unanswerable until you've
seen the geography, the history, and the machinery.

Each phase ends with a written test covering all its lessons, and Lesson 47
closes with a full cumulative test in the style of the citizenship exam.
Phase 9 comes after the exam on purpose: Phases 1–8 teach Canada as a subject;
Phase 9 (lessons 48–54) teaches it as a life — the childhood, the unwritten
rules, the errands, the winter, the food, the hockey, and the dialect that
someone raised there absorbs without noticing.
