# Lesson 52: Food and Drink

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **canon** (KAN-un) — the agreed list of classics everyone is expected to know.
> - **curds** (KURDZ) — fresh lumps of young cheese; the real thing squeaks when bitten.
> - **poutine** (poo-TEEN; Quebec French: poo-TSIN) — fries + curds + gravy.
> - **casse-croûte** (kass-KROOT) — a Quebec roadside snack shack.
> - **tourtière** (toor-tee-AIR) — Quebec's spiced meat pie; **réveillon** (ray-vay-YON) — the festive Christmas Eve midnight meal.
> - **tire d'érable** (teer day-RAHBL) — hot maple syrup poured on snow and rolled onto a stick.
> - **donair** (doh-NAIR) — Halifax's sweet-sauced spiced meat wrap.
> - **bannock** (BAN-uk) — pan-fried flat bread, a staple across Indigenous communities.
> - **Nanaimo bar** (nuh-NY-moh) — the no-bake chocolate-custard-coconut square from Nanaimo, BC.
> - **perogies** (peh-ROH-geez) — Ukrainian filled dumplings (Lesson 16's settlers, edible).
> - **peameal bacon** (PEE-meel) — cornmeal-rolled back bacon (never say "Canadian bacon" here).
> - **Clamato** (klah-MAH-toh) — clam-tomato juice, the Caesar's engine; **icewine** — sweet wine from grapes picked frozen (Niagara's specialty).
> - Names: **Schwartz's** (SHWORTS-iz — Montreal's famous smoked-meat deli), **Jiggs' dinner** (JIGZ — Newfoundland's salt-beef boil), **toutons** (TOW-tunz — Newfoundland fried dough).

## Concept

Ask "what is Canadian food?" and natives will laugh, then get
defensive, then produce two true answers at once: a short canon of
beloved folk dishes (poutine, butter tarts, maple everything) and
the real everyday answer — **the multicultural table** (L.43 as
dinner): shawarma in Ottawa, pho and sushi in Vancouver, dim sum in
Markham, roti in Scarborough, doubles in Brampton. A raised-in-
Canada palate holds both without contradiction: Kraft Dinner
nostalgia *and* the conviction that the best food in town is
whatever arrived most recently.

![A classic poutine: fries, cheese curds, and gravy](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Poutine.JPG/960px-Poutine.JPG)

*The national gravity well: poutine — fries, squeaky cheese curds,
hot gravy. Born in 1950s rural Quebec, mocked for decades, now on
every menu in the country including the parliamentary restaurant.
Cultural note: the curds must squeak; Quebec judges all outside
versions and is right to. Photo: [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Poutine.JPG).*

The deep structure: Canadian food identity is **regional folk
canon + immigrant layering + maple-flavoured self-mythology**, all
administered through the national social technologies of the
potluck, the barbecue, and the Tim Hortons run (L.45/50).

## How It Works

### The folk canon (know these on sight)

- **Poutine** — above; order it late-night or at a "casse-croûte"
  (Quebec snack shack) for the authentic experience; "loaded"
  variants are tolerated, curd-substitution is not.
- **Butter tarts** — small pastry shells of sugar-butter filling;
  Ontario's sacred bake-sale item; the raisins-or-no-raisins war
  is real, generational, and safe small talk (L.49).
- **Nanaimo bars** — no-bake chocolate/custard/coconut layered
  square from Nanaimo, BC; every funeral reception and church
  basement (L.48's bake-sale economy).
- **Maple syrup** — Quebec produces ~70% of the world supply
  (L.39), a strategic reserve exists (and was famously robbed —
  the Great Maple Syrup Heist is beloved trivia); folk knowledge:
  real syrup only, grades matter ("amber" vs "dark"), and
  **tire d'érable** — hot syrup poured on snow, rolled on a
  stick — is the sugar-shack (cabane à sucre) spring ritual
  (L.48's field trip, tasted).
- **Kraft Dinner ("KD")** — boxed mac-and-cheese; Canada eats
  more per capita than anyone; student food elevated to
  self-aware national dish (The Barenaked Ladies wrote it into
  "If I Had $1000000" — L.54's canon).
- **The Caesar** — Canada's cocktail: vodka, Clamato (clam-
  tomato juice), Worcestershire, hot sauce, celery-salt rim;
  brunch default; Americans find the clam part alarming, which
  Canadians enjoy.
- **BeaverTails** — flat fried-dough pastries (canal skating
  food — L.51); **Timbits** (L.50); the **butter-tart–adjacent**
  date square; **ketchup chips** and **all-dressed chips** (the
  chip aisle is genuinely nationalist territory).

### The regional table (L.02's regions, plated)

| Region | The dishes locals defend |
|---|---|
| Newfoundland | Jiggs' dinner (salt beef boil), toutons, fish & brewis; "scoff" = feast (L.54) |
| Maritimes | lobster rolls, Digby scallops, hodge podge; Halifax's **donair** (sweet-sauced kebab, official city food) |
| Quebec | tourtière (meat pie, Christmas Eve/réveillon), smoked meat (Schwartz's), Montreal bagels (wood-fired, sweeter — sold hot at 2 a.m.), sugar-shack everything |
| Ontario | butter tarts, peameal bacon sandwich (St. Lawrence Market), Ottawa's shawarma density |
| Prairies | perogies & garlic sausage (Ukrainian settlement — L.16, edible), Winnipeg's honey dill sauce, Saskatoon berry pie |
| BC | salmon (candied, smoked; Indigenous salmon culture first — L.06), sushi as default lunch, the California-roll-was-invented-in-Vancouver claim |
| North | Arctic char, bannock (fry bread — pan-Indigenous staple with a colonial-trade history, L.09), country food (caribou, seal — L.44's food-sovereignty file) |

### The multicultural table (the actual answer)

The points-system decades (L.43) rebuilt Canadian eating: **the
best "Canadian food" conversation is "where do we go for X?"** —
and every city's answer map is a settlement-history map (L.04/16/
43). Toronto eats in 200 languages; Vancouver's Asian food rivals
Asia's; Montreal adds the French technique layer; even small
cities now run on a Lebanese shawarma / Vietnamese pho / Punjabi
samosa infrastructure. Crucial native attitude: this isn't
"ethnic food" vs "Canadian food" — **the immigrant table IS the
Canadian table**, usually within one generation (the butter
chicken poutine exists and nobody's in charge of stopping it).

### The social technologies

- **The potluck** (L.49): default group-meal format — egalitarian,
  budget-kind, and multiculturalism's daily engine (bring your
  culture's dish = instant standing).
- **The barbecue:** May-two-four to Labour Day (L.45's calendar);
  burgers/dogs baseline, no gatekeeping.
- **Double-double diplomacy:** "I'll grab you a Tim's" is a
  workplace bonding move (L.50); knowing orders = caring.
- **Drinking culture:** beer as default (macro lagers + a serious
  craft scene), rye (Canadian whisky) as the traditional spirit,
  BC/Ontario/Nova Scotia wines defended with feeling (Okanagan,
  Niagara icewine — L.39); moderation norms plus provincial
  retail weirdness (L.50); the "two-four" case of beer as unit
  of measure and holiday namesake (L.54).

{: .note }
> **Menu-literacy cheat card**
> Poutine = fries/curds/gravy. Donair = Halifax's sweet-sauce
> kebab. Tourtière = Quebec meat pie. Peameal = cornmeal-rolled
> back bacon ("Canadian bacon" is the American name; saying it
> here marks you tourist). Caesar = the clam one, yes. Butter
> tart = pecan-pie's smaller cousin, raisin controversy
> included. Bannock = fry bread, order it at Indigenous-run
> spots (L.44). "Double-double" = coffee order, never food.
> And the trick question "what's Canadian food?" — the native-
> certified answer is a grin plus "poutine... and also literally
> everything; let me show you where we eat."

**Exercise 1 — the visiting-friend food tour.** A friend visits
you (pick Toronto, Montreal, or Halifax) for 48 hours and asks for
"real Canadian food." Build the itinerary — folk canon AND
multicultural table both represented — with a one-line
justification per stop citing this phase's logic.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Montreal model (the strongest hand): Day 1 — Schwartz's smoked
meat (folk canon, line included — the line is part of it);
St-Viateur or Fairmount bagel, hot, at night (the 2 a.m. bagel
is a Montreal citizenship act); late-night poutine at a
casse-croûte (curds at source). Day 2 — cabane à sucre in
season (the full sugar-shack liturgy: tourtière, oreilles de
crisse, taffy-on-snow — L.48's field trip, adult version) OR
Jean-Talon Market (the multicultural table under one roof);
dinner in Little Italy/Maghreb/Vietnamese Montreal — chosen
by asking a local "where do we go for X?" (the native
algorithm, this lesson). Justification architecture: two folk-
canon anchors + one regional ritual + one multicultural
evening = both true answers to "what is Canadian food?"
represented, which IS the lesson. (Toronto model swaps in
peameal at St. Lawrence Market, butter tarts, then Scarborough
for Sri Lankan/Chinese/Caribbean — the where-do-we-go map as
settlement history, L.43. Halifax: donair after midnight,
lobster roll, hodge podge if invited to a kitchen, then the
Lebanese layer that donair itself came from — the folk dish
IS immigrant food, one generation in: the lesson's thesis,
edible.)
</details>

**Exercise 2 — potluck strategy.** Your team potluck is Friday.
Design your contribution as a newcomer: what you bring, how you
label it, what you say when asked about it — and explain why the
potluck is this course's Lesson 43 in casserole form.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Bring a dish from your own food culture — mid-spice version,
clearly labeled (allergens matter: nuts especially — schools
and offices run serious allergy protocols; add a small
ingredients card, which reads as considerate, not fussy). Have
the ninety-second story ready ("my grandmother's version; the
proper one has X but I toned it down") — the story is half the
contribution; Canadians will ask, and the asking is the
integration ritual working (L.49's "where's your family from?"
warmth). Practical notes: bring a serving utensil, expect the
plate-of-everything eating style, and accept recipe requests
as the compliment they are. Why it's L.43 in casserole form:
the potluck is multiculturalism's actual daily mechanism —
retain your heritage (your dish), contribute it to the common
table (the mosaic's tile), and the "nation" is literally the
sum of what everyone brought; nobody assimilates to a national
casserole. It's also the cheapest cultural capital available
to a newcomer: one good dish with a story out-integrates
months of small talk. (Failure mode to avoid: bringing
store-bought generic — legal, but you've wasted the
mosaic's best move.)
</details>

**Exercise 3 — decode the food fights.** Explain what's really at
stake in: (a) raisins in butter tarts; (b) "that's not real
poutine" (said in Quebec about an Ontario version); (c) Montreal
vs New York bagels; (d) "Canadian bacon."

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) Raisins: a zero-stakes heritage war maintained BECAUSE it's
zero-stakes — families hold positions, columnists relitigate it
annually; it's the national pastime of safe disagreement
(L.49's conflict-aversion needs licensed outlets; food fights
are the licensed outlets). (b) Poutine authenticity: actual
cultural custody — the dish is rural-Quebec folk food that
English Canada mocked ("poutine" was used as an insult) before
adopting it nationally; Quebec's judging rights are the
memory of that arc (L.22/47: appropriation-after-contempt is
a sore Quebec pattern; the curd-squeak test is sovereignty in
snack form). (c) Bagels: Montreal's (wood-fired, hand-rolled,
honey-water boiled, smaller/sweeter) vs New York's is really
Montreal vs New York — a small city asserting artisanal
superiority over the imperial metropolis, and winning often
enough to keep the file open; also a Jewish-Montreal history
lesson (the Main, Mile End — L.43's older migration layers).
(d) "Canadian bacon" is the AMERICAN name for back bacon;
Canadians say "peameal" (Toronto) or just "back bacon" — the
fight is about being defined from outside (L.38/47's
elephant problem, breakfast edition): the tell that someone
learned Canada from American media is that they order
Canadian bacon and pronounce "about" wrong on purpose.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** *(culture-fact round)* Define: poutine (with the quality
test), the Caesar, tourtière, and a two-four — and what share of
world maple syrup does Quebec produce?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Poutine: fries + cheese curds + gravy, from 1950s Quebec; the
test is squeaky fresh curds (shredded mozzarella = imposter).
Caesar: the national cocktail — vodka, Clamato (clam-tomato
juice), Worcestershire, hot sauce, celery-salt rim; brunch
standard since its 1969 Calgary invention. Tourtière: Quebec's
spiced meat pie, the réveillon (Christmas Eve) centrepiece.
Two-four: a 24-bottle case of beer, and the namesake of the May
long weekend ("May two-four" — L.45). Quebec produces roughly
70% of the entire world's maple syrup (L.39), complete with a
strategic reserve and its famous heist.
</details>

**Q2.** State the two simultaneous true answers to "what is
Canadian food?" and explain why natives hold both without
contradiction.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Answer one: the folk canon — poutine, butter tarts, Nanaimo
bars, maple, KD, the Caesar, plus fierce regional tables
(Jiggs' dinner to donair to perogies). Answer two: the
multicultural table — the everyday eating built by immigration
(L.43): shawarma, pho, dim sum, roti as ordinary local food,
where "where do we go for X?" maps settlement history. No
contradiction because they're layers, not rivals: the folk
canon supplies ritual and nostalgia (childhood, holidays,
regional pride — L.48), the immigrant table supplies actual
dinner — and dishes migrate between layers within a
generation (donair was Lebanese-Halifax fusion; butter
chicken poutine exists). The mosaic model (L.43) predicts
exactly this: retained heritages summing to the shared table
rather than melting into one cuisine — Canadian food isn't a
dish, it's the potluck.
</details>

**Q3.** Why does Quebec hold special standing on poutine and
sugar-shack culture, and what larger course themes surface in
"that's not real poutine"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Both are Québécois folk creations: poutine from 1950s rural
snack shacks, the cabane à sucre from habitant spring
tradition (L.08's farm calendar surviving industrialization) —
and both were provincial intimacies before becoming national
brands. The custody claim in "not real poutine" carries L.22's
arc: English Canada mocked poutine as a hick dish (and used
the word as an ethnic sneer) within living memory, then
adopted it as THE national food — adoption-after-contempt,
which is also the O Canada story (L.45) and part of the
two-solitudes file (L.42/47). So the curd test is memory
enforcement: Quebec granting the country its dish while
keeping the authenticity keys. Larger themes: folk culture as
identity infrastructure, the regions' veto over national
myths, and the course rule that every Canadian symbol has a
footnote — even the gravy.
</details>

## Homework

Run the four-tastings project over a month: (1) a proper poutine
(curd-verified); (2) one folk-canon bake (butter tart or Nanaimo
bar — bake or buy from an independent bakery); (3) one regional
dish OUTSIDE your region's table; (4) one "where do we go for X?"
expedition — ask three locals for the city's best version of a
cuisine and go. Log each with a two-line review and one thing the
experience taught you that this lesson couldn't.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model log entries, for calibration: (1) the poutine review
should mention curd squeak and structural collapse timing
(gravy patience is real knowledge); (2) the butter tart entry
must declare a raisin position — you're a stakeholder now;
(3) the out-of-region dish (a donair in Toronto, tourtière out
west) usually teaches that regional dishes travel with
diaspora — the Halifax-style donair shop in Alberta is run by
Maritimers, and the finding-it conversation is a regional-
identity interview (L.47's regional counterweights, edible);
(4) the ask-three-locals expedition reliably produces the
lesson's thesis live: the answers disagree, passionately, by
neighbourhood and origin story, and following one lands you in
the multicultural table's daily reality (L.43) — plus the
asking itself is elite-tier Canadian small talk (L.49: food
recommendations rank just below weather and above sports as
safe passionate topics). The un-teachable thing students most
often report: taste is the fastest belonging — a food opinion
sincerely held ("the curds at X squeak better") is treated as
residency evidence no document matches. Four logs = full
marks; a defended raisin position = distinction.
</details>
