# Lesson 46: Arts, Sports, and the Shared Culture

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **CanCon** — "Canadian content": the legal quotas requiring radio/TV to carry Canadian music and shows; **CRTC** — the broadcast regulator enforcing them; **MAPL** — the points system certifying a song as Canadian.
> - **quota** — a required minimum share (35% Canadian music on radio).
> - **discoverability** — how easily an algorithm surfaces something to you; the streaming-era battleground.
> - **NFB** — the National Film Board, Canada's public documentary studio.
> - **téléroman** (tay-lay-roh-MAWN) — Quebec's hugely popular nightly serial dramas.
> - **star system** — a self-sustaining celebrity industry; Quebec runs its own, invisible to English Canada.
> - **the Original Six** — the NHL's six founding-era teams (two Canadian: Montreal, Toronto).
> - **dynasty** — a team dominating for years; **the Summit Series** — the 1972 Canada–USSR showdown.
> - **koan** (KOH-ahn) — see Lesson 45: the no-Stanley-Cup-since-1993 riddle.
> - Names: **Massey** (MASS-ee), **Gord Downie** (DOW-nee), **Munro** (mun-ROH), **Richler** (RICH-ler), **Arcand** (ar-KAWN), **Villeneuve** (veel-NUV), **Wickenheiser** (WIK-en-hy-zer), **Gretzky** (GRET-skee), **the Habs** (HABZ — the Montreal Canadiens).

## Concept

The touchstones that come up in any Canadian conversation — with
the structural story underneath: a small anglophone culture
sharing a border, a language, and a couch with the world's loudest
culture industry had to *choose* between absorption and
deliberate cultural machinery. It chose machinery:

```
   THE CULTURAL DEFENCE SYSTEM (build dates)

   CBC/Radio-Canada   1936 (L.19) — public broadcasting
                      born explicitly to counter US radio;
                      Hockey Night in Canada its cathedral
   NFB                1939 — the documentary tradition
                      (Obomsawin, L.44)
   Massey Commission  1951 — "the state or the States":
                      the founding cultural-policy report
   Canada Council     1957 — arts funding (the Massey
                      recommendation)
   CanCon rules       1971 — the CRTC's radio/TV Canadian-
                      content quotas: the reason the
                      radio plays The Tragically Hip
   the streaming fight 2023's Online Streaming Act —
                      CanCon logic vs Netflix/Spotify:
                      the 1951 question, re-asked

   AND THE ICE (the other shared church):
   hockey — 1972 Summit Series (the cold-war
   passion play), Gretzky, the Habs–Leafs schism,
   women's Olympic dynasty, Sidney's golden goal
   (2010), the McDavid era's beautiful agony:
   no Canadian team has won the Cup since 1993 —
   a national koan
```

Two honest frames to carry: the machinery *worked* (a
recognizable Canadian culture exists and exports — from Anne of
Green Gables to Drake) — and its premise is permanently
contested (protection vs crutch; and Quebec's culture, note,
needed less machinery: the language was the wall, L.42, behind
which a complete star system — *le star-système québécois* —
thrives largely unknown to English Canada: the two solitudes'
most cheerful example).

## How It Works

### Hockey: the shared church (attendance mandatory-ish)

The literacy kit: **the 1972 Summit Series** — Canada vs the
USSR, cold war on ice, the country stopped for game eight
(schools wheeled in TVs); Paul Henderson's goal with 34
seconds left is English Canada's "where were you" moment —
the closest thing to a shared national memory the country
possesses. **The Original Six schism:** Canadiens (24 Cups,
the francophone dynasty — Maurice Richard's 1955 suspension
riot is a Quiet Revolution prologue, L.22) vs the Maple
Leafs (1967's last Cup, the beloved national heartbreak
machine) — plus the Oilers' Gretzky dynasty (his 1988 trade
to LA was debated in Parliament; genuinely), the **women's
team** (Olympic dominance since 2002 — the rivalry with the
US is the best hockey played anywhere), **Sidney Crosby's
2010 golden goal** (Vancouver Olympics — the 1972 moment's
sequel), and the standing koan: **no Canadian NHL team has
won the Cup since Montreal, 1993** — the country that
supplies half the league's players and most of its passion
watches its own franchises fail annually, and the
sports-talk theology this generates ("the pressure, the
taxes, the markets...") is itself a cultural institution.
Politics knows the church: "hockey riding" metaphors,
leaders' obligatory rink photo-ops, and the **Hockey Night
in Canada** theme's 2008 licensing loss mourned like a
state funeral. Also real: the game's cost crisis (minor
hockey pricing out the middle class), the concussion and
abuse reckonings (Hockey Canada's 2022 scandals), and the
rising counter-fact that **soccer now out-registers hockey**
among kids — the church's demographics are moving, per
L.24's re-founding.

### The CanCon machinery and its verdict

The **Massey Report (1951)** stated the premise: without
deliberate support, Canadian culture drowns in the American
signal ("the state or the States"). The system built:
CBC/Radio-Canada (its dual soul: revered institution and
permanent political football — defund-the-CBC is a standing
platform plank, L.28's party map); the Canada Council;
**CanCon quotas** (1971 — commercial radio must play ~35%
Canadian music, TV carry Canadian programming, certified by
the MAPL system's points). The verdict data: pre-1971,
Canadian artists were curiosities on their own radio;
post-quota, an industry — the machinery incubated the
Guess Who → Rush → **The Tragically Hip** (the canonical
case: superstars at home, cult abroad — their 2016 farewell
concert, broadcast by CBC, watched by a third of the
country, PM in the crowd: the Summit Series of sad dad
rock) → Céline, Shania, Alanis, Drake, The Weeknd, Bieber
(the streaming-era irony: Toronto artists now dominate
global charts *without* needing the wall — Drake is the
biggest pop act of his era and the machinery can't claim
him, exactly the anti-protection argument). The literature
column: **Munro's Nobel (2013)**, **Atwood** (the
Handmaid's-era global omnipresence), Ondaatje, Richler
(Montreal's satirist), the L.44 renaissance — plus the
publishing-subsidy ecosystem underneath. Comedy as the
stealth export: SCTV → SNL's Canadian pipeline (Myers,
Carrey (adopted), Candy, Levy — *Schitt's Creek*'s Emmy
sweep as the tradition's crown) — the standing theory that
outsider-observation of America is Canada's comparative
advantage, monetized.

### Quebec's parallel star system

Behind the language wall (L.42), a complete culture
industry English Canada barely perceives: its own TV
megahits (*District 31* drawing NHL-scale nightly
audiences), its film tradition (Denys Arcand's Oscar;
Villeneuve and Dolan graduating to global cinema), its
music economy (Cowboys Fringants stadiums), its talk shows
(*Tout le monde en parle* setting the province's weekly
agenda), the **téléroman** as social institution. The
structural note: Quebec's cultural confidence needed
quotas less because French *is* the quota — which is
either the argument for Bill 101 (L.22) or against CanCon
(L.43's interculturalism debate, cultural edition),
depending which solitude is speaking. The Canadian
cultural whole is thus **two star systems + the Indigenous
renaissance (L.44) + the diaspora wave** (Kim's
Convenience → Scarborough's literature — L.24's
demography reaching the screen), sharing funding
machinery and a border, not an audience.

Model: "It's the Olympic hockey final against the US, and

**Exercise 3 — the CanCon trial, streaming era.** The
Online Streaming Act extends CanCon logic to Netflix,
Spotify, and YouTube — mandated Canadian-content funding
and discoverability. Prosecute and defend using the full
evidence file (the Hip vs Drake exhibits; Massey's premise;
Quebec's wall; the algorithm era's differences), then rule.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Prosecution: the machinery's premise died with the dial —
quotas made sense when three broadcasters owned scarce
shelf space; algorithms have infinite shelves, and Canadian
artists now win globally by being GOOD ON THE PLATFORMS
(Drake, The Weeknd, Bieber, Shawn Mendes: the biggest
Canadian cultural exports ever, zero quota assistance);
discoverability mandates risk making "Canadian" a
skip-button category (forced-feed resentment), the
certification system (MAPL points) rewards industrial
checklists over cultural content (a US star recording in
Toronto can qualify while a Canadian's diaspora story shot
abroad might not), and the compliance costs land on the
platforms' smallest creators — the YouTube generation the
Act claims to help, who built audiences precisely by
ignoring the national frame. Defence: Massey's premise
never died — it moved into the algorithm: the feed IS the
new scarce shelf (attention, not bandwidth, is the
bottleneck), and its owners are foreign monopolies with
zero stake in Canadian storytelling; the machinery's
track record is the country's actual culture (the
Hip-to-Munro ecosystem was BUILT, not born — pre-1971
radio proves the counterfactual); Drake disproves nothing —
pop's global winners never needed help; the machinery
exists for the mid-list where national culture actually
lives (the drama about Regina, the Cree-language series,
the téléroman — L.44's renaissance and Quebec's system
both run on the funding side of exactly this Act); and
sovereignty over cultural policy is the 1951/1988
question (L.38's culture exemption was fought for) —
declining to regulate the platforms is choosing the
States, in Massey's exact terms. Ruling (any honest one):
split the instrument — the FUNDING half (platform
contributions to Canadian production) inherits the
machinery's proven logic; the DISCOVERABILITY half
(algorithm mandates) fails the evidence test and risks
the backlash; and the deepest finding is the course
pattern: this is the 1951 question re-asked in each
technology generation — the state or the States — and
Canada's consistent answer (machinery, adjusted,
imperfect, renewed) is less a policy than a national
habit; expect the 2040s edition.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** *(citizenship-style)* What are Canada's two national
sports, what was the 1972 Summit Series, and what is the
standing "koan" of Canadian hockey?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Lacrosse (summer — Indigenous in origin, L.06) and hockey
(winter), designated jointly by the 1994 National Sports
Act. The Summit Series: the September 1972 eight-game
confrontation between Canada's NHL stars and the Soviet
national team at the cold war's height — Canada won on
Paul Henderson's goal in the final minute of game eight in
Moscow, with virtually the entire country watching:
English Canada's defining shared memory. The koan: no
Canadian NHL team has won the Stanley Cup since the 1993
Montreal Canadiens — the country that invented the game
and staffs the league watches its own franchises fail
annually, a fact processed as national comedy, theology,
and talk-radio economy simultaneously.
</details>

**Q2.** What problem did the Massey Commission name, what
machinery answered it, and what is the honest two-exhibit
verdict?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Massey (1951) named the structural problem of a small
anglophone culture beside the American industry: without
deliberate support, Canadian voices drown — "the state or
the States." Machinery: the CBC (already, 1936), the
Canada Council (1957), the NFB, and the CRTC's CanCon
quotas (1971) requiring Canadian music and programming on
licensed airwaves, plus publishing and film subsidy
systems. Two-exhibit verdict: Exhibit Hip — the quota era
demonstrably built a domestic industry and canon (from a
radio landscape without Canadian stars to the Guess
Who → Rush → Tragically Hip ecosystem, plus the
Munro/Atwood literary infrastructure); Exhibit Drake — the
streaming era's biggest Canadian exports succeeded
globally with no protection at all, reviving the standing
question of whether the machinery grows culture or
shelters it. Both exhibits are real; the policy argument
(the Online Streaming Act fight) is their unresolved
trial.
</details>

**Q3.** Describe Quebec's "parallel star system" and the
structural lesson it teaches about cultural protection.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Behind the language wall, Quebec runs a complete,
self-sustaining culture industry largely invisible to
English Canada: its own television hits with
proportionally enormous audiences, its own music and film
stars (Arcand's Oscar; Villeneuve and Dolan as global
graduates), agenda-setting talk shows, and the téléroman
tradition — francophone Quebecers consume overwhelmingly
domestic culture by choice. Structural lesson: language
is the strongest cultural protection ever devised —
Quebec needed quotas less because French itself filters
the American signal (Bill 101 as cultural policy, L.22),
while anglophone Canada, sharing English with the
elephant, needed engineered machinery (CanCon) to hold
any shelf space. The comparison cuts both ways in debate:
proof that walls work (Quebec's confidence), and proof
that anglo-Canada's walls are fighting language-gravity
that Quebec never faced (the CanCon skeptic's rejoinder).
</details>
