# Lesson 32: Citizenship — Rights and Responsibilities

## Concept

Canadian citizenship is young as a legal thing: before the
**Citizenship Act of 1947**, Canadians were simply "British
subjects" — Canada was the first Commonwealth country to create its
own citizenship. Today it operates on two doors and one ceremony:

```
   BECOMING CANADIAN

   DOOR 1: BIRTH                 DOOR 2: NATURALIZATION
   ─────────────                 ──────────────────────
   jus soli: born ON             1. permanent residence
   Canadian soil = citizen          (the years-long part:
   (near-unconditional —            Express Entry, family
   diplomatic children the          sponsorship, refugee
   main exception)                  protection — L.41/43)
   jus sanguinis: born           2. 1,095 days physically
   abroad TO a Canadian             present in the 5 years
   parent = citizen —               before applying
   FIRST GENERATION only         3. tax filings; language
   (the 2009 limit; under           (CLB 4, EN or FR,
   litigation/reform)               ages 18–54)
                                 4. THE TEST (ages 18–54):
                                    20 questions, 15 to
                                    pass, from Discover
                                    Canada — i.e., THIS
                                    COURSE, compressed
                                 5. THE OATH — the moment
                                    it becomes legal fact
```

The oath: *"I swear (or affirm) that I will be faithful and bear
true allegiance to His Majesty King Charles the Third, King of
Canada, His Heirs and Successors, and that I will faithfully observe
the laws of Canada, **including the Constitution, which recognizes
and affirms the Aboriginal and treaty rights of First Nations, Inuit
and Métis peoples**, and fulfil my duties as a Canadian citizen."*
The bolded phrase was added in 2021 — **TRC Call to Action #94**
(Lesson 24): every new citizen now explicitly joins the treaty
relationship (Lesson 17's "we are all treaty people," made oath).

## How It Works

### What citizenship adds to permanent residence

PRs already hold most daily rights: work, medicare, Charter
protections (most of it — s.6 mobility and s.3 voting are
citizens-only), social programs. Citizenship adds: **the vote and
candidacy** (all three levels); **the passport** (and consular
protection); **immunity from deportation and from PR
residency-obligation loss**; unrestricted **security-clearance and
some public-service** eligibility; and passing citizenship to
children born abroad (first generation). What it costs: nothing
material — Canada permits **dual (multiple) citizenship** without
restriction (since 1977), demands no renunciations, and taxes by
*residence*, not citizenship (unlike the US — a fact newcomers
from America discover with joy in one direction and paperwork in
the other, Lesson 35).

### Rights and responsibilities — the official list, annotated

*Discover Canada* pairs the lists; here they are with honest
annotations:

**Rights** (constitutional, Lessons 23/31): mobility; vote and run
for office; enter/remain in Canada; official-language use;
everything in the Charter. **Responsibilities**: **obey the law**
(the only legally enforced one — true of everyone, citizen or
not); **serve on a jury when summoned** (also legally compelled,
and PRs are exempt — jury lists draw from citizens; Lesson 33);
**vote** (a civic duty in rhetoric, legally optional — no
compulsory voting à la Australia); **help others in the
community** (volunteering — aspirational, though Canada's
volunteering rates give the aspiration real content, Lesson 36);
**protect our heritage and environment** (aspirational). The
honest annotation: Canada's enforced duty list is short — law,
juries, taxes (which is residence-based, not a citizenship duty
per se) — and the rest is culture. No conscription (since
Lesson 20's crises — and note there is no peacetime draft law),
no mandatory service, no compelled voting: the state asks little
formally and relies on the civic culture Phase 5's synthesis
identified as the system's real load-bearer.

### The ceremony (and the King problem)

Citizenship becomes legal at the **oath ceremony** — presided by a
citizenship judge or official, often with an RCMP officer in red
serge, frequently at hockey arenas, museums, even (for the
promotional photos) on Canada Day stages. You swear or *affirm*
(secular option), in either official language, receive the
certificate, and sing O Canada — newly yours in both lyric
versions (Lesson 45). The perennial objection — "why swear to a
King?" — got its legal answer in Lesson 25's decoder: courts
(*McAteer*, Ontario CA 2014) upheld the oath by reading it as
allegiance to **Canada's form of government personified**, not
personal fealty to Charles Windsor; the challenge failed,
citizenship's price remains one sentence of constitutional
symbolism. New citizens averaging ~200,000–300,000+ per year make
this among the world's largest naturalization operations
per-capita — and the ceremony is deliberately warm: Canada
stage-manages belonging the way it stage-manages little else
(Lesson 43 will ask what the warmth does and doesn't buy).

{: .note }
> **Citizenship by the numbers**
> ~85%+ of eligible permanent residents eventually naturalize —
> among the highest uptake rates in the world (the US runs far
> lower). Read the incentive stack: no dual-citizenship penalty,
> residence-based taxation, a strong passport, and a political
> culture that treats naturalized citizens as simply citizens
> (a quarter of Parliament is typically foreign-born or
> second-generation). The uptake rate is Canada's quietest
> immigration statistic and arguably its most important:
> newcomers don't just come — they join.

**Exercise 2 — scenario: the oath objection.** A friend about to
naturalize says: "I love Canada, but I can't swear loyalty to some
British king. I'm thinking of skipping citizenship over this."
Give the four-part answer: what the oath legally means, what the
courts said, the affirm option and the 2021 addition, and the
practical stakes of skipping.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "First, what you'd actually be swearing: Canadian courts
have ruled — in a case brought by people with exactly your
objection — that the oath to the 'King of Canada' is symbolic
allegiance to our constitutional system, which happens to be
personified by the Crown (Lesson 25's whole design), not personal
loyalty to a British family; the Ontario Court of Appeal upheld
it on precisely that reading, and republican new citizens swear
it daily while keeping their opinions. Second, you can
<em>affirm</em> instead of swear — no religion, no ceremony-
theatrics — and you'll also be affirming something you may like
better: since 2021 the oath explicitly includes faithfully
observing the Constitution 'which recognizes and affirms the
Aboriginal and treaty rights of First Nations, Inuit and Métis
peoples' — you'd be joining the treaty relationship, which is
arguably the oath's real content now. Third, the stakes of
skipping: no vote at any level, no Canadian passport, no
deportation immunity, no full mobility rights, no passing
citizenship to kids born abroad — you'd be governed permanently
by rules you can never vote on, over one sentence of symbolism
the courts have already drained of its literal meaning. Fourth,
the pragmatic framing: the monarchy question is Canada's to
settle by its own constitutional politics someday (good luck,
Lesson 25), and citizens get a say in that too — non-citizens
don't. Swear the sentence, keep the opinion; that combination is
extremely Canadian."
</details>

**Exercise 3 — PR vs citizen: the decision matrix.** Your
colleague, a PR for six years, asks whether citizenship is worth
the paperwork: "I already have healthcare and a job." Build the
honest matrix: what changes, what doesn't, the risks of staying
PR-forever, and the one scenario where waiting makes sense.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
What doesn't change: healthcare, work rights, most Charter
protections, taxes (residence-based either way), CPP/EI/benefits.
What changes: vote and candidacy at all three levels (the only
lever on the governments running your hospital, school, and
rent rules — Lesson 29); passport + consular protection;
absolute security of status — PRs can lose status by residency
shortfall (730 days/5 years) or serious criminality
(deportation applies to PRs, not citizens); clearance-gated
jobs; citizenship for future kids born abroad. Risks of
PR-forever: the residency-obligation trap for people whose
careers require long postings abroad (the exact people who
think they don't need citizenship); rule changes — immigration
law amends easily (Lesson 16's history says selection criteria
are politics), while citizenship, once granted, is nearly
irrevocable (fraud aside); and the compounding one — decades of
paying taxes under governments you can't vote out. When waiting
makes sense: essentially one scenario — if your origin country
punishes dual citizenship (loss of nationality, property or
inheritance rights, exit-visa issues) and that cost currently
outweighs the above; then the calculus is genuinely personal.
Otherwise the matrix is lopsided, which the ~85% uptake rate
(note above) suggests most PRs eventually compute. Closing
line for the colleague: 'You've done the 1,095 hard days
already — the remaining distance is a study guide you'd
enjoy and one sentence about a king who can't overrule a
parking ticket.'
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** *(citizenship-style)* What are the requirements to apply
for Canadian citizenship by naturalization?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Permanent resident status; physical presence of 1,095 days
(three years) within the five years before applying; filed taxes
for the required years; demonstrated ability in English or
French (CLB/NCLC 4, ages 18–54); passing the citizenship test on
rights, responsibilities, history, geography, and government
(ages 18–54; 15/20 to pass, based on Discover Canada); and
taking the Oath of Citizenship — the legal moment of becoming
Canadian. Dual citizenship is fully permitted.
</details>

**Q2.** Which citizen responsibilities are legally enforced and
which are cultural, and what does the shortness of the enforced
list tell you (connect to Phase 5's synthesis)?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Enforced: obeying the law (universal), jury service when
summoned (citizens only), and — as residents rather than
citizens per se — taxes. Cultural/aspirational: voting (no
compulsory voting), volunteering, environmental and heritage
stewardship; no military or civil service obligation exists.
The short list mirrors Phase 5's finding: Canada's institutions
run on convention and civic culture rather than legal
compulsion — the state formally demands little and depends on
voluntary participation (high naturalization, decent turnout,
world-leading volunteering) to keep the conventions priced.
The system trusts culture to do what other states do with
mandates — its characteristic strength, and single point of
failure.
</details>

**Q3.** What did the 2021 oath amendment add, and why is it more
than symbolic?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
It added that citizens will faithfully observe "the
Constitution, which recognizes and affirms the Aboriginal and
treaty rights of First Nations, Inuit and Métis peoples" —
implementing the TRC's Call to Action #94 (Lesson 24). More
than symbolic because: it makes every new citizen an explicit
party to the treaty relationship (operationalizing "we are all
treaty people," Lesson 17) at the exact moment of joining;
it installs Lesson 24's file into the citizenship curriculum
itself (the test now covers it); and — given ~300,000 oaths a
year — it means the fastest-growing segment of the citizenry
formally enters Canada through an acknowledgment the country's
founding documents took 250 years to make. The oath now
contains, in one sentence, this course's whole arc: Crown,
Constitution, and treaties, joined.
</details>
