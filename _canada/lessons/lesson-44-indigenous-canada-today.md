# Lesson 44: Indigenous Canada Today

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **modern treaty / comprehensive claim** — a full land-and-government agreement negotiated today (James Bay 1975, Nisga'a 2000), unlike the 1800s numbered treaties.
> - **self-government agreement** — a negotiated deal replacing Indian Act rule with a nation's own law-making government.
> - **fee simple** — ordinary full private ownership of land (what modern treaties grant over core lands).
> - **development corporation** — a nation-owned business arm investing settlement money.
> - **economic reconciliation** — repairing the relationship through ownership: equity stakes, procurement, business.
> - **hereditary chiefs** — leaders under a nation's pre-colonial governance system, passed through traditional law; **elected band council** — the Indian Act's elected system; both can claim to speak for a nation (the Wet'suwet'en conflict).
> - **blood quantum** — measuring Indigenous identity by ancestry percentage (a US approach; Canadian nations define their own citizenship).
> - **performative** — done for appearance rather than effect.
> - **land back** — the movement to return land ownership/control to Indigenous nations.
> - **throat singing** — the traditional Inuit vocal art (Tanya Tagaq's medium).
> - Names: **Wet'suwet'en** (wet-SOO-wet-en), **Nisga'a** (NISS-gah), **Tsilhqot'in** (sil-KOH-teen), **Monkman** (MUNK-man), **Tagaq** (TAH-gahk), **Obomsawin** (oh-BOM-sah-win), **Abenaki** (ab-eh-NAH-kee), **Wab Kinew** (WOB kih-NOO), **Membertou** (MEM-ber-too), **Eeyou Istchee** (EE-yoo ISS-chee — the James Bay Cree territory), **Kanien'kehá:ka** (gah-nyen-geh-HAH-gah — the Mohawk).

## Concept

This lesson completes the arc begun in Lesson 06: Indigenous peoples
as **present-tense nations** — not the historical chapter Canadian
schooling long made them, but the youngest, fastest-growing
population in the country, running governments, corporations,
courtrooms, and a cultural renaissance, while carrying Lesson 24's
unfinished files. The frame to install permanently:

```
   THE PRESENT TENSE, BY DOMAIN

   PEOPLES      1.8M+ (5%), fastest-growing, median age
                ~8 yrs below national; majority URBAN
                (the reserve-only image is decades stale)
   GOVERNMENT   Nunavut (L.01) at 25+; ~25 modern
                treaties covering 40%+ of the landmass;
                self-government agreements replacing the
                Indian Act nation by nation (L.17's cage,
                exiting one door at a time)
   LAW          the s.35 engine (L.24): duty to consult →
                Tsilhqot'in title → UNDRIP's consent
                standard — Indigenous law now a fact of
                every resource boardroom (L.39)
   ECONOMY      development corporations, equity stakes
                in pipelines/transmission/mines, procure-
                ment set-asides, ~60,000 Indigenous-owned
                businesses — "economic reconciliation"
   CULTURE      the renaissance: Monkman at the Met,
                Tagaq's Polaris, Obomsawin's cinema,
                Kinew as Manitoba premier (2023 — first
                First Nations premier of a province),
                Mary Simon as GG; languages reviving
                against the residential-school silence
   THE FILES    L.24's Column B: water, child welfare,
                MMIWG, incarceration — present, not past
```

And the two practices this lesson decodes because you'll meet
them weekly: **land acknowledgements** and the word
**"unceded"** — both precise legal-historical claims wearing
ceremonial clothes, both contested from multiple directions,
both belonging in your literate vocabulary.

## Going Deeper

### Nations rebuilding: the government layer

The Indian Act's replacement proceeds nation-by-nation
(Lesson 17's verdict — the cage opens by substitution):
**modern treaties** (comprehensive claims — James Bay 1975 →
Nisga'a 2000 → the Yukon and NWT agreements) create
constitutionally protected self-government with law-making
powers, land ownership in fee simple and traditional-territory
rights, and financial arrangements; **sectoral self-government**
(education authorities in Nova Scotia's Mi'kmaw Kina'matnewey —
graduation rates now *above* provincial average: the
counter-evidence to every deficit narrative; First Nations
child-welfare jurisdiction under the 2019 federal act);
**Nunavut** — a public government (all residents vote) born of
an Inuit land claim, governing a fifth of Canada in Inuktitut
and English; and the **Métis** governments signing their first
modern self-government agreements in the 2020s (Lesson 15's
143-year arc, still compounding). Honest ledger: modern
treaties took 20–30 years each to negotiate; most First
Nations still live under the Indian Act; and the BC treaty
process's slow grind produced the alternative route —
litigation (Tsilhqot'in) and agreement-by-agreement pragmatism.

### The economic turn

The structural shift of the 2010s–20s: from consultation
(being heard) to **equity** (owning the thing). Instances with
numbers: Indigenous-owned development corporations managing
billions (the Inuvialuit, Cree of Eeyou Istchee, Membertou);
loan-guarantee programs (federal and provincial) built
specifically so nations can buy into pipelines, transmission
lines, and LNG (the TMX Indigenous-consortium file, L.39);
procurement targets (5% federal); ~60,000 Indigenous
businesses; own-source revenues funding services the Indian
Act never did. The driver is Lesson 39's homework insight:
**consent became the cheaper path** — projects with nations as
owners don't spend a decade in court; capital noticed. The
in-community debate (respect it): development-vs-land-defence
splits are real politics inside nations (elected councils
signing agreements while hereditary leadership objects — the
Wet'suwet'en pipeline conflict of 2020, where both claims to
speak for the nation were authentic: Lesson 06's
no-monolith rule at its sharpest), and "economic
reconciliation" is embraced by many nations and critiqued by
others as reconciliation-priced-in-barrels. Your job as a
literate observer: name the specific nation, the specific
governance body, and the specific agreement — never "the
Indigenous position" (Lesson 06's rule, now with cap tables).

### Land acknowledgements and "unceded," decoded

The acknowledgement ("we gather on the traditional territory
of the X") originates in Indigenous diplomatic protocol
(guests naming whose land they enter — ancient practice,
Lesson 06's trade networks ran on it), institutionalized
post-TRC. **"Unceded"** is a precise claim: land never
surrendered by treaty nor lawfully taken — most of BC,
notably (the colony skipped the Royal Proclamation's
treaty-making, Lesson 10, which is why Vancouver says
"unceded Coast Salish territories" and why *Tsilhqot'in*
happened in BC): the word is a legal description with
billions in implications, not politeness. The debate you
should be able to stage: critics-from-solidarity (Indigenous
voices included) call rote acknowledgements "performative" —
naming the theft while keeping the goods, reconciliation as
theatre (the sharpest version: "an acknowledgement without a
rent cheque is a confession without restitution");
defenders answer that naming is a precondition — a
generation of Canadians now grows up knowing whose land
they're on, which was unsayable in public forty years ago,
and ritual is how societies rehearse truths into policy
(Lesson 21's homework: symbols acquire weight through
repetition). The synthesis position this course endorses:
judge any acknowledgement by what its institution does
*next* — the acknowledgement plus procurement, hiring,
curriculum, or land-back action is practice; alone, it's
aspiration; mocked, it's regression.

### The cultural renaissance (names to know)

**Kent Monkman** (Cree — monumental canvases re-painting
colonial art history with the colonizers as subjects;
commissioned into the Met's Great Hall); **Tanya Tagaq**
(Inuk throat-singer, Polaris Prize); **Alanis Obomsawin**
(Abenaki, the NFB's documentary conscience — *Kanehsatake*
on the Oka crisis, L.24); writers — **Richard Wagamese**
(*Indian Horse*), **Michelle Good** (*Five Little Indians*),
**Katherena Vermette**, **Thomas King** (*The Inconvenient
Indian* — this lesson's unofficial textbook); **Buffy
Sainte-Marie** (and the 2023 ancestry controversy — teaching
that identity claims are now seriously audited, itself a
sovereignty assertion: nations, not applicants, define
membership); **Wab Kinew** — residential-school survivor's
son, hip-hop artist, author, and from 2023 Premier of
Manitoba (Riel's province — Lesson 15's arc gets a
punchline history couldn't have scripted). Language
revival: Inuktitut official in Nunavut; Cree, Ojibwe,
Mi'kmaq immersion schools; the Indigenous Languages Act
(2019) — against the schools that beat the languages out
(L.17), every fluent graduate is a reversal executed.

{: .note }
> **Vocabulary maintenance (L.06 updated)**
> Current respectful defaults: *Indigenous* (capital I);
> *First Nations / Inuit / Métis* when specific; the
> nation's own name when known (Anishinaabe, Nêhiyaw,
> Kanien'kehá:ka...). *"Indian"* survives only in legal
> terms of art (Indian Act, status) and in some communities'
> own reclaimed usage — not yours to initiate. *"Aboriginal"*
> is aging out (still in s.35's text). *Survivor* (not
> "student") for residential schools. And the newest
> etiquette: ask which nation, not "how much" — blood
> quantum is American math; citizenship in a nation is the
> Canadian frame (and the nations' own).

**Exercise 2 — the performativity debate, staged fairly.**
A visiting colleague, after the third meeting-opening
acknowledgement of the day: "Honestly — does this actually
help anyone, or is it feel-good theatre?" Give the honest
both-sides answer (including the Indigenous critiques), your
own view, and the one-question test you'd propose.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "Both positions are held seriously, including by
Indigenous thinkers, so here's the fair version. The critique:
rote acknowledgements cost nothing, change nothing, and risk
becoming absolution — institutions naming the taking while
contesting the claims (some literally fight the named nation
in court the same week); the sharpest Indigenous critics call
it settler self-soothing, and repetition without content
does breed the eye-roll you're feeling. The defence: thirty
years ago whose land this was could not be said in a boardroom
— now every meeting rehearses it, a generation is growing up
with the names in their mouths, and ritual is how societies
pre-load truths for later policy (the anthem taught you the
country; this teaches the older one); plus the practice
originates in Indigenous protocol itself, not settler guilt.
My view [any honest version]: it's a floor, not an act of
reconciliation — valuable exactly insofar as it makes the
next concrete step harder to refuse. The test I'd propose:
ask the institution 'what did you DO for the named nation
since the last time you said this?' — if there's an answer
(procurement, hires, curriculum, a settled claim), the words
are the receipt; if there's silence, the words are the
substitute. Same test works on people, governments, and —
fair warning — dinner guests."
</details>

**Exercise 3 — the Wet'suwet'en case study.** 2020: a gas
pipeline crosses Wet'suwet'en territory with agreements
signed by all five elected band councils along the route —
and blockades led by hereditary chiefs asserting authority
over the traditional territory the bands' reserves sit
within; solidarity blockades halt rail nationally. Untangle
it with course tools: why BOTH authorities are real (name
the lessons), what the conflict actually tests, and why
"just ask the Indigenous community what it wants" fails as
policy here.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Both authorities real: elected band councils are Indian Act
creations (L.17) with jurisdiction over reserve lands and
statutory legitimacy — their agreements are lawful,
negotiated, and delivered real benefits their communities
voted for; hereditary chiefs carry the PRE-Act governance
system (L.06's continuing legal orders) whose authority
runs over the traditional TERRITORY — and it was
hereditary leadership, note carefully, who won Delgamuukw
(L.06/30): the very title jurisprudence at issue was built
by their system, over exactly these lands, which are
unceded (this lesson — BC's treaty gap). What the conflict
tests: whose consent satisfies s.35 and UNDRIP when
colonial law fractured a nation's governance into two
systems with different footprints — the reserve (Act) vs
the territory (title); Canada's institutions never decided,
because deciding requires acknowledging the Act's
governance displacement (L.17's design) and resolving what
the courts left open after Delgamuukw (title asserted, not
declared, for these lands). Why "just ask the community"
fails: there are two constitutionally colourable answers to
WHO the community's voice is — the question isn't the
community's preference (opinion within the nation divided
too, per L.06's no-monolith rule) but which GOVERNANCE
system speaks for which land: a jurisdictional question
Canada spent 150 years engineering into existence and
cannot outsource back to its victims as a survey. The
mature takeaways: (1) reconciliation's hardest files are
nation-REBUILDING files — governance reunification is
internal work outsiders can fund and respect but not
shortcut; (2) every future resource decade (L.39's
critical minerals) runs through this exact structure, so
the Wet'suwet'en pattern is the test case, not the
anomaly; (3) anyone offering you a one-sentence version
of this conflict — either sentence — is selling
something (the course's oldest rule, at maximum
difficulty).
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** *(citizenship-style)* Correct the stale image: give four
present-tense facts about Indigenous peoples in Canada that
contradict the historical-chapter framing.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Any four: 1.8M+ people, the youngest and fastest-growing
population (median age ~8 years below national); majority
live in cities, not on reserves; modern treaties and
self-government agreements cover 40%+ of Canada's landmass,
with nations exercising law-making powers (Nunavut's public
government, Nisga'a, Yukon nations, new Métis agreements);
a booming economic sector — development corporations, equity
stakes in major projects, ~60,000 businesses; constitutional
litigation that shapes national resource policy (duty to
consult, Tsilhqot'in title); a cultural renaissance from the
Met's Great Hall (Monkman) to the Polaris Prize (Tagaq); and
Indigenous leaders in the country's highest offices — Mary
Simon as Governor General, Wab Kinew as Premier of Manitoba.
</details>

**Q2.** What precisely does "unceded" mean, why does it apply
to most of BC, and what single case made the word expensive?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Unceded: land never surrendered by treaty nor acquired by
lawful means under the Crown's own rules (the Royal
Proclamation's consent requirement, Lesson 10) — a legal
description of a gap in title's chain, not a courtesy term.
BC: colonial authorities (after early Vancouver Island
treaties and Treaty 8's corner) simply skipped treaty-making
— settlement proceeded without cessions, so most of the
province rests on unsurrendered Indigenous title. The case:
Tsilhqot'in (2014) — the Supreme Court's first declaration
of Aboriginal title over specific territory, confirming
title includes the right to control (consent to) the land's
use; every "unceded" in a BC acknowledgement now carries
that precedent's weight, which is why the word moved from
ceremony to boardroom risk models (Lesson 39).
</details>

**Q3.** What is "economic reconciliation," what drove its
2010s–20s rise, and what are the two honest critiques from
within Indigenous communities?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The shift from consultation to ownership: nations as equity
partners in pipelines, transmission, mines, and LNG (via
loan-guarantee programs), development corporations managing
claim-settlement capital, procurement set-asides, and
own-source revenue funding self-government. Drivers: the
legal revolution priced consent into projects (duty to
consult + title + UNDRIP made opposition expensive and
partnership cheap — Lesson 39's homework), settlement monies
created Indigenous capital pools, and nations chose
prosperity tools the Indian Act denied (L.17's inalienable-
land capital block, finally bypassed). Critiques from
within: (1) development-vs-land-defence — equity in
extraction can conflict with stewardship obligations and
divides communities (Wet'suwet'en's two governance voices);
(2) reconciliation-priced-in-barrels — the concern that
Canada is buying consent for its economic model rather than
changing the relationship: partnership on whose terms?
Both critiques are live inside nations — cite specific
nations and bodies, never "the Indigenous view" (Lesson 06's
rule, this lesson's refrain).
</details>
