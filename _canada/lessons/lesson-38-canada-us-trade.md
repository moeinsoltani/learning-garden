# Lesson 38: The Elephant Next Door

## Concept

Pierre Trudeau, to the Washington Press Club, 1969: *"Living next to
you is in some ways like sleeping with an elephant. No matter how
friendly and even-tempered is the beast... one is affected by every
twitch and grunt."* Fifty-plus years later it remains the single
most accurate sentence ever spoken about the Canadian economy:

```
   THE ASYMMETRY, IN NUMBERS

   ~75%  of Canadian EXPORTS go to the US
   ~50%  of Canadian imports come from the US
   ~2/3+ of this is the largest bilateral trade
         relationship in world history (with China
         contesting the title some years)

   but for the US: Canada ≈ 17% of its trade —
   its largest-or-second customer, yet one of many

   → every Canadian trade negotiation happens
     inside this asymmetry: they are our economy;
     we are their file.
```

The relationship's history is Canada's economic history: the
Reciprocity Treaty (1854–66; its US cancellation helped drive
Confederation, Lesson 13); the National Policy's tariff wall
(1879, Lesson 14); the 1911 election *lost* on reciprocity ("No
truck nor trade with the Yankees"); the Auto Pact (1965,
Lesson 21) proving sectoral integration works; the **1988
election fought entirely on free trade** — Mulroney's FTA vs
Turner's "sale of Canada" — the closest thing to an economic
referendum Canada ever held; NAFTA (1994, adding Mexico); and
**USMCA/CUSMA** (2020, the Trump-forced renegotiation) with its
sunset-review clause guaranteeing the drama recurs. Verdict of
the century-long argument: integration won every round since
1965 — and the anxiety never dies, because the asymmetry is
structural.

## How It Works

### What actually crosses the border

Not mostly finished goods — **production sharing**: the auto
industry is one factory with a border through it (a part can
cross six times before a car ships — why tariffs on Canada
instantly wound Michigan); **energy** — Canada is the US's
largest foreign supplier of oil (~60% of US crude imports),
plus gas, uranium, and electricity (Lesson 05's hydro
provinces literally power New England and New York);
critical minerals rising fast; agriculture both ways; and
services, data, and people (the professional-visa channels;
Lesson 41). Border infrastructure is strategic in a way
Europeans wouldn't guess: the **Ambassador Bridge**
(Windsor–Detroit) carries ~25% of the entire merchandise
trade — one privately owned bridge (its long-awaited public
twin, the Gordie Howe Bridge, opening in the mid-2020s);
the 2022 convoy blockade of it moved auto production within
*days* — the vulnerability demonstrated live.

### The forever disputes (know these three)

- **Softwood lumber** — the longest-running trade fight in the
  relationship (rounds since the 1980s; roots older): the US
  lumber lobby argues Canadian stumpage (provincial Crown-land
  timber fees, Lesson 05/29 — provinces own the forests) is a
  subsidy; the US applies duties; Canada wins at
  panels/WTO more often than not; settlements lapse; repeat.
  Structural because: US process empowers the domestic lobby
  regardless of past rulings, and Canadian provinces won't
  redesign land tenure for peace.
- **Supply management (dairy, poultry, eggs)** — Canada's
  quota-and-tariff system guaranteeing farmer incomes (tariffs
  ~200–300% over quota): every negotiation's US demand, every
  Canadian government's untouchable (Quebec and Ontario dairy
  ridings, all parties pledged — the political economy of
  Lesson 35's homework: entrenched policy serving a
  concentrated few, priced across everyone's groceries,
  defended as food sovereignty and rural survival). USMCA
  pried the door open ~3.6%; the structure stands.
- **Autos & steel/aluminum national-security tariffs** — the
  2018 Section 232 tariffs (steel/aluminum, on *Canada*, as a
  security threat — Ottawa's response mixed retaliation with
  bewilderment) previewed the 2020s reality: US trade law's
  unilateral instruments can hit Canada at presidential
  discretion, agreement or no agreement. The USMCA's quotas,
  content rules (75% North American for autos, labour-value
  rules aimed at Mexico), and the 2026 joint review keep the
  file permanently open.

### The playbook Canada actually runs

Five moves, visible in every round: (1) **panels and rules** —
Canada is the world's most enthusiastic user of dispute
mechanisms (FTA Chapter 19 was Canada's 1988 red line;
its USMCA survival a declared victory), because rules are the
small party's only leverage; (2) **the Team Canada charm
swarm** — premiers, ministers, and business lobbying US
*states* and Congress with the "9 million American jobs
depend on Canada trade" map (every congressional district
has a number, deliberately); (3) **precision retaliation** —
tariff lists tuned to politically sensitive states (Kentucky
bourbon, Wisconsin dairy — the 2018 list was a political
science paper); (4) **diversification announcements** — CETA
(Europe), CPTPP (Pacific), endlessly announced, marginally
used (the gravity model always wins: trade follows proximity
and the US is *right there*); (5) **domestic compensation** —
whoever the deal exposes gets paid (dairy compensation
packages after each agreement, Lesson 13's better-terms
script, trade edition).

{: .note }
> **The 1988 election — when Canada voted on the elephant**
> The only modern federal election fought on one question:
> the Canada–US Free Trade Agreement. Turner's Liberals: free
> trade would hollow out sovereignty, medicare, and culture
> ("I will not let Mulroney sell out Canada"). Mulroney's
> Conservatives won; the FTA passed; the apocalypse didn't
> arrive (medicare intact — Lesson 34; culture exempted and
> thriving — Lesson 46), and neither did the promised
> productivity miracle in full (Lesson 37's gap persisted).
> Both 1988 camps can still claim vindication — which is
> why the debate merely hibernates between US presidencies.
> When tariff politics returns to the news, you are watching
> the 1911/1948/1988 argument's next season, with the
> asymmetry unchanged and the playbook already loaded.

## Lab — Source & Scenario

**Exercise 1 — trace the tariff.** A US administration imposes a
25% tariff on Canadian steel and aluminum, citing national
security. Trace the consequences concretely: (a) to Hamilton and
the Saguenay (find why aluminum lives there); (b) to a US car
plant in Ohio; (c) Canada's response, using the playbook's five
moves; (d) the asymmetry's bottom line.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) Hamilton (steel city since the National Policy — tariff-
born industry, L.14 irony noted) faces order cancellations and
layoffs within weeks; the Saguenay's aluminum smelters —
located there for Quebec's cheap hydro (L.05: electricity is
aluminum's main input; "solid electricity" is the trader's
name for it) — lose their US premium market. Provincial
budgets and EI (L.34) absorb round one. (b) The Ohio plant's
input costs jump — North American steel prices rise toward
the tariff — squeezing the very manufacturers the tariff
claims to protect; auto-sector integration (parts crossing
repeatedly) multiplies the cost each crossing: the tariff
taxes Ohio nearly as hard as Hamilton, which is the
load-bearing fact of Canadian counter-lobbying. (c) The
playbook: dollar-for-dollar retaliation list aimed at
bourbon, Harleys, and swing-state agriculture; premiers
touring state capitols with the jobs-map; a WTO/USMCA panel
filing for the record; a CPTPP-diversification press
conference (believed by no one, including the presenter);
and a compensation package for smelter towns. (d) Bottom
line: Canada can make the tariff cost America real money and
real politics — it cannot make America need the fight to end;
75% dependence means Canada must always be at the table, and
the elephant can nap mid-negotiation. Hence the permanent
strategic conclusions: rules over strength, ratified
agreements over goodwill, and the quiet file marked
"diversification, someday, seriously."
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the quote in context.** Read Trudeau Sr.'s full
elephant line (Concept, above). Then explain: why "friendly and
even-tempered" is doing real work in the sentence; what "every
twitch and grunt" has meant concretely (name three twitches from
this lesson or the news); and why the metaphor's real sting is
that the elephant *isn't hostile*.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
"Friendly and even-tempered" concedes the best case: this is
not an enemy, the relationship is the closest between any two
large countries — allies, kin, integrated economies — and the
sentence's point survives the concession, which is what makes
it devastating: even benign American choices, made with zero
thought of Canada, land here at seismic scale. Twitches (any
three): a US interest-rate cycle repricing every Canadian
mortgage (L.40); Section 232 steel tariffs citing "national
security" against the country whose troops died beside theirs
at Juno (L.20); Buy America procurement clauses excluding
Canadian firms as a side effect of domestic politics; the
softwood lobby's quadrennial duty cycle; a border-thickening
after 9/11 that re-engineered supply chains; the IRA's
EV-subsidy drafting that briefly threatened Ontario's auto
future until frantic lobbying won "North American" wording.
The sting: hostility could be answered — deterred, allied
against, morally mobilized; <em>indifference</em> cannot. The
elephant rarely aims at Canada; it simply rolls over in its
sleep, and Canada's entire trade diplomacy (the playbook) is
the science of being unrollable-upon: making Canadian
interests visible inside American domestic politics because
that is the only theatre where the elephant makes decisions.
The metaphor's final lesson is Lesson 11's founding one,
economically restated: Canada's permanent condition is
managing an asymmetry it can never resolve, only work.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — supply management: prosecute and defend.** The
policy: production quotas plus 200–300% over-quota tariffs for
dairy/poultry/eggs, guaranteeing stable farmer incomes; every
US (and CPTPP, and consumer-advocate) negotiator's target;
every Canadian party's pledge. Write the honest prosecution
(consumer, trade, equity), the honest defence (stability,
sovereignty, rural Quebec/Ontario), identify who actually pays
and who actually collects, and explain its political
invincibility with course concepts.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Prosecution: it's a regressive grocery tax — milk, butter,
chicken, eggs priced far above world levels, hitting poor
families hardest (the GST credit's logic, L.35, run in
reverse); it costs Canada in every negotiation (concessions
elsewhere purchased dairy's protection — sectors traded away
so quota could stand); quota itself capitalized into
million-dollar-per-farm licence values, so the "family farm"
defence protects asset-holders (average dairy net worth in
the millions), and new farmers can't afford in — protection
became a moat. Defence: it delivers what markets don't —
stable supply, no taxpayer subsidies (contrast US/EU direct
payments: Canadians pay at the till; Americans at the IRS),
survival of rural communities in Quebec/Ontario where dairy
anchors whole regions (L.08's parish landscape, modern
edition), food-security insurance the pandemic made
un-theoretical, and farmer suicide/boom-bust cycles visibly
milder than US dairy country's. Who pays: every consumer,
invisibly, always. Who collects: ~15,000 quota-holding farms,
visibly, existentially — the textbook concentrated-benefit/
diffuse-cost structure (L.35's homework law), amplified by
geography: dairy ridings cluster in Quebec (where touching it
reads as attacking the nation, L.22) and rural Ontario, both
electorally pivotal, while no consumer has ever voted on
butter. Invincibility explained: all parties pledge because
the benefit is organized and the cost is not — and the 1988
note's lesson applies: the policy will erode the way
everything entrenched erodes in Canada (L.29's shuffle) —
percentage points per trade deal, compensation cheques per
percentage point, never a repeal.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** *(citizenship-style)* Roughly what share of Canadian
exports goes to the United States, what is the largest export,
and what agreement currently governs the relationship?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
About three-quarters of Canadian exports go to the US — the
largest bilateral trade relationship in history — led by
energy (Canada is the US's biggest foreign oil supplier),
followed by autos and parts (one integrated industry),
minerals, forest, and farm products. Governing agreement:
USMCA/CUSMA (2020), successor to NAFTA (1994) and the
original Canada–US FTA (1989) — with a built-in joint review
guaranteeing periodic renegotiation. The defining feature is
asymmetry: the US is ~75% of Canada's trade; Canada is a
large-but-single-digit share of America's.
</details>

**Q2.** Explain the softwood lumber dispute's mechanics and why
it never permanently resolves.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
US producers allege that Canadian provinces' stumpage fees
(administratively set prices for cutting Crown timber —
provinces own the forests, Lessons 05/29) subsidize Canadian
lumber below market; the US imposes countervailing/anti-
dumping duties; Canada challenges at panels and usually
substantially wins; a managed-trade settlement follows,
lapses, and the cycle restarts — running in rounds since the
1980s. Never resolves because both sides' structures are
fixed: US trade law lets the domestic lobby re-trigger
duties regardless of past rulings, and Canadian provinces
will not privatize Crown-land tenure to satisfy it; with
housing demand making lumber politically salient in both
countries, the dispute is less a disagreement than a
permanent institution.
</details>

**Q3.** List Canada's five-move trade playbook and the
structural reason each exists.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
(1) Rules and panels — binding dispute settlement is the
smaller party's substitute for leverage (Chapter 19 was worth
an election). (2) Sub-national lobbying — the jobs-map tour of
US states/districts, because the elephant's decisions are made
in its domestic politics, so Canada must exist there.
(3) Precision retaliation — bourbon-and-dairy lists tuned to
pivotal states: make the cost land where votes live.
(4) Diversification (CETA, CPTPP) — announced each round to
signal alternatives; underused because gravity (proximity,
language, integration) always pulls trade back south.
(5) Domestic compensation — paying exposed sectors (dairy
cheques per agreement) to keep deals politically survivable:
Lesson 13's better-terms script applied to trade. Sum: the
diplomacy of asymmetry — Canada cannot make itself
indispensable in Washington's eyes, so it makes itself
expensive to ignore and cheap to accommodate.
</details>

## Homework

Diversification is announced every round and never arrives: the
US share of Canadian exports has hovered near 75% through CETA
(Europe), CPTPP (Pacific), and decades of "third option"
strategy (Trudeau Sr.'s 1970s version). Write a paragraph on
why — using the gravity model of trade (trade falls with
distance, rises with market size and shared language/border),
the integration facts of this lesson, and one honest scenario
in which the share actually would fall meaningfully. Then
answer: is 75% dependence a vulnerability to fix or a geographic
endowment to manage? Defend your verb.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Why gravity wins: the US is the world's largest market, sharing
the world's longest border, one language, one time-zone band,
integrated supply chains (a part crossing six times can't
detour through Rotterdam), NORAD-grade trust, and professional
mobility — every variable in the gravity equation is maxed;
Europe and Asia offer smaller coefficients at 6,000 km, so
CETA/CPTPP shift basis points while the Ambassador Bridge
carries percentage points. Integration compounds it: 
production-sharing means Canadian "exports" are often US
supply chains passing through — you can't diversify half a
factory. The honest falling-share scenarios: (1) sustained US
protectionism forcing costly re-routing (share falls by
subtraction, Canada poorer — diversification as damage, not
strategy); (2) an LNG/critical-minerals buildout genuinely
opening Asian energy markets (the one physical-capacity story
with real numbers — terminals and ports change gravity's
constants where agreements don't); (3) secular US relative
decline over decades. Verdict options: "manage" —
endowment framing: proximity to the richest market is the
luckiest economic fact any country possesses; the task is
insurance (rules, lobbying, the playbook) not escape, and
pretending otherwise wastes diplomacy on communiqués. Or
"fix" — vulnerability framing: 2018 and 2025-era tariff
politics showed the elephant's temper is no longer reliably
even; when your customer becomes coercive, concentration IS
the risk, and physical diversification capacity (ports,
pipelines, LNG) is sovereignty infrastructure worth paying
uneconomic premiums for — the insurance framing's own logic,
extended. Full marks for either verb with priced trade-offs;
extra credit for noticing both verbs describe the same
policy list, differently funded — which is why every
government announces "fix" and budgets "manage."
</details>
