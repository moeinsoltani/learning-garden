# Lesson 35: Taxes and Public Money

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **marginal tax bracket** — each tax rate applies *only* to the dollars inside its income band, never to your whole income (the lesson's core idea).
> - **withholding** — tax your employer deducts from each paycheque and sends ahead to the government.
> - **GST / HST / PST** — the federal sales tax / the combined ("harmonized") version / a province's own sales tax.
> - **excise tax** (EK-size) — extra tax on specific goods (alcohol, tobacco, fuel).
> - **capital gain** — profit from selling an asset for more than you paid; the **principal residence exemption** — your own home's gain is entirely tax-free.
> - **tax deferral** — postponing tax to a later year (the RRSP's trick).
> - **general revenue** — the government's single big pot of tax money (not earmarked).
> - **fiscal capacity** — how much revenue a province *could* raise at normal tax rates — what equalization measures.
> - **CRA** — the Canada Revenue Agency (the tax authority); **T4** — the annual slip from your employer showing pay and tax withheld; **SIN** — Social Insurance Number, your tax/benefits ID.
> - **clawback** — a benefit reduced as income rises (Lesson 34).
> - **third rail** — an issue so politically dangerous no politician will touch it (from the electrified subway rail).

## Concept

Two ideas organize everything: **marginal tax brackets** (the most
misunderstood mechanism in public life) and **the tax return as
Canada's central civic transaction** — simultaneously a bill, a
benefits application (Lesson 34's field note), and an annual
national ritual with its own season.

```
   WHERE THE MONEY COMES FROM / GOES (federal, stylized)

   IN                          OUT
   ──                          ───
   personal income tax ~50%    transfers to PERSONS ~25%
   corporate tax      ~15%       (OAS/GIS, CCB, EI)
   GST                ~12%     transfers to PROVINCES ~20%
   payroll (EI/CPP*)  ~ish       (health transfer, social
   other (customs,               transfer, EQUALIZATION)
   carbon-adjacent,            direct programs & defence
   excise on the vices)        debt interest (rises with
   *CPP is a fund, not          rates — the 1990s ghost)
   general revenue

   MARGINAL BRACKETS, THE ONLY DIAGRAM THAT MATTERS
   (illustrative 2020s-shaped federal rates)
   $0 ──── ~$56k ──── ~$112k ──── ~$174k ──── $247k+
     15%      20.5%       26%         29%      33%
   Each rate applies ONLY to dollars INSIDE its band.
   A raise can NEVER reduce your take-home pay.
   + provincial brackets stack on top (each province
   its own schedule — combined top rates ~44–54%)
```

The myth this lesson exists to kill, stated once with maximum
clarity: **"my raise pushed me into a higher bracket so I earn
less now" is arithmetically impossible in Canada.** Only the
dollars above each threshold are taxed at the higher rate; every
raise increases take-home pay. (Benefit clawbacks can create
high *effective* marginal rates for some families — the true
version of the folk myth — but gross income never falls from a
raise.)

## How It Works

### The taxes you'll actually meet

- **Income tax** (federal + provincial, one return — except
  Quebec, which files separately, of course): progressive
  brackets as diagrammed; employer withholding means most
  employees pre-pay through the year and reconcile in April.
  Canada taxes **residents on worldwide income** — residence,
  not citizenship (the US contrast, Lesson 32) — with foreign
  tax credits and treaties preventing double taxation.
- **GST/HST/PST**: the 5% federal GST (Lesson 38 will note it
  cost the government that introduced it nearly every seat it
  had); provinces add their own — harmonized into HST in the
  Atlantic provinces and Ontario (13–15%), separate PST in
  BC/SK/MB, Quebec's QST, and **Alberta: nothing** (resource
  revenue instead — Lesson 05's no-PST fact). The newcomer
  trap: **sticker prices exclude tax** — the till adds it.
  Low-income households get quarterly GST credits (file taxes!).
- **Property tax**: municipalities' only real revenue
  (Lesson 29's missing tier), funding schools partly too.
- **Payroll**: CPP/QPP contributions and EI premiums —
  matched by employers; not general taxes (they buy Lesson 34's
  layers) but they're the paystub lines that surprise.
- **Carbon pricing**: the 2020s' most litigated levy
  (Lesson 29's reference) — fuel charge plus rebates in
  backstop provinces; consumer-facing design ended/retooled in
  2025 politics, industrial pricing continuing — a live file;
  know the mechanism (price + rebate) rather than the headline.
- **Capital gains**: 50% inclusion (half your gain is taxed as
  income — the 2024 attempt to raise the rate for large gains
  died with prorogation politics); **your principal residence
  is entirely exempt** — the single most consequential tax
  rule in Canada: it turbocharges housing-as-wealth-strategy
  (Lesson 40's obsession has a tax engine).

### The shelters: RRSP and TFSA in one paragraph each

**RRSP** (1957): contribute pre-tax dollars (deduction now,
~18% of earned income/year in room), grow untaxed, pay tax on
withdrawal — a *tax-deferral* machine, best when your working
bracket exceeds your retirement bracket; withdrawals for first
homes (HBP) and education borrow from it. **TFSA** (2009):
contribute after-tax dollars (~$7k/year room, cumulative from
age 18/2009), grow and withdraw **tax-free forever** — a
*tax-elimination* machine, flexible for any goal. The
practitioner's shorthand: high earners fill RRSP first,
moderate earners TFSA first, everyone eventually wants both,
and the **FHSA** (2023) staples the two together for
first-home savers (deduction in, tax-free out — the best deal
in the code, courtesy of Lesson 40's politics). Plus **RESP**
for children's education: the state matches 20% (Lesson 34) —
unmatched free money.

### The return: April's ritual

Tax year = calendar year; filing deadline **April 30**
(June 15 self-employed, payment still April 30). The CRA
(Canada Revenue Agency) receives; free software and volunteer
clinics abound; most returns are simple (T4 employment slip +
deductions). File **even with no income**: the return triggers
the CCB, GST credit, carbon rebates, provincial credits — the
welfare state's delivery rail runs through it (the design
choice: benefits as tax code rather than welfare offices —
lower stigma, but it means non-filers, often the poorest, miss
billions in owed benefits; volunteer tax clinics are among
Canada's quietly heroic civic institutions, Lesson 36).

### Equalization, with numbers this time

The constitutional commitment (s.36, Lesson 23): provinces
with below-average revenue-raising *capacity* receive federal
top-ups so all can deliver "reasonably comparable services at
reasonably comparable levels of taxation." Mechanics that
defuse 80% of dinner-table fights if actually known: **it's
paid from general federal revenue** — all Canadian taxpayers —
**not** by provincial governments cutting cheques ("Alberta
sends money to Quebec" is shorthand for "Albertans pay federal
taxes like everyone and their province qualifies for
nothing"); capacity is measured, not need; oil revenue counts
in the formula, which is why Alberta never qualifies and why
Alberta resents the *asymmetry* (its taxpayers fund a program
its government can't draw on — the honest core of the
grievance, Lessons 05, 14). Perennial receivers: Quebec
(largest total, middling per-capita), the Maritimes, Manitoba;
Ontario dipped in and out post-2008. The fights are permanent
because the formula choices (what counts as capacity? natural
resources at what inclusion?) are genuinely contestable —
Lesson 19's Rowell–Sirois logic, forever renegotiated.

{: .note }
> **"Temporary" — the income tax's running joke**
> Introduced 1917 as a temporary war measure (Lesson 18), the
> Income War Tax Act's centenary passed without repeal.
> Canadian fiscal humour peaks each April with this fact. The
> serious version: war built the modern revenue state
> (Lesson 20's tax-rental agreements centralized collection),
> and the welfare state of Lesson 34 is what made it
> permanent — you cannot have Douglas's medicare and 1913's
> tax code. Every April 30, Canadians re-ratify the postwar
> bargain, grumbling — the grumble, too, is traditional.

## Lab — Source & Scenario

**Exercise 1 — compute the actual tax, kill the myth.** Using
the illustrative brackets above (federal only, ignore credits
for simplicity): your colleague earns $110,000 and refuses a
raise to $118,000 "because it puts me in the 26% bracket and
I'll lose money." Compute federal tax at both salaries, the tax
on the raise itself, and write the two-sentence correction.
Then name the *grain of truth* cases where high effective
rates are real.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
At $110,000: 15% × 56,000 (= $8,400) + 20.5% × 54,000
(= $11,070) = <strong>$19,470</strong> (dollars above $56k to
$110k all sit in band two). At $118,000: same $19,470 on the
first $110k... precisely: 15% × 56k + 20.5% × 56k (to $112k =
$11,480) + 26% × 6,000 (= $1,560) = <strong>$21,440</strong>.
Tax on the $8,000 raise: ~$1,970 — an effective ~25% on the
new dollars; take-home rises ~$6,030. Correction: "Only the
dollars above $112k get taxed at 26% — every dollar below
keeps its old rate, so your raise nets you about six thousand
after federal tax; refusing it is donating $6,000 to prove a
myth. There is no salary in Canada where earning more means
keeping less." Grains of truth: benefit clawback zones —
CCB phase-outs, GIS's 50% reduction rate, OAS clawback — can
stack with brackets to create effective marginal rates of
60–80%+ for specific families (a single parent around
median income, a GIS senior with RRSP withdrawals); THAT is
the real policy problem the myth gestures at, and it lives in
benefit design, not brackets. (Also honest: a raise can cross
program cliffs — subsidized childcare tiers, drug-plan
thresholds — cliffs are bad design, and rare federally.)
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the newcomer's first tax season.** Your
colleague arrived in September, worked October–December,
and asks in March: "Do I even file? I barely earned anything
and I'm not a citizen." Give the complete five-part answer:
obligation, why file anyway, what they'll need, the deadline
mechanics, and the worldwide-income warning.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "(1) You became a tax resident when you settled here
in September — citizenship is irrelevant to Canadian tax; you
file for the part-year, reporting worldwide income from your
arrival date. (2) File even though you owe little or nothing —
the return is the application form for money: GST credit,
carbon rebate where applicable, provincial credits, and if
you have kids the Canada Child Benefit — plus it starts your
RRSP room accumulating and documents residency for everything
from OAS proration someday to citizenship's tax-filing
requirement (Lesson 32). Newcomers who don't file leave real
cash unclaimed. (3) You'll need: your T4 from the employer
(arrives by end of February), your SIN, arrival date, and any
foreign income figures for the pre-arrival period (reported
for credit calculations, not taxed). Free help: the volunteer
tax clinics (CVITP) exist exactly for you, and certified free
software handles part-year returns fine. (4) Deadline April
30 — file even if you can't pay (penalties attach to late
FILING); refunds, which part-year newcomers usually get from
over-withholding, arrive in weeks with direct deposit.
(5) The warning: as a resident you now owe Canadian tax on
worldwide income — foreign rental income, dividends, that
account back home — with treaties and foreign tax credits
preventing double taxation, and foreign assets over $100k
carry a disclosure form (T1135) whose penalties are the
classic newcomer trap. When in doubt, one hour of a
cross-border accountant in year one is the best money you'll
spend."
</details>

**Exercise 3 — equalization at the dinner table.** Your
Calgary uncle: "Alberta writes an $X-billion cheque to Quebec
every year while they block our pipelines." Correct the
mechanism honestly, concede what's legitimately aggravating,
and end with the question that reframes the fight.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "First the plumbing: no province writes any cheque —
equalization is paid from federal general revenue, the same
pot everyone's income tax fills; Alberta's government sends
nothing, Albertans pay federal taxes like all Canadians, and
because Alberta's incomes are the country's highest, its
residents contribute disproportionately to everything federal
— equalization, the military, the CBC. Quebec qualifies
because the formula measures revenue-raising capacity, and
receives the largest total (though middling per person).
Now the legitimate grievance, conceded: Albertans fund a
program their province can, by construction, never draw
from — oil revenue counts in the formula, so Alberta stays
'rich' even in busts (the 2015–16 recession while still
receiving zero was the real radicalizing moment); and yes,
there is something galling about transfers flowing toward a
province whose politics obstructs the industry generating
the fiscal capacity being shared — that's not a myth, it's a
values collision (Lesson 39). The reframing question: 'Would
you rather be the province that pays or the one that
qualifies?' — equalization's payers are, definitionally, the
rich provinces, and no Albertan actually wants to swap
economies with the recipients; the fight is really about
respect and pipeline politics wearing a fiscal costume,
which is why fixing the formula never fixes the anger
(Lessons 13's homework pattern: grievance → better terms →
grumbling continuation)."
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** *(citizenship-style)* Explain marginal taxation in two
sentences, and state the filing deadline and the reason to file
with zero income.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Income is taxed in bands: each rate applies only to the dollars
inside its bracket, so higher rates on your top dollars never
touch the dollars below — a raise always increases take-home
pay. Deadline: April 30 (self-employed file by June 15, but
payment is due April 30). File at zero income because the
return is the delivery mechanism for benefits — GST credit,
Canada Child Benefit, carbon and provincial credits — plus it
builds RRSP room and the residency/filing record citizenship
and programs later require.
</details>

**Q2.** RRSP vs TFSA in one sentence each, and who should
prioritize which?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
RRSP: pre-tax in (deduction now), taxed on withdrawal — a
deferral play that wins when your contributing bracket beats
your retirement bracket. TFSA: after-tax in, all growth and
withdrawals tax-free forever — flexible for any goal at any
bracket. Priority: high earners exploit the RRSP deduction
first; moderate/uncertain earners fill TFSA first (no
withdrawal tax, no benefit-clawback interactions later —
GIS-bound savers especially should favour TFSA); parents take
the RESP's 20% match before either; first-home savers take
the FHSA's both-worlds deal first. The universal rule: free
matches, then whichever shelter fits your bracket path.
</details>

**Q3.** What is equalization — constitutional basis, who
literally pays, who receives, and the honest core of Alberta's
grievance?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A federal program (constitutional commitment in s.36, 1982;
logic from Rowell–Sirois, Lesson 19) topping up provinces
whose revenue-raising capacity falls below a national
standard, so all can fund comparable services at comparable
tax rates. Paid from federal general revenue — all taxpayers
nationally; no province transfers to another. Receivers:
historically Quebec (largest total), the Maritimes, Manitoba;
never Alberta. The honest grievance: Albertans, with the
highest incomes, contribute most per capita to federal
revenue while their province can essentially never qualify
(resource revenue counts as capacity) — even in deep
recession — and transfers flow toward provinces seen as
obstructing the resource economy that generates the capacity.
The program is defensible; the asymmetry is real; the fight
is permanent (Lesson 13's better-terms script, fiscal
edition).
</details>

## Homework

The principal-residence exemption — every dollar of gain on
your home, tax-free, uncapped — costs the treasury more than
most programs and is politically untouchable. Write a
paragraph: trace its effects through Lesson 04 (where people
live), Lesson 34 (retirement design), and Lesson 40's coming
housing file (make a prediction); then answer — if you were
finance minister with a magic wand and no election, would you
cap it, and what would you expect to break?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Effects: the exemption makes the family home Canada's supreme
investment vehicle — leveraged, tax-free, and (L.04) supply-
constrained in the three metros where everyone lives, so
national savings pour into housing rather than businesses
(a standing productivity-gap suspect, L.37); retirement
planning (L.34) quietly assumes it — "the house is my
pension" substitutes for thin private-pension coverage,
which then makes seniors rationally ferocious against
anything lowering prices; and politically it enrolls ~two-
thirds of households as investors in scarcity, which is the
prediction for L.40: every housing-affordability policy will
stop precisely at the line where existing owners' equity
would fall — build-more yes, price-drop never. Finance-
minister answer (either defensible if priced): capping it
(say, lifetime $500k–1M) is textbook-correct — horizontal
equity between renters and owners, revenue for supply
programs, cooling the speculative premium — and would break:
retirement plans built on the promise (transition rules
mandatory), realization lock-in (seniors never selling —
worsening supply short-term), a bookkeeping nightmare of
decades-old renovation receipts, and the government
proposing it (hence 'no election' in the prompt — the
exemption is the third rail's third rail). The honest
synthesis: it's bad policy that has become, through thirty
years of compounding, the country's de facto pension system
— you can't remove the distortion without replacing the
function, which is the general law of entrenched bad policy
everywhere in this course (Indian Act, L.17; FPTP, L.28;
supply management, L.38 next phase).
</details>
