# Lesson 45: Symbols, Holidays, and Rituals

## Concept

A country's symbols are its history compressed for daily carry —
and by now you've *earned* Canada's: every flag, animal, and
holiday below is a lesson you've already taken, wearing ceremonial
dress. This lesson is the decoder ring, assembled.

```
   THE NATIONAL FURNITURE, WITH BUILD DATES

   the flag          1965 (L.21 — the Great Debate;
                     Stanley's design, RMC's colours)
   O CANADA          music 1880 (Calixa Lavallée, for
                     Quebec's St-Jean!); English lyrics
                     evolved — "in all of us command"
                     2018 (L.26's private member's bill);
                     anthem status only 1980. The French
                     and English lyrics are DIFFERENT
                     POEMS, not translations.
   the beaver        official 1975 — but on the first
                     postage stamp 1851: the fur trade's
                     engine (L.09) as national animal —
                     "the only nation with a rodent
                     rampant," worn proudly
   the maple leaf    on coins/badges since the 1800s;
                     red maple on the flag; the LEAF
                     preceded the country
   the loon/loonie   1987 (L.40); the toonie 1996
   the Mounties      1873 (L.14) — serge, Musical Ride,
                     Dudley Do-Right: symbol vs L.33's
                     institution — hold both
   lacrosse+hockey   joint national sports (1994 Act:
                     lacrosse summer — L.06's gift —
                     hockey winter)
   "eh" / "sorry"    linguistics below; several
                     provinces passed Apology Acts so
                     "sorry" can't be used against you
                     in court — the most Canadian
                     statute in existence
```

Plus the calendar — the year's civic liturgy — where the course's
whole arc replays annually: Canada Day (L.13), St-Jean (L.22),
Remembrance Day (L.18), Truth and Reconciliation Day (L.24),
Victoria Day (L.25's Crown), Louis Riel Day (L.15's flipped
verdict). Reading the calendar *is* reading the country.

## How It Works

### The calendar, decoded

- **Canada Day (July 1):** Confederation 1867 (L.13) — renamed
  from Dominion Day only in 1982 (patriation year — the
  rebrand was the point); in Newfoundland, Memorial Day's
  morning precedes it (L.18's homework — the asymmetric
  calendar's flagship case).
- **Saint-Jean-Baptiste (June 24, Quebec's Fête nationale):**
  the older nationalism's day (1834 origins) — in Quebec it
  outweighs July 1 emphatically; attending both weeks in
  Montreal is a two-solitudes field course (L.22).
- **National Day for Truth and Reconciliation / Orange Shirt
  Day (Sept 30):** 2021, Call to Action #80 (L.24) — the
  newest and heaviest; the state mourning itself, still
  finding its liturgy.
- **Remembrance Day (Nov 11):** L.18's register — mourning,
  not triumph; the poppy fortnight; not a stat holiday in
  Ontario/Quebec (schools observe instead — a live annual
  debate).
- **Victoria Day (late May):** the Crown's birthday
  (officially the sovereign's) — functionally summer's
  starting gun ("May two-four weekend" — the case of beer
  pun is intentional and licensed); Quebec counter-programs
  it as National Patriots' Day (L.12's rebels — the same
  Monday honouring the Crown in Ontario and the men who
  fought it in Quebec: the country in one statutory holiday).
- **Louis Riel Day (MB, Feb) / Family Day (most provinces) /
  National Indigenous Peoples Day (June 21, solstice) /
  Acadian Day (Aug 15, L.42) / Thanksgiving (October —
  earlier than the US version, harvest-timed, lower-key,
  no founding myth attached).**

### The soundtrack and the script

**O Canada**'s biographical joke: composed 1880 by Calixa
Lavallée *for the Saint-Jean-Baptiste celebrations* — French
Canada's hymn, adopted by English Canada decades later; the
French lyrics (unchanged since 1880: "car ton bras sait porter
l'épée, il sait porter la croix" — sword and cross!) and the
English (multiply revised; gender-neutralized 2018) share only
the tune: two founding peoples singing different poems
simultaneously in official harmony — no metaphor in this
course tries harder. Protocol: sung at everything, often
bilingually (the Habs alternate verses); hand over heart is
American import (stand, sing, that's it). **"Sorry":** the
reflexive Canadian sorry is a politeness marker ("I
acknowledge our shared space"), not an admission — but enough
sorries were entering evidence that Ontario (2009), BC, and
others passed **Apology Acts** declaring apologies
inadmissible as liability admissions: the legal system
formally accommodating the national verbal tic. **"Eh":** a
confirmation tag inviting agreement ("nice day, eh?") —
linguistically a solidarity marker; usage is real but
regional/generational; deploying it as a newcomer earns
smiles, not offence.

### Symbol vs institution: the Mountie problem

The red serge Mountie is Canada's most exported symbol
(carefully licensed by the RCMP — Disney once held marketing
rights, a sentence to sit with) — and Lesson 33 gave you the
institution's honest file (contract-policing strains,
oversight debates), while Lessons 14/17 gave you the force's
founding functions (clearing the plains for the railway;
enforcing the pass system; collecting children for the
schools). The literate Canadian position isn't choosing
between the postcard and the file — it's *holding both*: the
Musical Ride is genuinely beloved; the history is genuinely
carried; the institution is genuinely contested; symbols
mature by acquiring their footnotes (the flag did — L.21's
homework rule — and the Mountie is mid-process, the
serge increasingly shared with Indigenous officers,
turban-wearing since 1990, L.43).

### Tim Hortons: folk religion, audited

The double-double, the roll-up-the-rim, "Timmies" as the
hockey-parent's chapel — the chain functions as Canada's
vernacular commons (its ads are national-identity essays with
coffee attached). The audit footnotes Canadians themselves
recite: founded by a Maple Leafs defenceman (1964), sold to
American investors, merged (2014) into a Brazilian-managed
conglomerate (RBI), coffee quality relitigated hourly — the
national shrine is foreign-owned fast food, and the nation
knows, and the ritual holds anyway. File under this course's
deepest symbols-lesson: **authenticity is conferred by
practice, not provenance** — the flag was a committee
compromise, O Canada was borrowed from a rival nationalism's
picnic, the beaver was a hat input (L.09), and all of them
are real because Canadians keep using them. Nations are
made of maintained habits — which is the Phase 6 civic
thesis, returned as culture.

{: .note }
> **Newcomer's ritual-competence card**
> Stand for the anthem (both verses if you can — instant
> credibility); poppy from Nov 1, removed after the 11th
> (L.18); orange shirt Sept 30 (L.24); "sorry" when jostled
> — receiving one, say "no worries" or counter-sorry;
> "double-double" = two cream two sugar (order one once
> for the passport stamp); the May two-four, Canada Day
> fireworks, and — the winter rite — learn to skate,
> however badly: the attempt is the citizenship. None of
> this is required (L.32's short duty list); all of it is
> noticed, warmly.

its Call to Action number; (6) Victoria Day's Quebec
counter-holiday and its lesson-number in this course;
(7) what an Apology Act does; (8) Dominion Day became Canada
Day in which year, and why is the year eloquent?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(1) 1965; George Stanley's design, red-white-red from the
Royal Military College ribbon (L.21). (2) They're separate

(L.09) → first postage stamp (1851) → official national

Act. (5) National Day for Truth and Reconciliation / Orange
Shirt Day — Call to Action #80 (L.24). (6) National
Patriots' Day, honouring the 1837 rebels — Lesson 12: the
same Monday celebrates the Crown in one province and its
armed opponents in another. (7) Makes apologies inadmissible
as admissions of liability — legal armour for the national
tic. (8) 1982 — patriation year: the constitutional
homecoming and the holiday rebrand were one gesture
(L.23), retiring "Dominion" (L.13's euphemism) exactly when
Britain's last legal thread was cut.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — the symbol-audit method, exported.** Take any
one symbol from this lesson and write its full audit in the
course's format: build date; what it compressed; what it
omits; its footnotes acquired since; verdict on current
health. (Model provided for the beaver — pick a different
one.)

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Beaver model (yours should differ): Build: informal from
1670s (HBC coat of arms), stamp 1851, statutory 1975.
Compressed: the fur trade — the partnership economy that
mapped half a continent (L.09), industriousness-as-virtue,
the improbable national origin (a hat). Omits: whose labour
and whose lands the trade ran on (the trapline's side of
L.09's supply chain); that the beaver was nearly
exterminated FOR the symbol's industry — the national
animal as survivor of the national business. Footnotes
acquired: conservation recovery (the comeback beaver as
accidental environmental icon); engineering rehabilitation
(dam-builder as ecosystem hero — science upgraded the
rodent); perennial mockery-and-defence cycles (the "rodent
rampant" pride). Health verdict: robust — self-deprecating
enough to be safe, historied enough to be real, and
genuinely earned (no committee invented it; the economy
did). Strong non-beaver audits typically pick: the Mountie
(the hardest — symbol/institution split, L.33's file, the
serge's licensing absurdities, the turban and Indigenous-
officer footnotes as maturation); O Canada (two-poems
structure, lyric politics, the standing Indigenous edit);
or the flag itself (L.21's homework, now with forty more
years of backpacks and podiums as evidence for the
weight-transfer rule). Full marks = all five audit fields
+ one honest omission the postcard version hides.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** *(citizenship-style)* Give the dates and meanings:
Canada Day, Remembrance Day, National Day for Truth and
Reconciliation, Saint-Jean-Baptiste, Victoria Day.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Canada Day — July 1: Confederation, 1867 (Dominion Day until
1982). Remembrance Day — November 11: the 1918 Armistice;
two minutes' silence at 11 a.m., the poppy, mourning
register. National Day for Truth and Reconciliation —
September 30 (Orange Shirt Day): residential-school
survivors and victims; statutory federally since 2021, per
TRC Call #80. Saint-Jean-Baptiste — June 24: Quebec's Fête
nationale, the francophone national day (outweighs July 1
in Quebec). Victoria Day — the Monday before May 25: the
sovereign's official birthday and summer's unofficial
opening ("May two-four"); observed in Quebec as National
Patriots' Day for the 1837 rebels instead.
</details>

**Q2.** Explain the O Canada situation — composer, occasion,
the two-lyrics fact, and the 2018 change — and why the anthem
is this course's best single metaphor.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Composed 1880 by Calixa Lavallée, lyrics by Judge Routhier —
for Quebec's Saint-Jean-Baptiste festivities: it was French
Canada's patriotic hymn first, adopted gradually by English
Canada (official anthem only in 1980, a century later). The
English lyrics are not a translation but a separate poem
(R. Stanley Weir, 1908, repeatedly revised — "in all of us
command" replacing "in all thy sons command" by Parliament
in 2018); the French text is unchanged since 1880, complete
with sword and cross. Best metaphor because: two founding
peoples simultaneously sing different words — different
histories, different theologies — to one shared tune, and
call the result unity; that is Lessons 10, 13, 22, 23, and
42's entire architecture, performable in ninety seconds
before any hockey game.
</details>

**Q3.** State the "authenticity by practice" thesis with three
supporting cases from this lesson, and its limit case.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Thesis: national symbols draw reality from maintained use,
not origins — provenance is routinely absurd; practice
sanctifies. Cases: the flag (a 1965 committee compromise
bitterly opposed by veterans, now beloved beyond question —
weight transferred through two generations of ceremonies,
podiums, and backpacks); O Canada (borrowed from a rival
nationalism's picnic, unified by a century of pre-game
repetition); Tim Hortons (foreign-owned fast food operating
as folk commons because millions practice it daily); the
beaver (a hat input elevated by three centuries of habit).
Limit case: symbols whose acquired footnotes carry live
institutional harm — the Mountie, where practice alone
can't sanctify while Lesson 33's file stays open: ritual
confers authenticity, but only maintenance of the
institution behind the symbol confers honour — the
distinction between the flag's debate (settled by time)
and the serge's (settled only by reform).
</details>

## Homework

Design a NEW Canadian symbol or holiday — seriously. Identify
a lesson from this course whose content the current furniture
under-represents (candidates: the fur-trade partnership era,
the Underground Railroad's terminus, the points-system
pivot, the Charter's birthday — April 17 passes unmarked!,
the volunteer economy, the internal-migration Newfoundland
diaspora...). Specify: the symbol/date, what it compresses,
its ritual (what do people DO), the constituencies who'd
object, and its fifty-year weight-transfer plan per
Lesson 21's homework rule. One page maximum.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model (Charter Day, April 17): Compresses — 1982's
patriation and the rights architecture (L.23/31), the one
national artifact polling beloved in every province
INCLUDING Quebec (the paradox is the feature: celebrate the
document, and the missing-signature conversation happens
annually, honestly, forever — institutionalized
renegotiation, the country's actual method, L.23). Ritual:
citizenship ceremonies scheduled nationally that day
(L.32's theatre at scale); schools run an Oakes-test moot
on a live case (L.31's lab as pedagogy); the year's
landmark ruling read aloud the way other nations read
founding declarations. Objectors, honestly mapped: Quebec's
government (celebrating 1982 is celebrating the Long
Knives — mitigation: pair the day with formal annual
acknowledgment of the unsigned status, making the wound
part of the liturgy rather than papered over — the L.18
Newfoundland model of asymmetric observance); s.33-using
premiers (the day indicts the override's normalization —
which is the point); and constitutional-fatigue Canadians
(mitigation: no long weekend claimed — an observance, not
a holiday, until earned). Weight-transfer plan: seed with
the ceremonies (emotion supply, L.32), let each year's
cases accrete meaning (the anthem method: repetition +
stakes), expect indifference for a decade, statutory
status when the first generation raised with it reaches
Parliament (~2050 — the flag took twenty years, L.21;
budget thirty for a document). Any proposal earns full
marks with all five fields and one honest objector it
does NOT try to mollify — symbols that offend no one
carry nothing, which is the lesson's final teaching.
</details>
