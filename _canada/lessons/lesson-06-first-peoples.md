# Lesson 06: The First Peoples

## Concept

Before a single European ship arrived, the land now called Canada had been
inhabited for **at least 12,000–14,000 years** — not by "one people," but by
**dozens of distinct nations** with different languages (from about a dozen
unrelated language *families* — more linguistic diversity than Europe),
different economies, different political systems, and often no more in common
with each other than Portugal has with Finland.

The essential correction to make in your head right now: "Indigenous" is a
category like "European," not a culture. A Haida carver on the Pacific coast,
a Cree hunter in the boreal forest, a Mohawk farmer-diplomat in the eastern
woodlands, and an Inuk seal hunter on Baffin Island lived in worlds as
different from one another as any four peoples on Earth.

Why this lesson comes before Cartier and Champlain: everything the Europeans
did — where they could go, what they could trade, whether they survived
winter — happened inside Indigenous worlds, on Indigenous terms, for the
first two centuries. You cannot understand the fur trade, the colonial wars,
or the treaties without first seeing the nations as *nations*.

## How It Works

### The cultural regions (match them to Lesson 02's map)

| Region | Peoples (examples) | Way of life |
|---|---|---|
| **Pacific Northwest coast** | Haida, Tsimshian, Nuu-chah-nulth, Coast Salish | The salmon economy — so rich it supported dense, *sedentary* villages without agriculture; cedar plank houses, ocean canoes, totem poles, and the **potlatch** (a ceremony of status through giving, later banned by Canada — Lesson 17) |
| **Plateau & Cordillera interior** | Interior Salish, Dakelh | Salmon rivers + hunting between mountain ranges |
| **Plains** | Blackfoot Confederacy, Plains Cree, Assiniboine | Bison culture: mobile, tipi-based, organized around the herds (communal hunts like Head-Smashed-In Buffalo Jump ran for 5,000+ years); transformed by the horse (1700s) |
| **Eastern Woodlands (farmers)** | **Haudenosaunee** (Iroquois Confederacy: Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca — later Tuscarora), Wendat (Huron) | Agriculture — corn, beans, squash ("Three Sisters") — supporting towns of longhouses; sophisticated confederacy politics; *matrilineal* clans where clan mothers chose (and could depose) chiefs |
| **Eastern Woodlands / Subarctic (hunters)** | Cree, Ojibwe (Anishinaabe), Innu, Dene, Mi'kmaq (Atlantic) | Mobile hunting, fishing, and trapping across the boreal forest — the peoples who would become the fur trade's indispensable partners |
| **Arctic** | **Inuit** | Sea-mammal hunting (seal, whale, walrus) plus caribou; technologies of genius for the hardest climate on Earth — the kayak, the igloo, the qulliq (oil lamp), tailored skin clothing |

### Government, trade, and knowledge before contact

- **The Haudenosaunee Confederacy** — five (later six) nations united under
  the **Great Law of Peace**, possibly centuries before contact: a federal
  system with a grand council of 50 chiefs, consensus decision-making, and
  women holding the power of appointment. It is one of the world's oldest
  continuously operating political systems, and Canadians arguing about
  federalism in Phase 5 are late arrivals to a local tradition.
- **Continental trade networks** moved Pacific shells to the Prairies, Lake
  Superior copper to the Atlantic, and obsidian across mountain ranges —
  along routes the fur trade would later simply *reuse*.
- **Oral tradition** carried law, history, and science across generations —
  and Canadian courts (in the landmark *Delgamuukw* case, 1997) now accept
  oral histories as legal evidence of land title, a point that matters
  enormously in Lessons 17 and 44.

### Population and names

Estimates for the pre-contact population of what's now Canada range from
roughly 200,000 to over two million; what's certain is that European diseases
(smallpox above all) later killed a large majority — in some regions 90% —
which is essential context for every "empty land" claim you'll ever hear.
On names: use **First Nations, Inuit, and Métis** (or "Indigenous peoples"
collectively — plural, because *nations*); "Indian" survives only in legal
terms of art (the *Indian Act*, "status Indian" — Lesson 17); "Eskimo" is
obsolete and unwelcome in Canada. Best of all: use the specific nation's
name, and its own form — Anishinaabe (Ojibwe), Haudenosaunee (Iroquois),
Nēhiyawak (Cree).

{: .note }
> **"Since time immemorial"**
> Archaeology gives dates (a 14,000-year-old village site on BC's Triquet
> Island; Bluefish Caves in Yukon possibly older). Many nations' own histories
> say simply: we have been here since the world took its present form. Both
> framings appear in modern land claims — and Canadian law has learned to
> hear both.

## Lab — Source & Scenario

**Exercise 1 — primary source.** From the opening of the **Great Law of
Peace** (Haudenosaunee, oral tradition, as later transcribed): *"I,
Dekanawidah, and the confederated chiefs now uproot the tallest pine tree and
into the cavity thereby made we cast all weapons of war... Thus shall the
Great Peace be established."* What kind of document is this — and what does
its existence tell you that the word "prehistory" would hide?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
It's a <em>constitution</em> — a founding legal-political text establishing a
federation, complete with institutions (the council fire, the roll of fifty
chiefs), procedures (consensus, condolence ceremonies for succession), and a
founding narrative (burying weapons beneath the Tree of Peace). Its existence
tells you that centuries before Confederation, this land held polities with
constitutional law, diplomacy, and federal design — transmitted orally with
professional precision (wampum belts served as mnemonic records). "Prehistory"
implies nothing happened until Europeans wrote it down; in fact the
Europeans arrived <em>into</em> functioning international systems and spent
150 years negotiating within them. Bonus: some historians argue the
Confederacy influenced American federalist thinking — contested, but the
comparison flows in a direction "prehistory" makes unthinkable.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — scenario.** In a meeting, a colleague says: "We should add an
Indigenous design element — you know, totem poles, dreamcatchers, that kind
of thing. It's all the same vibe." Write the gentle, two-part correction you'd
give afterward in private: (1) why it's wrong, (2) what to do instead.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "Hey — small thing from earlier, meant kindly. Totem poles are
specifically Pacific Northwest coast (Haida, Tsimshian and neighbours), and
dreamcatchers are originally Anishinaabe — mixing them is like decorating a
'European' event with Viking helmets and flamenco dresses as one vibe. There
are more than 600 First Nations plus Inuit and Métis, with completely
different art traditions. What works instead: figure out <em>whose</em>
territory we're on, and engage that specific nation — commission a local
Indigenous artist and credit them. It reads as respect instead of decoration,
and honestly the result is always better art." The two moves that matter:
replace the generic category with specific nations, and convert "use their
stuff" into "engage the actual people." (Lesson 44 covers appropriation
debates properly.)
</details>

**Exercise 3 — mapping cultures to geography.** Using Lesson 02's regions,
explain *why* the Pacific coast produced dense permanent villages without
farming, while the Plains produced mobile societies — in terms of the
resource base of each region.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
The Northwest coast held a food source that behaved like a harvest without
planting: predictable, massive salmon runs (plus shellfish, eulachon, and sea
mammals) concentrated at fixed river mouths every year. Food that reliably
comes <em>to a place</em> lets people settle that place permanently, store
surplus (dried salmon), support specialists (carvers), and develop steep
social hierarchies and property systems — everything we associate with
agricultural civilizations, minus agriculture. The Plains' resource — the
bison — was equally rich but <em>mobile</em>: herds moved seasonally over vast
grassland, so societies organized for mobility: tipis (portable), dog- then
horse-transport, flexible band structures that could mass for communal hunts
and disperse after. General law worth keeping: societies shape themselves
around how their calories move. It will explain the fur trade's geography in
Lesson 09 too.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** *(citizenship-style)* What three groups of Indigenous peoples does
Canada's Constitution recognize, and what distinguishes each?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Section 35 recognizes <strong>First Nations</strong> (the many distinct
nations historically living mostly south of the treeline — Cree, Ojibwe,
Mohawk, Mi'kmaq, Haida, Dene and hundreds more), <strong>Inuit</strong> (the
people of the Arctic — tundra and coasts north of the treeline, from Yukon to
Labrador), and <strong>Métis</strong> (a distinct nation that emerged from
fur-trade unions of First Nations women and European traders, with its own
language — Michif — and homeland centred on the Red River and Prairies).
Key point: three <em>categories</em>, and within the first, hundreds of
distinct nations — never "one culture."
</details>

**Q2.** Give three specific examples of political, economic, or technological
sophistication in pre-contact North America that contradict the "empty
wilderness" picture.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Any three of: the Haudenosaunee Confederacy's Great Law of Peace — a
functioning federal constitution with consensus procedure and matrilineal
appointment power; Three Sisters agriculture supporting longhouse towns in
the eastern woodlands; continent-spanning trade networks (Superior copper,
Pacific shells, obsidian moving thousands of kilometres); Northwest coast
economies dense enough for permanent villages, professional artists, and the
potlatch property system; communal bison-hunting infrastructure like
Head-Smashed-In Buffalo Jump used for over five millennia; Inuit cold-climate
engineering (kayak, igloo, tailored clothing) that European explorers died
for lack of; and oral legal traditions precise enough that Canadian courts
now accept them as evidence of land title (Delgamuukw, 1997).
</details>

**Q3.** Why is "the Indigenous perspective" a phrase to be careful with —
and what's the better habit?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because it implies one viewpoint where there are many nations with different —
sometimes opposing — histories, interests, and positions (on a pipeline, one
First Nation may be an equity partner while a neighbouring one litigates
against it; Lesson 44). The better habit: name the specific nation or
organization ("the Haida position," "the Assembly of First Nations says," "the
Inuit of Nunavut negotiated...") and treat disagreement among Indigenous
nations as normal politics between polities — exactly as you would among
European states — rather than as a puzzle about what "they" think.
</details>

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Model (Vancouver): "Vancouver sits on the traditional territories of the
Musqueam, Squamish, and Tsleil-Waututh — three Coast Salish nations whose

the land is <em>unceded</em>, meaning it was never surrendered by treaty or

former federal lands into housing projects." Toronto model would feature the
Mississaugas of the Credit, the Toronto Purchase (Treaty 13) and its 2010
settlement; Montreal, the Kanien'kehá:ka (Mohawk) of Kahnawake and the
long-disputed question of whose traditional territory the island is; Ottawa,
the Algonquin Anishinaabe and their unresolved modern land claim covering the
capital itself. Full marks for any answer with all four elements and a
present-tense verb in the last one — the point of the exercise.
</details>
