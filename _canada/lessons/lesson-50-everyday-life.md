# Lesson 50: Everyday Life

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **GST / HST / PST** — the sales taxes added *at the till*, never shown on the shelf price (Lesson 35).
> - **tip-flation** — the grumbled trend of tip screens suggesting ever-higher percentages.
> - **Interac / e-transfer** — Canada's debit network / its email money transfer, the standard way friends pay each other back.
> - **loonie / toonie** — the $1 and $2 coins (Lessons 40/45).
> - **scrip** — substitute paper money issued by a company (Canadian Tire money).
> - **LCBO / SAQ** (say the letters / ess-ah-KEW) — Ontario's and Quebec's government liquor stores; **the Beer Store** — Ontario's separate beer retailer; **dépanneur** (day-pan-NUR) — a Quebec corner store.
> - **Timbits** — Tim Hortons donut holes; **double-double** — coffee with two creams, two sugars.
> - **klick** — slang for kilometre.
> - **"first and last"** — first and last month's rent paid up front, the standard Ontario lease deposit.
> - **block heater** — the plug-in engine warmer for −30° mornings (Lesson 51).
> - **metrication** (met-rih-KAY-shun) — the 1970s switch to metric, famously left half-finished.

## Concept

Culture's densest layer is the boring one: how you pay, shop, tip,
measure, and run errands. It's dense precisely because natives
learned it at six and can't explain it — nobody teaches you that the
price tag is lying by 13%, that milk comes in bags in Ontario, or
that the country runs on a coin called a loonie. This lesson is the
missing manual for the Canadian errand.

```
   A SATURDAY, ANNOTATED

   09:00  Timmies drive-thru — "large double-double" (L.45's
          liturgy, ordered correctly in four words)
   10:00  Canadian Tire — the everything-store national institution
          (car stuff, hockey tape, BBQs, and its own funny money)
   11:00  grocery run — bilingual boxes (L.42), milk in BAGS (ON),
          bring your own bins, tap to pay
   12:30  LCBO/SAQ/private store (province decides how you buy
          wine — L.29 in retail form)
   14:00  the bank that is one of five banks (L.40)
   15:00  hockey practice drop-off (L.48/53)
   18:00  checkout total is NEVER the shelf price (tax added after)
          tip screen suggests 18% and everyone mildly resents it
```

The through-line: everyday Canada is **regulated, regionalized, and
quietly weird** — and the weirdness (bag milk, coin names, two
measurement systems at once) is where the belonging lives: master
five oddities and you read as local.

## How It Works

### Money at street level

- **The tax surprise:** shelf and menu prices exclude sales tax —
  GST 5% federal plus provincial portions: HST 13% in Ontario,
  ~15% Atlantic, GST+PST combos out west, GST-only in Alberta
  (L.35 taught the machinery; the street lesson is *add ~5–15% in
  your head, always*).
- **Tipping is real and rising:** restaurants 15–20% pre-tax
  baseline; a couple of dollars for coffee counters is optional
  but the tablet will ask (the "tip-flation" grumble is safe
  national small talk); tip hairdressers, taxis, delivery;
  don't tip at true counters (fast food) or professionals.
- **The coins:** $1 = **loonie** (a loon on it, L.45), $2 =
  **toonie**; the penny died in 2013, so cash rounds to nickels
  while card charges stay exact — nobody blinks. Bills are
  plastic and colour-coded; the $100 is faintly suspect in
  corner stores.
- **Paying:** tap-everything is default (Interac debit is the
  domestic institution — L.40); cheques survive only for rent and
  grandparents; "e-transfer" (Interac email transfer) is how
  friends settle debts — "I'll e-transfer you" is standard
  sentence-length Canadian.

### The measurement mash-up (nobody will explain this)

Canada metricated in the 1970s (L.21's era) — partially, forever:

| Metric wins | Imperial survives |
|---|---|
| weather (°C, always) | body height/weight (feet, pounds) |
| distance/speed (km, km/h) | cooking (cups, oven °F) |
| gas (litres) | houses (square feet), lumber (2×4) |
| groceries priced/kg… | …but deli ordered in pounds; pizza in inches |

The native pattern: **°C outside, °F in the oven; kilometres on
the road, feet for your height** — run the mash-up unremarked and
you pass. ("Klick" = kilometre; "it's about twenty klicks" — L.54.)

### Retail nationalism, a field guide

- **Tim Hortons** — L.45 gave you the theology; the practice:
  double-double (2 cream 2 sugar), Timbits (donut holes; the
  kids'-hockey sponsor tier is literally "Timbits hockey" —
  L.53), roll-up-the-rim in spring.
- **Canadian Tire** — despite the name: housewares, sports,
  tools, and the beloved-anachronism **Canadian Tire money**
  (paper loyalty scrip hoarded in every junk drawer).
- **Loblaws/No Frills/Sobeys/Metro** groceries; **Shoppers Drug
  Mart** (pharmacy-plus-everything; "Shoppers"); **Costco** as
  weekend pilgrimage; **the Bay** (HBC — yes, L.09's fur company
  as a department store, 350+ years old; struggling, mourned in
  advance).
- **Alcohol is provincial** (L.29's purest retail expression):
  Ontario's LCBO and Quebec's SAQ are government monopolies-ish,
  Alberta is private, and "the beer store" is a literal Ontario
  institution; drinking age 19 (18 in AB/MB/QC); public drinking
  rules vary and are policed politely.
- **Milk in bags** — Ontario/Quebec/Maritimes sell 4L as three
  pouches; you buy a plastic pitcher, snip a corner, and defend
  the system to bewildered visitors for the rest of your life.
  (The West uses jugs and finds the East ridiculous. Both are
  correct. This is Canada — L.29, dairy edition.)

### Services, bureaucracy, dailiness

- **Health card first** (L.34): each province issues one; it's
  the wallet's most Canadian object — appointments, walk-ins,
  ER, no bill; the family-doctor shortage is the honest asterisk
  and a safe complaint.
- **"Hydro"** = your electricity bill in much of Canada
  (hydroelectric heritage, L.05/22 — "the hydro's out" confuses
  every newcomer once).
- **Two-week rhythm:** pay is biweekly; rent monthly;
  "first and last" (months' rent) is the standard lease deposit
  in Ontario.
- **Weather-driven car liturgy:** winter tires (mandatory in
  Quebec, wise everywhere — L.51), the block-heater plug dangling
  from prairie hoods, the windshield-scraper in every trunk, and
  the spring pothole discourse.
- **Garage sales, curb culture, and the "FREE" box:** suburban
  summer institutions; putting decent stuff on the curb with a
  FREE sign is normal recycling (L.36's civic-trust economy at
  the driveway scale).

{: .note }
> **Order like a local, week one**
> "Large double-double, please" (Tim's). "Box of twenty Timbits —
> assorted's fine." Grocery: bring bins/bags (plastic-bag bans),
> expect "points card?" ("no, thanks"), tap, take your receipt for
> the door-checker at Costco only. Deli: "about half a pound of
> the honey ham, sliced thin." Restaurant: water is free and
> automatic, the debit machine comes TO the table, you tip on it,
> and you can linger — no one brings the bill until you ask
> ("just the bill when you get a chance, thanks!").

## Lab — Source & Scenario

**Exercise 1 — the receipt forensics.** A menu burger is $18.00 in
Toronto. Reconstruct the plausible card total with 18% tip (tipped
pre-tax, as natives do), naming each line — then explain to a
just-landed friend why the menu "lied," and the one province where
the lie is smallest.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
$18.00 burger + HST 13% (Ontario's harmonized GST+PST, L.35) =
$20.34; tip 18% on the PRE-tax $18.00 = $3.24 (tipping pre-tax
is the traditional native calculation, though tablets often
compute post-tax — noticing that IS a Canadian hobby); total ≈
$23.58, or ~31% over menu. Why the menu "lies": Canadian retail
convention displays pre-tax prices — tax appears at the till
(unlike VAT-inclusive Europe), a transparency-of-taxation norm
that doubles as a national arithmetic drill. Smallest lie:
Alberta — GST only (5%), no provincial sales tax; the $18
burger totals ~$18.90 before tip, and Albertans mention this
(L.29/35/39 — the no-PST identity is real).
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the settling-in shopping list.** A newcomer friend
has an apartment, a job, and a Canadian winter eight weeks out.
Build their top-ten first-purchases list with one-line justifications
from this phase's lessons.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model list: (1) proper winter coat rated to real cold — L.51's
survival infrastructure, bought before the first cold snap, not
during; (2) insulated waterproof boots — sidewalk slush is the
enemy; (3) toque, gloves, scarf — L.54 for the word, L.51 for
the physics; (4) window scraper + snow brush (car owners) or

L.49's shoes-off protocol has hardware; (6) provincial health

(7) a milk pitcher if Ontario/Quebec — bag-milk compliance,

from Canadian Tire or Play It Again — L.45's "the attempt is
the citizenship," L.53's social infrastructure. Grading: full
marks for any list that puts COLD-rated gear before aesthetics
and includes the health card — the two errors newcomers
actually make (fashion coats in January; discovering the
health-card processing lag while sick).
</details>

**Exercise 3 — the mash-up test.** Write one natural Canadian
sentence each using: (a) weather, (b) a road trip, (c) your own
height/weight, (d) a recipe, (e) a deli order — using the CORRECT
measurement system for each — then state the rule a machine could
follow.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
(a) "It's minus twelve but feels like minus twenty with the
windchill" (°C, plus the windchill clause — L.51's grammar).
(b) "Kingston's about 260 klicks from Toronto — two and a half
hours if the 401's behaving" (km + highway-number-with-'the',
L.54). (c) "I'm five-ten, about a hundred and seventy pounds"
(imperial for bodies, always). (d) "Preheat to 375 and add two
cups of flour" (°F ovens, cup measures — inherited American
kitchenware). (e) "Half a pound of the smoked turkey, please"
(deli counters run imperial though the scale prints grams).
Machine rule: OFFICIAL/OUTDOOR/GOVERNMENT domains are metric
(weather, roads, fuel, printed labels — the 1970s conversion's
reach); BODY/KITCHEN/HOUSE domains are imperial (self-
description, recipes, real estate, lumber — domains where
conversion required replacing private habits and hardware, so
it never completed). Canadians run the split unconsciously;
running it correctly is among the fastest ways to sound raised
here — and EXPLAINING it, as you just did, is something most
natives can't do. (L.21's metrication, filed under "boldly
begun, quietly abandoned halfway," a recurring national
pattern.)
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** *(culture-fact round)* Name the $1 and $2 coins, what
happened to the penny, and how cash vs card totals differ because
of it.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The loonie ($1 — named for the loon on it, L.45) and toonie
($2 — "two" + loonie; national wordplay standards are modest).
The penny was withdrawn in 2013: cash transactions round to the
nearest five cents (a $9.42 total becomes $9.40 cash), while
card payments charge the exact amount — a two-price system
Canadians navigate without noticing.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is buying wine a completely different errand in
Ontario, Quebec, and Alberta — and what course-level principle does
the difference instantiate?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because alcohol retail is provincial jurisdiction: Ontario runs
the government LCBO (plus the separate, privately-owned-but-
quasi-official Beer Store, with grocery/convenience sales
arriving recently); Quebec runs the SAQ with beer/wine freely in
dépanneurs and groceries (and an 18 drinking age); Alberta
privatized retail entirely in the 1990s (19 to drink, liquor
stores everywhere). The principle: L.29's federalism reaches all
the way into your shopping bag — anything touching daily life
(booze, milk containers, health cards, school systems, minimum
wages) is provincial, so "how does Canada do X?" usually has
thirteen answers, and the native reflex is "depends on the
province."
</details>

**Q3.** Decode: "the hydro's out," "I'll e-transfer you," "grab it
at Canadian Tire — I've got Canadian Tire money," and "large
double-double with a box of Timbits."

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"Hydro's out" — the electricity is off ("hydro" = electric
utility, from hydroelectric dominance — L.05/22; Hydro-Québec,
BC Hydro, Manitoba Hydro). "I'll e-transfer you" — I'll send
money by Interac e-Transfer, the universal friend-debt
settlement (L.40's concentrated banking made one rail standard).
"Canadian Tire money" — the retailer's paper loyalty scrip,
issued since 1958, hoarded nationally, spendable in-store; a
beloved absurdity. "Large double-double, box of Timbits" — a
large coffee with two creams and two sugars plus assorted
donut holes, at Tim Hortons: the full folk-liturgy order
(L.45), executed in native word order.
</details>

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
No single right answer — grading criteria instead: the Tim's

Common findings for the reflection: the foreign moments cluster
around the tip tablet (percentage anxiety with an audience) and
bag milk (pure disbelief); the felt-like-home moment is
usually the checkout small talk or the door-hold chain (L.49)
— the protocols internalize fastest because they're rehearsed
dozens of times daily. The year-later receipt is the real
assignment: this phase's material stops being visible once
learned, exactly like the natives' version — keep one artifact
from when it was still strange.
</details>
