# Lesson 47: What Is a Canadian? (Capstone)

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **capstone** — the final stone that completes a building; a course's crowning final project.
> - **thesis** (THEE-siss) — the central claim an essay argues for; the course's "**method-thesis**" = Canada is a method (permanent negotiation), not a finished identity.
> - **counterweight** — here: a region or nation whose reality disproves any simple national story.
> - **hyphenated Canadian** — an identity joining origin + Canada (Ukrainian-Canadian); once an insult, now policy.
> - **parasitic** — living off something else; "not American" is parasitic because it needs its rival to exist.
> - **old-stock** — informal term for families in Canada for many generations.
> - **la survivance** (lah sur-vee-VAWNSS) — Quebec's survival tradition (Lesson 08); **POGG** — "Peace, Order, and good Government" (Lesson 13).
> - **alibi** (AL-ih-by) — an excuse that deflects blame; the essay must decide whether "slowly, in public" is a virtue or an alibi.
> - **romanticize** — to describe something as prettier than it really is.
> - **koan / liturgy / mosaic** — see Lessons 45–46 and 43; the capstone reuses the whole vocabulary.

## Concept

Forty-six lessons ago this course promised you could eventually
hold your own in this conversation. The conversation is now
yours to finish. First, the candidate answers Canadians
themselves have offered — each true, each insufficient, each
traceable to lessons you've taken:

```
   THE CANDIDATE ANSWERS (with their file numbers)

   "NOT AMERICAN"        the founding negative (L.11's
                         Loyalists, 1812; L.38's elephant)
                         — true, durable, and parasitic:
                         an identity that needs its rival

   THE MOSAIC            L.43's official story — true as
                         posture, exaggerated as sociology,
                         and silent on the founding pillars

   PEACE, ORDER & GOOD   the constitutional temperament
   GOVERNMENT            (L.13) vs life-liberty-happiness:
                         deference, order, insurance over
                         upside (L.41's columns) — the
                         deepest true one, least marketable

   LA SURVIVANCE         Quebec's answer (L.08/10/22):
                         a nation is what refuses to stop
                         existing — the answer English
                         Canada borrowed without noticing

   THE NORTH             the anthem's claim, the 0.3%
                         reality (L.03) — myth doing
                         real work

   THE TREATY COUNTRY    the oldest and newest answer
                         (L.10/17/24/32): Canada as a
                         relationship still being honoured
                         into existence

   POLITENESS/SORRY      the folk answer (L.45) — a real
                         behavioral pattern (accommodation
                         as habit) mistaken for niceness
```

And the thesis this course has been assembling since Lesson 10,
now stated plainly: **Canada is not a completed identity but a
method — a country held together by argument, accommodation,
and choice, permanently renegotiated and never finalized.** The
unsigned constitution that binds (L.23), two anthems in one
tune (L.45), treaties still being performed (L.17), a
federation that shuffles instead of settling (L.29) — the
pattern was never a failure to become a nation; it *is* the
nation. Your capstone task: test that thesis, or beat it with
your own.

## How It Works

### The regional counterweights (no national answer survives them)

Any "the Canadian is X" claim must clear: **Newfoundland**
(a nation that joined at 52.3% within living memory, grieving
its own day before celebrating Canada's — L.18/21);
**Alberta** (the National Policy's aggrieved creditor —
L.14/35's file makes "Ottawa" a fighting word in ways no
national story includes); **Quebec** (a nation within, running
its own answer — L.22); **the North and the fly-in
communities** (L.03/04 — for whom most "Canadian" imagery is
southern postcard); **the metros** (L.24 — majority-minority
cities whose median resident's Canada began after 1990); and
**the First Peoples** (L.06–17, 44 — for whom "what is a
Canadian" is a question about the *other* party to the
treaties). A national identity that requires forgetting any
of these is a seat map pretending to be the country
(L.28's monolith rule, final application).

### The hyphen and the oath

**Hyphenated Canadians** — Ukrainian-Canadian,
Somali-Canadian, Chinese-Canadian — were once an accusation
(L.16/18's "enemy aliens"); official multiculturalism
(L.43) made the hyphen policy, and the data (L.43's
convergence evidence) made it uncontroversial: the hyphen's
left side demonstrably does not weaken its right. The
deeper observation for your essay: in Canada the *right*
side of the hyphen is itself hyphenated — the "Canadian"
being joined is already French-and-English-and-treaty —
which may be why joining works here: newcomers aren't
asked to enter a finished identity, but a negotiation
already in progress (L.32's oath, whose 2021 text — Crown,
Constitution, treaties — is literally a summary of the
negotiation's parties). "The world needs more Canada"
(the export slogan) and "Canada is broken" (the domestic
complaint du jour) are, note, both claims about the same
method — its supply and its exhaustion.

### The method, stress-tested honestly

Your essay should not romanticize. The method's failures
are documented in your own coursework: the argument-and-
accommodation country *excluded by design* for its first
century (the Act, the Head Tax, the None-is-too-many file —
L.16/17/20); accommodation has repeatedly meant
accommodation *among elites over others' heads* (1867's
missing parties, L.13; 1981's kitchen, L.23);
never-finalizing can mean never-delivering (the water
advisories, L.24; the amending formula's freeze, L.23);
and a country held together by choice must keep being
chosen — the 1995 margin was 54,288 (L.22), and the 2020s
strains (L.24/40/43) are the method's current
maintenance bill. The strongest version of the thesis
survives these because it *predicts* them: a
negotiation-country's sins are bad-faith negotiation, and
its repairs — apologies with receipts (L.14/16/20),
courts enforcing old promises (L.15/17/42), the oath
rewritten to widen the table (L.32) — are the method
correcting itself, slowly, in public, on the record.
Whether "slowly, in public, on the record" is a national
virtue or a national alibi is precisely the judgment call
your essay exists to make.

## Lab — The Capstone Essay

**Write "What is a Canadian?" — 500 words.** Constraints:
draw on at least **five** lessons by name or content; engage
at least **one** regional or national counterweight honestly;
take a position (the course's method-thesis, a candidate
answer defended, or your own) — hedging to a survey is the
one failing grade. Write it *before* opening the model.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model essay (yours should disagree somewhere — that's the
tradition): "A Canadian is a member of a country that
decided, in 1774, not to decide — and has been governing
by that genius ever since. Britain, holding a conquered
French society, chose accommodation over erasure
(Lesson 10), and the choice became an architecture:
federalism so Quebec could exist (Lesson 13), two anthems
in one tune (Lesson 45), a constitution that binds a
province that never signed it (Lesson 23), treaties whose
performance is still being litigated into good faith
(Lessons 17, 24). Every rival answer to this question is
a fragment of that architecture: 'not American' is the
method seen from the border — the counter-revolution's
descendants (Lesson 11) preferring peace, order, and good
government to self-evident truths; the mosaic (Lesson 43)
is the method applied to newcomers — join the negotiation,
keep your name; even 'sorry' is the method in miniature,
a society lubricated by pre-emptive accommodation
(Lesson 45's Apology Acts being the giveaway that it's
structural). I must be honest about the counterweights.
The Cree of Eeyou Istchee were not accommodated; they
were administered (Lesson 17), and their present
negotiation (Lesson 44) is less the method's triumph than
its overdue extension to the people it was practiced ON
for a century — a Canadian is, whether they know it or
not, a party to treaties their state spent generations
breaking, and the citizenship oath now says so out loud
(Lesson 32). And Alberta would remind me that perpetual
renegotiation looks, from the paying end, like perpetual
extraction (Lesson 35). So my definition sharpens: a
Canadian is someone who holds membership in an
UNFINISHED agreement — and whose citizenship consists,
concretely, in keeping the agreement's arguments alive
rather than winning them. The evidence is that this is
learnable, because I am learning it: the country's
deepest institutions — the confidence convention
(Lesson 12), the notwithstanding clause's political
pricing (Lesson 31), equalization's eternal formula
fights (Lesson 35) — all assume citizens who show up to
the argument, and the country's failures — the water
advisories, the 54,288-vote near-death — are what
happens when showing up flags. A Canadian, finally, is
not a culture. It is a practice: reading both sides of
the cereal box (Lesson 42), standing for two poems in
one tune, filing the tax return that is also a benefits
application (Lesson 35), answering the jury summons
(Lesson 33), and re-choosing, each ordinary civic day, a
country whose entire history says it exists only as
long as it keeps being chosen. 152 years of evidence
suggest the practice holds. It has never once suggested
the practice is finished."
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** State three candidate answers to "what is a Canadian,"
each with its strongest evidence and its failure mode.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Examples (any three): "Not American" — evidence: two
founding refusals (Loyalists, 1812) and a political culture
measurably choosing insurance over upside (L.41); failure:
parasitic — an identity requiring its rival, mute on Quebec
and Indigenous nations who define themselves against other
poles entirely. The mosaic — evidence: world-leading
integration outcomes and the official story's real effect
on belonging (L.43); failure: exaggerated sociology
(convergence data), and a flat ontology that mis-files the
founding peoples (L.43's category objections). POGG —
evidence: the constitutional text itself, the boring-
institutions pattern (L.25–31), the insurance economy
(L.34/40); failure: a temperament isn't a membership —
it describes how Canada governs, not who Canadians are.
(Also creditable: survivance, the North, the treaty
country, politeness — each with L-numbers per the Concept
table.)
</details>

**Q2.** State the course's method-thesis in two sentences,
with its three strongest supporting artifacts and its two
strongest counter-artifacts.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Thesis: Canada is not a finished identity but a method —
argument, accommodation, and choice, institutionalized;
the country's unity lies in keeping its foundational
negotiations permanently open rather than resolving them.
Supporting artifacts: the 1982 constitution binding an
unsigned Quebec while everything functions (L.23 —
legal validity riding on managed legitimacy-gap); the
treaties as live documents with courts enforcing
150-year-old terms (L.15/17); the anthem's two different
poems in one tune (L.45) — plus honourable mentions: the
confidence convention's unwritten rule running everything
(L.12), asymmetric federalism's quiet accommodations
(L.29). Counter-artifacts: the Indian Act century —
the method's own machinery practicing administration,
not negotiation, on the First Peoples (L.17 — the thesis
survives only by reading reconciliation as the method's
overdue extension); and the delivery problem — perpetual
renegotiation coinciding with undrinkable water and a
frozen amending formula (L.23/24): never-finalizing can
shade into never-delivering, which is the alibi reading
the thesis must answer.
</details>

**Q3.** Why does this course insist that no national answer
survives the regional counterweights — and what does that
insistence do to the identity question itself?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because each counterweight falsifies a universal claim:
Newfoundland's Memorial Day morning (L.18), Quebec's
June 24 (L.22/45), Alberta's ledger (L.35), the North's
distance from its own mythology (L.03), the metros'
post-1990 majority (L.24), and the First Nations' position
as treaty counter-parties rather than members (L.17/44) —
any "the Canadian is X" must ignore at least one, and
ignoring is what the seat-map does, not what the country
is (L.28). Effect on the question: it converts "what is a
Canadian?" from a definition problem into a relationship
problem — the honest answer describes not a shared
content but a shared PRACTICE among differing parts
(the method-thesis), which is why the course's final
assignment is a conversation, not a flashcard: the
question's Canadian answer is the asking, done well,
with each of the counterweights in the room.
</details>
