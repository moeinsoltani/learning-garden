# Lesson 25: The Crown and the Constitutional Monarchy

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **constitutional monarchy** — a system where a king/queen is head of state but real power is exercised by elected government under the constitution.
> - **head of state** vs **head of government** — the person who *embodies* the country (the King) vs the person who *runs* it (the PM).
> - **Governor General (GG)** — the King's stand-in in Canada; **Lieutenant Governor** — the same role in each province. Canadian pronunciation: **lef-TEN-ant** (not "loo-tenant").
> - **reserve powers** — the tiny set of decisions a GG may still make personally (who forms a government in unclear cases; granting or refusing an election).
> - **prorogation** (pro-roh-GAY-shun) — suspending Parliament for a while *without* dissolving it for an election; all business pauses.
> - **hung parliament** — an election result where no party has a majority.
> - **throne speech** — the government's agenda, read aloud by the GG to open each parliament.
> - **honour of the Crown** — the legal doctrine that the Crown must keep its promises to Indigenous peoples honourably (Lessons 15, 17).
> - **republican** — here: someone who wants to replace the monarchy with an elected head of state (not the US party).
> - **viscount** (VY-count) — a rank of British nobility; **ermine** (ER-min) — the white fur of royal robes, a symbol of monarchy.
> - Names: **Byng** (BING), **Meighen** (MEE-en), **Michaëlle Jean** (mee-shah-EL ZHAWN), **Payette** (pay-ET).

## Concept

Canada is a **constitutional monarchy**: the head of state is the King
of Canada (currently Charles III — a separate legal crown from
Britain's, worn by the same person), represented federally by the
**Governor General** and in each province by a **Lieutenant Governor**.
And here is the concept that unlocks the whole system:

**The Crown holds nearly all the power, and exercises almost none of
it.**

```
   THE CANADIAN STATE, FORMALLY

   THE CROWN ("His Majesty in right of Canada")
   owns: executive power, command of forces, assent
   to every law, summoning/dissolving Parliament,
   appointing the PM...
        │
        │  ...ALL exercised on the ADVICE of
        ▼     ministers who hold the Commons'
   GOVERNOR GENERAL        confidence (Lesson 12's
   (the Crown's stand-in)   1848 rule — still the
        │                   whole game)
        ▼
   PM + CABINET  ◄── the people who actually decide
        │
   things happen: laws signed, wars declared,
   parliaments dissolved — in the Crown's name,
   at the government's direction
```

Why keep the costume? Because the Crown solves a real design problem:
**someone must embody the state itself, above party** — so that the
head of *government* (the PM) can be fought, mocked, and fired without
the state's legitimacy going down with them. Canada's answer: a head
of state with maximal dignity and minimal discretion. The design is
Lesson 12's Elgin signature, institutionalized: the Crown reigns; the
confidence of the elected House rules.

## Going Deeper

### The pieces

- **The King of Canada:** a distinct legal office (since the Statute of
  Westminster — the crowns are separable; Canada's succession law is
  its own). He appears on the $20 bill and coins, in the citizenship
  oath (Lesson 32), and in "His Majesty's Government"; he exercises no
  personal discretion in Canadian affairs and visits occasionally.
- **The Governor General:** appointed by the King *on the PM's advice*
  (in practice: chosen by the PM), typically for ~5 years. Recent
  convention alternates anglophone/francophone; the office has been
  held by refugees, journalists, an astronaut (Julie Payette), and
  since 2021 **Mary Simon**, the first Indigenous GG (Inuk — file with
  Lesson 24). Functions: the ceremonial 95% (assent, throne speeches,
  honours — the Order of Canada, Lesson 21 — troop visits, state
  funerals) and the constitutional 5% (below).
- **Lieutenant Governors:** the same office provincially — appointed
  federally, which mattered in old federal–provincial wars and
  occasionally still does.
- **"The Crown" as legal person:** sue the government, you sue *His
  Majesty in right of Canada* (or *of Ontario* — Lesson 29: the Crown
  is divisible by jurisdiction); Crown land, Crown corporations, Crown
  attorneys, and — load-bearing for this course — **the honour of the
  Crown** in treaty law (Lessons 15, 17): the treaties were made with
  the Crown, which is partly why the institution's abolition is more
  complicated than its critics assume (First Nations hold that their
  nation-to-Crown relationship predates and outranks the government
  of the day).

### The reserve powers — the famous 5%

The GG retains genuine discretion only where the confidence rule
itself needs an umpire: **who gets to form a government** when an
election is unclear (call on whoever can command the House — not
necessarily the biggest party, Lesson 27), **whether to grant
dissolution** to a PM who just lost confidence but wants a fresh
election instead of letting an alternative government try, and — in
constitutional-emergency theory — refusing advice that would subvert
the constitution itself.

The one great precedent is the **King–Byng affair (1926)** (promised
since Lesson 19): PM Mackenzie King, governing as a minority and
facing a scandal vote, asked GG **Lord Byng** for dissolution; Byng
refused — reasonably, by the rules: an untested alternative
(Meighen's Conservatives, the larger party!) existed — and invited
Meighen to govern; Meighen's government fell within days; the ensuing
election King fought *against the GG's interference* and won.
Results: (a) no GG has refused a PM's dissolution since — the reserve
power survives on paper, wrapped in the affair's cautionary tale;
(b) the 1926 Imperial Conference's Balfour Declaration (dominion
equality → Statute of Westminster, Lesson 19) was accelerated —
Canada's full independence owes a genuine debt to one stubborn
viscount. Modern echo: the 2008 **prorogation crisis** — a PM facing
a confidence vote asked the GG to suspend Parliament instead;
Michaëlle Jean granted it (after two hours' reflection, preserving
the mystery), the opposition coalition dissolved in the interval,
and constitutional scholars still argue. The pattern: the reserve
powers work best as *deterrent ambiguity* — everyone behaves because
nobody's sure.

### The republican question

Polls run mildly republican-curious and deeply change-averse — for
one structural reason above the sentimental ones: abolishing the
monarchy requires **unanimous consent** (the 1982 amending formula's
hardest lock, Lesson 23), i.e., reopening mega-constitutional
politics (Meech's ghost, Quebec's price list, the treaties'
Crown-partner problem, republic-model wars) to replace a
functioning ornament. Canada's operative position, once more from
the pattern book: the status quo annoys many and blocks none —
renegotiating it risks everything for symbolism. So the King stays
on the twenty.

{: .note }
> **Oath decoder (you'll meet this at Lesson 32)**
> New citizens swear allegiance to "the King of Canada, his heirs
> and successors." Read legally, this is not a pledge to a British
> family: the King of Canada is Canada's own legal embodiment of
> the state — the same "Crown" that signed the treaties and heads
> the constitution — and courts have upheld the oath on exactly
> that reading: allegiance to the constitutional order, personified.
> You may keep any private opinion of monarchy; the oath binds you
> to Canada's form of government, not to fandom.

## Lab — Source & Scenario

**Exercise 1 — sort the powers.** Sort into "theoretical (never
exercised personally)" vs "actually exercised with real discretion":
(a) refusing royal assent to a bill; (b) choosing the PM after a
hung election; (c) declaring war; (d) granting dissolution mid-term;
(e) appointing senators; (f) refusing a dissolution request;
(g) dismissing a PM.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Theoretical: (a) assent — never refused federally in Canadian
history (a lieutenant governor last reserved a bill provincially in
1961, to national astonishment); (c) war — the Crown's name, the
cabinet's decision (Lesson 20's September 10 vote was Parliament's
politics, the declaration formally the King's); (e) senators — GG
appoints, but exclusively on PM's advice (Lesson 26's whole
problem); (g) dismissal — live in Australia (1975), never used
federally in Canada; the ultimate deterrent-ambiguity power.
Real discretion: (b) hung parliaments — genuine judgment about who
can command confidence, exercised within tight conventions
(incumbent gets first try; Lesson 27's lab); (d)/(f) dissolution —
normally automatic, but King–Byng establishes refusal is
<em>possible</em> early in a parliament when an alternative
government exists; 2008's prorogation is the modern cousin.
Scoring insight: the discretionary set is exactly the set where
the confidence rule gives no unique answer — the GG is the
tie-breaking algorithm of last resort, and the system's health is
measured by how rarely the algorithm runs.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — scenario (the American question).** Your American
colleague: "So King Charles can just overrule your Parliament?
That's insane for a democracy." Give the accurate four-sentence
answer, including one thing the Crown genuinely does that a US
president can't.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model: "Opposite, actually — the King can't overrule anything: every
Crown power is exercised only on the advice of ministers who hold
the elected House's confidence, and royal assent hasn't been refused
in Canadian federal history; functionally, our head of state has
less personal policy power than your president's veto pen. The
design point is separation of <em>state</em> from <em>government</em>:
the Crown embodies the country so the prime minister can be just a
politician — fireable any afternoon by a confidence vote, no
impeachment drama required (Lesson 27). What the Crown genuinely
does that your system can't: it gives the state a non-partisan
referee for the edge cases — who forms the government when an
election is a mess — so those calls aren't made by whoever already
holds power; your equivalent moments (2000, 2020) go to courts and
brinksmanship. And one more thing: Canada's treaties with First
Nations run nation-to-Crown, a continuous legal person since 1763 —
the monarchy is, among other things, the counterparty of record to
the country's oldest contracts."
</details>

**Exercise 3 — King–Byng replay.** It's the near future: a minority
PM, eight months after an election, loses a confidence vote on a
corruption scandal and requests dissolution. The opposition leader
writes to the GG claiming she can govern with a signed support
agreement from two other parties totalling a Commons majority. You
are the GG's constitutional advisor: memo (5–7 sentences) on
options, the King–Byng precedent's guidance, and your
recommendation.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Model memo: "Your Excellency has two lawful options: grant
dissolution (an election eight months after the last), or invite
the opposition leader to form a government and test the House's
confidence. The governing conventions: a PM who has lost confidence
is entitled to <em>request</em> dissolution but not to receive it
automatically; refusal is constitutionally available where (1) the
parliament is young, and (2) a viable alternative government exists
— both conditions present here, per King–Byng (1926) and the
scholarly consensus that Byng's refusal was correct even though
the politics burned him. The signed multi-party agreement
distinguishes this case from 1926's fatal weakness (Meighen's
support was never secured — his government fell in days): viability
here is documented, not conjectural. Recommendation: invite the
opposition leader, conditional on the agreement's publication, and
state publicly that the decision applies settled convention —
sparing voters a redundant election is the convention's purpose,
and the scandal context makes granting the incumbent an escape-
hatch election particularly corrosive. Risk note: expect the
King–Byng playbook in response — the outgoing PM campaigning
against 'unelected interference' at the next opportunity; the
office's protection is scrupulous proceduralism and silence
thereafter. The deeper principle for the file: the reserve power
exists precisely so that losing the House's confidence has
consequences a PM cannot unilaterally outrun."
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** *(citizenship-style)* Who is Canada's head of state, who
represents him federally and provincially, and who actually directs
the government?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The King of Canada (Charles III) — a legally distinct Canadian
crown — is head of state, represented by the Governor General
federally (currently appointed on the PM's advice for roughly five
years) and by a Lieutenant Governor in each province. Actual
direction of government belongs to the Prime Minister and Cabinet,
who hold office by commanding the confidence of the elected House
of Commons; every Crown power is exercised on their advice, with
narrow reserve-power exceptions. Formula worth memorizing: the
Crown reigns, the Commons' confidence rules.
</details>

**Q2.** What are the reserve powers, when were they last seriously
used or tested federally, and why do they persist mostly as
"deterrent ambiguity"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The GG's residual personal discretions: choosing who forms a
government when confidence is unclear, granting or refusing
dissolution, and (in extremis) refusing unconstitutional advice or
dismissing a ministry. Tests: King–Byng 1926 — Byng refused King's
dissolution, invited Meighen, Meighen promptly fell, and King won
the ensuing election campaigning against the "interference"; no
federal refusal since. Modern test: the 2008 prorogation grant to
avoid a confidence vote. They persist as ambiguity because
codifying them would either neuter the umpire function (if
narrowed) or legitimize its use (if specified) — the current
equilibrium disciplines both PMs (who can't be sure a cynical
request will be granted) and GGs (who know 1926's political price),
which is the point.
</details>

**Q3.** Give three reasons — one legal, one Indigenous, one
pragmatic — why abolishing the Canadian monarchy is harder than
its poll numbers suggest.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Legal: the office of the King is protected by the unanimity rule of
the 1982 amending formula — all ten provinces plus Parliament —
the same threshold that hasn't been achievable for any purpose
since 1982, and reopening it invites every other constitutional
demand (Lesson 23's Meech lesson). Indigenous: the treaties are
nation-to-Crown compacts predating Canada; many First Nations
regard the Crown relationship as senior to the federal government
of the day, and unilaterally replacing the counterparty raises
genuine treaty-law and trust objections. Pragmatic: the system
works — the monarchy costs little, decides nothing, and provides
the non-partisan umpire and continuity functions; every
replacement design (elected president? appointed by whom?) imports
new problems Canada's neighbours demonstrate daily. Net: high
constitutional price, contested consent, negligible functional
payoff — so the status quo persists on inertia that is itself a
considered Canadian choice.
</details>

## Homework

The Crown is Canada's oldest continuous institution — the same
legal person that issued the 1763 Proclamation, signed the numbered
treaties, assented to the Quebec Act, and appears in the 2021
appointment of an Inuk Governor General. Write a paragraph arguing
either (a) this continuity is a genuine asset for reconciliation
(the counterparty still exists and can be held to its word), or
(b) it's a legal fiction that lets the actual responsible party —
the government of the day — hide behind ermine. Then write one
sentence conceding the best point of the side you didn't take.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Strong (a): The honour of the Crown doctrine (Lessons 15, 17) only
has teeth because the Crown is one continuous person across
centuries — Manitoba Métis Federation succeeded in 2013 against
promises made in 1870 precisely because no statute of limitations
runs against the Crown's honour and no change of government resets
the counterparty; First Nations negotiators consistently invoke the
Crown relationship as the stable ground beneath transient
ministries, and Mary Simon's appointment closes a circle the
institution itself keeps legible. Strong (b): "The Crown" signed
nothing — ministers did, and the fiction lets each government
treat breaches as a predecessor's file while claiming the
relationship's dignity; treaties were honoured or broken by
cabinets responding to voters, and reconciliation advances when
<em>governments</em> are sued, ordered, and embarrassed — the
ermine adds ceremony, not accountability (the Queen never answered
for the pass system; departments did, eventually, in court).
Concessions: from (a) — it is true that Crown continuity has
historically served as cover for governmental discontinuity of
responsibility; from (b) — it is true that the continuous-person
doctrine is the specific legal hook on which every major treaty
victory has hung, and no plaintiff would trade it away. Either
paragraph earns full marks by using at least two concrete cases
from earlier lessons rather than abstractions.
</details>
