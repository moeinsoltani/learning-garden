# Lesson 48: Growing Up Canadian

{: .note }
> **Words to know** *(simple definitions + how to say the names)*
> - **shibboleth** (SHIB-oh-leth) — a small word or habit that instantly reveals whether you belong to a group ("grade 9" vs "9th grade").
> - **sugar shack** (in Quebec: *cabane à sucre*, kah-BAN ah SOOK-rr) — the maple-syrup farm you visit in early spring to eat syrup poured on snow.
> - **recorder** — the cheap plastic flute every Canadian child is forced to learn in music class.
> - **house league** — the friendly, everyone-plays level of kids' sports (opposite of competitive "rep" teams).
> - **pledge sheet** — the form where neighbours promise money per kilometre for a charity run.
> - **PA (system)** — the loudspeaker system in schools ("announcements over the PA").
> - **prom** — the formal graduation dance; **sleepover camp** — summer camp where kids stay for weeks.
> - **cottage / cabin / camp** — the summer lake house; the word depends on region (Lesson 54 maps it).
> - **potluck** — a shared meal where every guest brings a dish.
> - **4-H** — a rural youth club (farming/skills); **powwow** (POW-wow) — an Indigenous celebration with dancing, drumming, and regalia; **Katimavik** (kah-TIM-ah-vik) — a national youth-volunteering program.
> - Names: **Terry Fox** — the runner with an amputated leg who ran across Canada for cancer research in 1980 (Lesson 53 tells the full story).

## Concept

When two Canadian adults meet, they can run a whole conversation on a
shared childhood neither has mentioned before: the pizza day, the
snow-day radio vigil, the Terry Fox Run, the recorder unit in music
class, the field trip to the sugar shack or the science centre. None
of it is in Discover Canada. All of it is what "being Canadian" is
actually made of for the people you'll work and live with — and this
lesson is your borrowed childhood, so the references land.

```
   THE CANADIAN CHILDHOOD, SCHEMATICALLY

   Sept          school starts (labour day weekend ends it all)
   Oct           Thanksgiving (the October one); Halloween is
                 ENORMOUS — costumes engineered to fit over parkas
   Nov 11        Remembrance assembly: poppies, In Flanders Fields
                 recited by a nervous grade 6 (L.18, met at age 8)
   Dec           holiday concert (carefully multi-faith, L.43)
   Jan–Mar       the long winter: rink season, snow pants mandatory,
                 indoor recess at -25°C, March Break as survival raft
   Apr–June      the melt; year-end field trips; Terry Fox Run
                 (some schools run it in Sept — his marathon month)
   July–Aug      THE SUMMER: day camp, the cottage/lake/camping,
                 late northern sunsets, no school uniforms missed
```

The deep pattern to notice: a Canadian childhood is **seasonal,
public, and mildly institutional** — organized around public school,
public rinks, public libraries, and a calendar of low-key rituals
that everyone does and nobody thinks of as rituals.

## Going Deeper

### School, the shared operating system

- **Structure:** public school is free, local, and where ~92% of kids
  go; kindergarten around age 5, then Grades 1–12 (Quebec ends at
  Grade 11 and inserts CEGEP — L.22's education reforms live on).
  You say "**grade 9**," never "9th grade" — instant shibboleth.
  Education is provincial (L.29), so details differ by province;
  there's no national curriculum and no national exam.
- **Texture:** no uniforms at most public schools; teachers addressed
  Mr./Ms.; the anthem over the PA every morning (kids mumble it;
  the mumbling is traditional); "show and tell"; the gym that smells
  the same in every province; pizza day and bake sales funding
  everything; French class from mid-elementary (resented, then
  regretted — L.42), with **French immersion** as the ambitious
  parents' stream.
- **Rituals every Canadian did:** the **Terry Fox Run** (annual
  school fundraiser since 1981 — Lesson 53 gives Terry the full
  treatment he deserves); Remembrance Day assemblies; winter and
  spring concerts; track-and-field day; the grade 8 (or 6) year-end
  trip — often Ottawa, to see Parliament (L.26, aged 13, bored,
  retroactively grateful).
- **High school:** part-time jobs from ~15–16 (grocery store,
  Timmies — L.45 — lifeguarding) are normal and respected;
  driver's licence at 16 as the freedom event; "prom" exists but
  smaller-stakes than the US movie version; graduation is
  celebrated but university admissions have no SAT — marks and
  province-specific applications instead.

### The summer counterweight

Winter's cultural twin (L.51 handles winter itself): Canadian
summers are short, luminous, and treated as sacred. The institutions:
**the cottage** (Ontario), **the cabin/camp** (elsewhere — L.54 will
map the vocabulary), the provincial-park camping trip, the lake you
swam in until your lips went blue, mosquitoes as the national bird
(joke — L.54 will teach you to make it), and day camps or the
mythic **sleepover camp** with canoes and campfire songs — the canoe
being the fur trade's canoe (L.09), demoted to childhood in three
centuries. Not every family has a cottage (they're expensive and
regional); *every* family has access to the public version —
provincial parks, city pools, splash pads. The class marker is real
but soft.

### What's different from what you might expect

- **Less flag, more shrug:** patriotism is quiet (L.47); kids don't
  pledge allegiance to anything; the flag appears on backpacks
  abroad more than on porches at home.
- **Multicultural as default, not event:** in the big cities (L.04,
  L.43) a school photo *is* the census; kids grow up assuming the
  potluck has samosas, pierogi, and jollof, and that "where's your
  family from?" is a normal, friendly question with a long answer.
- **Safety-net childhood:** the paediatrician visits, the
  vaccinations, the broken arm at the rink — no bill arrives
  (L.34). Kids absorb medicare as the natural order; adults defend
  it as identity (L.47's "medicare-as-flag").
- **Hockey is present even in non-hockey homes** — as background
  radio, as the neighbour kids' driveway game, as the reason gym
  class includes skating. Lesson 53 explains why opting out is
  possible but *not noticing* is impossible.

{: .note }
> **The newcomer-parent decoder**
> If you raise kids in Canada you'll meet: **snow day** (buses
> cancelled, school technically open, nobody goes — announced by
> radio/app at 6 a.m., the happiest words in a child's year);
> **pizza day / hot lunch** (pay $5, skip the lunchbox);
> **agenda** (a school-issued planner parents must sign nightly);
> **field trip forms** (sign everything); **skates and helmet**
> (gym class will eventually require them — buy used, everyone
> does); and **the birthday-party invite for the whole class**
> (inclusion norms are strong). None of this is written down
> anywhere official. Now it's written down here.

## Lab — Source & Scenario

**Exercise 1 — decode the reminiscence.** A colleague says: *"Man,
grade 7 was peak — pizza days, we won the house-league tournament,
my Terry Fox pledge sheet was longer than my arm, and we got two
snow days in one week."* Translate every reference for a newcomer,
and name what each reveals about Canadian childhood structurally.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Grade 7 (not "7th grade") — age ~12, middle school. Pizza days —
the school-lunch fundraiser ritual: cheap, communal, weirdly
beloved; reveals the low-key institutional texture (schools run on
bake-sale economics). House-league tournament — recreational
hockey's lowest, friendliest tier (L.53): organized sport as
default childhood, but "house league" specifically = the
non-elite, everyone-plays level; reveals the participation norm
over the excellence norm. Terry Fox pledge sheet — the annual
school run raising money for cancer research since 1981; a long
pledge sheet = many neighbours canvassed; reveals that the
national hero is a charity runner (L.53) and that door-to-door
civic fundraising was a childhood job. Two snow days — buses
cancelled by storm, school effectively closed, sledding ensues;
reveals winter as event and the radio-vigil ritual (L.51).
Structural summary: seasonal, public, institutional, participatory
— a childhood of shared low-stakes rituals, which is why adults
can bond on them instantly.
</details>

**Exercise 2 — the school-choice scenario.** A newcomer friend with
a 7-year-old asks: "Public, Catholic, or private? And what is French
immersion — is it only for francophone kids?" Answer as a Canadian
parent would, including what's genuinely provincial about the answer.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Public is the default and fine — most Canadian kids, including
most professionals' kids, are public-school kids; quality tracks
neighbourhood more than sector. The Catholic option: several
provinces (ON, AB, SK) publicly fund separate Catholic boards —
a Confederation-era minority-protection bargain (L.13, s.93)
surviving as a modern school choice; families of any background
often use them; Quebec deconfessionalized its boards (language,
not faith, is its dividing line — L.22/42). Private exists but is
a small minority — no US-style "public schools are failing"
narrative. French immersion: a PUBLIC-school stream where
anglophone (or allophone — L.42) kids do their schooling largely
IN French; it's explicitly for NON-francophone kids (francophones
have their own French-language boards, a Charter s.23 right —
L.31). It's popular, oversubscribed, and mildly famous as a
middle-class sorting mechanism; the honest advice: great if the
kid is happy, not a tragedy to skip. Provincial caveat: all of
this — funding, boards, immersion availability, even the number
of grades (Quebec: 11 + CEGEP) — varies by province because
education is s.92/93 provincial turf (L.29): the correct
Canadian-parent reflex is "check your province," which is the
correct Canadian reflex for most things.
</details>

**Exercise 3 — build the childhood calendar.** From this lesson,
reconstruct the September-to-August year of a 10-year-old in
Winnipeg as a month-by-month list of expectable events — then mark
which three items would differ most for the same kid in Vancouver,
and why (L.03's climate map is the answer key).

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
Winnipeg model: Sept — school starts, Terry Fox Run; Oct —
Thanksgiving dinner, Halloween costume engineered over a snowsuit
(first snow can beat Halloween); Nov — Remembrance assembly,
poppy on the parka; Dec — holiday concert, outdoor rink floods
open; Jan–Feb — deep freeze (-30 stretches), indoor recess,
skating on the river trail, a snow day or two; Mar — March Break,
maybe a first thaw; Apr — the melt, mud, street hockey resumes
full-time; May — soccer sign-up, May long weekend opens cottage/
camping season; June — track day, year-end trip, school out; July–
Aug — day camp, the lake, mosquitoes, endless light. Vancouver
diffs: (1) the snow-day items vanish — Vancouver gets rain and
the occasional chaos-snowfall that closes everything at 5 cm
(L.03: mild Pacific maritime vs Winnipeg's extreme continental);
(2) outdoor-rink culture is replaced by year-round soccer and
mountains (skiing at Grouse/Seymour as the local winter rite);
(3) Halloween needs a raincoat, not a snowsuit. The lesson under
the lesson: "Canadian childhood" is real but climate-modulated —
regional variation (L.02/03) goes all the way down to age 10.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** *(culture-fact round)* What is a "snow day"? What is the
Terry Fox Run and how often does a Canadian schoolchild do it? And
what does "grade 9" vs "9th grade" signal?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A snow day: a storm severe enough that school buses are cancelled
— school may technically stay open, but attendance collapses and
children treat it as a festival; announced early morning by
radio/app, and culturally cherished far beyond its actual
frequency. The Terry Fox Run: the annual school (and community)
charity run for cancer research held every year since 1981 in
memory of Terry Fox's 1980 Marathon of Hope — a Canadian child
does it every single school year, making it the most repeated
civic ritual of childhood. "Grade 9" is the Canadian form
("9th grade" is American) — small, but it's one of the fastest
tells of a Canadian-raised speaker, along with "washroom" and
"zed" (L.54).
</details>

**Q2.** Why does education vary so much by province, and name two
concrete childhood-visible consequences (one Quebec, one
elsewhere).

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because education is exclusive provincial jurisdiction under
ss. 92–93 of the 1867 Constitution (L.13/29) — there is no
federal ministry of education, no national curriculum, no
national exam; thirteen systems, one per province and territory.
Quebec consequence: school ends at Grade 11, followed by CEGEP
(free pre-university/technical college) before university — a
Quiet Revolution structure (L.22). Elsewhere: several provinces
(Ontario, Alberta, Saskatchewan) publicly fund Catholic school
boards as a surviving Confederation minority-rights bargain
(s.93, L.13) — so "public or Catholic?" is a normal
school-choice question in Toronto and a nonsensical one in
Vancouver. (Both illustrate the course's standing rule: L.29 —
if it touches daily life, it's probably provincial.)
</details>

**Q3.** A Canadian describes someone as having "cottage friends"
and a "hockey-parent schedule." Decode both, including the class
and regional footnotes.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"Cottage friends" — the families whose lakeside summer places you
visited growing up; signals the Ontario-centric summer institution
(the cottage; "cabin" out west, "camp" in NB/northern ON — L.54)
and, softly, class: cottages are owned by the comfortable, but the
public versions (provincial parks, camping) democratize the same
July. "Hockey-parent schedule" — the 6 a.m. rink drives, weekend
tournaments, and Timbits-to-house-league pipeline (L.53) that
organizes whole family winters; signals commitment (and cost —
minor hockey is expensive, a live cultural sore spot), and
explains the Tim Hortons cup in every rink lobby photo (L.45).
Both phrases are complete sociologies in two words — which is
what this phase exists to make audible.
</details>

## Homework

Interview one Canadian-raised person (colleague, neighbour, friend)
for fifteen minutes about their childhood using three prompts: "What
did your school do every year that you assumed was universal?",
"What did summer smell like?", and "What did you watch after
school?" Write half a page mapping their answers onto this lesson —
what confirmed it, what was regional, what was generational, and
one thing this lesson missed.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answer</summary>
<br>
A strong write-up will find: confirmations (some version of Terry
Fox, snow-day joy or its Vancouver rain-shadow absence, pizza
day, the anthem mumble); regional signatures (rink vs mountain vs
beach winters — L.02/03; cottage vs cabin vs camp — L.54; Catholic
board or CEGEP mentions pinning the province — this lesson);
generational markers (after-school TV answers date people
precisely: Mr. Dressup and Degrassi for one cohort, YTV for the
next, YouTube for the youngest — L.54's canon has the details);
and almost always one miss this lesson can absorb (common
harvests: 4-H and rodeo culture on the Prairies, Air Cadets,
Katimavik, francophone scouting, powwow summers — L.44 — or the
immigrant-kid double childhood of Saturday heritage-language
school, L.43's lived version). The exercise's real payload:
noticing that the person LIT UP — childhood nostalgia is the
fastest trust-building conversation in Canadian social life, and
you now hold enough of the shared childhood to ask real
questions about it. That's the phase's method: borrowed memory,
honestly borrowed.
</details>
