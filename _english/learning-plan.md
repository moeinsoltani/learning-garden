# English for Work Learning Plan

A curriculum for professional English as it's actually used at a software company:
Slack messages, meetings, design documents, and feedback. The goal is not essays —
it's writing accurate sentences quickly, and sounding **warm and natural** while
doing it. Grammar is taught only where it changes how your messages land, and
every rule comes with work examples, never textbook ones.

**Lab format:** this track has no terminal. Each lab is a **rewrite drill** — you
get realistic broken or blunt messages, you rewrite them yourself, then reveal a
model rewrite with the reasoning. Every lesson ends with a **phrase bank** you can
start using the same day. Checkpoints and homework follow the site's standard
hidden-answer format.

This track pairs with [Engineering Leadership]({{ '/leadership/learning-plan.html' | relative_url }}):
that one teaches *what* to say in hard situations; this one teaches *how* to say it.

{: .note }
> The early lessons target the highest-frequency error patterns for non-native
> speakers — subject–verb agreement, articles, and dropped words — because fixing
> those three makes everything you write noticeably more accurate.

## [Phase 1: Sentence Mechanics](lessons/phase-01-sentence-mechanics.html)

### [Lesson 1: The sentence core](lessons/lesson-01-sentence-core.html)
**Goal:** Every sentence you write has exactly one clear subject and one main verb — find them before you hit send.
**Topics:** Subject + verb + object as the skeleton; how English word order carries meaning; fragments (missing verb) and how they happen in fast typing; the two-second "who does what?" check.
**Drill:** Fix ten real Slack-style fragments and word-order errors.

### [Lesson 2: Articles I — a/an vs the](lessons/lesson-02-articles-a-the.html)
**Goal:** Choose between *a* and *the* correctly by asking one question: "does the reader already know which one?"
**Topics:** New vs known information (the core rule); first mention *a*, later mentions *the*; *the* for shared context ("the deploy", "the bug we discussed"); a/an by sound not spelling.
**Drill:** Insert articles into stripped-down engineering messages.

### [Lesson 3: Articles II — when to use nothing](lessons/lesson-03-articles-zero.html)
**Goal:** Master the zero article — the case that causes the most errors.
**Topics:** No article for plurals & uncountables in general statements ("tests are failing" not "the tests are failing" — unless specific ones!); uncountable tech nouns (feedback, information, code, access); proper nouns and product names; the countable/uncountable traps ("a code" ✗).
**Drill:** The classic confusions: "the production", "a feedback", "the management" — fix a realistic message full of them.

### [Lesson 4: Verb tenses for work](lessons/lesson-04-verb-tenses.html)
**Goal:** Cover 95% of workplace writing with three tenses, chosen automatically.
**Topics:** Present simple (facts, habits: "the job runs nightly"); present continuous (in progress: "I'm working on it"); present perfect (done, matters now: "I've deployed the fix"); simple past for finished + timestamped; the future with *will* vs *going to* vs present continuous.
**Drill:** Write a status update that naturally needs all three core tenses.

### [Lesson 5: Subject–verb agreement](lessons/lesson-05-subject-verb-agreement.html)
**Goal:** Make the verb match the subject even when words come between them.
**Topics:** The distance trap ("the list of issues *is*…"); uncountables take singular ("the content *is*", "the data *is*" in modern usage); *each/everyone* singular; teams and companies; the "find the real subject" habit.
**Drill:** Twenty rapid-fire corrections drawn from real engineering sentences.

### [Lesson 6: Prepositions](lessons/lesson-06-prepositions.html)
**Goal:** Get the high-frequency work prepositions right by learning them as chunks, not rules.
**Topics:** Time (in/on/at, by vs until); the tech set (deployed *to*, works *on* my machine, depends *on*, agree *with* a person / *on* a plan, discuss ∅); "explain me" and other missing-*to* traps; when in doubt, learn the whole phrase.
**Drill:** Fill-the-preposition on messages, then spot the three wrong ones planted in a paragraph.

### [Lesson 7: Connecting ideas without run-ons](lessons/lesson-07-connecting-ideas.html)
**Goal:** Join two thoughts correctly — the difference between clear and confusing messages.
**Topics:** The comma splice ("I deployed it, it broke" ✗) and its three fixes; because/so/but/although; starting sentences with But and So (fine in chat!); when to just make two sentences (usually).
**Drill:** Untangle five run-on messages into clean, short sentences.

### [Lesson 8: Punctuation for chat](lessons/lesson-08-punctuation-chat.html)
**Goal:** Use punctuation to control tone and clarity in informal writing.
**Topics:** The period that sounds angry in chat (and when to drop it); question marks on indirect questions ("I wonder if…" takes none); commas that prevent misreadings; dashes and parentheses for asides; ellipsis… reads as passive-aggressive; exclamation marks as warmth (one, not three).
**Drill:** Re-punctuate a message thread so the tone matches the intent.

### [Lesson 9: Error clinic — your personal checklist](lessons/lesson-09-error-clinic.html)
**Goal:** Build your own pre-send checklist from the patterns in Lessons 1–8.
**Topics:** The top-10 error list for this phase; self-editing in 10 seconds (subject-verb? articles? one idea per sentence?); keeping an error log; using AI/tools to check without depending on them.
**Drill:** Edit a full day of (provided) messages using only your checklist — then compare against the model edit.

## [Phase 2: Words That Sound Natural](lessons/phase-02-natural-words.html)

### [Lesson 10: Common confusions — do/make, say/tell, and friends](lessons/lesson-10-do-make-confusions.html)
**Goal:** Fix the verb pairs that instantly mark writing as non-native.
**Topics:** do/make ("make a change", "do a review"); say/tell (tell needs a person); remember/remind; borrow/lend; look/watch/see; learn these as fixed phrases with work nouns.
**Drill:** Choose-the-verb across thirty work phrases, then write five sentences using the trickiest ones.

### [Lesson 11: Phrasal verbs at work](lessons/lesson-11-phrasal-verbs.html)
**Goal:** Understand and use the ~25 phrasal verbs that dominate office English.
**Topics:** follow up, push back, roll out, roll back, look into, figure out, set up, bring up, point out, sort out, reach out, circle back, hand off, wrap up; separable vs not ("set it up" ✓); formal one-word equivalents for docs (investigate vs look into).
**Drill:** Translate a stiff, formal message into natural phrasal-verb English — and back.

### [Lesson 12: Translated-sounding sentences](lessons/lesson-12-translated-sentences.html)
**Goal:** Catch the sentences that are grammatically fine but structured like your first language.
**Topics:** Long openings before the point ("As I checked the logs and considered…" → "I checked the logs. The issue is…"); over-formal vocabulary in chat (utilize, kindly, revert); double politeness stacking; the natural English rhythm: short, front-loaded, active.
**Drill:** Ten "correct but foreign-sounding" sentences to naturalize.

### [Lesson 13: Concise word choice](lessons/lesson-13-concise-words.html)
**Goal:** Say the same thing in 30% fewer, plainer words.
**Topics:** Cutting *very/really/actually/basically*; one verb beats verb+noun ("decide" vs "make a decision" — when each fits); redundant pairs ("end result", "future plans"); simple beats fancy (use > utilize, start > commence).
**Drill:** Cut a 120-word update to 70 words without losing meaning or warmth.

## [Phase 3: Tone & Warmth](lessons/phase-03-tone.html)

### [Lesson 14: Choosing your register](lessons/lesson-14-register.html)
**Goal:** Match formality to the audience and channel automatically.
**Topics:** The three work registers (teammate chat / cross-team & manager / external & exec); what shifts between them (contractions, greetings, phrasal verbs, emoji); reading the other person's register and mirroring; the safe default when unsure.
**Drill:** One request, three registers — write all three versions.

### [Lesson 15: Softeners and hedging](lessons/lesson-15-softeners-hedging.html)
**Goal:** Take the edge off statements without hiding your meaning — the #1 warmth tool.
**Topics:** The softener toolkit (*could, might, seems, I think, probably, just, a bit*); softening disagreement ("I'm not sure that's right" vs "that's wrong"); questions as soft statements ("could we try X?"); over-hedging until you disappear — the limit.
**Drill:** Soften five blunt messages; then un-hedge two mushy ones that lost their point.

### [Lesson 16: Warm openers and closers](lessons/lesson-16-openers-closers.html)
**Goal:** Start and end messages so they feel human, not transactional.
**Topics:** Openers that cost nothing ("Hope the release went well!", "Thanks for the quick turnaround"); when to skip the opener (thread replies, urgent); closers that keep doors open ("happy to hop on a call", "no rush on this"); sign-off calibration by register.
**Drill:** Add appropriate warmth to a cold-but-correct set of messages — and identify the one where warmth padding would annoy.

### [Lesson 17: Requests that don't sound like orders](lessons/lesson-17-requests.html)
**Goal:** Ask for anything — small favors to big asks — at the right politeness level.
**Topics:** The request ladder ("do X" → "can you" → "could you" → "would you mind" → "when you get a chance, could you possibly"); matching ladder rung to imposition size; deadlines stated kindly ("by Thursday if possible — it blocks the release"); requesting from seniors vs juniors vs peers.
**Drill:** Write requests for five situations of increasing awkwardness (including chasing someone the second time).

### [Lesson 18: Disagreeing and saying no kindly](lessons/lesson-18-disagreeing-no.html)
**Goal:** Push back clearly while keeping the relationship warm.
**Topics:** Acknowledge-then-differ ("Good point — my worry is…"); disagreeing with ideas not people; the no-sandwich for requests you must refuse (appreciate + no + alternative); "let me think about it" as legitimate; pushing back upward politely.
**Drill:** Say no to a PM's scope request, disagree with a senior's design, decline a meeting — all in writing.

### [Lesson 19: Apologies and thanks that sound natural](lessons/lesson-19-apologies-thanks.html)
**Goal:** Calibrate both — non-native speakers usually over-apologize and under-thank.
**Topics:** Apology sizes (typo ≠ prod outage); "sorry for the delay" alternatives ("thanks for your patience"); the over-apologizing habit and what it signals; specific thanks beats "thanks!!" ; acknowledging help publicly.
**Drill:** Fix a message thread where someone apologizes five times; write three thank-yous of different weights.

### [Lesson 20: Sounding engaged, not flat](lessons/lesson-20-sounding-engaged.html)
**Goal:** Add the enthusiasm signals that text strips away.
**Topics:** Why neutral text reads as cold from a non-native writer; engagement markers ("Nice!", "That makes sense", "Interesting — tell me more"); reacting before redirecting; emoji as tone repair (👍 🙌 😅 and channel norms); the flat "ok." problem.
**Drill:** Reply to five teammate messages (good news, bad news, long explanation, joke, question) with warm, natural reactions.

## [Phase 4: Slack Communication](lessons/phase-04-slack.html)

### [Lesson 21: Message shapes — lead with the ask](lessons/lesson-21-message-shapes.html)
**Goal:** Structure any Slack message so a busy reader gets it in one pass.
**Topics:** Ask-first, context-second ("Can you review PR #42? It fixes the login bug…"); the no-hello rule (don't send "hi" and wait); one message per topic; formatting (bold ask, bullets, code blocks); TL;DR for long messages.
**Drill:** Restructure five buried-ask messages.

### [Lesson 22: Status updates](lessons/lesson-22-status-updates.html)
**Goal:** Write the update that answers questions before they're asked.
**Topics:** The done/doing/blocked shape; being specific ("fixed 3 of 5 test failures" not "making progress"); flagging risk early without alarm; tense choices from Lesson 4 doing real work; the weekly-summary variant.
**Drill:** Turn a messy work-in-progress situation into a crisp daily update and a weekly summary.

### [Lesson 23: Asking for help](lessons/lesson-23-asking-for-help.html)
**Goal:** Ask so that helping you is easy — and you look professional, not lost.
**Topics:** The help-request shape (goal, what I tried, exact error, specific question); don't ask to ask ("can I ask a question?"); timeboxing before asking (and saying so); asking in public channels vs DM; thanking + closing the loop ("that fixed it — it was X").
**Drill:** Rewrite two bad help requests: one with zero context, one with a five-paragraph life story.

### [Lesson 24: Answering and unblocking others](lessons/lesson-24-answering-unblocking.html)
**Goal:** Be the person whose answers actually unblock people.
**Topics:** Answer the question first, then add context; "I don't know, but X might" beats silence; partial answers with a timeline ("short answer: yes. Longer answer after standup"); linking instead of retyping; the teaching answer vs the fast answer — choosing per situation.
**Drill:** Answer four real-shaped questions: one you know, one you half-know, one that's the wrong question, one asked to the wrong person.

### [Lesson 25: Async etiquette](lessons/lesson-25-async-etiquette.html)
**Goal:** Master the unwritten rules that make teams love or resent a coworker.
**Topics:** Threads vs channel replies; @channel/@here budgets; response-time expectations by channel type (and stating yours); the after-hours message (schedule-send!); editing vs correcting-in-reply; emoji reactions as lightweight acknowledgment.
**Drill:** Ten etiquette judgment calls — decide and justify each.

### [Lesson 26: Announcements to wide channels](lessons/lesson-26-announcements.html)
**Goal:** Write the high-visibility message: deploys, incidents, changes that affect many people.
**Topics:** The announcement shape (what's changing, when, what you must do, where to ask); severity-calibrated tone (maintenance ≠ incident); the incident update rhythm (even "no update" is an update); pre-writing for review; the follow-up/all-clear.
**Drill:** Write three: a planned-maintenance notice, a live incident update mid-outage, and the all-clear with cause.

## [Phase 5: Meetings — Speaking](lessons/phase-05-meetings.html)

### [Lesson 27: Agendas and opening a meeting](lessons/lesson-27-agendas-opening.html)
**Goal:** Open any meeting with a clear frame in under a minute.
**Topics:** The opening formula (purpose, outcome, time, order); sending the agenda before (and what "agenda" minimally means); the opening phrases ("Thanks for joining — we have 30 minutes to decide X"); role assignment (notes, time).
**Drill:** Write and rehearse openings for a decision meeting, a retro, and an awkward "why did this break" meeting.

### [Lesson 28: Facilitation phrases](lessons/lesson-28-facilitation.html)
**Goal:** Steer a live discussion with ready-made language.
**Topics:** Moving on ("Let's take this offline / park that"); drawing people out ("Sara, you've been close to this — thoughts?"); managing the dominator politely; keeping time out loud ("Two minutes left on this one"); the neutral-facilitator stance when you have opinions.
**Drill:** Script your lines for five facilitation moments (derail, dominator, silence, conflict, overtime).

### [Lesson 29: Interrupting and clarifying politely](lessons/lesson-29-interrupting-clarifying.html)
**Goal:** Get into fast native-speaker conversations without feeling rude — and get clarity without embarrassment.
**Topics:** Interruption phrases ("Sorry to jump in — quick question"); the raised-hand + phrase combo on video calls; clarifying without blame ("Just to make sure I follow, we're saying X?"); asking someone to slow down or repeat gracefully; reclaiming your turn after being interrupted.
**Drill:** Script the phrases for six live moments; say them out loud until they're automatic.

### [Lesson 30: Disagreeing in real time](lessons/lesson-30-disagreeing-live.html)
**Goal:** Voice disagreement in the meeting, not in the hallway after.
**Topics:** The live disagreement shape (acknowledge → concern → question); softeners under time pressure; disagreeing with the most senior person in the room; "strong opinions, loosely held" language; when to concede gracefully out loud.
**Drill:** Respond live-style to four statements you disagree with, escalating stakes each time.

### [Lesson 31: Summarizing decisions and actions](lessons/lesson-31-summarizing-actions.html)
**Goal:** Close every discussion with spoken and written alignment.
**Topics:** The verbal recap ("So we're agreed: X by Friday, owned by Sam?"); catching silent disagreement in the recap moment; the written follow-up shape (decisions / actions+owners+dates / open questions); recap language for messy meetings that decided nothing.
**Drill:** Watch (read) a meandering meeting transcript; deliver the verbal recap and write the follow-up message.

### [Lesson 32: Presenting your work](lessons/lesson-32-presenting-work.html)
**Goal:** Talk through your own work — demos, design walkthroughs, sprint reviews — clearly and confidently.
**Topics:** The demo narrative (problem → what I did → what you're seeing → what's next); narrating while screen-sharing without ums; handling "it broke during the demo"; answering questions you don't know ("I'll check and follow up" without panic); time discipline.
**Drill:** Write the full script for a 3-minute demo of a recent piece of your work; deliver it out loud twice.

### [Lesson 33: Small talk and rapport](lessons/lesson-33-small-talk.html)
**Goal:** Survive — then enjoy — the two minutes before the meeting starts.
**Topics:** Safe topics and dead ends; the question-back habit ("Good, thanks — how was your weekend?"); remembering and reusing details (kids' names, trips, hobbies); culture notes on humor and directness; exiting small talk into the agenda smoothly.
**Drill:** Script natural exchanges for four situations: Monday call, new teammate, senior exec early on the call, returning from vacation.

## [Phase 6: Spoken Fluency](lessons/phase-06-spoken-fluency.html)

### [Lesson 34: Buying thinking time naturally](lessons/lesson-34-thinking-time.html)
**Goal:** Replace panicked silence with natural fillers while your brain catches up.
**Topics:** The filler toolkit ("That's a good question", "Let me think for a second", "So, the way I see it…"); slowing down beats speeding up; it's fine to pause — natives do; restarting a sentence gracefully ("Actually, let me put it differently").
**Drill:** Answer five unexpected questions out loud, using a different time-buyer each time.

### [Lesson 35: Paraphrasing to confirm understanding](lessons/lesson-35-paraphrasing.html)
**Goal:** Never leave a conversation unsure what was said — confirm without embarrassment.
**Topics:** The paraphrase frame ("So if I understand right, …"); confirming numbers/dates/names explicitly (the expensive ones to get wrong); paraphrasing as a stalling+understanding double tool; what to do when you understood nothing (honest recovery lines).
**Drill:** Paraphrase four dense spoken-style explanations back in one sentence each.

### [Lesson 36: Asking people to repeat or slow down](lessons/lesson-36-asking-repeat.html)
**Goal:** Handle not-understanding gracefully — the skill that removes the fear from live calls.
**Topics:** The graceful repeat requests ("Sorry, I missed that — could you say the last part again?"); blaming the channel, not yourself or them ("the audio cut out"); asking for the written version ("could you drop that in the chat?"); when accents defeat you: strategy, not shame.
**Drill:** Script requests for five situations, from a mumbled name to an entire explanation you lost.

### [Lesson 37: Contractions and spoken rhythm](lessons/lesson-37-contractions-rhythm.html)
**Goal:** Sound natural, not robotic — and understand fast natural speech better.
**Topics:** Contractions are the default in speech (I'll, we've, that's, won't); the reduced forms you must *recognize* (gonna, wanna, kinda, "dunno"); stress carries meaning ("*I* didn't say that" vs "I didn't say *that*"); chunking sentences the way natives do.
**Drill:** Read a paragraph aloud twice — full forms vs contracted — and mark the stress in five ambiguous sentences.

## [Phase 7: Design Docs & Proposals](lessons/phase-07-docs.html)

### [Lesson 38: Document structure](lessons/lesson-38-doc-structure.html)
**Goal:** Use one reusable skeleton for every design doc and proposal.
**Topics:** Context → problem → options considered → recommendation → risks → open questions; why options-considered builds trust; headings that carry the argument (a skimmer gets the story from headings alone); how long each section should be (shorter than you think).
**Drill:** Outline a design doc for a (provided) messy situation — headings and one-line section summaries only.

### [Lesson 39: Plain language](lessons/lesson-39-plain-language.html)
**Goal:** Write docs in short, active, concrete sentences — clarity is a feature.
**Topics:** Active voice as default ("the service retries" not "retries are performed"); one idea per sentence; concrete beats abstract ("takes 40s" not "suboptimal performance"); defining terms once; Phase 1 & 2 skills applied at doc length.
**Drill:** Translate a passive, abstract paragraph into plain language — target: half the words, twice the information.

### [Lesson 40: Paragraphs that flow](lessons/lesson-40-paragraphs-flow.html)
**Goal:** Build paragraphs where each sentence sets up the next.
**Topics:** Topic sentence first (the paragraph's TL;DR); old-information-before-new inside sentences; linking words used sparingly (however/therefore — not every sentence); paragraph length discipline; the one-sentence paragraph for emphasis.
**Drill:** Reorder a scrambled paragraph, then write one clean paragraph explaining a technical decision.

### [Lesson 41: Executive summaries](lessons/lesson-41-exec-summaries.html)
**Goal:** Compress any document into the five sentences an executive will actually read.
**Topics:** The exec summary formula (situation, recommendation, cost, risk, ask); writing it last, placing it first; numbers up front; no suspense — conclusion first (BLUF again); the one-sentence version for the meeting invite.
**Drill:** Write the exec summary for a (provided) 3-page migration proposal — 5 sentences max.

### [Lesson 42: Proposals that persuade](lessons/lesson-42-persuasive-proposals.html)
**Goal:** Structure a proposal so saying yes is easy.
**Topics:** Anticipating objections in the doc (the "you might be wondering" section); quantifying the pain of doing nothing; the small-ask ladder (pilot before program); tone: confident, not salesy; pairing with leadership Lessons 36/48.
**Drill:** Turn a complaint ("our CI is too slow") into a one-page proposal with a pilot-sized ask.

### [Lesson 43: Commenting on other people's docs](lessons/lesson-43-doc-comments.html)
**Goal:** Give document feedback that improves the doc and encourages the author.
**Topics:** Comment on the argument, not just grammar; question-shaped suggestions ("would X handle the Y case?"); marking severity like code review (blocking vs nit); praising specifically in doc margins; resolving vs re-litigating.
**Drill:** Leave eight comments on a (provided) flawed design doc — mixed severity, all warm.

## [Phase 8: Feedback & Hard Conversations](lessons/phase-08-feedback.html)

### [Lesson 44: Feedback language](lessons/lesson-44-feedback-language.html)
**Goal:** Put the SBI model (leadership Lesson 19) into precise, kind English.
**Topics:** Observation language ("I noticed…", "in the last two standups…") vs judgment adjectives; impact phrasing ("the effect on me/the team was…"); invitation endings ("how do you see it?"); tense and hedging choices that keep it factual.
**Drill:** Write the exact wording for three feedback conversations of increasing difficulty.

### [Lesson 45: Code review comments that don't sting](lessons/lesson-45-code-review-comments.html)
**Goal:** Review code kindly and clearly at Slack/GitHub speed.
**Topics:** Questions beat commands ("what do you think about extracting this?" vs "extract this"); "we" and the code as subject ("this function could…" not "you should…"); severity labels (blocking/suggestion/nit); praise comments; the comment you should make a call about instead.
**Drill:** Rewrite ten harsh-but-correct review comments; leave the full review on a (provided) diff.

### [Lesson 46: Performance conversations](lessons/lesson-46-performance-conversations.html)
**Goal:** Handle the highest-stakes wording: reviews, expectations, and improvement talks.
**Topics:** Clear-but-kind phrasing for "this isn't meeting expectations"; specific examples as the backbone; avoiding softener overdose when clarity is the kindness; the written summary after; scripting openings so you don't dodge.
**Drill:** Write the opening two minutes, word for word, of an expectations conversation — then the follow-up summary message.

### [Lesson 47: Delivering bad news](lessons/lesson-47-bad-news.html)
**Goal:** Say the hard thing early, clearly, and with a path forward.
**Topics:** No-burying structure (bad news in sentence one or two); ownership language without groveling ("we missed this in review" not "mistakes were made"); the path-forward paragraph; channel choice (never only Slack for big ones); the slipped-deadline message pattern.
**Drill:** Write three: a slipped deadline to stakeholders, a "we lost your data" to a customer, a cancelled project to the team that built it.

### [Lesson 48: De-escalating when someone is upset](lessons/lesson-48-de-escalating.html)
**Goal:** Respond to anger or distress in writing and speech without pouring fuel.
**Topics:** Acknowledge feeling before facts ("I get why this is frustrating"); slowing the exchange down (channel switch: "can we hop on a call?"); not matching tone; the non-defensive clarification; following up after tempers cool.
**Drill:** Reply to an angry customer email and a frustrated teammate's DM; script the opening of the call that follows.

## [Phase 9: Capstone & Habits](lessons/phase-09-capstone.html)

### [Lesson 49: Your error checklist v2](lessons/lesson-49-error-checklist-v2.html)
**Goal:** Rebuild your Lesson 9 checklist with everything the track added — your permanent pre-send filter.
**Topics:** Merging mechanics + tone + structure into one 15-second check; your personal top-5 (everyone's list differs); the send-then-edit trap; when to stop checking (fluency needs mistakes).
**Drill:** Apply the v2 checklist to a (provided) mixed bag of messages — under time pressure this time.

### [Lesson 50: Rewrite a real week](lessons/lesson-50-rewrite-week.html)
**Goal:** Capstone — apply the whole track to your own actual writing.
**Topics:** Collect a week of your real messages (Slack, comments, one doc section); rewrite the ten most important with the full toolkit; the before/after diff as your personal evidence; identifying which phase's skills you reach for most.
**Drill:** The rewrite itself, plus a half-page reflection: your three biggest before/after changes.

### [Lesson 51: Daily habits to keep improving](lessons/lesson-51-daily-habits.html)
**Goal:** Leave the course with a system, because language skill decays without use.
**Topics:** The 10-minute daily loop (read one good writer, steal one phrase, use it same day); a phrase notebook that actually gets opened; feedback sources (a trusted colleague, AI review of drafts — with judgment); measuring progress every quarter (re-do Lesson 50); speaking practice options ranked by effort.
**Drill:** Design your personal weekly practice plan — specific, small, and scheduled.
