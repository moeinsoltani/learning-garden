# Lesson 11: Phrasal Verbs at Work

## Concept

Phrasal verbs are verb + particle combinations (*follow up*, *roll out*, *push
back*) that mean something different from the verb alone. Native speakers use them
constantly in office communication — and using them makes your English sound
natural, while avoiding them (using only formal single words) can make writing
sound stiff or textbook-like.

```
   FORMAL / STIFF              NATURAL (phrasal verb)
   ─────────────              ─────────────────────
   "I will investigate it"    "I'll look into it"
   "I will contact you"       "I'll reach out to you"
   "We will deploy it"        "We'll roll it out"
   "I disagree with the plan" "I want to push back on the plan"
   "Please continue"          "Please go ahead"
```

You don't need hundreds — about 25 phrasal verbs dominate office English, and
learning those makes you sound natural in most work situations. The tricky part is
that some are **separable** (the object can go in the middle: "set it up") and some
aren't, but you can learn the common ones as chunks.

---

## How It Works

### The ~25 office phrasal verbs

The high-frequency ones, grouped by meaning:

- **Following/communicating**: follow up (check back on something), reach out
  (contact), circle back (return to a topic later), touch base (briefly check in),
  loop in (include someone), sync up (align)
- **Work/tasks**: look into (investigate), figure out (solve/understand), sort out
  (resolve), work on (be busy with), wrap up (finish), take on (accept work), hand
  off (transfer)
- **Shipping/changes**: roll out (release/deploy), roll back (revert), set up
  (configure), spin up (create/start), bring up (raise a topic), point out (note)
- **Disagreeing/deciding**: push back (resist/disagree), go ahead (proceed), rule
  out (eliminate), hold off (wait)

### Separable vs inseparable

Some phrasal verbs let the object go between the verb and particle (**separable**),
some don't (**inseparable**):

- **Separable**: "set **it** up" / "set up the server" (both work); "roll **it**
  out"; "figure **it** out." With a pronoun (it/them), it *must* go in the middle:
  ✓ "set it up," ✗ "set up it."
- **Inseparable**: "look into **it**" (not "look it into"); "reach out to **them**";
  "follow up on **it**." The object stays after the particle.

You don't have to memorize which is which — learn the common ones as chunks, and
note that pronouns (it/them) usually go in the middle for separable ones ("set it
up," "figure it out").

### Formal equivalents for docs

Phrasal verbs are great for chat and informal communication, but in *formal
documents*, single-word equivalents are sometimes preferred (they're more concise
and formal): "investigate" (vs look into), "implement" (vs set up), "resolve" (vs
sort out), "deploy" (vs roll out). Know both: phrasal verbs for natural
conversation/Slack, formal single words for formal writing when appropriate.

{: .note }
> **Phrasal verbs are a big part of sounding natural</br>**
> If your English sounds slightly stiff or textbook-like even when it's correct,
> under-using phrasal verbs is often why — you're saying "I will investigate the
> issue and contact you" where a native colleague would say "I'll look into it and
> reach out." Learning the ~25 office phrasal verbs and using them in chat is one of
> the fastest ways to sound more natural and less formal-textbook. Start with the
> ones you'd use most (follow up, reach out, look into, figure out, roll out, push
> back) and work them into your messages.

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why does using phrasal verbs make your English sound more natural, and when
might you prefer the formal single-word equivalent instead?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Using phrasal verbs makes your English sound more natural because <strong>native
speakers use them constantly in everyday and office communication</strong> — they're
the normal, conversational way to express these ideas, so using them makes you sound
like a native colleague, while <em>avoiding</em> them (using only formal single-word
verbs) makes writing sound stiff, formal, or textbook-like. "I'll look into it and
reach out once I figure out the cause" sounds like how people actually talk; "I will
investigate it and contact you once I determine the cause" sounds correct but
noticeably formal and un-conversational — the kind of thing that marks writing as
non-native (or as a formal document dropped into casual chat). Phrasal verbs are
woven so deeply into natural English that under-using them is one of the most common
reasons correct-but-stiff writing sounds "off" — the grammar is fine, but the
register is wrong for the context (too formal for chat). So learning and using the
~25 office phrasal verbs (follow up, reach out, look into, figure out, roll out, push
back) is one of the fastest ways to sound more natural in workplace communication.
When to prefer the formal single-word equivalent: <strong>in formal documents and
more formal contexts</strong>, where the single word is often more concise and more
appropriately formal — "investigate" (vs look into), "implement" (vs set up),
"resolve" (vs sort out), "deploy" (vs roll out) read better in a design doc, a formal
report, or an executive summary than the casual phrasal versions. Also, single words
are sometimes more precise or concise, which matters in formal writing. So the skill
is knowing <em>both</em> and matching to register: phrasal verbs for chat, Slack,
casual email, and conversation (where they sound natural and friendly); formal
single-word verbs for formal documents, official communication, and contexts where a
more formal register is expected. This is register-matching (which Phase 3 develops
more): the same idea has a natural-casual form (phrasal) and a formal form (single
word), and choosing the right one for the audience and context is part of sounding
appropriate — using the formal version in casual chat sounds stiff, and using the
casual version in a formal document can sound too informal. For your goal (natural,
warm communication, mostly in Slack), leaning toward phrasal verbs is usually right,
with the formal equivalents reserved for genuinely formal writing.
</details>

**Q2.** Fix and explain: "Can you look it into?" and "I'll set up it tomorrow."

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>"Can you look it into?" → "Can you look into it?"</strong> — "look into"
(meaning investigate) is an <strong>inseparable</strong> phrasal verb, so the object
must stay <em>after</em> the particle ("into"): "look into it," never "look it into."
You can't split an inseparable phrasal verb. <strong>"I'll set up it tomorrow." →
"I'll set it up tomorrow."</strong> — "set up" (meaning configure) is a
<strong>separable</strong> phrasal verb, and when the object is a <em>pronoun</em>
(it, them, him), it <em>must</em> go in the middle, between the verb and the particle:
"set it up," not "set up it." (With a full noun object, both orders work: "set up the
server" or "set the server up" — but with a pronoun, only the middle position:
"set it up.") So the two rules: (1) <strong>inseparable</strong> phrasal verbs (look
into, reach out to, follow up on, deal with) keep the object after the particle —
never split them; (2) <strong>separable</strong> phrasal verbs (set up, figure out,
roll back, turn off) can split, and a pronoun object <em>must</em> go in the middle.
The tricky part is knowing which phrasal verbs are separable vs inseparable — and
there's no fully reliable rule, so you learn the common ones as chunks (like the
prepositions and collocations of earlier lessons). But one practical shortcut covers
many cases: <strong>with a pronoun object (it/them), if it sounds wrong after the
particle, put it in the middle</strong> — "set up it" sounds wrong (→ "set it up"),
"look it into" sounds wrong (→ "look into it") — trusting your ear, developed through
exposure, handles most of it. And the pronoun-in-the-middle pattern for separable
verbs ("set it up," "figure it out," "roll it back," "turn it off") is worth locking
in because it's frequent and the "set up it" error is common and noticeable. For
inseparable ones, the object simply follows the particle, which is the more intuitive
order anyway ("look into it," "reach out to them"). Learn the common phrasal verbs as
chunks with their typical object placement, and the separation issues mostly resolve
through familiarity.
</details>
