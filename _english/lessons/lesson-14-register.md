# Lesson 14: Choosing Your Register

## Concept

"Register" is the level of formality in your communication — and matching it to the
audience and channel is the foundation of sounding appropriate. The same message
should sound different to a teammate in Slack, a manager in email, and a customer.
Getting the register wrong (too formal for chat, too casual for an exec) makes you
sound off even when the words are correct.

```
   THREE WORK REGISTERS:

   CASUAL        teammates, quick Slack — relaxed, contractions, phrasal
                 verbs, emoji ok:  "hey, can you take a look at this? 🙏"

   PROFESSIONAL  cross-team, your manager — friendly but polished:
                 "Hi Sarah, could you review this when you have a chance?"

   FORMAL        execs, customers, official — polished, complete:
                 "Hi Dr. Chen, I wanted to share an update on the project."
```

The good news for your goal: **warmth works at every register.** Casual and formal
aren't "warm" vs "cold" — you can be warm in all three; register is about
*formality* (contractions, word choice, structure), not warmth. This lesson is
about reading the situation and matching the formality level, so your messages fit
the context.

---

## Going Deeper

### The three registers and what shifts between them

- **Casual** (teammates, quick Slack): contractions (I'll, it's, don't), phrasal
  verbs (look into, reach out), casual greetings (hey, morning), emoji, short
  fragments ("on it!"), plain words. This is most of your day-to-day chat.
- **Professional** (cross-team, your manager, most email): friendly but more
  polished — still contractions and warmth, but complete sentences, a proper
  greeting (Hi [name]), fewer fragments, more structure. The safe default when
  unsure.
- **Formal** (execs, customers, official documents): polished and complete —
  fewer contractions, more formal greetings (Hi [title/name]), careful structure,
  no slang or emoji (usually). Reserved for the situations that call for it.

### Reading the register you should use

Match the register to two things: **who** (teammate = casual; manager = professional;
exec/customer = formal) and **channel** (Slack = more casual; email = more
professional; a document = more formal). A quick read: casual for your team in
chat, professional for most other work communication, formal for the small number
of exec/customer/official situations.

### Mirroring the other person

A reliable trick: **mirror the register of the person you're talking to.** If your
manager writes to you casually ("hey, quick q — can you..."), reply casually; if
they write formally, match that. If a customer is formal, be formal. Mirroring is a
safe default because it matches the register the other person set as appropriate for
the interaction — and it signals you're on the same wavelength. When you're unsure
what register to use, match theirs.

### Warmth is register-independent

The key point for your goal: **you can be warm at any register.** Casual warm: "hey!
nice work on this 🙌 could you also check X when you get a sec?" Formal warm: "Hi Dr.
Chen, thank you so much for your patience — I'm pleased to share that the project is
on track." Both are warm; they just differ in formality. So choosing a more formal
register (for an exec, say) doesn't mean being cold — you adjust the formality
(fuller sentences, formal greeting) while keeping the warmth (thanks, appreciation,
friendly framing). Register and warmth are separate dials.

{: .note }
> **When unsure, go professional-warm</br>**
> If you can't tell which register fits (a new contact, an unclear situation), the
> safe default is <strong>professional and warm</strong>: a proper greeting ("Hi
> [name]"), complete friendly sentences, contractions and warmth, but not slang or
> emoji. It's rarely wrong — casual-warm can occasionally feel too informal for a
> serious context, and formal can feel stiff for a relaxed one, but professional-
> warm fits almost everywhere. Then adjust toward casual (if they're casual) or
> formal (if it's an exec/customer) as the situation clarifies. Professional-warm
> is your reliable middle default.

## Lab — Rewrite Drill

Write the same request — "I need them to review my pull request today because it's
blocking the release" — three ways, one for each register. Keep all three warm.

**Your answer (write all three):**
1. **Casual** (to a teammate in Slack)
2. **Professional** (to your manager in Slack/email)
3. **Formal** (to a senior stakeholder you don't know well)

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answers</summary>
<br>
<strong>1. Casual (teammate, Slack):</strong> "hey! could you review my PR today
when you get a chance? 🙏 it's blocking the release so kind of time-sensitive —
thanks!" (Contractions, "hey," lowercase, emoji, phrasal-free casual, "kind of" —
relaxed and warm.)
<br><br>
<strong>2. Professional (your manager):</strong> "Hi [name], could you review my PR
today when you have a moment? It's blocking the release, so it's a bit time-
sensitive. Thanks so much!" (Proper greeting, complete sentences, still warm and
with contractions, but more polished than the casual version — no lowercase-only, no
emoji necessarily, but friendly.)
<br><br>
<strong>3. Formal (senior stakeholder you don't know well):</strong> "Hi [name], I
hope you're well. Would you be able to review my pull request today? It's currently
blocking the release, so it's fairly time-sensitive. I'd really appreciate it —
thank you." (Fuller greeting ("I hope you're well"), more complete and careful
sentences, "Would you be able to" (more formal than "could you"), fewer
contractions, no emoji — but still warm through the appreciation and friendly
framing.)
<br><br>
Notice: all three are <strong>warm</strong> (thanks, appreciation, friendly), but the
<em>formality</em> increases from 1 to 3 — more complete sentences, more formal
phrasing, fuller greetings, less casual punctuation/emoji. The register (formality)
changed; the warmth stayed constant. That's the key skill: adjust formality to the
audience while keeping warmth throughout.
</details>

## Phrase Bank

Same idea across registers:

| Element | Casual | Professional | Formal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Greeting | "hey!" / "morning" | "Hi [name]," | "Hi [name], I hope you're well." |
| Request | "could you…?" | "could you… when you have a moment?" | "Would you be able to…?" |
| Thanks | "thanks! 🙏" | "Thanks so much!" | "I'd really appreciate it — thank you." |
| Contractions | yes (I'll, it's) | yes | fewer |
| Emoji | ok | sometimes | usually not |

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What is "register," and why is matching it to the audience and channel
important even when your words are grammatically correct?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Register is the <strong>level of formality</strong> in your communication — how
casual or formal your word choice, sentence structure, greetings, and style are.
English has a range from very casual (relaxed chat with a friend) to very formal
(an official document or a message to a senior stranger), and workplace
communication uses roughly three: casual (teammates in quick Slack), professional
(cross-team, your manager, most email), and formal (execs, customers, official
communication). Matching register to the audience and channel matters even when
your words are correct because <strong>register communicates something beyond the
literal words — it signals your read of the relationship and the situation, and a
mismatch feels wrong (jarring, awkward, or inappropriate) regardless of
grammatical correctness</strong>. Two failure modes: (1) <strong>too formal for the
context</strong> — writing a stiff, formal message ("Dear colleague, I am writing to
inquire whether you would be so kind as to...") to a teammate in casual Slack sounds
odd, distant, over-serious, and un-collegial; it's grammatically fine but socially
off, making you seem stiff or like you don't understand the casual norms of the
channel. (2) <strong>too casual for the context</strong> — writing a very casual,
emoji-filled, fragment-heavy message ("hey lol can u just do the thing 😅") to a
senior executive or an external customer sounds unprofessional, disrespectful of the
formality the situation calls for, and can undermine how you're perceived; it's also
grammatically fine but contextually wrong. In both cases, the words are correct but
the <em>register</em> is mismatched to the audience/channel, and that mismatch is
noticed and matters — it affects how appropriate, professional, and socially-aware
you seem, and can create the wrong impression even though nothing is technically
wrong. Register is part of communicating appropriately, alongside correctness: you
have to say the right thing (accuracy) AND say it at the right formality level
(register). The good news for warmth (your goal): register is about formality, not
warmth — you can be warm at any register (casual-warm, professional-warm, formal-
warm) — so matching register doesn't conflict with being warm; you adjust the
formality to fit the audience while keeping the warmth throughout. The skill is
reading the situation (who + what channel) and matching the formality level, which
makes your messages fit the context and feel appropriate — and when unsure, the
professional-warm middle is a safe default that fits almost everywhere.
</details>

**Q2.** "You can be warm at any register." Explain what this means and why it
matters for someone whose goal is to sound warm.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"You can be warm at any register" means that <strong>warmth and formality are
separate, independent dials</strong> — register (how formal/casual you are) and
warmth (how friendly/kind/human you are) are different properties, and you can
combine any level of one with any level of the other. So there's casual-warm ("hey!
nice work on this 🙌 could you check X when you get a sec? thanks!"), professional-
warm ("Hi Sarah, great job on this — could you review X when you have a moment?
Thanks so much!"), and formal-warm ("Hi Dr. Chen, thank you so much for your
patience — I'm delighted to share that the project is on track"). All three are
warm; they differ only in formality (casual has contractions/emoji/relaxed style,
formal has fuller sentences/formal greetings/no emoji). Warmth comes from things
like appreciation, friendly framing, acknowledgment, and softeners — which you can
include at <em>any</em> formality level. Why this matters for someone whose goal is
to sound warm: it means <strong>you never have to sacrifice warmth to be
appropriate</strong>. A common worry is that being more formal (for an exec or
customer) requires being cold or distant — but that's a false trade: you can be
formal AND warm (formal-warm), adjusting the formality for the audience while keeping
the warmth through appreciation and friendly framing. Similarly, being casual doesn't
automatically make you warm — a casual message can be cold ("k. do it.") just as a
formal one can be warm. So warmth is a choice you make independently of register, at
every register. This is liberating for your goal: you don't have to figure out "which
register is warm" (none is inherently warmer) — you choose the register that fits the
audience (casual for teammates, formal for execs) AND separately choose to be warm
(through the warmth tools this phase teaches: softeners, warm openers/closers,
appreciation, engagement), and you get appropriate-AND-warm at whatever formality the
situation needs. The practical upshot: when you write to an exec (formal register),
don't let the formality make you cold — keep the warmth (thank you, appreciation,
friendly framing) while adjusting the formality (fuller sentences, formal greeting);
and when you write to a teammate (casual register), being casual doesn't guarantee
warmth — add the warmth deliberately. Warmth is always available and always your
choice, at every register — which means your goal (sounding warm) is achievable in
every situation, just executed with different formality. The rest of Phase 3 teaches
the warmth tools; this lesson establishes that they apply at every register, so you're
never stuck choosing between appropriate and warm.
</details>

## Homework

For a few days, notice the register of messages you receive and send. Identify which
register fits each situation (teammate = casual, manager = professional, exec/
customer = formal), and check whether your messages match. Try mirroring the register
of whoever writes to you. Then take one message you'd send to a teammate and rewrite
it for a more formal audience (a director, say) — keeping the warmth while raising the
formality. Notice: did you keep it warm at the higher register, or did formality make
it cold? That's the skill to practice.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds register-awareness and the crucial warm-at-every-register skill.
A strong response notices the register of incoming and outgoing messages, checks for
matches/mismatches, and practices mirroring (matching the register of whoever writes
to you — a reliable default). The register-shift exercise (rewriting a teammate
message for a more formal audience) is the key one, and the common finding is
revealing: many people, when raising formality, accidentally make the message
<em>colder</em> — they strip out the warmth along with the casualness, producing a
formal-but-cold message ("Dear [name], Please find the attached. Regards.") rather
than a formal-but-warm one ("Hi [name], I hope you're well — I wanted to share this
update, and I'd love your thoughts. Thank you!"). This reveals the exact skill to
practice: <strong>raising formality while keeping warmth</strong> — adjusting the
formality dial (fuller sentences, formal greeting, fewer contractions/emoji) without
touching the warmth dial (keep the appreciation, friendly framing, acknowledgment).
The realization that matters for your goal: formality and warmth are independent
(Q2), so a formal message can and should be warm — and the tendency to conflate
"formal" with "cold" is a trap to avoid, because it makes your messages to important
people (execs, customers, senior stakeholders) come across as distant when you could
be both appropriately formal AND warm. For someone aiming to sound warm, this is
important because the higher-stakes, more-formal situations (where warmth matters
most for relationships) are exactly where the formality-makes-me-cold trap strikes —
so consciously keeping warmth at the formal register is a high-value skill. The
mirroring practice is also valuable: matching the register others use is a safe,
low-effort way to get the formality right, and it signals you're on the same
wavelength. The meta-point: register (matching formality to audience/channel) makes
your communication <em>appropriate</em>, and doing it while keeping warmth (which is
always available at every register) makes it appropriate AND warm — the combination
you want. When unsure of the register, the professional-warm middle default fits
almost everywhere. Add "am I at the right register, and am I keeping warmth even at
higher formality?" to your awareness. The rest of Phase 3 gives you the specific
warmth tools (softeners next, then openers/closers, requests, disagreeing, thanks,
engagement) that let you be warm at whatever register the situation calls for.
</details>
