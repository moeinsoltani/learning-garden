# Lesson 29: Interrupting and Clarifying Gracefully

## Concept

Live conversation moves fast, and two things trip up non-native speakers most: **getting
a word in** (when do I jump in without being rude?) and **not understanding** (someone
said something I didn't catch, or an idiom I don't know — how do I ask without looking
lost?). Both have graceful, standard phrasings. Knowing them means you can participate
fully instead of staying silent because you're unsure how to interject or admit you didn't
follow.

```
   INTERRUPTING GRACEFULLY          CLARIFYING GRACEFULLY
   ──────────────────────           ─────────────────────
   "Can I jump in here?"            "Sorry, can you say that again?"
   "Building on that —"            "Just to make sure I follow —"
   "Quick thought —"               "When you say X, do you mean…?"
   "Before we move on —"           "Can you give an example?"

   Interjecting is normal. Asking to clarify is SMART, not weak.
```

The key reframe: **interrupting politely is normal and expected** in meetings (you're not
being rude — everyone does it with the right phrasing), and **asking someone to repeat or
clarify is a sign of engagement, not weakness** (the smartest people ask "what do you mean
by that?" — it's how you make sure everyone's aligned). Have the phrases ready and you'll
speak up more.

---

## How It Works

### Getting a word in

In a flowing conversation, you rarely get a natural gap — you have to make one, politely.
Standard openers signal "I'd like to speak" without being rude:

- "Can I jump in here?" / "Can I add something?" — asks for the floor.
- "Building on that —" / "To add to what Priya said —" — connects to the current point
  (smooth, non-abrupt).
- "Quick thought —" — signals it's brief.
- "Before we move on —" — flags you want to say something before the topic changes.

Say the opener, then a half-second pause, then your point — the opener claims the floor so
you're not talking over someone. On video calls, you can also raise a hand or use the
reaction, but a verbal opener is the core skill.

### Asking someone to repeat

If you didn't catch something (spoke fast, bad audio, unfamiliar accent), just ask — it's
completely normal:

- "Sorry, could you say that again?" / "I didn't quite catch that — one more time?"
- "You cut out for a second — could you repeat the last part?" (blames the connection,
  easy on video)

Everyone mishears sometimes; asking is normal and better than nodding along lost.

### Clarifying meaning

If you heard the words but aren't sure of the meaning (an idiom, an ambiguous term, jargon):

- "Just to make sure I follow — when you say 'ship it', do you mean deploy to prod now?"
- "Can you give an example?" / "Can you unpack that a bit?"
- "Sorry, I'm not familiar with that term — what does [X] mean?"

Asking to clarify is a strength: it makes sure everyone's aligned (often others were
confused too and didn't ask), and it's how careful, engaged people operate. Never nod
along to something you didn't understand — the misunderstanding compounds.

### Confirming your understanding (paraphrase)

A powerful clarifying move is to say back what you understood: "So if I've got this right,
you're proposing we cache at the edge and drop the Redis layer — is that it?" This confirms
alignment, gives the speaker a chance to correct you, and is a lesson in itself (Lesson 35,
paraphrasing). It's especially useful when English is a barrier — it catches
misunderstandings before they cause problems.

{: .note }
> **Interrupting politely is normal; asking to clarify is smart, not weak**
> Two things silence non-native speakers in fast meetings: not knowing how to get a word
> in, and not wanting to admit they didn't understand. Both fears are unfounded with the
> right framing: (1) interrupting *politely* — with an opener like "can I jump in?" — is
> completely normal and expected; everyone does it, and the opener makes it graceful, not
> rude. (2) Asking someone to repeat or clarify is a sign of *engagement and care*, not
> weakness — the smartest people ask "what do you mean by that?"; it ensures alignment
> (often others were lost too), and nodding along to something you didn't understand is
> the actual mistake. Have the phrases ready, and you'll participate fully instead of
> staying quiet — which is exactly what you want as a lead.

The patterns: use an opener to claim the floor ("can I jump in?"); ask to repeat blamelessly
(blame the audio); ask about unfamiliar terms as "making sure I follow" (smart, not weak);
and paraphrase back to confirm understanding.

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is "interrupting politely is normal" an important reframe, and what are the
standard ways to get a word in without being rude?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"Interrupting politely is normal" is an important reframe because <strong>many people —
especially non-native speakers or those from cultures where interrupting is rude — stay
silent in fast meetings, missing chances to contribute, because they're waiting for a
natural gap that never comes and they think jumping in would be rude</strong>. In a flowing
conversation, there often isn't a clean pause — the discussion moves continuously, and if
you wait for a gap, you may never speak. So the belief "I shouldn't interrupt" translates,
in practice, to "I don't get to contribute" — you have good points but they go unsaid
because you're waiting for an opening that doesn't arrive. The reframe fixes this:
<strong>interrupting <em>politely</em> — with the right opener — is completely normal and
expected in meetings</strong>. Everyone does it; that's how multi-person conversations
work. The key is doing it politely (with an opener that signals "I'd like to speak" and
gives the current speaker a graceful moment to yield), not abruptly (just barging over
someone mid-word). With the right phrasing, interjecting isn't rude — it's normal
participation. The standard ways to get a word in without being rude: (1) <strong>ask for
the floor</strong> — "Can I jump in here?" / "Can I add something?" — a brief opener that
signals you'd like to speak, then a half-second pause (which lets the current speaker
finish their word/thought and yield), then your point; (2) <strong>connect to the current
point</strong> — "Building on what Alex said —" / "To add to that —" — which is smooth and
non-abrupt because you're linking to the ongoing discussion rather than cutting against it;
(3) <strong>signal brevity</strong> — "Quick thought —" — which makes people willing to
yield because it's short; (4) <strong>flag before a transition</strong> — "Before we move
on —" — to get your point in before the topic changes. The mechanics: say the opener, pause
briefly (claiming the floor without talking over anyone), then make your point. On video,
you can supplement with a raised hand or reaction, but the verbal opener is the core skill.
The point: with these phrases ready, you can participate fully in fast conversations
instead of staying silent — which matters especially as a lead (you need to be able to
steer and contribute in real time). Interjecting politely is a normal, learnable skill, not
a rudeness.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is asking someone to repeat or clarify a strength rather than a weakness, and
why is nodding along to something you didn't understand the actual mistake?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Asking someone to repeat or clarify is a strength rather than a weakness because
<strong>it ensures alignment — that you (and often others) actually understand what's
being discussed — which is exactly what careful, engaged people do</strong>. The fear is
that asking "what do you mean?" or "can you repeat that?" makes you look like you weren't
paying attention or aren't smart enough to follow. But the reality is the opposite:
(1) <strong>the smartest, most engaged people ask clarifying questions</strong> — "when you
say X, do you mean Y?" — because they care about actually understanding, not just appearing
to; asking shows engagement (you're tracking closely enough to notice you need clarity),
not inattention; (2) <strong>you're usually not the only one confused</strong> — when
something is unclear or jargon-y, often several people didn't fully follow but nobody asked
(everyone assuming they should already know); by asking, you help everyone, and people are
often relieved someone asked; (3) <strong>it catches misunderstandings early</strong> —
clarifying ensures everyone's aligned before decisions are made on a shared (correct)
understanding, rather than people leaving with different interpretations. So asking is a
service to the whole room's alignment, and a sign of the care that good work requires. Why
nodding along to something you didn't understand is the actual mistake: <strong>the
misunderstanding doesn't go away — it compounds</strong>. If you nod along to something you
didn't catch or didn't understand (an idiom, a term, a fast-spoken point), you now proceed
on a wrong or missing understanding: you might do the wrong thing, agree to something you
didn't grasp, or miss a key point — and the gap surfaces later, at a worse time, when it's
harder and more embarrassing to unwind ("wait, I thought we agreed to X" — "no, I said the
opposite"). Nodding along <em>feels</em> safer in the moment (you avoid the small
discomfort of asking), but it trades a tiny, normal discomfort now for a real problem
later. And it's especially risky when English is a barrier — an unfamiliar idiom or fast
speech is easy to mis-hear, and pretending to follow lets the error through. So the mistake
isn't asking; it's <em>not</em> asking. The right move: whenever you don't catch or don't
understand something, ask — "sorry, could you say that again?" or "just to make sure I
follow, when you say X…?" — reframing it (correctly) as engagement and care, not weakness.
A powerful version is to paraphrase back what you understood ("so you're saying X — is that
right?"), which confirms alignment and gives the speaker a chance to correct you. For a
non-native speaker especially, having these phrases ready and using them freely means you
participate accurately and confidently, rather than silently accumulating misunderstandings
— which is a genuine strength.
</details>

## Homework

For a week, practice both skills in live conversations: (1) when you have a point but
there's no gap, use an opener ("can I jump in?" / "building on that —") to get the floor
rather than staying silent; (2) whenever you don't catch or don't understand something,
ask ("sorry, could you repeat that?" / "when you say X, do you mean…?") rather than nodding
along — and try paraphrasing back to confirm. Notice that interjecting politely works fine
and that asking to clarify is met well (often others were confused too). Add to your
habits: "spoke up when I had a point? asked when I didn't understand — never nodded along
lost?"
