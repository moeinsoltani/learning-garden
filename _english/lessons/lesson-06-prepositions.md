# Lesson 06: Prepositions

## Concept

Prepositions (*in, on, at, to, for, with, of, by, about*...) are small words that
cause big trouble, because they rarely follow logical rules — they're mostly
**fixed by convention**, and different languages pair different prepositions with
the same idea. The winning strategy is not to memorize rules but to **learn the
whole phrase as a chunk**.

```
   Don't learn:  "depend" + [rule for which preposition?]
   DO learn:     "depend ON"  as one unit
                 "agree WITH a person" / "agree ON a plan"  as units
                 "deployed TO production"  as a unit

   When in doubt, learn the verb + preposition TOGETHER, like one word.
```

You'll never get every preposition right (even advanced speakers slip), and that's
fine — prepositions are low-stakes for being understood. But a handful of
high-frequency work phrases are worth locking in, plus one common error pattern to
avoid: adding *to* where English drops it (or dropping it where English needs it).

---

## Going Deeper

### Time: in / on / at

A rough hierarchy from big to small: **in** (large periods — in 2024, in March, in
the morning), **on** (days and dates — on Monday, on the 5th), **at** (specific
times — at 3pm, at noon).

- ✓ "The meeting is **at** 3pm **on** Monday **in** March."
- ✓ "I'll deploy **in** the morning" but "I'll deploy **at** 9am."

Two work-critical ones: **by** (deadline — "by Friday" = no later than Friday) vs
**until** (up to a point — "until Friday" = the whole time up to then). "Finish it
**by** Friday" (deadline) ≠ "I'm on it **until** Friday" (duration).

### The high-frequency tech chunks

Learn these as fixed units:

- deployed **to** production / **to** staging
- works **on** my machine
- depends **on** [something]
- agree **with** [a person] / agree **on** [a plan or thing]
- responsible **for** [something]
- based **on** [something]
- blocked **by** / blocked **on** [something]
- migrate **to** [new] / migrate **from** [old]
- integrate **with** [a system]

### The "explain me" trap (and its family)

A very common non-native error is adding or dropping *to*:

- ✗ "explain me the bug" → ✓ "explain the bug **to** me" (explain needs "to me,"
  not "me" directly)
- ✗ "discuss about the plan" → ✓ "discuss the plan" (discuss takes no preposition —
  no "about")
- ✗ "reply me" → ✓ "reply **to** me"
- ✗ "I'll answer to your question" → ✓ "I'll answer your question" (answer takes no
  "to")

These don't follow a single rule — they're just how each verb works, so learn the
verb with (or without) its preposition as a chunk: *explain [something] to [someone],
discuss [something] (no prep), reply to, answer [something] (no prep)*.

{: .note }
> **Prepositions are the "good enough" category**
> Unlike the earlier lessons (sentence core, articles, agreement — which affect
> accuracy and clarity), prepositions are usually low-stakes: "it depends of the
> config" instead of "depends on" is a small slip that rarely causes confusion.
> So don't stress about getting every one perfect. Do lock in the handful of
> high-frequency work chunks above (deployed to, depends on, responsible for), and
> watch the "explain me" family (add/drop "to"), because those are the noticeable
> ones. For the rest, learn phrases as chunks over time and don't worry — being
> understood matters more than preposition perfection.

Part A: fill in the right preposition. Part B: three of the sentences have a wrong
or extra/missing preposition — find and fix them.

**Your answer:**

*Part A — fill the blank:*
1. "The fix depends ___ the config change landing first."
2. "I deployed it ___ production this morning."
3. "Can you finish the review ___ Friday?" (deadline)
4. "I'm responsible ___ the auth service."

*Part B — find and fix the 3 errors in this paragraph:*
"Let me explain you the problem. The migration depends of the schema change, and I

<strong>Part A:</strong> (1) depends <strong>on</strong> the config change. (2)

<br><br>
<strong>Part B — the three errors:</strong>
<br>• "explain <strong>you</strong> the problem" → "explain the problem <strong>to

me" trap).
<br>• "depends <strong>of</strong> the schema change" → "depends <strong>on</strong>
the schema change" (depend takes "on," not "of" — a very common slip, since many
languages use the equivalent of "of").
<br>• "discuss <strong>about</strong> the timeline" → "discuss the timeline" (discuss
takes no preposition — drop "about").
<br>And a bonus fourth: "answer <strong>to</strong> your questions" → "answer your
questions" (answer takes no "to"). So the fixed paragraph: "Let me explain the
problem to you. The migration depends on the schema change, and I want to discuss
the timeline with you. I'll answer your questions in the meeting."
<br><br>
The chunk-learning takeaway: <em>explain [X] to [someone]</em>, <em>depend on</em>,
<em>discuss [X]</em> (no prep), <em>answer [X]</em> (no prep) — memorize each verb
with (or without) its preposition as one unit.
</details>

## Phrase Bank

The high-frequency work chunks — learn each as one unit:

| Idea | Chunk |
|---|---|
| Something requires | "depends **on** X" |
| Ship it | "deployed **to** production / staging" |
| Own it | "responsible **for** X" |
| Deadline | "by Friday" (no later than) — vs "until Friday" (the whole time) |
| It's grounded in | "based **on** X" |
| Something is stuck | "blocked **on** / **by** X" |
| Clarify to a person | "explain X **to** [someone]" (never "explain [someone] X") |
| Talk about a topic | "discuss X" (no "about") · "talk **about** X" (with "about") |
| Respond | "reply **to** X" · "answer X" (no "to") |
| Move systems | "migrate **from** [old] **to** [new]" · "integrate **with** X" |

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is "learn the whole phrase as a chunk" a better strategy for
prepositions than trying to learn rules?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because prepositions in English are largely <strong>fixed by convention, not by
logical rules</strong> — there usually isn't a rule that tells you "depend" takes
"on" (not "of" or "from"), or that "explain" needs "to [someone]" while "answer"
takes no preposition; these pairings are arbitrary conventions that developed over
time, and they often differ from the equivalent in other languages (which is exactly
why non-native speakers slip — they carry over their language's preposition, saying
"depend of" because their language uses the equivalent of "of"). Since there's no
reliable rule to apply, trying to reason out which preposition to use fails — you'd
need a rule that doesn't exist. Learning the whole phrase as a chunk sidesteps this:
you memorize "depend on," "deployed to production," "responsible for," "explain X to
someone" as single units — like vocabulary words rather than grammar to be computed —
so you retrieve the correct preposition automatically as part of the phrase, without
needing a rule. This is how native speakers actually know prepositions: not by rule,
but by having heard and used the chunks so many times that the right preposition just
sounds right (and the wrong one sounds wrong). For a learner, deliberately learning
the high-frequency work chunks as units (rather than the individual words plus a
guess at the preposition) is far more efficient and reliable. There are a few loose
patterns worth knowing (in/on/at for time, from big to small), but even those have
exceptions, and the verb+preposition pairings are essentially all convention. The
practical approach: for the prepositions that matter (the common work phrases), learn
them as chunks; for the rest, don't stress — prepositions are usually low-stakes for
being understood (a wrong preposition rarely causes real confusion), so getting the
high-frequency chunks right and not worrying about preposition perfection elsewhere
is the efficient strategy. Chunk-learning turns an unlearnable rule-problem into a
manageable vocabulary-problem.
</details>

**Q2.** Fix the preposition errors: "Let me explain you the fix. It depends of the
config, so I'll answer to your question after the deploy."

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Fixed: <strong>"Let me explain the fix to you. It depends on the config, so I'll
answer your question after the deploy."</strong> Three errors, all from verbs that
take (or don't take) a preposition differently than many languages expect: (1)
"explain <strong>you</strong> the fix" → "explain the fix <strong>to you</strong>" —
<em>explain</em> requires "to [someone]"; you can't "explain someone something," you
"explain something to someone" (the "explain me" trap — a very common non-native
error). (2) "depends <strong>of</strong> the config" → "depends <strong>on</strong>
the config" — <em>depend</em> takes "on," not "of" (the "of" comes from languages
whose equivalent verb uses their word for "of"). (3) "answer <strong>to</strong> your
question" → "answer your question" — <em>answer</em> takes no preposition; you
"answer a question" directly, not "answer to a question." The lesson: these are all
convention, not rule — learn each as a chunk (<em>explain X to someone</em>,
<em>depend on</em>, <em>answer X</em>) rather than trying to derive the preposition,
because there's no derivation that works.
</details>

## Homework

Pick five verb+preposition chunks from the phrase bank that you use most at work
(depends on, deployed to, responsible for, blocked on, explain to...) and use each
in a real sentence today — deliberately, until the preposition feels automatic. Then
watch specifically for the "explain me / answer to / discuss about" family in your
writing this week (add/drop "to"/"about" errors), since those are the most noticeable
preposition mistakes. Note which one or two chunks you tend to get wrong — those go
on your checklist. Don't try to fix all prepositions; just lock in your high-frequency
chunks.

**Your answer:**

The exercise builds the chunk-learning habit for the prepositions that actually matter
(the high-frequency work phrases) while deliberately <em>not</em> trying to master all

get learned) and identifies the specific one or two the person tends to get wrong —
commonly "depend of" (should be "depend on"), the "explain me" family, or "discuss
about" (should be no "about"). These become targeted checklist items. The key mindset
this reinforces (and it's a relief): prepositions are the <strong>"good enough"
category</strong> — unlike the sentence core, articles, and agreement (which affect
clarity and accuracy and deserve real attention), a wrong preposition ("it depends of
the config") is a small slip that almost never causes misunderstanding, so it's
low-stakes. Therefore the efficient strategy is: (1) lock in the handful of

about the rest) is far better use of effort than trying to internalize the whole

never fully master, and which they don't need to, because prepositions rarely block
communication. Add your one or two problem chunks to the checklist (Lesson 9), and
move on — the next lessons (connecting ideas, punctuation) return to higher-stakes

Phase 1 into your personal pre-send checklist.
