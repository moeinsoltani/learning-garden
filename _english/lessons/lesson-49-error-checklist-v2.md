# Lesson 49: Your Personal Error Checklist

## Concept

Everyone who writes in a second language has a *personal set of recurring errors* — the same few
mistakes, again and again. The single most efficient way to improve is not to study all of English
grammar, but to **know your own top errors and check for exactly those.** This lesson builds your
personal error checklist: a short, tuned list of the specific things you get wrong, that you scan
for before sending anything important.

```
   THE PERSONAL ERROR CHECKLIST (yours, tuned to you):
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ □ Subject-verb agreement  "the content ARE" → "IS"     │
   │ □ Articles (a/the/zero)   "I opened the PR" (which?)   │
   │ □ Dropped words           "I will send" not "I send"   │
   │ □ [your other patterns]                                │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

   A SHORT list of YOUR errors > all of English grammar.
```

The reframe: **you don't need to fix all of English — you need to fix your handful of recurring
patterns.** Most of your errors come from a small set of repeated mistakes; a short checklist of
*those* (not a grammar textbook) is what actually raises your accuracy fast. Scanning your own
top-5 list before sending catches the great majority of your errors in seconds.

---

## Going Deeper

### Your errors are a short, repeating set

Second-language errors aren't random — each person has characteristic patterns they repeat.
Yours (from your known profile) center on: **subject–verb agreement** ("the content are" → "the
content is"), **articles** (when to use a / the / no article), and **dropped words** (missing
auxiliaries, articles, or subjects — "I will send" not "I send it tomorrow"). Because they repeat,
targeting exactly these gives you the highest return: fix the patterns you actually make, not ones
you don't.

### Build the checklist from real examples

Make your checklist concrete and personal: for each error type, note a real example you've made and
the fix. "Subject–verb: I wrote 'the tests is passing' → 'the tests are passing'." Real examples
make the pattern memorable and the check specific. Gather them from feedback you've received, past
messages, and the drills in this course. Keep it to your top 4–6 — a short list you'll actually use.

### The pre-send scan

Before sending anything important (a message to your manager, a design doc, a PR description), do a
quick scan against your checklist — literally check for each item: subject-verb agreement right?
articles correct? no dropped words? This takes seconds once it's habit, and catches the majority of
your errors, because your errors are concentrated in those few patterns. The scan is the payoff of
having the list.

### Your top-three patterns, in detail

- **Subject–verb agreement:** the verb must match the subject in number. "The content **is**"
  (content is singular/uncountable), "the tests **are**", "each of the files **is**" (each = singular).
  Watch especially uncountable nouns (content, information, feedback — all singular: "the feedback
  **is**") and "each/every" (singular). (Lesson 05.)
- **Articles:** use **the** for specific/known ("the PR I opened"), **a/an** for one-of-many
  non-specific ("a bug", "a meeting"), and **no article** for general plurals/uncountables ("tests
  should pass", "feedback is welcome"). When unsure, ask: specific and known to the reader? → the.
  (Lessons 02–03.)
- **Dropped words:** English rarely omits the subject, article, or auxiliary. "I **will** send it
  **the** tomorrow" ✗ → "I **will** send it tomorrow" ✓; "**It** looks good" not "Looks good" (in
  formal writing); "we need **a** plan" not "we need plan". Read to check nothing essential is
  missing. (Lesson 01.)

### Update it as you learn

Your checklist isn't fixed — as you notice new recurring errors (or master old ones), update it.
When someone corrects you, or you catch a repeated mistake, add it. When a pattern becomes automatic
(you no longer make it), you can drop it. The list evolves with you, always reflecting your *current*
top errors — which keeps it short and relevant.

{: .note }
> **Fix your handful of recurring errors, not all of English grammar**
> Everyone writing in a second language has a small set of characteristic, repeating errors — for
> you, subject–verb agreement, articles, and dropped words. The most efficient path to accuracy

## Phrase Bank — Your Error Quick-Reference

| Pattern | Wrong → Right |
|---|---|
| Uncountable + verb | "the content are" → "the content **is**" |
| each/every + verb | "each service have" → "each service **has**" |
| Article: one of many | "I opened PR" → "I opened **a** PR" |
| Article: specific | "on design" → "on **the** design" |
| Article: general plural | "**the** tests should pass" → "tests should pass" |
| Dropped article | "send document" → "send **the** document" |
| for + verb | "for fix" → "**to** fix" |
| Dropped tense | "I finish it yesterday" → "I **finished** it yesterday" |

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is a short personal error checklist more efficient for improving accuracy than studying
all of English grammar?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A short personal error checklist is more efficient than studying all of English grammar because
<strong>your errors are not spread randomly across all of grammar — they concentrate in a small set
of recurring patterns, so targeting exactly those patterns catches the great majority of your
mistakes for a tiny fraction of the effort</strong>. The key insight is that second-language errors
are <em>characteristic and repeating</em>: each person makes the same few types of mistakes over and
over (for you: subject–verb agreement, articles, dropped words), rather than making random errors
across every grammar rule. This has a powerful implication for how to improve efficiently: (1)
<strong>studying all of grammar is mostly wasted effort</strong> — the vast majority of English
grammar rules cover things you already get right (you don't make most possible errors), so studying
them comprehensively spends enormous effort on patterns that aren't your problem, with little
return; it's inefficient to review all of grammar when your actual errors are concentrated in a few
spots. (2) <strong>a short checklist of your actual errors targets exactly what you get wrong</strong>
— because your mistakes cluster in a few patterns, a list of just those patterns covers the great
majority of the errors you actually make; checking those few things catches most of your errors,
while ignoring the many rules you don't break. So the checklist gives you nearly all the accuracy
benefit for a fraction of the effort — it's the Pareto principle (80/20) applied to grammar: a small
number of patterns cause most of your errors, so fixing/checking those few yields most of the
improvement. (3) <strong>the checklist is actually usable</strong> — a short list of your top 4–6
errors is something you can actually scan against before sending (in seconds, once it's habit),
whereas "all of English grammar" is not something you can practically check a message against; the
brevity is what makes it a practical tool rather than an overwhelming study project. (4) <strong>it's
personalized and concrete</strong> — built from real examples you've actually made, the checklist
targets <em>your</em> specific patterns (not generic ones), making it maximally relevant and the
checks specific and memorable. The efficiency argument is thus about <strong>focus and leverage</strong>:
improving accuracy fast comes not from broad comprehensive grammar study (high effort, much of it
irrelevant to your errors) but from identifying your handful of recurring patterns and checking for
exactly those (low effort, targeting precisely your actual mistakes). A short list of your errors
beats a grammar textbook because it concentrates your attention on the small number of things that
actually cause most of your errors, and it does so in a form you'll actually use (a quick pre-send
scan). This is the highest-leverage accuracy habit precisely because it's tuned to you — it fixes
what you get wrong, ignores what you get right, and is short enough to apply every time. And it's
freeing: you don't have to master all of English to write accurately; you have to handle your few
patterns, which is a far more achievable and motivating goal. (The list also evolves — you add new
recurring errors you notice and drop patterns you've mastered — so it always reflects your current
top errors, staying short and relevant as you improve.)
</details>

**Q2.** What are your three main error patterns, and what's the quick check for each?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The three main error patterns (from your known profile) and the quick check for each: (1)
<strong>Subject–verb agreement</strong> — the verb must match the subject in number (singular subject
→ singular verb, plural → plural). <em>Quick check:</em> find the real subject and ask "singular or
plural?", then make sure the verb matches — watching especially two traps: <strong>uncountable
nouns</strong> (content, information, feedback, code, data-as-a-mass) are singular, so "the content
<strong>is</strong>", "the feedback <strong>is</strong>", "the information <strong>is</strong>" (not
"are"); and <strong>"each/every/one of"</strong> are singular, so "each service <strong>has</strong>",
"every test <strong>passes</strong>", "one of the files <strong>is</strong>" (the verb agrees with
"each"/"one", not the plural noun after "of"). Don't be fooled by a plural noun sitting near the verb
if it's not the real subject. (2) <strong>Articles (a / the / zero)</strong> — English needs an
article decision for most nouns. <em>Quick check:</em> for each noun, ask "is this specific and known
to the reader?" → if yes, use <strong>the</strong> ("the PR I opened", "the design"); if it's one of
many, non-specific, and singular countable, use <strong>a/an</strong> ("a bug", "a meeting", "a
question"); if it's a general plural or uncountable, use <strong>no article</strong> ("tests should
pass", "feedback is welcome"). The most common slip is dropping the article entirely ("I opened PR" →
"I opened <strong>a</strong> PR"), so specifically check that each singular countable noun has an
article. (3) <strong>Dropped words</strong> — English rarely omits the subject, article, auxiliary
verb, or object that the grammar requires. <em>Quick check:</em> read the sentence and make sure
nothing essential is missing — is there a subject? an article where needed? the auxiliary ("will",
"is", "have")? the object? Common drops: articles ("send <strong>the</strong> document", "have
<strong>a</strong> question"), the correct verb form/tense ("I <strong>finished</strong> it
yesterday" not "I finish it yesterday"), and "to" vs "for" before a verb ("<strong>to</strong> fix
the bug" not "for fix the bug"). The overall quick check across all three: before sending, scan the
sentence for (a) subject–verb matches (esp. uncountables and each/every), (b) an article decision on
each noun (specific → the, one-of-many → a, general → none), and (c) nothing essential dropped
(article, auxiliary, correct verb form). Because these three patterns cause the great majority of
your errors, running this quick scan before anything important catches most of them in seconds — which
is exactly the payoff of knowing your own patterns rather than studying all of grammar. (And the
checklist is yours to refine — add any other recurring patterns you notice, drop ones you've mastered
— so it stays tuned to your current top errors.)
</details>
