# Lesson 05: Subject–Verb Agreement

## Concept

The verb must match its subject in number: a *singular* subject takes a singular
verb, a *plural* subject takes a plural verb. Simple in short sentences — "the bug
**is** fixed," "the bugs **are** fixed" — but it goes wrong when words come between
the subject and the verb, or when the real subject is hard to spot.

```
   "The list of open issues IS long."     ← subject is "list" (singular) → is
                     ↑
        "of open issues" is just describing the list — IGNORE it
        when choosing the verb. The verb matches "list," not "issues."

   ✗ "The list of open issues ARE long."  ← wrong: matched "issues," not "list"
```

This lesson connects directly to one of your most common error patterns — writing
"the content **are**" instead of "the content **is**." The fix is a reliable habit:
find the *real* subject (the actual noun the verb belongs to), ignore the words in
between, and match the verb to it.

---

## Going Deeper

### The distance trap

When a phrase sits between the subject and the verb, the writer often matches the
verb to the *nearest* noun instead of the real subject. Ignore the in-between
phrase:

- "The **list** of open issues **is** long." (subject: list → is; NOT "issues are")
- "The **status** of the deploys **is** unclear." (subject: status → is)
- "One **of** the tests **is** failing." (subject: one → is; not "tests are")
- "The **set** of changes **was** reviewed." (subject: set → was)

The trick: mentally delete the "of ..." phrase and see what's left — "the list [of
open issues] is" → "the list is." The verb matches the noun before "of."

### Uncountable nouns are singular

The uncountable nouns from Lesson 3 (feedback, information, code, content, data*)
are singular — they take *is/was*, not *are/were*:

- ✓ "The **content is** ready." (NOT "the content are") — *this is your pattern*
- ✓ "The **feedback is** helpful."
- ✓ "The **information is** correct."
- ✓ "The **code is** clean."

*Data is technically the plural of "datum," but in modern tech usage "the data
**is**" is standard and fine.

### each / every / everyone are singular

Words like *each, every, everyone, everything, someone* are singular, even though
they feel like they refer to many:

- ✓ "**Each** of the services **has** its own config." (each → has, not have)
- ✓ "**Everyone is** on the call." (everyone → is)
- ✓ "**Every** test **passes**." (every test → passes)

### Teams and companies

A team or company name is usually treated as singular in American English: "The
**team is** shipping this week," "**Google is** hiring." (British English sometimes
treats them as plural, but singular is the safe default.)

{: .note }
> **Your fix: find the real subject, then match**
> Your specific pattern — "the content are," "the data are helpful" — comes from
> uncountable nouns being singular. The one-line fix for your checklist: for
> *content, feedback, information, data, code, software, progress*, use **is/was**
> (singular), never are/were. And for the distance trap ("the list of X"), delete
> the "of X" phrase and match the verb to what's left. These two checks catch
> nearly all subject–verb agreement errors in workplace writing.

## Phrase Bank

| Subject | Verb | Example |
|---|---|---|
| content / feedback / information / data / code | **is / was** | "The content **is** ready." |
| the list/set/status **of** [things] | **is / was** | "The status of the deploys **is** unclear." |
| one of [things] | **is** | "One of the tests **is** failing." |
| each / every / everyone / everything | **is / has** | "Each service **has** its own config." |
| the team / [Company] | **is** | "The team **is** shipping this week." |
| plural (bugs, tests, changes) | **are / were** | "The tests **are** passing." |

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What is the "distance trap" in subject–verb agreement, and what's the
reliable trick to avoid it?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The distance trap is matching the verb to the <em>nearest</em> noun instead of the
<em>real</em> subject, when a phrase sits between the subject and the verb. In "the
list of open issues is long," a phrase ("of open issues") separates the subject
("list") from the verb ("is"), and the trap is to match the verb to the nearby
"issues" (plural → "are") instead of the actual subject "list" (singular → "is").
The nearby plural noun "attracts" the verb — an attraction error. The reliable trick:
<strong>find the real subject by mentally deleting the in-between phrase</strong> —
the words after "of," "from," "about," "with." "The list [of open issues] is long" →
delete "of open issues" → "the list is long" → the verb obviously matches "list"
(singular → "is"). The phrase between the subject and verb <em>describes</em> the
subject, it isn't the subject, so ignore it when choosing the verb. When there's a
phrase between what you think is the subject and the verb, strip it out and check the
verb against the bare subject.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is "the content are ready" wrong, and what's the one-line rule that
fixes this and similar errors (feedback, information, data)?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
"The content are ready" is wrong because <strong>content is an uncountable noun,
which is grammatically singular</strong>, so it takes "is" — "the content
<strong>is</strong> ready." Uncountable nouns refer to a mass or concept rather than
countable items, and grammatically behave as singular, so they take is/was, never
are/were. The one-line rule for your checklist: <strong>content, feedback,
information, data, code, software, progress — all use is/was (singular), never
are/were.</strong> High-value because it's a specific, common error pattern easy to
catch with a targeted check. Note it combines two things: the noun is uncountable
(Lesson 3 — no "a," no plural), <em>and</em> singular for agreement (this lesson —
is/was). So "the feedbacks were helpful" has two errors: "the feedback was helpful."
These uncountables are the ones most common in tech writing, and the singular-verb
error with them is frequent and noticeable — fixing it (with the distance trap)
handles the majority of agreement mistakes.
</details>
