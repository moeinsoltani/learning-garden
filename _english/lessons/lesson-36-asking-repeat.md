# Lesson 36: Asking People to Repeat or Slow Down

## Concept

Someone speaks too fast, mumbles, has an unfamiliar accent, or the audio drops — and you
miss what they said. The instinct (especially in a second language) is to nod along and hope
to catch up, which lets a misunderstanding through. The skill is a set of easy, graceful
phrases to **ask them to repeat, slow down, or clarify** — with zero embarrassment. This is
non-optional for participating accurately: you cannot respond well to what you didn't hear.

```
   DON'T:  nod along pretending you caught it  →  misunderstanding compounds

   DO, gracefully:
   • "Sorry, could you say that again?"
   • "You're coming through a bit fast — could you slow down slightly?"
   • "I didn't catch the last part — from '[last words you got]'?"
   • "Could you spell that / put it in the chat?"   (for names, terms)
```

The reframe (from Lesson 29, expanded): **asking someone to repeat or slow down is normal and
blameless — everyone does it, and it's smarter than guessing.** There are neutral, easy ways
to ask that don't imply anything's wrong with you or them (blame the audio, the speed, the
noise). Having these ready means a missed word is a two-second fix, not a silent
misunderstanding.

---

## Going Deeper

### Ask to repeat — blamelessly

The easiest fixes attribute the miss to something neutral, not to you "not being good enough":

- "Sorry, could you say that again?" / "One more time?"
- "You cut out for a second — could you repeat the last bit?" (blames the connection)
- "It's a bit noisy on my end — say that once more?" (blames the environment)

None of these imply you're slow; they're what everyone says when they miss something. Missing
a word is universal, not a language failing.

### Ask to slow down — kindly

If someone consistently speaks too fast for you to follow, it's completely fine to ask them
to slow down — most people don't realize their pace and will happily adjust:

- "Would you mind slowing down just a little? I want to make sure I catch everything."
- "You're covering a lot — could we take it a bit slower?"

Framing it as "I want to catch everything" (interest) rather than "you're too fast" (blame)
keeps it warm. This is a reasonable request; a considerate speaker appreciates knowing.

### Pinpoint what you missed

Instead of a vague "what?", pinpoint the gap — it's more efficient and shows you were
following:

- "I caught the part about the cache, but not what comes after — from there?"
- "You said we should use… what was the last word?"

This gets you just the missing piece (they don't have to repeat everything) and demonstrates
you were tracking, just missed a bit.

### For names, numbers, and terms: spell it / chat it

Names, unfamiliar terms, and numbers are the hardest to catch by ear — ask for them in
writing:

- "Could you spell that name for me?"
- "Would you mind dropping that in the chat?" / "Can you type the exact number?"

This is standard and sensible (even native speakers ask names to be spelled), and it gets you
the precise detail that's easy to mishear.

### Don't nod along — the key discipline

The one thing to avoid is pretending you caught something you didn't. Nodding along feels
easier in the moment but lets the misunderstanding through, and it surfaces later at a worse
time. The two-second cost of asking always beats the downstream cost of a missed detail.
Reframe asking as diligence, not deficiency — the careful person asks.

## Lab — Say Drill

Handle each "I missed it" moment gracefully.

**Your answer (write what you'd say):**

1. **Missed it entirely:** On a call, someone said something but you didn't catch any of it
   (bad audio). Ask them to repeat, blamelessly.

   losing the thread. Ask them to slow down, kindly.
3. **Missed just the end:** You caught most of a sentence — "we should move the auth logic

4. **A name you can't catch:** Someone mentions a person or a library name you can't make out
   by ear. Get it precisely.

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is nodding along to something you didn't catch the wrong move, and what makes the
various ways of asking to repeat "blameless"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Nodding along to something you didn't catch is the wrong move because <strong>you cannot
respond well to what you didn't hear, so pretending you caught it lets a misunderstanding
through that surfaces later at a worse time</strong>. When you nod along to something you
missed (spoken too fast, an accent, dropped audio), you now proceed without the information:
you might respond inappropriately (answering as if you understood when you didn't), agree to
something you didn't actually hear, miss a key instruction or detail, or act on a wrong guess.
The misunderstanding doesn't go away because you nodded — it's still there, undetected, and it
compounds: the gap surfaces later (you did the wrong thing, or "wait, I thought you said X" —
"no, the opposite"), when it's more disruptive and more embarrassing to unwind than it would
have been to just ask in the moment. Nodding along <em>feels</em> easier (it avoids the small
discomfort of asking and the fear of looking slow), but it trades a trivial, normal
two-second cost now for a real problem later — a bad trade. And it's especially risky in a
second language, where missing words is more common (fast speech, accents, idioms), so the
"nod and hope" habit lets many errors through. The right move is always to ask — the
two-second cost of asking beats the downstream cost of a missed detail every time. What makes
the ways of asking "blameless": <strong>they attribute the miss to a neutral, external cause —
the audio, the speed, the noise — rather than implying a deficiency in you (or an insult to
the speaker)</strong>. "You cut out for a second — could you repeat that?" blames the
connection; "it's a bit noisy on my end — one more time?" blames the environment; "would you
mind slowing down? I want to catch it all" frames it as your interest in getting everything,
not their failing or yours. None of these say or imply "I'm not good enough at English to
follow you" or "you're speaking badly" — they're neutral, everyday phrasings that everyone
uses when they miss something, because <strong>missing a word is universal, not a language
failing</strong>. Native speakers miss things and ask to repeat constantly (bad audio, fast
talkers, unfamiliar names) — it's a normal part of conversation, not a mark of inadequacy.
Framing the ask around a neutral cause (audio/noise/speed) or your own diligence (wanting to
catch it all) keeps it blameless and comfortable, removing the fear that asking exposes a
weakness. The reframe: <strong>asking to repeat is diligence, not deficiency</strong> — the
careful person asks to make sure they've got it right, rather than the careless one who nods
along and gets it wrong. Internalizing that (asking is normal, blameless, and smart; nodding
along is the actual mistake) means you'll ask freely and participate accurately, rather than
silently letting misunderstandings accumulate — which is essential for operating well in a
second language.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is pinpointing exactly what you missed (or asking for a name in writing) better
than a generic "sorry, what?"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Pinpointing exactly what you missed is better than a generic "sorry, what?" for two reasons:
<strong>efficiency and signal</strong>. On efficiency: a generic "what?" or "could you repeat
that?" makes the speaker repeat the <em>whole</em> thing, most of which you actually caught —
which wastes time and can be mildly frustrating (they don't know which part you need, so they
redo all of it). Pinpointing — "I caught 'move the auth logic into…' but not where — into
what?" — tells them exactly the piece you're missing, so they supply just that bit. It's
faster and more precise: you get the specific missing information without a full redo. On
signal: a generic "what?" can read as "I wasn't listening / I missed everything," whereas
pinpointing <strong>demonstrates you were following closely</strong> — you caught most of it
and just missed one specific part ("I got the cache part, but not what comes after"). This
shows engagement (you were tracking the substance, not zoned out), which is reassuring to the
speaker and reflects well on you — you're clearly attentive, just missed a detail. So
pinpointing both gets you the information more efficiently and signals attentiveness rather
than inattention. The same logic applies to asking for a name (or number or unfamiliar term)
in writing — "could you spell that?" / "mind dropping it in the chat?": <strong>names, terms,
and numbers are the hardest things to catch and confirm by ear</strong> (they're arbitrary —
you can't infer them from context the way you can a common word, and small mishearings matter:
"fifteen" vs "fifty," a name spelled slightly differently, an unfamiliar library name). Asking
for them in writing gets you the <em>exact</em> detail rather than a guess or an approximate
sound, which prevents the specific, consequential errors that mishearing a name or number
causes (emailing the wrong person, using the wrong number, searching for a misspelled
library). It's more reliable than "repeat it" for these items (repeating a name by voice can
still be mmisheard), and it's completely standard — even native speakers ask names to be
spelled and numbers to be confirmed, because ear-catching arbitrary details is genuinely
error-prone for everyone. So both techniques — pinpointing the gap, and getting names/numbers
in writing — are about getting the <em>right</em> information <em>efficiently</em>: pinpointing
targets exactly the missing piece and shows you were following; writing captures the precise
arbitrary details that are easy to mishear. Both beat a vague "what?" that triggers a full,
imprecise redo and signals inattention. For a non-native speaker, these make asking not just
blameless but <em>skillful</em> — you recover missed information precisely and efficiently
while demonstrating you were engaged, turning a potential awkward moment into a display of
careful attention.
</details>

## Homework

For the next couple of weeks, whenever you miss something, ask — never nod along. Practice the
blameless phrasings (blame the audio/noise, or frame it as wanting to catch everything), and
try pinpointing exactly what you missed ("I got X, but not the part after") rather than a
generic "what?". For names, numbers, and unfamiliar terms, ask for the spelling or the chat.
If someone consistently speaks too fast, kindly ask them to slow down. Notice that asking is
met with zero judgment and keeps you accurate. Add to your habits: "asked when I missed
something instead of nodding along? got names/numbers in writing?"
