# Lesson 04: Verb Tenses for Work

## Concept

English has many tenses, but workplace writing needs only a handful — and three of
them cover about 95% of what you write. The good news: you already know these; the
skill is choosing the right one automatically.

```
   THE THREE CORE TENSES for work:

   PRESENT SIMPLE      facts, habits, how things work
     "The job runs every night."  "The API returns JSON."

   PRESENT CONTINUOUS  happening right now, in progress
     "I'm working on the fix."  "The tests are running."

   PRESENT PERFECT     done, and it matters now
     "I've deployed the fix."  "We've fixed the bug."
```

A status update naturally uses all three: what's *done* (present perfect: "I've
finished X"), what's *in progress* (present continuous: "I'm working on Y"), and
general *facts* (present simple: "the job runs nightly"). Get these three right and
you cover almost all of it. The two others worth knowing: **simple past** (finished
and timestamped: "I deployed it yesterday") and the **future** (will / going to /
present continuous — covered below).

---

## Going Deeper

### Present simple: facts and habits

Use it for things that are generally true, how things work, and repeated actions:

- "The nightly job **runs** at 2am." (a fact/habit)
- "This function **returns** null on error." (how it works)
- "We **deploy** on Fridays." (habit)

Note the "-s" on the verb with *he/she/it* and singular subjects: "the job run**s**,"
"it return**s**" (this connects to subject–verb agreement, Lesson 5).

### Present continuous: happening now

Use *am/is/are* + *-ing* for something in progress right now:

- "I**'m working** on the login bug." (right now)
- "The tests **are running**." (in progress)
- "The deploy **is failing** repeatedly." (happening now)

The common error is dropping the *am/is/are*: ✗ "I working on it" → ✓ "I'm working
on it." The *-ing* verb always needs the *be* helper.

### Present perfect: done, and relevant now

Use *have/has* + past participle for something completed whose result matters now —
especially common for status ("what's done"):

- "I**'ve deployed** the fix." (done, and now it's live — relevant now)
- "We**'ve fixed** the bug." (done, matters now)
- "The build **has finished**." (completed, result relevant)

Present perfect vs simple past: use **present perfect** when the *result* matters
now and you don't say exactly when ("I've fixed it" — it's fixed now); use
**simple past** when it's finished and you give a specific time ("I fixed it
yesterday"). Both are fine; present perfect is the natural choice for "done"
statuses.

### The future: three ways

- **will** — decisions and promises, often made now: "I**'ll** deploy after lunch."
- **going to** — plans/intentions already decided: "We**'re going to** migrate next
  sprint."
- **present continuous** — arranged/scheduled things: "I**'m meeting** the PM at
  3pm." (also used for the near, arranged future)

For most work purposes, "**I'll** [do it]" (will) is the natural, safe choice for
what you're about to do.

{: .note }
> **The status-update tense recipe</br>**
> A status update almost always needs all three core tenses, and choosing them is
> automatic once you see the pattern: <strong>done</strong> → present perfect ("I've
> finished the API"), <strong>in progress</strong> → present continuous ("I'm
> working on the tests"), <strong>blocked/facts</strong> → present simple ("the
> deploy fails because X" / "I need access to Y"). If your status update sounds
> off, check that "done" things are in present perfect, "doing" things in present
> continuous, and you didn't drop an "is/am/are" or "have." (Lesson 22 in Phase 4
> builds the full status-update skill on top of this.)

send you the doc — actually, I'm sending it now."</strong> ("I'll send" (future/will)

sending it now.")
<br><br>
<strong>9.</strong> "The tests pass now, I've fixed them this morning." → <strong>"The
tests pass now — I fixed them this morning."</strong> ("The tests pass now" ✓
(present simple fact). But "I've fixed them this morning" — with the specific time
"this morning" (a finished time period), simple past "I fixed" is more natural than
present perfect.)
<br><br>
<strong>10.</strong> "We going to migrate the database next sprint." → <strong>"We're
going to migrate the database next sprint."</strong> (Dropped the helper — "going
to" needs am/is/are: "we're going to migrate.")

The patterns: don't drop the helper verb (am/is/are for -ing; have/has for perfect);
present simple for facts/habits, present continuous for right-now, present perfect
for done-and-relevant, simple past for done-at-a-specific-time.

## Phrase Bank

| Situation | Tense | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Something is done (status) | present perfect | "I've deployed the fix." "We've merged it." |
| Working on it now | present continuous | "I'm working on X." "The tests are running." |
| A fact / how it works | present simple | "The job runs nightly." "It returns JSON." |
| Finished at a set time | simple past | "I deployed it yesterday." "We shipped it Monday." |
| About to do it | will | "I'll send it now." "I'll deploy after lunch." |
| Planned / decided | going to | "We're going to migrate next sprint." |

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Which three tenses cover almost all workplace writing, and what does each
one signal? Give the "done / doing / fact" mapping.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The three core tenses are: (1) <strong>Present perfect</strong> (have/has + past
participle) — signals <strong>done, and it matters now</strong>: "I've deployed the
fix." This is the natural tense for the "done" part of a status. (2) <strong>Present
continuous</strong> (am/is/are + -ing) — signals <strong>in progress right
now</strong>: "I'm working on the tests." (3) <strong>Present simple</strong> (base
verb, with -s for he/she/it) — signals <strong>facts, habits, how things
work</strong>: "the job runs nightly," "I need access." The mapping that makes
tense-choice automatic: <strong>DONE → present perfect, DOING → present continuous,
FACT → present simple</strong>. A status update naturally uses all three, and once
you see this pattern, choosing the tense becomes automatic. The two supporting
tenses: <strong>simple past</strong> for things finished at a specific time ("I
deployed it yesterday"), and the <strong>future</strong> ("I'll do it"). The most
common errors are dropping the helper verb ("I working" instead of "I'm working")
and using the wrong tense for done things ("I already deploy it" instead of "I've
deployed it").
</details>

**Q2.** What's wrong with "Yesterday I have deployed the hotfix," and what's the
rule about present perfect and specific times?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The error is combining present perfect ("have deployed") with a specific past time
("yesterday"). The fix is <strong>"Yesterday I deployed the hotfix"</strong> (simple
past). The rule: <strong>present perfect cannot be used with a specific past
time</strong> (yesterday, last week, at 3pm, on Monday). Present perfect means "it's
done and the result is relevant now," deliberately without pinning it to a specific
moment; the instant you add a specific past time, you're locating the action
definitely in the past, which is what <strong>simple past</strong> is for. So: use
present perfect when you're not saying when and the result matters now ("I've
deployed the fix" — it's live); use simple past when you give a specific time ("I
deployed it yesterday"). Quick check: if the sentence has a specific past time word,
use simple past. (Words like "already," "just," "yet," "recently" go <em>with</em>
present perfect — "I've already deployed it" — because they don't pin a specific
time; only definite past times force simple past.)
</details>

Write a real status update (or take one you sent) covering: something you finished,
something you're working on now, a fact or a thing you need. Check each verb: is
"done" in present perfect? Is "in progress" in present continuous? Did you drop any
helper verb (am/is/are/have)? Are specific past times paired with simple past, not
present perfect? Fix any errors, and note your most common tense mistake for your
checklist.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise applies the done/doing/fact mapping to real writing. A strong response
produces a status update using present perfect for finished items ("I've finished
the API"), present continuous for in-progress ("I'm working on the tests"), and

mistakes are: (1) <strong>dropping the helper verb</strong> — the most frequent,

yours makes your checklist targeted. If dropping helpers is your pattern, the check
is a presence check: "does every -ing verb have am/is/are, and every past-participle
a have/has?" The reassuring point: workplace tense-choice is simple once you
internalize done/doing/fact — you don't need the full English tense system, just the
three core tenses chosen by asking "done, doing, or fact?", plus not dropping the
helper and not pairing present perfect with a specific time. Add your pattern to the
checklist (Lesson 9).
</details>
