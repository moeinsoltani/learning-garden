# Lesson 10: Common Confusions — do/make, say/tell

## Concept

Some English word pairs mean almost the same thing but are used in specific,
fixed ways — and choosing the wrong one instantly marks writing as non-native,
even when everything else is correct. The biggest offenders: **do vs make**, **say
vs tell**, and a few others. Like prepositions (Lesson 6), these are mostly
**learned as fixed combinations**, not from rules.

```
   do   → tasks, activities, work in general
          "do a review", "do the work", "do testing"
   make → creating/producing something, decisions, changes
          "make a change", "make a decision", "make a mistake"

   say  → the words themselves (no person needed)
          "he said it's broken", "say that again"
   tell → informing a PERSON (needs a person)
          "tell me what happened", "he told the team"
```

You won't master every one, but the handful of high-frequency work collocations
(do/make + common work nouns, say/tell) are worth locking in, because they come up
constantly and the errors are noticeable.

---

## Going Deeper

### do vs make

Rough guide (with exceptions — learn the common ones as chunks): **do** for tasks,
activities, and work in general; **make** for creating/producing something, and for
decisions and changes.

- **do**: do a review, do the work, do testing, do research, do a task, do your
  best, do a demo
- **make**: make a change, make a decision, make a mistake, make progress, make an
  effort, make sure, make a plan, make a suggestion

Common errors: ✗ "make a review" → ✓ "do a review"; ✗ "do a decision" → ✓ "make a
decision"; ✗ "do a mistake" → ✓ "make a mistake"; ✗ "make the testing" → ✓ "do the
testing."

### say vs tell

The key difference: **tell** needs a *person* (you tell *someone*); **say** doesn't
(you say *something*, no person needed).

- **tell** + person: "tell **me**," "tell **the team**," "he told **us**" — ✓ "tell
  me what happened"
- **say** + words (no person): "he **said** it's broken," "**say** that again," "as
  I **said**" — ✓ "he said it would be late"

Common errors: ✗ "he said me" → ✓ "he told me" (say doesn't take a person directly);
✗ "tell that it's broken" → ✓ "say that it's broken" OR "tell me that it's broken"
(tell needs the person).

### The other frequent pairs

- **remember vs remind**: you *remember* something yourself; you *remind* someone
  else. ✗ "remember me to send it" → ✓ "remind me to send it."
- **borrow vs lend**: you *borrow* from someone (take); you *lend* to someone
  (give). ✗ "can you borrow me your charger" → ✓ "can you lend me your charger" (you
  want to borrow it, so they lend it).
- **look vs watch vs see**: *look at* (direct attention), *watch* (observe over
  time), *see* (perceive). "Look at this error," "watch the deploy," "I see the
  problem."

{: .note }
> **Learn them as chunks with work nouns</br>**
> Don't try to derive do vs make from a rule (the rule has too many exceptions).
> Instead, memorize the high-frequency work collocations as fixed units: <em>do a
> review, do testing, make a change, make a decision, make a mistake, make
> progress, make sure</em>. And for say/tell, the one reliable rule that covers
> most cases: <strong>tell needs a person, say doesn't</strong> — "tell me," "say
> it." These few chunks and that one rule handle the large majority of the errors,
> which are the noticeable ones at work.

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What's the one reliable rule for say vs tell, and why is "he said me it's
broken" wrong?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The one reliable rule: <strong>tell needs a person (you tell <em>someone</em>); say
doesn't (you say <em>something</em>, no person needed)</strong>. "Tell" is used with a
direct person object — tell me, tell the team, tell him — you're informing someone.
"Say" is used with the words/message and does not take a direct person object — say
it, say that, he said it's broken, as I said. So "he <strong>said me</strong> it's
broken" is wrong because "say" cannot take a person directly ("said me") — you can't
"say someone" something. The two correct fixes: use "tell" (which does take the
person): "he <strong>told me</strong> it's broken"; or keep "say" but drop the direct
person: "he <strong>said</strong> it's broken" (no person), or add "to": "he
<strong>said</strong> it's broken <strong>to me</strong>" (though "told me" is more
natural). The most natural fix is almost always "he told me," because when you're
mentioning who was informed, "tell + person" is the natural construction. This
person-test is the reliable discriminator: if there's a person receiving the
information right after the verb (told <em>me</em>, tell <em>the team</em>), use
"tell"; if there's no person right after the verb, just the words/message (said
<em>it</em>, said <em>that it would be late</em>), use "say." It handles the large
majority of say/tell choices, and the "said me / say someone" error is one of the
most common and noticeable non-native mistakes, so this one rule (tell = with a
person, say = without) is high-value to lock in. (There are a few fixed expressions
that don't perfectly follow it — "tell a story," "tell the truth," "tell the time" —
but those are memorized chunks, and the person-rule covers the everyday cases.)
</details>

**Q2.** Why is trying to learn do vs make from a rule less effective than memorizing
the common combinations, and which work chunks are most worth locking in?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Trying to learn do vs make from a rule is less effective because <strong>the rule has
too many exceptions to be reliable</strong>. The rough guide (do = tasks/activities,
make = create/produce/decisions) captures the general tendency, but there are many
cases that don't cleanly follow it or that could plausibly go either way by the rule
yet have a fixed correct answer — "make a decision" (a decision isn't obviously
"creating something"), "do your best," "make an effort," "do a favor," "make sure" —
these are just conventional pairings you have to know, and applying the rule would
lead you to guess wrong on a meaningful fraction. Since the pairings are largely
conventional (like prepositions and collocations generally), the reliable approach is
to <strong>memorize the high-frequency combinations as fixed chunks</strong> — learn
"make a decision," "do a review," "make a mistake" as single units, the way you'd
learn vocabulary, so you retrieve the right verb automatically without needing a rule
that would fail. This is faster and more accurate than reasoning from a rule that has
too many exceptions to trust. The work chunks most worth locking in (because they're
frequent and the errors are noticeable): <strong>with "do"</strong> — do a review, do
testing, do research, do the work, do a demo, do a task; <strong>with "make"</strong>
— make a change, make a decision, make a mistake, make progress, make sure, make a
plan, make a suggestion, make an effort. These cover the collocations that come up
constantly in engineering work, and getting them right (do a review, not "make a
review"; make a decision, not "do a decision"; make a mistake, not "do a mistake")
removes some of the most common and noticeable non-native markers. The general
principle (shared with prepositions, Lesson 6): for word-partnership choices that are
convention rather than rule, learn the frequent pairs as chunks rather than trying to
derive them — it's how native speakers actually know them (by familiarity, not rule),
and it's the efficient path for a learner. Add your most-confused pairs to your
checklist, and let the high-frequency chunks become automatic through use.
</details>

automatic. Watch specifically for "make a review/decision" mix-ups and "said me /
tell that" errors — the most common ones. Note which pairs you tend to get wrong for
your checklist. Also try the other pairs (remind/remember, lend/borrow) when they
come up.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds the chunk-familiarity that makes these word choices automatic,
and identifies which specific pairs are yours to watch. A strong response uses the
high-frequency chunks in real messages (the way collocations actually get learned —
through repeated use until the right pairing "sounds right") and honestly notes which
pairs trip you up. The most common errors to watch, and their fixes: "make a review"
→ "do a review"; "do a decision" → "make a decision"; "do a mistake" → "make a
mistake"; "he said me" → "he told me"; "remember me to X" → "remind me to X"; "borrow
me your X" → "lend me your X." These become targeted checklist items — but note that,
like prepositions (Lesson 6), these are relatively low-stakes for being understood (a
do/make slip rarely causes confusion), so they're worth fixing because they're
<em>noticeable</em> markers of non-native writing, not because they block
communication. The efficient approach: lock in the frequent chunks (so the common
ones are right), watch the specific pairs you tend to confuse, and don't stress about
mastering every collocation in English (an endless task). The meta-point of this
phase (Words That Sound Natural): Phase 1 made your writing <em>accurate</em>; this
phase makes it sound <em>natural</em> — and natural word choice comes largely from
using the conventional collocations (do a review, make a decision) rather than
grammatically-plausible-but-unusual combinations ("make a review"), which is learned
through familiarity with the chunks. As these become automatic, your writing sheds
the small non-native markers that even correct grammar leaves, and sounds more like
how a native colleague would write. Add your confused pairs to the checklist, and use
the chunks until they're automatic. Next: phrasal verbs (follow up, roll out, push
back) — the informal verbs that dominate office English and are a big part of
sounding natural.
</details>
