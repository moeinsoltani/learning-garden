# Lesson 19: Apologies and Thanks That Sound Natural

## Concept

Apologies and thanks are warmth tools — but non-native speakers often get the balance
wrong in a specific way: they **over-apologize** (saying sorry for things that don't
need it, which paradoxically undermines confidence) and **under-thank** (missing
chances to appreciate people, which is the warmer move). Fixing this balance makes you
warmer AND more confident.

```
   OVER-APOLOGIZING (common)          RIGHT-SIZED
   ─────────────────────────          ───────────
   "Sorry, sorry to bother you,       "Quick question when you have a sec —"
    so sorry, I have a question..."    (no apology needed for a normal ask)

   UNDER-THANKING (common)            WARMER
   ──────────────────────             ──────
   "ok" (after real help)             "Thank you so much — that really
                                       helped, I owe you one!"
```

Two shifts: **apologize less** (only for real mistakes, sized to the mistake; don't
apologize for normal things like asking a question) and **thank more** (specifically,
for anything someone did that helped). Over-apologizing signals low confidence and
gets tiring; genuine, specific thanks builds warmth and relationships.

---

## Going Deeper

### Apologize only for real mistakes, sized right

Save "sorry" for actual mistakes, and size the apology to the mistake:

- **Small mistake** (a typo, a minor delay): a light "sorry about that" or "oops, my
  bad!" is enough.
- **Real mistake** (broke prod, missed a deadline that mattered): a genuine, brief
  apology that owns it: "I'm sorry — that was my mistake. Here's what happened and how
  I'm fixing it." (Own it, don't grovel.)
- **NOT a mistake** (asking a question, making a normal request, having a different
  view): no apology needed at all. Don't apologize for normal work interactions.

Over-apologizing for non-mistakes (sorry to bother you, sorry for the question, sorry
for asking) is the pattern to cut — it's not needed and it signals you feel you're
imposing when you're not.

### "Sorry" alternatives that aren't apologies

Often what you're really doing isn't apologizing — replace "sorry" with the accurate
warm phrase:

- "Sorry for the delay" → "Thanks for your patience!" (turns a self-focused apology
  into warm appreciation of them)
- "Sorry to bother you" → "Quick question when you have a sec —" (no apology; you're
  not bothering them)
- "Sorry, I don't understand" → "Could you clarify X?" (no apology for asking)

Notice how "thanks for your patience" (thanking them) is warmer than "sorry for the
delay" (apologizing) — the thanks version centers them positively rather than centering
your fault.

### Thank specifically, not generically

"Thanks" is fine, but *specific* thanks is much warmer — it shows you noticed what they
actually did:

- Generic: "thanks!"
- Specific: "Thank you so much for jumping on that bug so fast — you saved the
  release!" / "Really appreciate you taking the time to explain that, it clicked for
  me."

Specific thanks (naming what they did and its impact) feels genuine and builds real
warmth; generic thanks is fine but forgettable. When someone genuinely helped, take the
extra few words to thank them specifically.

### Acknowledge help publicly

A powerful warmth move: thank people *publicly* (in a channel, in front of the team)
for real help — "huge thanks to [name] for staying late to fix the deploy 🙌". Public
recognition is warmer and more valuable than private thanks (it gives them credit in
front of others), and it builds goodwill and a positive team culture. (This connects to
the leadership track's praise lesson.)

{: .note }
> **The two shifts: apologize less, thank more</br>**
> For your warmth goal, the specific rebalancing most non-native speakers need: (1)
> <strong>cut the reflexive apologies</strong> for normal things (asking questions,
> making requests, having views) — you're not imposing, so don't say sorry; save
> "sorry" for real mistakes, sized right. (2) <strong>Add specific thanks</strong> —
> notice when someone helped and appreciate it specifically, ideally publicly. The net
> effect is warmer AND more confident: over-apologizing reads as anxious and
> self-diminishing, while genuine specific thanks reads as warm and secure. Replacing
> "sorry for the delay" with "thanks for your patience" captures the whole shift in
> one move — same situation, but warm-and-confident instead of apologetic.

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why does over-apologizing (saying sorry for non-mistakes) undermine you, and
what should you save "sorry" for?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Over-apologizing — saying "sorry" for things that aren't mistakes (asking a question,
making a normal request, taking someone's time for legitimate work, having a different
view) — undermines you for several reasons: (1) <strong>It signals low confidence and
that you feel you're imposing when you're not</strong> — apologizing for asking a
question or making a request implies you believe you're a burden, an inconvenience, or
doing something wrong, when you're just doing normal work; this reads as anxious,
insecure, and self-diminishing, and it can make others perceive you as less confident
or less sure of your right to be there. (2) <strong>It's tiring and dilutes real
apologies</strong> — constant reflexive sorries are wearing to read, and if you
apologize for everything, your apologies lose meaning, so when you <em>do</em> make a
real mistake, the genuine apology has less weight (you've cried wolf with "sorry"). (3)
<strong>It centers your fault rather than the interaction</strong> — "sorry to bother
you" makes the message about your imposition; a normal ask doesn't need that framing,
and the apology adds an unnecessary negative self-focus. (4) <strong>It's often
padding</strong> — the apologies add length without value (Lesson 13). The paradox is
that over-apologizing, which feels polite and considerate, actually reads as anxious
and undermines how you come across — it's <em>less</em> warm-and-secure than simply not
apologizing for normal things. What you should save "sorry" for: <strong>real
mistakes, sized to the mistake</strong>. A small mistake (typo, minor delay) gets a
light "sorry about that" / "oops, my bad." A real mistake (broke prod, missed a
deadline that mattered, made an error that affected others) gets a genuine, brief
apology that <em>owns</em> it without groveling ("I'm sorry, that was my mistake —
here's what happened and how I'm fixing it"). And things that aren't mistakes at all
(asking questions, making requests, having views, taking legitimate work time) get
<em>no</em> apology — because there's nothing to apologize for. The shift for non-native
speakers (who often over-apologize, sometimes because their language uses apology-like
politeness more, or from anxiety about imposing in a second language): cut the
reflexive sorries for normal interactions, and reserve "sorry" for actual mistakes.
This makes you warmer AND more confident — you stop signaling that you're a burden, and
you come across as secure and appropriately gracious (apologizing genuinely when you
should, not constantly when you shouldn't). A useful replacement move: often "sorry"
can become "thanks" — "sorry for the delay" → "thanks for your patience" — which turns
a self-diminishing apology into warm appreciation of the other person, capturing the
apologize-less-thank-more shift in one move (Q2). For your goal, right-sizing apologies
(genuine for real mistakes, none for normal things) contributes to warm-and-confident
communication, whereas over-apologizing paradoxically undercuts both warmth and
confidence.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is specific thanks warmer than generic thanks, and why is "thanks for your
patience" often better than "sorry for the delay"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
<strong>Specific thanks is warmer than generic thanks</strong> because it demonstrates
genuine attention and shows you actually noticed and valued what the person did.
"Thanks!" is fine but generic — it could follow anything, so it reads as a polite
reflex rather than real appreciation. "Thank you so much for jumping on that bug so
fast — you saved the release!" is specific: it names <em>what</em> they did (jumped on
the bug quickly) and <em>its impact</em> (saved the release), which shows you paid
attention to their specific contribution and genuinely value it. This makes the person
feel truly seen and appreciated — their particular effort was noticed and mattered —
which builds real warmth and strengthens the relationship, far more than a generic
"thanks" that acknowledges the help exists but not what it was or why it mattered.
Specific thanks is also more <em>credible</em> — it's obviously genuine (you couldn't
say it without knowing what they did), whereas generic thanks could be automatic. So
when someone genuinely helped, taking the few extra words to thank them specifically
(name the help and its impact) is a high-value warmth move — it's the difference between
appreciation that lands and appreciation that's forgettable. Why "thanks for your
patience" is often better than "sorry for the delay": they describe the same situation
(you were late/slow), but they frame it oppositely. "Sorry for the delay"
<strong>centers your fault</strong> — it's a self-focused apology that highlights your
shortcoming (you were late) and casts the interaction in a negative, apologetic light.
"Thanks for your patience" <strong>centers the other person positively</strong> — it
reframes the same delay as an opportunity to appreciate <em>them</em> (their patience,
their understanding), turning a self-diminishing apology into warm gratitude directed at
them. The person feels appreciated (you thanked them) rather than just receiving your
apology (which is about you), and the interaction ends on a positive, warm note rather
than a negative, apologetic one. It also embodies the apologize-less-thank-more shift
(Q1) — replacing an unnecessary self-focused apology with warm other-focused thanks —
in a single move: the delay probably didn't warrant an apology (unless it caused real
harm), and "thanks for your patience" is both warmer (it appreciates them) and more
confident (it doesn't dwell on your fault) than "sorry for the delay." The general
principle: <strong>where you can, frame warmth as appreciation of the other person
(thanks) rather than apology for yourself (sorry)</strong> — thanking is warmer and more
confident than apologizing, it centers the other person positively, and it builds the
relationship; so look for chances to convert reflexive apologies into genuine, specific
thanks. For your goal, this is a powerful move: specific, other-focused thanks
(especially public thanks for big help) is one of the warmest things you can do, and
it's the positive counterpart to cutting over-apologizing — together they rebalance you
toward warm-and-confident (thank more, apologize less) rather than anxious-and-
apologetic.
</details>

## Homework

start to write "sorry" for a non-mistake (a question, a request, a normal delay), stop
and either drop it or convert it to thanks ("sorry for the delay" → "thanks for your
patience"). (2) Thank people specifically and, when warranted, publicly — when someone
helps, name what they did and its impact, and consider recognizing big help in a
channel. Notice how these two shifts feel (warmer AND more confident). Add to your
checklist: "am I apologizing for a non-mistake? (cut it / convert to thanks) — and did
I thank specifically where someone helped?"
