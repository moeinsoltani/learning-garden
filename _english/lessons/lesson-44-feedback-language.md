# Lesson 44: Feedback Language

## Concept

Giving feedback — especially critical feedback — is one of the hardest things to say well, and
one of the most important for a lead. Say it too bluntly and it wounds; say it too softly and
the message gets lost. The skill is language that is **clear about the issue and kind in
delivery** — so the person hears what needs to change *and* feels supported, not attacked. There
are proven structures (like SBI) and specific phrasings that make this reliably work.

```
   THE SBI STRUCTURE (a reliable frame):
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ SITUATION  when/where: "In yesterday's standup…"       │
   │ BEHAVIOR   what you observed (facts, not judgment):    │
   │            "…you cut off Sana before she finished."    │
   │ IMPACT     the effect: "…and she seemed to shut down    │
   │            and didn't share her idea."                  │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

   Facts + impact, not character judgment. Then a conversation.
```

The reframe: **feedback is about specific behavior and its impact, not about the person's
character** — "you interrupted Sana and she went quiet" (behavior + impact) not "you're
dismissive" (a character verdict). Describing what you observed and its effect, kindly, lets the
person hear it without feeling attacked and shows them exactly what to change. This — plus warmth
and genuine care — is how feedback helps rather than hurts.

---

## How It Works

### Separate behavior from character

Feedback lands well when it targets **specific behavior**, not the person's character. "You've
missed the last three standups" (behavior) is workable; "you're unreliable" (character) is an
attack that invites defensiveness and offers nothing to fix. People can change behavior; a
character label just wounds and sticks. Always describe *what happened*, not *what kind of person
they are*.

### Use SBI: Situation, Behavior, Impact

A reliable structure: state the **Situation** (when/where — "in yesterday's review"), the
**Behavior** you observed as neutral fact ("you rewrote the section without mentioning it to
Sam"), and the **Impact** it had ("Sam felt his work was dismissed, and it created some
tension"). SBI keeps feedback specific, factual, and about effect rather than judgment — which
makes it clear <em>and</em> non-attacking. Then open a conversation ("what's your view?").

### Lead with care and intent

Signal that the feedback comes from wanting them to succeed, not from criticism. "I'm sharing
this because I want you to do well here" / "I really value your work, and I want to flag
something" frames it as support. When people know feedback is well-intentioned (you're on their
side), they receive it far better than when it feels like an attack. This is the heart of caring
feedback — clarity <em>and</em> care.

### Be direct — don't bury the message

Warmth doesn't mean vagueness. Over-softening ("maybe, possibly, it's totally fine, but perhaps
consider…") buries the message so the person doesn't realize it's important — which is unkind in
its own way (they don't get the chance to fix it). Be genuinely kind <em>and</em> genuinely clear:
say the actual issue plainly, wrapped in warmth. "Radical candor" — care personally AND challenge
directly. The goal is clear + kind, not one at the cost of the other.

### Make it a dialogue, not a verdict

Feedback should open a conversation, not deliver a sentence. After SBI, ask for their view —
"how do you see it?" / "was there something going on?" — because you might be missing context, and
because people commit to changes they help shape. A monologue verdict invites resistance; a
dialogue invites ownership.

### Timely, private, and specific

Give critical feedback **privately** (never criticize in public — it humiliates and breaks trust),
**soon** after the event (so it's fresh and actionable, not a surprise months later), and
**specifically** (a concrete example they can learn from, not a vague "communication issues").
These aren't language points exactly, but they determine whether the feedback can land at all.

{: .note }
> **Feedback is about behavior and impact, delivered with care — clear AND kind**
> Critical feedback is hard to say well and vital for a lead. The key is language targeting
> specific <em>behavior and its impact</em> ("you interrupted Sana and she went quiet"), not the
> person's <em>character</em> ("you're dismissive") — behavior can change, a character label just
> wounds. The SBI structure (Situation, Behavior, Impact) makes this reliable: facts and effect,
> not judgment. Lead with care (you want them to succeed), be direct enough that the message isn't
> buried (kind AND clear, not vague), make it a dialogue (ask their view), and give it privately,
> soon, and specifically. Done this way, feedback helps people grow and strengthens trust; done
> bluntly it wounds, done vaguely it's lost. Clear + kind, from genuine care, is the goal.

## Lab — Rewrite Drill

Turn each poor feedback into clear, kind, SBI-style feedback.

**Your answer:**

1. **Character attack:** "You're really disorganized and unreliable."
2. **Too vague/buried:** "So, um, I mean it's totally fine and no big deal at all, but maybe, if
   you get a chance, possibly think about, like, the way you communicate sometimes? But really
   it's fine!" (There's a real issue: they've been dismissive of a junior in reviews.)
3. **Public + blunt:** In a team channel, you write "@Jordan this PR is sloppy, you didn't test
   it." Rewrite as private, behavior-focused feedback.
4. **A missed message:** You want to tell a senior engineer that their habit of rewriting others'
   code without discussion is hurting the team. Give SBI feedback, with care.

<details>
<summary>Show Model Answers</summary>
<br>
<strong>1. → behavior + impact:</strong> "I've noticed the last few tasks slipped past their dates
without a heads-up (behavior), and it made it hard for the team to plan around them (impact). I
wanted to talk about what's getting in the way — is something making these hard to hit?" (Specific
behavior and impact, opens a dialogue — versus "you're disorganized and unreliable," a character
attack that wounds and gives nothing to fix.)
<br><br>
<strong>2. → clear AND kind:</strong> "I want to share something because I think it'll help you —
in the last couple of code reviews, some of the comments to Priya came across as a bit dismissive
(e.g., 'this is obviously wrong'), and I noticed she got quieter afterward. I don't think you
meant it that way, but I wanted to flag the impact. Could we talk about it?" (Direct about the
real issue (not buried under "it's totally fine"), specific example, states the impact, assumes
good intent, opens dialogue — kind AND clear, versus so-softened the person won't realize there's
anything to address.)
<br><br>
<strong>3. → private + behavior-focused:</strong> (In a DM, not the channel) "Hey Jordan — quick
thing on the auth PR: I noticed it didn't have tests for the new endpoints, and it caused a bug
that slipped to staging. Could we make sure new endpoints get test coverage going forward? Happy
to pair on it if useful. Thanks for the quick turnaround otherwise!" (Private (not public
humiliation), specific behavior (no tests) + impact (bug in staging), a clear ask, offer to help,
and a warm note — versus the public "this PR is sloppy," which humiliates and attacks.)
<br><br>
<strong>4. → SBI with care:</strong> "I really value your technical judgment, which is why I
wanted to raise this (care/intent). In the last few PRs, I noticed you rewrote parts of Sam's and
Priya's code directly without discussing it first (behavior). I think it came from wanting the
code to be great, but the effect was that they felt their work was dismissed, and it created some
tension on the team (impact). How are you seeing it? I'd love to find a way that keeps your high
bar but brings people along." (Leads with care, states behavior and impact via SBI, assumes good
intent, opens a dialogue, and points at a constructive path — clear about a real problem, kind in
delivery.)
<br><br>
The patterns: target specific behavior + impact (not character), use SBI, lead with care/intent,
be direct enough that the message lands (not buried), keep it private and specific, and open a
dialogue.
</details>

## Phrase Bank

| Move | Phrase |
|---|---|
| Lead with care/intent | "I'm sharing this because I want you to do well." |
| Situation | "In yesterday's review…" / "The last few PRs…" |
| Behavior (fact) | "I noticed you [specific observable action]." |
| Impact | "The effect was [specific impact]." |
| Assume good intent | "I don't think you meant it that way, but…" |
| Open dialogue | "How do you see it? Was something going on?" |
| Point forward | "Going forward, could we…?" / "I'd love to find a way that…" |
| Warm close | "I really value your work — happy to talk more anytime." |

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why should feedback target specific behavior and its impact rather than the person's
character, and what is the SBI structure?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Feedback should target specific behavior and its impact rather than character because <strong>a
person can change their behavior, but a character label only wounds and offers nothing actionable
— and it triggers defensiveness that blocks the feedback from landing</strong>. Compare "you
interrupted Sana and she went quiet" (behavior + impact) with "you're dismissive" (character): (1)
<strong>behavior is actionable, character isn't</strong> — telling someone a specific behavior
("you missed the last three standups", "you rewrote Sam's code without discussing it") shows them
exactly what to change; telling them they're "unreliable" or "dismissive" gives them nothing
concrete to do differently — it's a verdict on who they are, not a fixable action. (2)
<strong>character labels attack identity and trigger defensiveness</strong> — "you're dismissive"
attacks the person's sense of who they are, which feels like a fundamental judgment (not just "I
did a thing" but "I am a bad thing"), so they defend their character rather than engage with the
feedback; behavior feedback ("this specific action had this effect") is about a thing they did, not
who they are, so it's far less threatening and they can consider it without their identity being
under attack. (3) <strong>character labels wound and stick</strong> — being told you're "unreliable"
or "difficult" hurts deeply and lingers (it's a statement about your worth), whereas "you missed
these deadlines" is a workable observation; the character version damages the relationship and the
person's morale for no added benefit. So targeting behavior + impact makes feedback actionable
(they know what to change), non-threatening (about an action, not their character, so they stay
open), and kind (it doesn't wound their sense of self). The SBI structure operationalizes this — a
reliable three-part frame: (1) <strong>Situation</strong> — state when/where it happened, to
anchor it in a specific concrete instance ("in yesterday's review", "in the last few PRs"), not a
vague generalization; (2) <strong>Behavior</strong> — describe the specific behavior you observed
as neutral fact, without judgment ("you rewrote the section without mentioning it to Sam", "you
cut off Sana before she finished") — observable actions, not interpretations or character claims;
and (3) <strong>Impact</strong> — state the effect the behavior had ("Sam felt his work was
dismissed and it created tension", "Sana went quiet and didn't share her idea") — which shows
<em>why</em> the behavior matters (its real consequence) without labeling the person. SBI works
because it keeps feedback (a) specific — anchored to a concrete situation and observable behavior,
not a vague "you always…"; (b) factual — describing what happened, not judging character, so it's
hard to dispute and non-attacking; and (c) focused on impact — explaining the effect, which both
motivates change (they see the real consequence) and keeps it about outcomes rather than judgment.
After SBI, you open a dialogue ("how do you see it?") because you might be missing context and
because people commit to changes they help shape. So SBI is a reliable frame that makes feedback
clear (specific behavior and its impact) and kind (facts and effect, not character judgment) at the
same time — exactly the clear-AND-kind combination that makes feedback help people grow rather than
wound them. For a non-native speaker, having a reliable structure like SBI is especially valuable:
it gives you a dependable frame for these high-stakes conversations, so you're not improvising the
hardest kind of communication.
</details>

**Q2.** Why is over-softening feedback (burying the message) unkind in its own way, and what does
"clear AND kind" mean in practice?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Over-softening feedback is unkind in its own way because <strong>if you bury the message so much
that the person doesn't realize there's a real issue, you deprive them of the chance to fix it —
which ultimately hurts them</strong>. It feels kind to soften heavily ("it's totally fine, no big
deal, but maybe possibly consider…") — you avoid the discomfort of saying something critical, and
it seems gentle. But the effect is unkind: (1) <strong>the person doesn't get the message</strong>
— so buried under hedges and reassurances that "it's fine," they don't realize there's actually
something important to address, and they carry on with the problem behavior, unaware; (2)
<strong>they lose the chance to improve</strong> — feedback exists to help someone grow or fix
something; if they don't receive it (because it was too buried to register), they can't act on it,
so the problem persists and may grow, eventually causing them real harm (a pattern that could've
been fixed early becomes a bigger issue — a bad review, lost trust, a missed promotion — that
blindsides them); (3) <strong>it can be a bigger, harsher surprise later</strong> — the issue you
softened into invisibility doesn't go away; it surfaces later (in a performance review, a conflict,
a crisis) as a much harder blow than timely clear feedback would have been. So over-softening,
though it feels kind (and comfortable for you), actually fails the person — it prioritizes your
comfort and the appearance of niceness over their genuine benefit (knowing what to fix). Truly
caring about someone means being willing to tell them the hard thing they need to hear, clearly
enough that they get it — because that's what helps them. Vagueness that leaves them in the dark
isn't kindness; it's a failure to help disguised as niceness. What "clear AND kind" means in
practice: <strong>saying the actual issue plainly (clear) while delivering it with genuine warmth
and care (kind) — both fully, neither sacrificed for the other</strong>. It's not a trade-off where
more kindness means less clarity; it's doing both at once. Concretely: (1) <strong>clear</strong> —
state the real issue directly and specifically, so the person genuinely understands what needs to
change ("in the last couple of reviews, some comments to Priya came across as dismissive, and she
got quieter") — not buried under so many hedges that the message is lost; the person should come
away knowing there's something real to address and what it is. (2) <strong>kind</strong> — deliver
that clear message with warmth: lead with care and good intent ("I'm sharing this because I want
you to do well"), assume the best ("I don't think you meant it that way"), target behavior not
character, use a gentle tone, and open a dialogue — so the person feels supported and respected,
not attacked. The combination is "radical candor" (Kim Scott): <em>care personally AND challenge
directly</em> — you care about the person (kind) and you tell them the truth they need (clear). The
two aren't in tension; the warmth is what makes the clarity receivable (a clear message delivered
with care lands as help, not attack), and the clarity is what makes the kindness real (genuine
kindness includes telling them what they need to know). The failure modes are both directions:
too blunt (clear but not kind — wounds and creates defensiveness) and too soft (kind but not clear
— the message is lost, which isn't actually kind). "Clear AND kind" hits both — the person hears
exactly what needs to change (clear) and feels you're on their side (kind), which is what makes
feedback both land and strengthen the relationship. For a lead, this is the essential feedback
skill: the courage to be clear combined with the care to be kind, so your feedback genuinely helps
people grow while keeping their trust.
</details>

## Homework

Situation, the observable Behavior (fact, not character judgment), and its Impact. Lead with care
("I'm sharing this because I want you to do well"), be clear enough that the message isn't buried
(clear AND kind, not vague), assume good intent, and open a dialogue ("how do you see it?"). Give
it privately, soon, and specifically. Notice that behavior-and-impact feedback, delivered warmly,
lands as help rather than attack. Add to your habits: "behavior not character? SBI? led with care?
clear AND kind? private, timely, dialogue?"
