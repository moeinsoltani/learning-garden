# Lesson 09: Error Clinic — Your Personal Checklist

## Concept

This lesson assembles everything from Phase 1 into the one tool you'll actually use:
a **personal pre-send checklist** of *your* specific recurring errors. The insight
that makes this work: you don't have 100 different errors — you have maybe 5, and you
repeat them. Find your 5, build a 10-second check for them, and your writing gets
dramatically more accurate.

```
   THE MYTH:  "I need to master all of English grammar."
   THE TRUTH: "I make ~5 recurring errors. I need to catch THOSE."

   Your checklist (built from YOUR patterns in Lessons 1–8):
   □ dropped a subject/verb?  (is/am/are, I/it/we)     — L1, L4
   □ article: "the production"→production, "a feedback"→feedback  — L2, L3
   □ agreement: "content are"→"content is"             — L5
   □ tense: "I already deploy"→"I've deployed"         — L4
   □ tone: does this read warm or cold?                — L8
```

The most common errors for non-native engineers cluster in a small set: dropped
words, articles, subject–verb agreement, tenses. Your job in this lesson is to
identify *which* of these are yours (from the patterns you noticed in Lessons 1–8)
and turn them into a fast, personal check.

## Going Deeper

### Your top-10 candidate errors (from Phase 1)

The recurring errors this phase covered, any of which might be yours:

1. **Dropped subject or verb** — "working on it" (→ "I'm working on it"), "not sure
   why" (→ "I'm not sure why") — L1
2. **Word order** — "the PR review can you" (→ "can you review the PR") — L1
3. **Missing article** — "I found bug" (→ "I found a bug") — L2
4. **Wrong a/the** — "the bug" on first mention (→ "a bug") — L2
5. **Article on uncountables/environments** — "a feedback," "the production" (→
   "feedback," "production") — L3
6. **Dropped helper verb** — "I working," "the build failing" (→ "I'm working,"
   "the build is failing") — L4
7. **Wrong tense for done** — "I already deploy" (→ "I've deployed") — L4
8. **Subject–verb agreement** — "the content are" (→ "the content is") — L5
9. **Preposition slips** — "depends of," "explain me" (→ "depends on," "explain to
   me") — L6
10. **Comma splice / run-on** — "I did X, it broke Y" (→ split or join properly) —
    L7

Plus the tone check (L8): does this read warm or cold?

### Build YOUR checklist (the key step)

From Lessons 1–8, you noticed which patterns you actually repeat. Pick your **top
3–5** (not all 10 — just yours) and write them as a short, personal checklist. For
most non-native engineers, the highest-frequency personal errors are some subset of:
dropped words, articles (missing or on uncountables), agreement (content are), and
tense (dropped helpers). Your checklist should be short enough to run in your head in
10 seconds before sending an important message.

### The 10-second self-edit

The habit: before sending a message that matters (not a quick "ok"), read it once and
run your checklist. It takes 10 seconds and catches most of your errors *before*
they're sent. The check is fast because it's *targeted* — you're not proofreading
against all of English grammar, just looking for your 3–5 known patterns. This is far
more effective than either not checking at all or trying to check everything.

### Tools without dependence

AI writing tools (Grammarly, ChatGPT, etc.) can check your writing and are genuinely
useful — but use them to *learn*, not to depend. When a tool corrects something,
notice *what* it corrected and *why* — if it's one of your patterns, that's
reinforcement; if it's new, add it to your understanding. The goal is that your own
writing improves, so the tool catches less over time. Use tools as a safety net and a
teacher, not a crutch that keeps you from learning your own patterns.

{: .note }
> **Keep an error log**
> Beyond the checklist, keep a running note of specific errors you catch (or that
> someone/a tool corrects) — the actual sentence, and the fix. Over a few weeks,
> patterns emerge clearly, and re-reading your log reinforces the corrections. This
> log is also how you'll update your checklist over time (Lesson 49 rebuilds it with
> everything the later phases add). The log turns scattered corrections into a clear
> picture of <em>your</em> English, which is exactly what makes improvement fast:
> you're not fixing "English," you're fixing your specific handful of patterns.

## Lab — Rewrite Drill

Here's a full day of one engineer's messages, each with one or more errors from
Phase 1. Edit them all — then compare with the model edit, and notice which error
types appear most (that tells you what a checklist targets).

**Your edit (fix each):**

1. "Morning! I working on the login bug, will have update soon."
2. "The tests are failing but I think is a flaky test, not real."  (context: general/flaky)
3. "I deployed to the production, everything looks good."
4. "Can you give me a feedback on the PR when you get chance?"
5. "The list of open tickets are getting long, we should triage."
6. "I already fix the timeout issue, it's live now."
7. "Not sure why the build failing, let me look into."
8. "Thanks for helping, I owe you." (make it warm)

<details>
<summary>Show Model Edit</summary>
<br>
<strong>1.</strong> "Morning! I working on the login bug, will have update soon." →
<strong>"Morning! I'm working on the login bug — I'll have an update soon."</strong>
(Dropped helper "I'm" [L4]; dropped article "an update" [L2]; dropped subject "I'll"
[L1]; comma splice fixed with a dash [L7].)
<br><br>
<strong>2.</strong> "The tests are failing but I think is a flaky test." → <strong>"The
tests are failing, but I think it's a flaky test."</strong> (Dropped subject+verb "it's"
[L1/L4] — "is a flaky test" is missing "it"; comma before "but" [L7].)
<br><br>
<strong>3.</strong> "I deployed to the production, everything looks good." → <strong>"I
deployed to production — everything looks good."</strong> ("the production" → "production,"
no article on the environment name [L3]; comma splice → dash/period [L7].)
<br><br>
<strong>4.</strong> "Can you give me a feedback on the PR when you get chance?" →
<strong>"Can you give me feedback on the PR when you get a chance?"</strong> ("a
feedback" → "feedback," uncountable [L3]; "get chance" → "get a chance," dropped
article [L2].)
<br><br>
<strong>5.</strong> "The list of open tickets are getting long." → <strong>"The list
of open tickets is getting long."</strong> (Agreement — subject is "list" (singular),
not "tickets"; the distance trap [L5]. → "is," not "are.")
<br><br>
<strong>6.</strong> "I already fix the timeout issue, it's live now." → <strong>"I've
already fixed the timeout issue — it's live now."</strong> (Wrong tense for done:
"already fix" → "I've already fixed," present perfect [L4]; comma splice → dash [L7].)
<br><br>
<strong>7.</strong> "Not sure why the build failing, let me look into." → <strong>"I'm
not sure why the build is failing — let me look into it."</strong> (Dropped subject
"I'm" [L1]; dropped helper "is failing" [L4]; dropped object "into it" [L1] — "look
into" needs an object; comma splice → dash [L7].)
<br><br>
<strong>8.</strong> "Thanks for helping, I owe you." → <strong>"Thanks so much for
your help — I owe you one! 🙏"</strong> (Warmed up [L8]: "Thanks so much," one
exclamation mark, an emoji; "I owe you one" is the natural idiom.)
<br><br>
<strong>Which errors appeared most?</strong> Count them: dropped words (subjects,
verbs, articles, objects) appeared in almost every message — that's the #1 pattern
for many non-native engineers. Then articles (uncountable "a feedback," environment
"the production," dropped "a chance"), tense (dropped helpers, "already fix"), and
agreement (the distance trap). If this were <em>your</em> day of messages, your
checklist would target exactly these — dropped words first, then articles, tense,
agreement. That's the whole point: your errors cluster, and a targeted check catches
them.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** Why is a short, personal checklist of *your* recurring errors more effective
than trying to master all of English grammar or proofreading against everything?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Because of a simple but powerful fact: <strong>you don't make a hundred different
errors — you make a small number (maybe 5) and repeat them.</strong> Non-native
writers' errors cluster into a handful of personal patterns (for many engineers:
dropped words, articles, agreement, tense) that recur across nearly everything they
write, rather than being scattered randomly across all of English grammar. Given
that, three approaches compare: (1) <strong>Trying to master all of English
grammar</strong> is inefficient and demoralizing — English grammar is vast, much of
it you already handle fine, and most of it isn't where your errors are; spending
effort on grammar you don't get wrong is wasted, and the sheer scope makes it feel
hopeless. (2) <strong>Proofreading against everything</strong> (checking each message
for every possible error) is slow, mentally exhausting, and unfocused — you can't
hold all of English grammar in your head while checking, so you either miss things or
spend too long, and it's not sustainable for everyday messages. (3) <strong>A short
personal checklist of your recurring errors</strong> is fast, focused, and effective:
you're looking for only <em>your</em> 3–5 known patterns, which you <em>can</em> hold
in your head, so the check takes ~10 seconds and catches the large majority of your
actual errors (because your actual errors ARE those patterns). It works precisely
because it matches effort to reality — your errors are few and recurring, so a
targeted check for those few catches most of them, while ignoring the vast grammar
you don't get wrong. This is the whole philosophy of Phase 1: not "learn all of
English" (impossible and unnecessary) but "identify your specific recurring errors
and build a quick check for them" (achievable and highly effective). The checklist is
efficient (short, fast), personalized (targets your patterns, not generic grammar),
and sustainable (10 seconds before an important message, doable every day). And it
improves over time — as you catch and internalize a pattern, it stops being an error,
and you can drop it from the checklist and add any new pattern you notice (Lesson 49
rebuilds it). The meta-insight worth internalizing: accurate writing for a non-native
speaker isn't about knowing more grammar — it's about knowing <em>your</em> handful
of errors and catching them, which is a far smaller and more achievable target than
"mastering English," and which produces dramatically more accurate writing for far
less effort. Match the check to your actual errors, and you get most of the benefit
for a fraction of the work.
</details>

**Q2.** What are the highest-frequency error patterns for a non-native engineer, and
how would you turn "I catch myself dropping words" into an actionable checklist item?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The highest-frequency error patterns for non-native engineers cluster in a small set
(covered across Phase 1): (1) <strong>dropped words</strong> — subjects (I/it/we),
helper verbs (is/am/are/have), articles (a/the), and sometimes objects — the words
that vanish when typing fast because your brain filled them in; (2) <strong>articles</strong>
— missing them, wrong a/the, or adding them where English uses none ("a feedback,"
"the production"); (3) <strong>subject–verb agreement</strong> — especially "content/
data/feedback ARE" (should be IS) and the distance trap ("the list of X are"); (4)
<strong>verb tense</strong> — dropped helpers ("I working") and wrong tense for done
things ("I already deploy" vs "I've deployed"). These four cover the large majority of
what actually goes wrong. Turning "I catch myself dropping words" into an actionable
checklist item: the vague awareness ("I drop words") becomes a <em>specific check you
can run</em> — for example: <strong>"Before sending: does every sentence have its
subject and main verb? (find 'who does what') Did I include the article? Are the
is/am/are/have helpers present?"</strong> The key is making it a concrete
<em>action</em> (a specific thing to look for) rather than a general worry. The
sentence-core check from Lesson 1 ("who does what?") is the actionable form of "don't
drop the subject/verb" — it's a specific question you ask, not a vague reminder to be
careful. Similarly, "watch articles" becomes "check for a/the before nouns, and for
'a feedback/the production' specifically"; "watch agreement" becomes "if the subject
is content/data/feedback, use IS." The pattern: convert each vague awareness into a
concrete, checkable question or a specific list of words/situations to look for — so
that when you run the check, you know exactly what you're looking <em>for</em>, which
makes the check fast and reliable. A good personal checklist item is specific enough
that you can definitively answer "yes I did that" or "no, fix it" in a second, rather
than a general "is this good?" that you can't act on. So the homework of building your
checklist is really about translating the patterns you noticed (Lessons 1–8) into
concrete checkable questions targeting your specific errors — and the more specific
each item, the more useful the checklist. Your dropped-words pattern, being the most
common, becomes the first and most important item: the "who does what, with all its
small words?" check before sending.
</details>

## Homework

Build your personal checklist. Review the patterns you noticed across Lessons 1–8 and
write down your **top 3–5** recurring errors as specific, checkable items (e.g., "did
I drop a subject/verb/article?", "content/data → IS not are", "done → I've done"). Make
it short enough to run in your head in 10 seconds. Then use it: for one week, run your
checklist before every message that matters, and keep an error log of anything you
catch (or that gets corrected). At the end, refine the checklist — drop any pattern
you've stopped making, add any new one. This checklist is the practical tool you'll
carry forward through the whole course.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
This is the capstone of Phase 1, and the deliverable — your personal checklist — is
the single most valuable thing you take from these nine lessons. A strong response
produces a genuinely personal checklist (based on the patterns <em>you</em> noticed,
not a generic list) with 3–5 specific, checkable items, short enough to run mentally
in seconds. For most non-native engineers it will include some subset of: (1) the
dropped-words check ("who does what? — is every subject, verb, article, and object
present?") — usually the #1 item, since dropped words are the most common pattern; (2)
the article check ("a feedback → feedback, the production → production; did I include
articles where needed?"); (3) the agreement check ("content/data/feedback/information
→ IS not are; the list of X → match the real subject"); (4) the tense check ("done →
I've done; helpers not dropped"). The exact items depend on your patterns, and that
personalization is the point — someone who never drops helpers but always slips on
articles has a different checklist than someone with the reverse. The week of use
builds the habit (running the check before important messages until it's automatic)
and the error log makes your patterns concrete and reinforces the fixes (seeing the
same error repeatedly in your log drives home what to watch for, and correcting it
each time trains the fix). The refinement step keeps the checklist alive: as you
internalize a pattern (it stops being an error because the correct form is now
automatic), you drop it, and you add any new pattern you notice — so the checklist
always targets your <em>current</em> errors, staying short and relevant. The
reassuring, empowering meta-point that closes Phase 1: <strong>you now have a concrete,
achievable system for accurate writing</strong> — not "master all of English" (which
felt impossible and vague), but "catch my specific handful of recurring errors with a
10-second check" (which is doable and effective). This reframes the whole challenge
from an overwhelming, endless one (learn all of English grammar) into a manageable,
finite one (identify and catch my ~5 patterns), and it delivers most of the accuracy
benefit for a fraction of the effort. Your writing will get noticeably more accurate
just from running this check, and it will keep improving as patterns internalize and
drop off the list. You'll rebuild this checklist as v2 in Lesson 49, incorporating
everything the later phases add (word choice, tone, structure) — but this Phase 1
version, focused on sentence accuracy, is the foundation, and it's already the tool
that will most improve your day-to-day writing. Carry it forward: the next phases
build on this accurate foundation to add natural word choice (Phase 2), warmth and
tone (Phase 3), and the specific skills for Slack, meetings, docs, and feedback — but
it all rests on the accurate sentences this phase taught you to write, checked by the
personal checklist you just built.
</details>
