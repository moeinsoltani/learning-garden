# Lesson 17: Requests That Don't Sound Like Orders

## Concept

Asking people to do things is a huge part of work — and a bare command ("Do X")
sounds like an order, even to a peer. English has a "politeness ladder" for
requests, and the skill is matching the politeness level to the size of what you're
asking. Too blunt sounds bossy; too elaborate for a tiny ask sounds odd.

```
   THE REQUEST LADDER (least → most polite):

   "Do X."                          ← an order (avoid for peers)
   "Can you do X?"                  ← casual, fine for small asks
   "Could you do X?"                ← polite, good default
   "Would you mind doing X?"        ← more polite (bigger ask)
   "When you get a chance, could    ← very polite (imposition or
    you possibly do X?"                asking a busy person)

   MATCH the rung to the SIZE of the ask.
```

The core move: use "**could you**" instead of a bare command for almost any request
(it's the polite default), and add more politeness ("would you mind," "when you get a
chance," "if possible") for bigger asks or busier people. Small asks don't need heavy
politeness; big asks do.

---

## Going Deeper

### The request ladder

Matched to the size of the ask:

- **"Can you X?"** — casual, fine for small routine asks between peers ("can you
  merge this?").
- **"Could you X?"** — the polite default; works for almost everything ("could you
  take a look at this?"). Slightly more polite than "can you."
- **"Would you mind X-ing?"** — more polite, good for a bigger ask or a busier
  person ("would you mind reviewing this today?").
- **"When you get a chance, could you...?"** / **"If you have time, could you...?"**
  — very polite; for impositions or asking someone busy/senior.

### Add "when you get a chance" to remove pressure

A hugely useful phrase: **"when you get a chance"** (or "no rush," "whenever works").
It makes a request feel considerate rather than demanding by signaling you respect
their time and aren't pressuring them:

- "Could you review this **when you get a chance**?" — warm, no pressure.
- "**No rush**, but could you look at X sometime this week?"

If it IS urgent, say so kindly with the reason: "Could you review this today if
possible? It's blocking the release — sorry for the quick turnaround."

### State deadlines kindly

When there's a real deadline, state it — but frame it with a reason and warmth, not
as a demand:

- ✗ "I need this by Thursday." (demand)
- ✓ "Could you get this to me by Thursday if possible? It feeds into the release, so
  that timing would really help. Thanks!"

The reason ("it feeds into the release") makes the deadline feel legitimate rather
than arbitrary, and the warmth keeps it friendly.

### Requesting up, down, and sideways

- **From your manager or someone senior**: a bit more politeness and often a reason
  ("Could you help me get access to X when you have a moment? I need it for the
  migration.").
- **From a peer**: "could you" is plenty ("could you review my PR?").
- **From someone junior or someone you're delegating to**: still "could you" (never
  a bare order), but you can be more direct about the expectation while staying warm
  ("Could you take this on? Happy to help if you get stuck.").

{: .note }
> **"Could you" is your default — never a bare command</br>**
> The simplest rule: <strong>replace bare commands with "could you"</strong>. "Fix
> this" → "Could you fix this?"; "Send me the doc" → "Could you send me the doc?";
> "Review the PR" → "Could you review the PR?" This one change turns orders into
> polite requests and is right for almost every situation. Then add "when you get a
> chance" (to remove pressure) or more politeness (for bigger asks) as needed. A
> bare command sounds bossy even to a peer; "could you" is warm and appropriate
> nearly everywhere. For a non-native speaker, defaulting to "could you" is the
> easiest way to never sound like you're giving orders.

<br><br>

[name]! Just gently bumping this — no worries if you've been busy 🙂 whenever you get a

most — friendly, no hint of annoyance, "gently bumping," "no worries if you've been
busy" removes any pressure/guilt, keeps it warm despite it being a follow-up.)
<br><br>
<strong>5. Asking your manager for a day off:</strong> "Hi [name]! I'd like to take
next Thursday off if that works — happy to make sure anything time-sensitive is
covered before then. Let me know if that's okay. Thanks!" (Polite, offers to handle
coverage (considerate), gives them the decision, warm.)
<br><br>
The ladder in action: the politeness scales with the ask — a tiny peer ask is light
and casual (1), a normal ask is politely framed with a reason (2), a big ask of a
busy person gets more politeness and acknowledgment of their time (3), a second-chase
gets extra warmth to avoid any annoyance (4), and a personal ask to your manager is
polite and considerate of the impact (5). Throughout: "could you"/"would you," "when
you get a chance"/"no rush," reasons for deadlines, and warmth — never a bare order.

## Phrase Bank

| Situation | Phrase |
|---|---|
| Default polite request | "Could you…?" |
| Bigger ask | "Would you mind…?" / "Would you have time to…?" |
| Remove pressure | "when you get a chance" / "no rush" / "whenever works" |
| Urgent (with reason) | "Could you… today if possible? It's blocking X — sorry for the quick turnaround." |
| Deadline with reason | "Could you get this by [day]? It feeds into [X], so that would really help." |
| Chasing the 2nd time | "Just gently bumping this — no worries if you've been busy!" |

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What's the "request ladder," and why should the politeness level match the
size of the ask?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The request ladder is the range of ways to ask for something, from least to most
polite: <strong>bare command ("Do X") → "Can you X?" → "Could you X?" → "Would you
mind X-ing?" → "When you get a chance, could you possibly X?"</strong> Each rung is
more polite (and more softened) than the last, and the skill is choosing the right
rung for a given request. Why the politeness should match the size of the ask: an
ask imposes on someone — it costs them time, effort, or a favor — and the politeness
level should be <em>proportional to the imposition</em>, for two reasons. (1)
<strong>Too little politeness for a big ask sounds presumptuous or bossy</strong> —
asking a busy senior person for an hour of their time with a bare "Help me debug
this" sounds demanding and entitled, as if you're ordering them; a big imposition
needs more politeness ("Would you have time to help me with this? Totally understand
if you're swamped") to acknowledge that you're asking for something significant and
to show respect for their time and their right to say no. (2) <strong>Too much
politeness for a tiny ask sounds odd or over-formal</strong> — piling elaborate
politeness onto a trivial request ("I would be enormously grateful if you might
possibly, when convenient, consider merging this one-line PR") sounds strange,
stilted, or even sarcastic, because the heavy politeness doesn't match the tiny
imposition; a small ask just needs a light "could you merge this when you get a sec?"
So matching politeness to imposition size is about <em>proportionality</em> — the
politeness signals how much you're asking and how much you respect their time/choice,
and it should be calibrated so a small ask feels light and a big ask feels
appropriately deferential. Getting this right makes your requests feel natural and
considerate; getting it wrong (bossy for big asks, over-formal for small ones) feels
off. The practical calibration: tiny peer asks → casual "can you/could you… when you
get a sec"; normal asks → "could you…?" (the polite default); bigger asks or busy/
senior people → "would you mind" / "would you have time," plus acknowledgment of
their time and flexibility; impositions or second-chases → extra warmth and
pressure-removal. The one universal: <strong>never a bare command</strong> (it sounds
like an order even to a peer) — always at least "could you." For a non-native speaker,
defaulting to "could you" and scaling up the politeness for bigger asks is the
reliable way to make requests that sound like friendly asks rather than orders, which
is exactly the warm tone you want when asking people to do things (a frequent and
tone-sensitive part of work).
</details>

**Q2.** Chasing someone a second time is a request situation where tone matters
especially. Why, and how do you keep the follow-up warm?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Chasing someone a second time is especially tone-sensitive because <strong>a
follow-up inherently risks sounding annoyed, impatient, or accusatory</strong> —
you're pointing out that they didn't respond to your first request, and without care,
that can come across as "you ignored me" or "why haven't you done this yet?", which
creates friction, makes the person defensive or guilty, and damages the relationship.
The second ask carries an implicit "you didn't do what I asked," and if your tone
lets that implication show (even unintentionally — and text strips tone, so it's easy
for a neutral follow-up to read as annoyed, per Lesson 18/the tone lessons), it feels
like a reprimand rather than a friendly reminder. This matters more than the first
request because the first was a neutral ask, while the second has the loaded subtext
of non-response. So you have to actively counteract the risk of sounding annoyed. How
to keep the follow-up warm: (1) <strong>Frame it as a gentle nudge, not a
complaint</strong> — "just gently bumping this," "just a friendly nudge on this,"
"bringing this back to the top of your inbox" — language that signals a light reminder,
not a demand for explanation. (2) <strong>Explicitly remove pressure and give them an
out</strong> — "no worries if you've been busy!", "totally understand if this fell off
your radar," "no rush at all" — this preemptively excuses the non-response, so they
don't feel guilty or defensive; you're signaling you're not annoyed and you assume
good faith (they were busy, not ignoring you). (3) <strong>Keep it warm and
friendly</strong> — a friendly opener, an emoji (🙂), a light tone — so the message
reads as a warm reminder from a considerate colleague, not an impatient chase. (4)
<strong>Don't imply blame</strong> — avoid "you still haven't..." or "I'm still
waiting on..." (which point at their failure); instead center it neutrally ("whenever
you get a chance to look at the doc would be great"). So a good second-chase: "Hey
[name]! Just gently bumping this — no worries if you've been busy 🙂 whenever you get a
chance to look at the doc would be great. Thanks!" — it's a follow-up, but it reads as
warm, patient, and understanding rather than annoyed. The underlying principle: the
follow-up needs <em>more</em> warmth than the original request precisely because it
carries the risk of sounding impatient, so you over-invest in friendliness and
pressure-removal to counteract that risk. This is important for maintaining good
relationships — how you chase people (warmly vs impatiently) significantly affects how
they feel about working with you, and a warm follow-up keeps the relationship good
while still getting your reminder across, whereas an impatient one gets the reminder
across at the cost of goodwill. For your warm-tone goal, mastering the warm follow-up
is high-value because chasing is frequent and it's exactly where tone can slip into
coldness/annoyance without you intending it — so deliberately making follow-ups extra
warm (gentle nudge + remove pressure + no blame + friendly) ensures even your reminders
land as considerate.
</details>

## Homework

Take five requests you need to make (of varying sizes — a tiny peer ask, a normal
one, a bigger ask of someone busy, a follow-up, a personal ask to your manager) and
write each at the right politeness level. Make sure none is a bare command — default
to "could you." Add "when you get a chance"/"no rush" to remove pressure, and a reason
for any deadline. For the follow-up especially, make it extra warm (gentle nudge, no
implied blame). Add to your checklist: "is this a request? then 'could you' (not a
command), politeness matched to size, and pressure removed / reason given."

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds the request-politeness skill and the calibration of politeness to
ask-size. A strong response writes each request at the appropriate rung — light and
casual for a tiny peer ask, politely framed for a normal one, more deferential with
time-acknowledgment for a big ask of a busy person, extra-warm-and-blameless for a
follow-up, and considerate for a personal ask — with none being a bare command. The
key habits it builds: (1) <strong>defaulting to "could you"</strong> instead of bare
commands (the single most important move — it turns orders into polite requests, and
is right for almost everything); (2) <strong>adding pressure-removal</strong> ("when
you get a chance," "no rush") which makes requests considerate rather than demanding;
(3) <strong>giving reasons for deadlines</strong> (which makes them feel legitimate,
not arbitrary); and (4) <strong>scaling politeness to the ask</strong> (light for
small, more deferential for big). The follow-up is the standout practice because it's
the most tone-sensitive (Q2) — making it extra warm with a gentle nudge and no implied
blame is a high-value skill since chasing is frequent and easily slips into sounding
annoyed. The common realization: <strong>your requests may have been landing more like
orders than you intended</strong> — a bare "Fix this" or "Send me the doc," while not
meant as bossy, reads as commanding, and simply adding "could you" and a bit of
warmth transforms it into a friendly ask. This matters a lot for your goal because
asking people to do things is constant at work, and it's a frequent place where tone
can inadvertently be cold or demanding — so mastering warm requests (could you,
matched politeness, pressure removed, reasons given, extra-warm follow-ups) directly
improves how warm and considerate you come across in a high-frequency, high-stakes
tone situation. Add the request check to your checklist: whenever you're asking
someone to do something, verify it's "could you" (not a command), the politeness fits
the size, and you've removed pressure or given a reason. As this becomes automatic,
your requests will consistently sound like friendly asks between colleagues rather
than orders — which is a big part of being the warm, considerate communicator you want
to be. The requests build directly on softeners (Lesson 15 — "could you" and "would
you mind" are request-softeners) and warm framing (Lesson 16). Next: the hardest tone
situations — disagreeing and saying no kindly (where warmth is hardest and most
important), then natural thanks and apologies. Requests, disagreement, and refusals
are the three things that most easily sound cold, so the warmth tools matter most for
them.
</details>
