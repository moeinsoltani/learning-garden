# Lesson 12: Translated-Sounding Sentences

## Concept

Some sentences are grammatically correct but still sound "foreign" — because
they're structured the way your first language structures thoughts, then
translated word-by-word into English. The grammar is fine; the *shape* is wrong.
Learning to catch and reshape these is the last big piece of sounding natural.

```
   TRANSLATED SHAPE                    NATURAL ENGLISH SHAPE
   ────────────────                    ────────────────────
   long wind-up before the point:      point first, then detail:
   "As I was checking the logs and     "I checked the logs. The problem
    considering the various possible     is the database timeout."
    causes, I came to think that the
    problem might be the database..."

   over-formal words in chat:          plain words:
   "Kindly revert to me"               "Let me know" / "get back to me"
   "Please do the needful"             "Please handle it"
```

The most common patterns: (1) **long openings before the point** (English prefers
the point first — a legacy of BLUF, Lesson 13 in leadership), (2) **over-formal
vocabulary** in casual contexts ("kindly," "revert," "do the needful," "utilize"),
and (3) **double politeness stacking** (piling up polite phrases in a way English
doesn't). The fix: point first, plain words, one layer of politeness.

---

## Going Deeper

### English front-loads the point

Many languages build up to the main point at the end; English usually puts the
point *first*, then the supporting detail. A word-by-word translation from a
build-up-first language produces a long wind-up that sounds indirect in English:

- ✗ "As I checked the logs and looked at several possibilities, considering the
  recent changes, I think the issue might be the database." (long wind-up)
- ✓ "I think the issue is the database. I checked the logs and it points to a
  timeout." (point first, then support)

The fix: state your conclusion or main point in the first sentence, then explain.
Short, front-loaded, direct.

### Over-formal vocabulary in casual contexts

Some words feel polite/formal in translation but sound stiff, dated, or odd to
native English speakers in casual work chat:

- "**kindly** do X" → "please do X" or just "could you do X"
- "**revert** to me" (meaning reply) → "get back to me" / "let me know" (in most
  English, "revert" means "go back to a previous state," not "reply")
- "do the **needful**" → "handle it" / "take care of it" (this phrase sounds very
  formal/dated to most native speakers)
- "**utilize**" → "use" (utilize is just a longer "use"; plain "use" is better)
- "**as per** our discussion" → "based on our discussion" / "like we discussed"

### One layer of politeness

Some translations stack multiple politeness markers, which sounds excessive in
English:

- ✗ "I would kindly like to humbly request if you could possibly please review..."
  (too many)
- ✓ "Could you review this when you get a chance?" (one polite frame is enough)

English politeness usually uses *one* softening frame ("could you," "when you get a
chance," "would you mind") — not several stacked together. More isn't more polite;
it sounds awkward. (Phase 3 covers softeners in detail.)

{: .note }
> **The naturalness test: read it aloud</br>**
> The best way to catch translated-sounding sentences is to <em>read them aloud</em>
> and ask "would a native colleague say it this way?" Word-by-word translations often
> <em>look</em> fine but <em>sound</em> off when spoken — the rhythm is wrong, the
> wind-up is too long, the words are too formal. Reading aloud (or imagining a native
> colleague saying it) catches the shape problems that grammar-checking misses. When
> something sounds off even though it's "correct," it's usually a translated shape —
> front-load the point, plainer words, one layer of politeness.

## Lab — Rewrite Drill

Each sentence is grammatically correct but sounds translated/foreign. Naturalize
each — front-load the point, use plainer words, one layer of politeness.

**Your rewrite (naturalize each):**

1. "Kindly revert to me once you have reviewed the document."
2. "As I was investigating the issue and considering the various logs, I have come to the conclusion that the root cause is a memory leak."
3. "I would like to kindly and humbly request if you could please possibly help me with the deployment."
4. "Please do the needful regarding the failing tests."
5. "As per our discussion, I will utilize the new framework for this feature."
6. "Regarding the matter of the API being slow, I wanted to bring to your kind attention that we should perhaps consider adding caching."
7. "It is requested that you provide the access to the staging environment at your earliest convenience."

<details>
<summary>Show Model Rewrites</summary>
<br>
<strong>1.</strong> "Kindly revert to me once you have reviewed the document." →
<strong>"Let me know once you've reviewed the doc."</strong> ("Kindly" → drop it or
"please"; "revert to me" → "let me know" / "get back to me" — "revert" doesn't mean
"reply" in most English.)
<br><br>
<strong>2.</strong> "As I was investigating... I have come to the conclusion that
the root cause is a memory leak." → <strong>"The root cause is a memory leak. I found
it while going through the logs."</strong> (Front-load the point — the conclusion
first, then the how. The long wind-up becomes a short supporting sentence.)
<br><br>
<strong>3.</strong> "I would like to kindly and humbly request if you could please
possibly help me with the deployment." → <strong>"Could you help me with the deploy
when you have a moment?"</strong> (Stacked politeness → one polite frame. "Could
you... when you have a moment" is plenty.)
<br><br>
<strong>4.</strong> "Please do the needful regarding the failing tests." →
<strong>"Could you take a look at the failing tests?"</strong> ("do the needful" is
formal/dated — say what you actually want: take a look / handle / fix.)
<br><br>
<strong>5.</strong> "As per our discussion, I will utilize the new framework." →
<strong>"Like we discussed, I'll use the new framework."</strong> ("As per" → "like
we discussed" / "based on our discussion"; "utilize" → "use.")
<br><br>
<strong>6.</strong> "Regarding the matter of the API being slow, I wanted to bring
to your kind attention that we should perhaps consider adding caching." →
<strong>"The API is slow — I think we should add caching."</strong> (Front-load the
point, cut the formal wind-up ("regarding the matter of," "bring to your kind
attention"), one clear suggestion.)
<br><br>
<strong>7.</strong> "It is requested that you provide the access to the staging
environment at your earliest convenience." → <strong>"Could you give me access to
staging when you get a chance?"</strong> (Passive formal → direct request; "provide
the access" → "give me access" (no "the"); "at your earliest convenience" → "when
you get a chance.")
<br><br>
The patterns: front-load the point (don't wind up to it), use plain words (use not
utilize, let me know not revert, take a look not do the needful), and one layer of
politeness (not stacked). Reading each aloud makes the translated shape obvious — the
naturalized versions sound like a native colleague.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What makes a sentence sound "translated" even when the grammar is correct,
and what are the three main fixes?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
A sentence sounds "translated" when its <strong>structure and word choices follow
your first language's patterns rather than natural English patterns</strong> — the
grammar is correct, but the <em>shape</em> of the thought is wrong for English,
because it was built the way another language builds thoughts and then translated
word-by-word. The grammar-checker passes it (it's technically correct), but a native
speaker senses it's "off" — the rhythm, the order, the register don't match how
English is naturally spoken. The three main fixes: (1) <strong>Front-load the
point</strong> — English usually puts the main point or conclusion <em>first</em>,
then the supporting detail, whereas many languages build up to the point at the end;
a translated sentence often has a long wind-up ("As I was checking the logs and
considering the possibilities, I came to think...") that sounds indirect in English,
and the fix is to state the point first ("The problem is X. I found it in the logs.").
(2) <strong>Use plainer, less formal words</strong> — translated sentences often use
over-formal or dated vocabulary that feels polite in the source language but sounds
stiff, odd, or archaic in English ("kindly," "revert" for reply, "do the needful,"
"utilize," "as per"); the fix is plain everyday words ("please" or nothing, "let me
know," "handle it," "use," "like we discussed"). (3) <strong>One layer of
politeness</strong> — some translations stack multiple politeness markers ("I would
kindly humbly request if you could please possibly...") in a way that sounds excessive
and awkward in English, which typically uses just one softening frame ("could you...
when you get a chance?"); the fix is a single polite frame, not several stacked. The
underlying issue is that translating word-by-word or thought-shape-by-thought-shape
from another language produces English that's <em>correct but not idiomatic</em> —
and idiom (the natural shape and word choices) is what the last stretch of sounding
natural is about. The best detection method: <strong>read it aloud</strong> and ask
"would a native colleague say it this way?" — translated shapes look fine on the page
but sound off when spoken (the wind-up drags, the words are too formal, the politeness
is piled up), and reading aloud catches what grammar-checking misses. When something
sounds off despite being correct, it's usually a translated shape, and the three fixes
(point first, plain words, one layer of politeness) naturalize it.
</details>

**Q2.** "Kindly revert to me at your earliest convenience regarding the matter" — why
does this sound translated to a native speaker, and how would you naturalize it?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
This sentence hits several translated-sounding markers at once, which is why it reads
as distinctly non-native to a native English speaker even though it's grammatically
fine. The specific problems: (1) <strong>"Kindly"</strong> — used as a politeness
marker before a request, "kindly" sounds formal, dated, and slightly stiff in modern
English (it's common in some varieties of English influenced by other languages, but
sounds unusual to many native speakers); natural English uses "please" or just a
polite question form. (2) <strong>"revert to me"</strong> — this is the big one:
"revert" in standard English means "return to a previous state" (revert a commit,
revert to an old version), NOT "reply/respond," so "revert to me" (meaning "reply to
me") sounds wrong or confusing to a native speaker — they'd expect "get back to me,"
"reply," or "let me know." (3) <strong>"at your earliest convenience"</strong> — a
very formal, slightly old-fashioned phrase; natural English says "when you get a
chance" or "when you can." (4) <strong>"regarding the matter"</strong> — formal and
vague; natural English says "about [the specific thing]" or just names it. Stacked
together, these make the sentence sound like a formal translation rather than
something a native colleague would write in chat. Naturalized: <strong>"Let me know
about [the specific thing] when you get a chance."</strong> or <strong>"Could you get
back to me on X when you have a moment?"</strong> — the fixes: "kindly" → drop it or
use a polite question form; "revert to me" → "let me know" / "get back to me" (the
correct meaning); "at your earliest convenience" → "when you get a chance"; "regarding
the matter" → "about [the actual thing]" (and name it specifically rather than saying
"the matter"). The naturalized version is shorter, plainer, uses the correct word for
"reply," and sounds like normal workplace English. This is a good example because it
concentrates several common translated-sounding patterns, and it shows how the fixes
(plain words, correct idiom, one layer of politeness, naming things specifically)
transform a correct-but-foreign sentence into natural English. The "revert" example
is especially worth remembering because it's not just stiff — it's actually using a
word to mean something it doesn't mean in standard English, which can cause genuine
confusion, not just an odd tone. For your writing, watching for these specific markers
(kindly, revert, do the needful, as per, at your earliest convenience, regarding the
matter) and replacing them with the plain equivalents removes some of the most
recognizable non-native-formal patterns.
</details>

## Homework

Take three of your recent messages and read each one aloud, asking "would a native
colleague say it this way?" Look for the three translated patterns: long wind-ups
before the point (front-load it), over-formal words (kindly, revert, utilize, as per
— use plain words), and stacked politeness (use one frame). Rewrite any that sound
off. Then notice which pattern is most yours — do you wind up before the point, use
formal vocabulary, or over-stack politeness? Add it to your checklist as a "read it
aloud" naturalness check.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds the naturalness check — reading aloud and asking "would a native
colleague say it this way?" — which is the best tool for catching translated shapes
that grammar-checking misses. A strong response identifies which of the three patterns
is most yours (winding up before the point, over-formal vocabulary, or stacked
politeness) and reshapes the messages accordingly. The common findings depend on your
first language's patterns: some languages build to the point at the end, producing
long wind-ups (fix: front-load the conclusion); some have a formal-politeness register
that translates to over-formal English words (fix: plain words — the kindly/revert/
utilize/as-per family); some stack politeness markers (fix: one frame). Identifying
which is <em>yours</em> makes your naturalness check targeted. The key tool is
<strong>reading aloud</strong> — translated shapes look fine on the page (correct
grammar) but sound off when spoken (wrong rhythm, too formal, too indirect), so
reading aloud (or imagining a native colleague saying it) surfaces the shape problems
that silent reading and grammar-checkers miss. This is a different kind of check than
Phase 1's accuracy checks: it's not about correctness (the sentences are correct)
but about <em>idiom</em> and <em>register</em> — sounding like natural English rather
than translated English. It's the last big piece of sounding natural, on top of Phase
1's accuracy and Phase 2's word choice (do/make, phrasal verbs). The reassuring point:
you don't need to eliminate every trace of your first language (a slight accent in
writing is fine and human) — you need to catch the specific patterns that make writing
sound noticeably translated (the long wind-ups, the formal-dated vocabulary, the
stacked politeness), which are a small set, and the read-aloud check catches them.
Add the "read it aloud — does this sound like a native colleague?" check to your
checklist for important messages, especially watching for your specific pattern. As
you catch and reshape these over time, your writing sheds the translated feel and
sounds increasingly natural — which, combined with accuracy (Phase 1) and natural word
choice (Phase 2's other lessons), gets you most of the way to writing like a native
colleague. Next: concise word choice — saying the same thing in fewer, plainer words,
which is the last lesson of Phase 2 and complements the naturalness work here (concise
+ natural + accurate = clear, good writing).
</details>
