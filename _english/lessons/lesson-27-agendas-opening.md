# Lesson 27: Agendas and Opening a Meeting

## Concept

A meeting lives or dies in its first two minutes. If people leave knowing *why they were
there* and *what was decided*, it worked; if they leave unsure what the point was, it
didn't — and that's usually set by the agenda and the opening. As a lead you'll run
meetings, and the words you use to open one shape whether it's focused or drifts.

```
   THE OPENING (first 60-90 seconds):
   ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │ WHY we're here (the purpose / goal of today)   │
   │ WHAT we'll cover (the agenda, briefly)         │
   │ WHAT we need by the end (the outcome/decision) │
   │ → then hand off to the first item              │
   └────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

   "Thanks for coming. The goal today is to decide X.
    We'll cover A, then B. By the end I'd like a decision on X.
    Let's start with A — [name], want to kick us off?"
```

An agenda sent ahead lets people prepare; a clear verbal opening orients everyone in the
room. Both answer the same question — *why are we here and what does done look like?* —
which is the single most useful thing a meeting can make obvious up front.

---

## How It Works

### The agenda (sent ahead)

A good agenda, shared before the meeting, lists: the **purpose** (one line — why this
meeting exists), the **topics** (with rough time or priority), and any **prep** (what to
read or think about beforehand). It lets people decide if they need to attend, prepare so
the meeting is productive, and know what "done" looks like. Even a three-line agenda in
the calendar invite ("Goal: decide on the caching approach. We'll review the two options
and pick one. Please skim the design doc first.") transforms a meeting.

### The verbal opening

Even with an agenda, open the meeting out loud — people don't all re-read the invite.
In the first 60–90 seconds, state:

- **Why we're here** — the purpose ("The goal today is to decide our caching approach").
- **What we'll cover** — the agenda, briefly ("We'll look at the two options, then pick").
- **What we need by the end** — the outcome ("By the end I'd like a decision we all
  own").

Then hand off to the first item. This orients everyone, sets the destination, and signals
you're running a focused meeting.

### Timeboxing and the parking lot

State roughly how long each part will take, and when a tangent comes up, "park" it: "Good
point — let me add that to a parking lot so we don't lose it, and we'll come back if we
have time." This keeps the meeting on track without shutting people down (you're saving
their point, not dismissing it). The parking lot is a visible list of off-topic items to
revisit later or take offline.

### For recurring meetings

Standing meetings (standups, syncs) still benefit from a light structure and a clear
opening — even "Let's do a quick round: what you're on, anything blocked" orients people.
And periodically ask "is this meeting still useful?" — the best meeting is sometimes a
cancelled one (a message would do).

{: .note }
> **The first two minutes decide whether a meeting works**
> A meeting's success is largely set by whether people know, from the start, *why they're
> there* and *what done looks like*. The agenda (sent ahead) and the verbal opening both
> answer that — purpose, topics, desired outcome. Skip it and the meeting drifts (people
> unsure of the point, tangents unchecked, no clear decision); do it and the meeting is
> focused and productive. As a lead, opening meetings clearly is a high-leverage habit —
> a small amount of structure that makes everyone's time count, and marks you as someone
> who runs meetings well (a visible leadership skill).

<summary>Show Model Answers</summary>
<br>
<strong>1. Vague opening → clear opening:</strong> "Thanks for coming, everyone. The goal
today is to <strong>decide our caching approach</strong> so we can start building next

end I'd like a decision we all feel good about. Let's start with the Redis option —
Priya, want to walk us through it?" (Why (decide caching approach) + why it matters (so we

hand-off to the first item. Confident and oriented, versus the meandering "I guess we're
here to talk about the caching thing.")
<br><br>
<strong>2. Three-line agenda:</strong> "<strong>Goal:</strong> Decide our caching
approach (Redis vs. in-process) so we can start building next week.<br>
<strong>Agenda:</strong> (1) Quick recap of the two options (5 min), (2) discuss
trade-offs (15 min), (3) decide (10 min).<br>
<strong>Prep:</strong> Please skim the caching design doc [link] beforehand so we can go
straight to the discussion." (Purpose + topics with rough timing + prep — lets people
come ready and know what done looks like. Three lines transform a bare "Caching sync"
invite.)
<br><br>
<strong>3. Redirect a tangent warmly:</strong> "That's a really good point about the
deploy pipeline — I don't want to lose it. Let me drop it in the parking lot and we'll
either come back at the end or I'll set up a separate chat on it. For now, let's keep
focused on the caching decision so we get that settled — where were we…" (Validates their
point (good point, don't want to lose it), parks it visibly (so they know it's saved, not
dismissed), and redirects to the goal — keeping the meeting on track without making them
feel shut down.)
<br><br>
The patterns: open with why/what/desired-outcome then hand off; a three-line agenda
(goal/topics/prep) beats a bare title; and redirect tangents by parking them (validate +
save + refocus), not dismissing them.
</details>

## Phrase Bank

| Moment | Phrase |
|---|---|
| Open with purpose | "The goal today is to [decide/align on/figure out] X." |
| State the outcome | "By the end I'd like [a decision / a plan / everyone aligned] on X." |
| Preview the agenda | "We'll cover A, then B, then decide." |
| Hand off | "Let's start with A — [name], want to kick us off?" |
| Park a tangent | "Good point — let me add that to a parking lot so we don't lose it." |
| Timebox | "Let's spend about 10 min here, then move on." |
| Close the loop | "So, to confirm — we've decided X. [name] will do Y." |

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What should the first 60–90 seconds of a meeting communicate, and why does
opening this way matter so much?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The first 60–90 seconds should communicate three things: (1) <strong>why we're here</strong>
— the purpose or goal of the meeting ("the goal today is to decide our caching approach");
(2) <strong>what we'll cover</strong> — the agenda, briefly ("we'll review the two
options, then pick"); and (3) <strong>what we need by the end</strong> — the desired
outcome ("by the end I'd like a decision we all own"). Then you hand off to the first
item. Why this matters so much: <strong>a meeting's success is largely determined by
whether people know, from the start, why they're there and what "done" looks like</strong>.
If people are oriented up front — they know the purpose, the plan, and the target outcome
— the meeting is focused: everyone's pulling toward the same goal, tangents are easier to
check against "does this serve the goal?", and there's a clear finish line (the decision/
outcome). If people <em>aren't</em> oriented — the meeting starts vaguely ("so, yeah, the
caching thing…") — it drifts: people are unsure of the point, discussion wanders,
tangents go unchecked, and it may end without a clear decision (and people leave unsure
what was accomplished). The opening is high-leverage because it's a tiny investment (60–90
seconds) that shapes the whole meeting — orienting everyone at the start prevents the
drift that wastes the entire meeting's time. It also sets a tone: opening crisply signals
you're running a focused meeting and respects everyone's time, which both makes the meeting
better and marks you as someone who runs meetings well (a visible leadership skill —
people notice who runs a tight, purposeful meeting versus a meandering one). So the
opening isn't a formality; it's the single highest-leverage moment for making the meeting
work, and it's the same principle as leading with the point in writing (Lesson 21) applied
to meetings: state the destination first so everyone can navigate toward it.
</details>

**Q2.** Someone raises a good but off-topic point mid-meeting. Why is "parking" it better
than either following the tangent or shutting the person down — and how do you park it
warmly?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
Parking an off-topic point is better than the two alternatives because it avoids both of
their costs. If you <strong>follow the tangent</strong>, the meeting derails: you spend
the limited time on something that isn't the meeting's purpose, and may not get to the
actual goal (the decision you came to make) — the tangent, even if it's a good point,
crowds out the reason everyone's there. If you <strong>shut the person down</strong>
("that's off-topic, let's move on"), you get back on track but at a cost: the person feels
dismissed, their (possibly valuable) point is lost, and you've discouraged people from
speaking up (which hurts participation — people who get shut down stop contributing). So
following the tangent sacrifices the meeting's goal; shutting down sacrifices the point
and the person's goodwill. <strong>Parking</strong> gets the benefits of both without the
costs: you acknowledge and save the point (so it's not lost and the person feels heard),
AND you keep the meeting focused on its goal (so you don't derail). It's the both/and
move. How to park it warmly: (1) <strong>validate the point</strong> — "that's a really
good point, I don't want to lose it" — so the person feels heard, not dismissed; (2)
<strong>save it visibly</strong> — "let me add it to the parking lot" (or "I'll note it
and we'll come back / set up a separate chat") — so it's genuinely captured, not brushed
off (the visible saving is what makes it feel respected rather than shut down); (3)
<strong>redirect to the goal</strong> — "for now let's keep focused on the caching
decision so we get it settled" — explaining why (to reach the goal) so the redirect is
clearly about the meeting's purpose, not about their point being unwelcome; and (4)
<strong>follow through</strong> — actually revisit parked items (at the end, or in a
follow-up) so "parking lot" doesn't become "black hole." Done this way, parking respects
both the meeting's focus and the person — the person's point is safe and will be addressed,
and the meeting stays on track. It's a small but important facilitation skill: keeping a
meeting focused <em>and</em> keeping people feeling heard, which is exactly the balance a
good meeting-runner strikes (and it uses the warmth tools — validate + soften + explain —
from Phase 3, applied live).
</details>

## Homework

the invite. Then open the meeting out loud with the why / what / desired-outcome in the
first 60–90 seconds, and hand off to the first item. When a tangent comes up, park it
warmly (validate + save + refocus) rather than following it or shutting it down. Notice
whether the meeting feels more focused. Add to your meeting habits: "opened with why/what/
outcome? parked tangents? closed with the decision?"
