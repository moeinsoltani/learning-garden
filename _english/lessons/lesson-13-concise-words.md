# Lesson 13: Concise Word Choice

## Concept

Good workplace writing is concise — it says the same thing in fewer, plainer words.
Wordiness makes messages harder to read and buries the point; cutting the excess
makes writing clearer and more confident. This is a skill that helps everyone, but
it especially helps non-native writers, because shorter and plainer is easier to get
right (fewer words = fewer chances for error) and reads better.

```
   WORDY                                CONCISE
   ─────                                ───────
   "at this point in time"              "now"
   "in order to"                        "to"
   "due to the fact that"               "because"
   "I wanted to reach out to let         "Quick question: …"
    you know that I had a question"
   "make a decision"                    "decide"  (sometimes)
   "in the event that"                  "if"
```

Two moves do most of the work: **cut filler** (words that add length but no meaning
— very, really, actually, basically, just; and wordy phrases like "at this point in
time") and **prefer plain, short words** (use over utilize, start over commence, help
over facilitate). The goal isn't to be terse or cold — it's to remove the words that
don't earn their place, keeping the warmth and meaning.

---

## How It Works

### Cut the filler words

Some words add length without adding meaning. In most sentences they can be deleted:

- **very, really, actually, basically, quite, just** — often removable: "this is
  **very** important" → "this is important" (or use a stronger word: "this is
  critical"). "I **just** wanted to ask" → "I wanted to ask" (though *just* can also
  soften — Phase 3; here it's often filler).
- **wordy phrases** → short words: "at this point in time" → "now"; "in order to" →
  "to"; "due to the fact that" → "because"; "in the event that" → "if"; "a large
  number of" → "many."

### Prefer plain, short words

Longer, fancier words rarely improve writing — plain words are clearer and more
natural:

- utilize → **use**
- commence → **start** / begin
- facilitate → **help** / make easier
- endeavor → **try**
- in the vicinity of → **near** / about
- terminate → **end** / stop

Simple beats fancy: the plain word is almost always better in workplace writing.
(This connects to Lesson 12 — fancy words often sound translated/formal too.)

### One verb beats verb+noun (sometimes)

Sometimes a single verb replaces a verb+noun phrase and is tighter:

- "make a decision" → "decide" (when appropriate)
- "give consideration to" → "consider"
- "conduct an investigation" → "investigate"
- "provide assistance" → "help"

But don't over-apply this — sometimes the verb+noun is natural and fine ("make a
change" is more natural than "change" as a noun in some contexts). Use it where the
single verb is cleaner, not mechanically.

### Cut redundant pairs

Some phrases say the same thing twice:

- "end **result**" → "result" (a result is already an end)
- "**future** plans" → "plans" (plans are already future)
- "**advance** planning," "**close** proximity," "**basic** fundamentals" — the
  first word is redundant.

{: .note }
> **Concise, not cold</br>**
> Cutting words is about removing what doesn't earn its place (filler, wordy
> phrases, fancy words, redundancy) — NOT about stripping out warmth or becoming
> terse. A concise message can still be warm: "Thanks so much — could you take a
> look when you get a chance?" is concise AND warm. The words to cut are the ones
> that add length without adding meaning OR warmth; keep the ones that add either.
> Don't confuse "concise" with "curt" (Lesson 8's cold-tone problem) — the goal is
> clear and warm, just without the padding.

## Lab — Rewrite Drill

Cut this 120-word update to about 70 words without losing meaning OR warmth. Then
compare with the model.

**Your rewrite (cut to ~70 words, keep it warm):**

"Hi team! I just wanted to reach out and let you all know that at this point in time,
I have basically finished the work on the authentication feature that I was working
on. Due to the fact that there were a large number of edge cases, it actually took a
bit longer than I had originally anticipated, but the end result is that everything
is now working as expected. In order to make sure that everything is good, I would
really appreciate it if someone could possibly do a review of the PR when they get a
chance. Thanks so much everyone!"

<details>
<summary>Show Model Rewrite</summary>
<br>
<strong>~70-word version (warm, concise):</strong> "Hi team! I've finished the
authentication feature. It took a bit longer than expected because there were many
edge cases, but everything's working now. Could someone review the PR when you get a
chance? Thanks so much! 🙏"
<br><br>
That's about 40 words — even tighter than the target, and it keeps all the meaning
and warmth. What got cut and why: "just wanted to reach out and let you all know
that" → deleted (pure filler wind-up — just say the point); "at this point in time"
→ "now" (implied); "basically finished" → "finished" (basically is filler);
"the work on the... that I was working on" → "the authentication feature" (redundant);
"due to the fact that" → "because"; "a large number of" → "many"; "it actually took"
→ "it took" (actually is filler); "originally anticipated" → "expected" (simpler);
"the end result is that everything is now working as expected" → "everything's working
now"; "in order to make sure that everything is good, I would really appreciate it if
someone could possibly do a review of the PR" → "could someone review the PR" (all the
padding removed, one clean request). Crucially, the warmth stayed: "Hi team!",
"Thanks so much!", the emoji, and the friendly "when you get a chance" — concise
didn't mean cold. The message is now easy to read, gets to the point, and still feels
warm and human — which is the goal.
</details>

## Checkpoint

**Q1.** What are the two main moves for making writing more concise, and why does
concise writing help non-native writers specifically?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The two main moves: (1) <strong>Cut filler</strong> — remove words and phrases that
add length without adding meaning: filler words (very, really, actually, basically,
just — often deletable) and wordy phrases that have short equivalents ("at this point
in time" → "now," "in order to" → "to," "due to the fact that" → "because," "a large
number of" → "many"). These are pure padding — they make the message longer to read
without conveying anything, so cutting them makes writing clearer and gets to the
point faster. (2) <strong>Prefer plain, short words</strong> — use the simple word
over the fancy one: "use" not "utilize," "start" not "commence," "help" not
"facilitate," "try" not "endeavor." Plain words are clearer, more natural, and easier
to read; fancy words rarely improve writing and often make it sound stiff or
translated (Lesson 12). (Two supporting moves: replace verb+noun with a single verb
where it's cleaner — "decide" not "make a decision" — and cut redundant pairs — "end
result" → "result.") Why concise writing helps non-native writers <em>specifically</em>:
(a) <strong>Fewer words means fewer chances for error</strong> — every word is a
potential mistake (wrong article, wrong preposition, agreement error, awkward
phrasing), so a shorter message has fewer places to go wrong, making accuracy easier;
the padding you cut was extra error surface. (b) <strong>Plain, short words are easier
to use correctly</strong> — simple everyday words are ones you know well and use
right, while fancy words are more likely to be used slightly wrong or in unnatural
ways; sticking to plain words plays to your strengths. (c) <strong>Shorter, plainer
writing reads better and clearer regardless of who wrote it</strong> — so conciseness
directly improves your writing's quality and readability, and it's a skill where
"less" genuinely is "more." (d) It compounds with the other lessons: shorter sentences
(Lesson 7) plus concise word choice plus plain words plus natural phrasing (Lesson 12)
all point the same direction — toward clear, simple, natural writing that's easier to
get right and better to read. The important caveat (Q2): concise doesn't mean cold —
you cut the words that add neither meaning nor warmth, but keep the ones that add
warmth (the friendly opener, the "thanks," the "when you get a chance"). So conciseness
for a non-native writer is a double win: it makes writing clearer AND easier to get
right, by cutting the padding that was both extra reading and extra error-surface —
while keeping the warmth that matters for tone. Plain and concise is not a limitation;
it's the target that both improves your writing and makes it more accurate.
</details>

**Q2.** How do you make writing concise without making it cold? What's the difference
between cutting "padding" and cutting "warmth"?

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
You make writing concise without making it cold by <strong>cutting only the words
that add neither meaning nor warmth (padding), while keeping the words that add
warmth even if they don't add strict information</strong>. The distinction: (1)
<strong>Padding</strong> — filler words (very, really, actually, basically), wordy
phrases ("at this point in time," "in order to," "due to the fact that"), fancy words
(utilize, commence), redundancy (end result), and empty wind-ups ("I just wanted to
reach out and let you know that...") — these add length and reading effort but convey
nothing and warm nothing; they're pure cost, so cut them. (2) <strong>Warmth</strong>
— friendly openers ("Hi team!"), gratitude ("thanks so much!"), softening frames
("when you get a chance"), acknowledgment ("nice work!"), and human touches (an
emoji) — these may not add <em>information</em>, but they add <em>warmth and human
connection</em>, which is a real and valuable thing a message carries (especially for
your goal of warm communication), so keep them. The key insight is that conciseness
targets <em>informational and stylistic</em> padding (words that don't earn their
place on meaning or warmth), NOT <em>relational</em> content (words that carry
warmth). Cutting "I just wanted to reach out and let you know that" is cutting padding
(a meaningless wind-up); cutting "thanks so much!" would be cutting warmth (a genuine
relational signal) — and you should do the first, never the second. The failure mode
to avoid is confusing "concise" with "curt" (Lesson 8's cold-tone problem): stripping
a message down to just the bare information ("Finished auth. Review the PR.") is
concise but cold — it cut the warmth along with the padding, and reads as terse or
abrupt. The right target is concise <em>and</em> warm: "I've finished the auth
feature. Could someone review the PR when you get a chance? Thanks so much! 🙏" — this
cut all the padding (it's much shorter than a wordy version) but kept all the warmth
(the friendly request frame, the thanks, the emoji), so it's clear AND friendly. The
test for each word/phrase you might cut: "does this add meaning OR warmth?" If it adds
neither (it's padding), cut it. If it adds either (information or warmth), keep it. This
lets you get the benefits of conciseness (clarity, readability, and for non-native
writers, fewer errors) without the cost of coldness — which matters a lot for your
goal, since warm tone is exactly what you're aiming for. Concise and warm aren't in
tension; the words that make writing warm are different from the words that make it
padded, so you can cut the padding freely while keeping the warmth. The lab
demonstrated this: the 120-word version was both wordy AND warm; the concise version
was much shorter but kept every warm element — proving you can have conciseness and
warmth together, which is the goal (clear, accurate, natural, AND warm — the
combination this whole track builds toward).
</details>

## Homework

Take a recent message you wrote that felt a bit long or wordy. Cut it by about a
third: delete filler words (very, really, actually, basically, just-as-filler),
replace wordy phrases with short ones (at this point in time → now, in order to → to),
swap fancy words for plain ones (utilize → use), and cut any empty wind-ups (I just
wanted to reach out to let you know that...). Crucially: keep every bit of warmth (the
openers, thanks, softeners). Compare the before and after — is it clearer? Still warm?
Note your most common padding pattern for your checklist.

**Your answer:**

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>
<br>
The exercise builds the conciseness skill while protecting warmth — the double goal of
clear-and-warm writing. A strong response cuts the message by roughly a third (or more)
by removing padding (filler words, wordy phrases, fancy words, empty wind-ups) while
deliberately keeping every warm element (openers, thanks, softeners, emoji), and the
before/after comparison shows the concise version is clearer and easier to read
<em>without</em> being colder. The common realization: <strong>a lot of your writing
length is padding, not content</strong> — the wind-ups ("I just wanted to reach out
and let you know that..."), the filler (very, really, basically, actually), the wordy
phrases (at this point in time, in order to, due to the fact that), and the fancy
words (utilize, commence) add length and reading effort but convey nothing, and cutting
them makes the message noticeably tighter and clearer while losing no meaning. And
crucially, the exercise shows that <strong>concise and warm coexist</strong> — cutting
the padding doesn't require cutting the warmth, because they're different kinds of
words (padding adds neither meaning nor warmth; warmth words add warmth); a message
can be much shorter AND still friendly (as the lab's 120→40 word version demonstrated,
keeping "Hi team!", "thanks so much!", and the emoji). The most common padding pattern
to identify for your checklist is often the empty wind-up ("I just wanted to reach out
to let you know that...") — a very common way to start messages that adds nothing and
delays the point, and simply cutting it (starting with the actual point) tightens
messages immediately. Other common patterns: filler words (very/really/basically),
wordy phrases (at this point in time), and fancy words (utilize). Add your pattern to
your checklist as a "cut the padding" check for messages that feel long. This closes
Phase 2 (Words That Sound Natural): you now have accuracy (Phase 1) plus natural word
choice — the right verbs (do/make, say/tell), phrasal verbs for natural register,
naturalized sentence shapes (not translated), and concise plain wording. Together these
make your writing accurate AND natural AND clear. The reassuring theme across Phase 2:
sounding natural is largely about <em>using the natural forms</em> (the conventional
collocations, the phrasal verbs, the English sentence shapes, the plain concise words)
rather than the grammatically-plausible-but-unusual alternatives — and it's learnable
through the targeted awareness these lessons built (which chunks, which phrasal verbs,
which shapes, which padding to cut). The next phase (Tone & Warmth) builds on this
natural, accurate foundation to focus specifically on your central goal: sounding warm
and soft rather than cold or blunt — the register, softeners, warm openers/closers, and
kind ways to disagree, request, and say no that make your messages land as friendly as
you intend. Accuracy and naturalness (Phases 1–2) are the foundation; warmth (Phase 3)
is what you most wanted, and it sits right on top.
</details>
